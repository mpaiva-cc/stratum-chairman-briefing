00 · Headline read

Where Stratum can credibly win.

15Five's distinguishing positioning is manager effectiveness — coaching managers to run better 1:1s, deliver feedback, hit OKRs. The product surface is weekly check-ins, 1:1s, OKRs, pulse surveys, high-fives, performance reviews [1][2]. The AI surface is two named products: Kona (joins 1:1s in Zoom/Meet/Teams, captures wins/blockers/action items, filters out personal-conversation noise) and AMAYA (AI thought partner that lets HR leaders ask plain-language questions about performance, engagement, retention, compensation, adoption across a single connected dataset) [1][2][3]. Both are scoped, named, and operationally credible.

This is a complementary partner for Stratum, not a competitor. 15Five wins continuous-feedback and coaching-led deals; Culture Amp wins benchmarking-led deals; Lattice wins structured-performance-led deals. Stratum's recruiting + people-graph fits naturally underneath any of them — new hires from Stratum Recruiter flowing into 15Five manager-coaching workflows via the graph.

01 · Company overview

Manager-effectiveness with two named AI products.

15Five was founded in 2011 in San Francisco by David Hassell. The product started as a continuous-feedback weekly-check-in tool and has grown into a performance + engagement platform with a distinctive coaching-oriented design philosophy. Customer base concentrates in growth-stage tech, professional services, and creative industries. 2025–2026's strategic moves are the two named AI products: Kona (meeting coach) and AMAYA (insights thought partner) [1][2][3].

02 · Positioning

"Coaching managers" — continuous feedback, light-touch reviews.

15Five positions as the platform that makes managers better. Implicit critique: structured-review-cycle tools (Lattice) and engagement-survey tools (Culture Amp) both focus on the system; 15Five focuses on the manager-employee relationship. The AI products (Kona, AMAYA) reinforce this — both are designed to make managers and HR leaders more effective at their daily work, not to automate work away.

03 · Target customers

Small to mid-market tech and growth.

  • Core ICP: 50–2,000 employees, growth-stage tech and professional services, coaching-culture organizations.
  • Geography: US-anchored.
  • Not their fit: Enterprise (Workday Talent owns that), pure-engagement-survey (Culture Amp), benchmarking-heavy (Culture Amp), structured-review-heavy at large scale (Lattice).
04 · Product strengths

What 15Five actually does well.

  • Manager-coaching design philosophy. The product is built around making managers better; rare focus in HR-tech.
  • Kona, the AI meeting coach. Joins 1:1s in Zoom/Meet/Teams, captures wins/blockers/action items, filters personal-conversation noise — a specific, named, scoped AI product [1][2].
  • AMAYA, the AI thought partner for people insights. Plain-language Q&A across performance, engagement, retention, compensation, adoption [1][3].
  • Approachable UX. "Clean and approachable interface that doesn't feel like enterprise software" [1].
  • Continuous-feedback rhythm. Weekly check-ins, 1:1s, OKRs, high-fives — the cadence is built in.
05 · AI claims — verified vs marketing

What is real, what is copy.

Verified AI capabilities

  • Kona — meeting-coach AI in Zoom/Meet/Teams [1][2]
  • Kona's personal-conversation filter — explicitly scoped to performance-relevant content [1]
  • AMAYA — plain-language Q&A across people-data domains [1][3]
  • Connected dataset across engagement, performance, turnover, compensation, adoption [1]

Marketing claims (unverified or thin)

  • No public MCP, no agent specification
  • Kona-Zoom/Meet/Teams privacy boundary — claim of filtering personal content; methodology not deeply published
  • No public AEDT bias-audit (performance AI has fairness concerns even outside hiring)
  • No third-party audit relationship named
06 · Compliance & accessibility

Posture: standard certifications.

  • Security: SOC 2, GDPR. Standard.
  • Bias auditing: No public AEDT page; performance + 1:1-summarization AI raises distinct fairness concerns (calibration, promotion decisions). Worth published evidence; absent.
  • Privacy of meeting-content: Kona's design includes explicit personal-conversation filter — operationally credible but not independently audited.
  • EU AI Act: Performance/feedback AI is plausibly Annex-III-adjacent; no public conformance.
07 · Recent launches

What 15Five shipped.

  • 2025–2026 · AMAYA, AI thought partner. People-data Q&A [1][3].
  • Through 2025 · Connected-dataset expansion. Engagement + performance + turnover + comp + adoption unified.
08 · Customer complaints

Recurring patterns.

Pattern 1 · Depth at enterprise scale
"Less suited for very large companies that need meticulous data and tools."

Culture Amp wins on enterprise scale. [1][4]

Pattern 2 · Structured-review depth
"Lattice's structured review tools are deeper for formal performance cycles."

Tradeoff of the coaching-led philosophy. [3]

Pattern 3 · Benchmarking absence
"No benchmarking corpus comparable to Culture Amp's 1B+ responses."

Data-network-effect gap. [1][2]

Pattern 4 · Reporting customization
"Complex reporting requires workarounds; export to BI tools sometimes needed."

Standard.

Pattern 5 · Kona conversation-filter privacy concerns
"Some users uncomfortable with AI joining 1:1s, even with personal-content filter."

Cultural rather than technical; relevant for buyer trust conversation.

Pattern 6 · Integration breadth
"Integrates with major HRIS but depth varies; HRIS-of-record handling is partner-dependent."

Standard.

09 · Differentiation opportunities for Stratum

Where Stratum can credibly own ground.

Dimension
Stratum's defensible ground
01 · Partnership, not competition
Stratum Recruiter feeds 15Five manager coaching

New hires flow into Kona-coached 1:1s via people-graph. Build the integration.

02 · Agent specification publication
Documented agents vs named-product AI

Kona and AMAYA are well-scoped named products but not customer-extensible agent specifications. Stratum's MCP architecture is the platform-extensibility story.

03 · Privacy-design as inspectable artifact
Kona's filter is a claim; Stratum's eval-set is the artifact

Kona's personal-content filter is operationally credible but not independently auditable. Stratum's continuous eval-set can include explicit probes for meeting-content sensitivity — a concrete artifact 15Five does not match.

04 · Recruiting-outcome to manager-coaching telemetry
Closing the loop from hire to coach

Stratum's hire data + 15Five's manager-coaching data is a joint analytics surface no one else owns end-to-end.

05 · Governance as artifact
Eval set + ledger + Compact refusals

Standard wedge; performance-AI fairness probes specifically applicable.

06 · Honest naming of AI products
Stratum can adopt the same discipline

15Five's two named, scoped AI products is honest and credible — a contrast to vendors with "AI-everywhere" framing. Stratum should adopt the same discipline: name agents, scope them, publish their spec. This is a category practice worth borrowing.

10 · Eglin's recommendation

The play.

Partnership-shaped. Build the integration, publish a joint architectural picture (Stratum Recruiter → people-graph → 15Five manager coaching), be visible at deals where recruiting + manager-effectiveness are both buying criteria.

Single most important insight. 15Five's two named, scoped AI products (Kona, AMAYA) is the most disciplined AI-product naming in this competitor set. Stratum should adopt the same practice: name agents, scope them, publish what they do and don't do. This is a category-level positioning lesson worth borrowing from a partner.
