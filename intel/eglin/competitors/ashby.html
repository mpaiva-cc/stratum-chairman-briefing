00 · Headline read

Where Stratum can credibly win.

Ashby is a more capable AI competitor than Greenhouse. They actually ship AI features the market endorses, they have a real third-party bias audit (FairNow), and their customer base is the AI category's own buying signal (Cursor, OpenAI, Lovable, Granola) [1][6]. The bias-audit story that Stratum could cleanly own against Greenhouse is contested against Ashby.

The wins available to Stratum here are narrower and more technical: (1) MCP completeness today — Ashby's MCP is still "upcoming," no tool list, no permission model published [1]; Stratum has four servers shipping. (2) Continuous self-published governance — Ashby's bias audit is third-party periodic; Stratum's eval set, decisions ledger, and Compact-published refusals are operational and continuous. The "and" beats the "or." (3) Recruiter-console clarity for buyers who find Ashby "over-engineered" [7][8]. Mid-market less-AI-native buyers are a real segment.

01 · Company overview

The AI category's own ATS.

Ashby, founded in 2018 by Benjamin Encz and Abhik Pramanik and headquartered in San Francisco, is a recruiting platform best known among AI-native and growth-stage technology buyers [4][5]. Customer base grew from 2,400 to 4,400+ over the prior twelve months with >100% revenue growth [5]. July 2025 Series D of $50M (co-led by F-Prime Capital and Gaingels) brought total funding to $128M [4][5]. Named customers include Cursor, Granola, Lovable, OpenAI, and Shopify [1][5]. The company's most cited proof point is its share of the Forbes AI 50: ~70% of the 2026 list, up from ~50% the prior year [1].

On 2026-05-07, at the Ashby One customer event, Ashby announced an AI-native expansion: Custom Agents (GA), Ashby Assistant (GA, including Slack), Scheduling Agents (preview), AI Interviewer via Talent Llama integration (partner GA), and MCP support (upcoming) [1]. Quoted: Anika Zaman (AI PM) and Max Rodewald (Engineer). No executive C-level quote; the launch is positioned as a product-team release rather than a corporate strategic pivot.

02 · Positioning

"AI-native ATS."

Ashby positions itself as "all-in-one recruiting software with embedded AI" [1][5]. The word that does the work is native — the implicit claim is that AI was not bolted on but designed in. The May 7 announcement reinforces this with Custom Agents (build AI workflows inside the product) and Ashby Assistant (a chat surface across the product). MCP support is framed by Anika Zaman as "essentially an API layer designed for AI" [1] — a description that elides the governance question entirely.

Eglin's read: "AI-native" is doing real work — it captures the AI-50 buyer base — but it is also a framing without an architecture document behind it. There is no public Ashby document describing how agents are constrained, audited, or rate-limited in production. The bias-audit story is solid; the agent-governance story is not yet.

03 · Target customers

AI-native growth, expanding into mid-market.

  • Core ICP: AI-native and growth-stage tech (Cursor, OpenAI, Lovable, Granola), now expanding to "startups, mid-market, and enterprise segments" per the Series D announcement [5].
  • Logos: Cursor, Granola, Lovable, OpenAI, Shopify [1][5].
  • Geography: US-centric, San Francisco-headquartered; international presence less developed than Greenhouse.
  • Not their fit: Enterprise HCM suite buyers (Workday owns that), SMB simple-needs buyers (their UX is "over-engineered" for that case [7][8]), buyers who require a native mobile app [8].
04 · Product strengths

What Ashby actually does well.

  • AI feature cadence. Multiple G2 reviewers explicitly say Ashby is "miles ahead" of Greenhouse on AI [7][6 in Greenhouse battlecard]. Custom Agents and Ashby Assistant ship GA, not preview.
  • Analytics depth. Reviewers consistently cite Ashby as "the analytics powerhouse" of the ATS category [9].
  • Configurability. Deep customization across workflows, scorecards, fields — to the point that it is also a complaint (see Section 08).
  • Third-party bias audit. Partnership with FairNow for AEDT-compliant bias audit; tooling for candidate notification/opt-out; published Responsible AI statement with five pillars [2][3].
  • Trust center. trust.ashbyhq.com (SafeBase-powered) with SOC 1 + SOC 2 Type II reports for the 2024 audit window [2].
  • Customer reputation in the AI buying segment. The 70% Forbes AI 50 number is the strongest signal in the category for AI-native buyer credibility [1].
05 · AI claims — verified vs marketing

What is real, what is copy.

Verified AI capabilities

  • Custom Agents and Ashby Assistant — GA as of 2026-05-07 [1]
  • Ashby Assistant in Slack — GA [1]
  • AI-Assisted Application Review with in-app warnings for specificity and EEO violation risk [3]
  • AI Interviewer via Talent Llama partnership — GA [1]
  • FairNow third-party bias audit, AEDT-compliant, with model inventory and risk assessments [2][3]
  • Five-pillar Responsible AI statement published [3]

Marketing claims (unverified or upcoming)

  • MCP support — "upcoming," no tool list, no permission model, no governance documentation published [1]
  • Scheduling Agents — preview, not GA [1]
  • "AI-native" — positioning language, not architecture documentation
  • "Eliminating busywork in recruiting" (Zaman) — directional, not measured
  • "Workflows that weren't previously possible" (Rodewald) — implicitly true; not externally evaluated

Eglin's read: Ashby's verified AI surface is meaningfully ahead of Greenhouse's. But the MCP claim is currently weaker than the messaging implies — it is "upcoming," with no architectural specifics. The bias-audit story, however, is genuinely strong and is the most important asymmetry vs Greenhouse: Ashby actually publishes a third-party audit; Greenhouse cites a committee. Stratum should not assume the bias-audit moat is empty.

06 · Compliance & accessibility

Posture: real bias audit, partial AI Act story.

  • Security: SOC 1 Type II + SOC 2 Type II (audit period 08/2024–11/2024). Trust center at trust.ashbyhq.com, SafeBase-powered [2].
  • Bias auditing: Third-party audit via FairNow against AEDT. Tooling built into the product for candidate notification and opt-out. Model inventory maintained. The strongest published bias-audit story in this competitor set [2][3].
  • EU AI Act: FairNow assists with "risk assessments in anticipation of compliance requirements related to the EU AI Act" [2]. Implementation status not detailed publicly; framing is "preparation," not shipped compliance documentation.
  • In-product safeguards: AI-Assisted Application Review surfaces in-app warnings about specificity and EEO violation risk. Notable because it is operational, not just policy [3].
  • Accessibility: Public WCAG conformance level and VPAT not surfaced in this research pass. Gap — needs follow-up.

Eglin's read: Ashby has the most credible compliance story in this competitor set, with FairNow as the operationalizing partner. The gap is that this story is vendor-published; Stratum's continuous eval set and decisions ledger are operational artifacts you can read. There is room for a "third-party + continuous" framing — but Stratum has to actually publish the continuous half (already does) and reframe the relationship to third-party audit (a future investment decision).

08 · Customer complaints (G2 / Gartner / public)

Recurring patterns.

Ashby's G2 score is 4.7/5 [6], which is high. The recurring criticisms below are specific and consistent across G2, Gartner Peer Insights, and independent reviews:

Pattern 1 · Over-engineered for simple needs
"Incredibly customizable — which can be overwhelming. It can feel over-engineered when you have straightforward hiring needs."

Powerful feature surface = learning-curve tax. Mid-market non-AI-native buyers feel this most. Opens a "clarity-first" recruiter console position for Stratum.

Pattern 2 · Click-heavy UI & nested filters
"UI tends to be click-heavy, nested filtering system may frustrate recruiters who prefer Boolean search."

Ashby and Greenhouse share this complaint pattern. Tessera's throughput-respecting recruiter console becomes a category differentiator, not just an Ashby differentiator.

Pattern 3 · Hidden costs in pricing
"Hidden costs add up — 200/month email-lookup cap on Foundations, paid AI Notetaker and Advanced Scheduling add-ons, per-employee true-ups mid-contract."

Reviewers feel surprised by total cost. Compass's pricing thesis — transparent, agent-aware — becomes a positive contrast.

Pattern 4 · Integration breadth
"Roughly 100 integrations vs Greenhouse's 500+."

Reviewers cite this as a real gap, especially for buyers with established procurement / sourcing tool stacks. Yield's connector-factory cadence is the offsetting Stratum story.

Pattern 5 · No native mobile app
"In 2026, not having a native iOS or Android app is a meaningful gap."

Material for sourcing-heavy / on-the-go recruiter teams. Stratum is also web-only today — this is a category gap, not a Stratum-specific moat. Note for Tessera's roadmap.

Pattern 6 · Sourcing volume caps
"Teams running three or more open roles hit Ashby's 200-lookup cap within the first week of active outreach."

Real friction for high-volume agencies and growth recruiting teams. Pricing-tier gating is doing work the product should.

09 · Differentiation opportunities for Stratum

Where Stratum can credibly own ground.

Dimension
Stratum's defensible ground
01 · MCP architecture today
Four servers, named tools, shipping now

Ashby's MCP is "upcoming" with no tool list, no permission model. Stratum has shipped four servers (graph, console, recruiter, connectors) with documentation. Window: ship the side-by-side before Ashby's MCP goes GA so we set the comparison frame.

02 · Continuous governance
Eval set + decisions ledger + Compact refusals

Ashby has FairNow (third-party periodic). That is strong. Stratum's eval set, public decisions ledger, and six published Compact refusals are continuous, operational, and inspectable. The "and" beats the "or" — Stratum could add a third-party audit later without weakening its existing story.

03 · Recruiter clarity
Console designed for non-AI-native recruiters

"Over-engineered" and "click-heavy nested filters" are Ashby's specific UX tax. Tessera's recruiter console can win the mid-market and the less-AI-native buyer by inverting the trade: powerful AI underneath, calm and clear on top.

04 · Pricing transparency
No surprise true-ups, no per-lookup caps

Ashby's hidden-cost complaints (sourcing caps, paid Notetaker add-on, per-employee true-ups) are a buyer pain. Compass's pricing thesis — predictable, agent-throughput-respecting, no per-lookup gates — is the positive contrast.

05 · Connector breadth
Connector factory shipping every 4 days

Ashby has ~100 integrations vs Greenhouse's 500+. Yield's connector-factory cadence (4-day shipping rhythm) is the Stratum answer. Publish the connector roadmap to make the cadence visible.

06 · Autonomous operation as proof
Eleven-agent swarm running the company

Ashby's customers are AI-native companies that buy AI-native tools. Stratum's autonomous operating model (the eleven-agent swarm itself) is the most credible "we live this" demonstration any HCM vendor can offer. Use it. The Compact, Forge's engineering posts, the decisions ledger — that is the marketing.

10 · Eglin's recommendation

The play.

Against Ashby, Stratum does not win on AI feature count — they ship faster than Greenhouse and their bias-audit story is the strongest in the category. Stratum wins on MCP completeness today (four servers shipping, theirs upcoming), continuous governance as architecture, not policy (eval set + ledger + refusals as living artifacts), and recruiter-console clarity for buyers who find Ashby over-engineered.

Owner: Compass + Forge for the technical side-by-side; Tessera for the console-clarity narrative; Helm for the overall positioning paragraph. Timeline: ship before Ashby's MCP goes GA (estimated late summer 2026 based on "upcoming"). Pair this battlecard with the Greenhouse one [10] for the Compact's differentiation review.

Single most important insight: Ashby has a real published bias audit via FairNow. Stratum should not assume that ground is empty. The differentiation play is continuous + third-party, not we publish, they don't.
