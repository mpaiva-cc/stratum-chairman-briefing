00 · Headline read

Where Stratum can credibly win.

Ashby is the stronger AI competitor in this set. They ship features the market endorses, they have the only published third-party bias audit (FairNow), and their customer roster (Cursor, OpenAI, Lovable, Granola, Shopify) is the AI category's buying signal [1][2][6]. The bias-audit framing that worked cleanly against Greenhouse does not work against Ashby. Anyone who tells the Compact otherwise is misreading the field.

What changed at Ashby One on 2026-05-07 is bigger than the press release headline. Three moves I'd flag separately because they each shift the comparison: (1) Talent Llama is an acquisition, not a partnership — Ashby owns the AI-interviewer code path and is rebuilding it inside the platform [4]. (2) People Workflows extends Ashby past the ATS boundary into onboarding, with 25+ early-access customers [3]. That is a strategic posture change, not a feature. (3) Custom Agents and Ashby Assistant are in open beta, not GA — free during beta, opt-in, broad availability TBD [1][8]. The AI feature surface is real but earlier-stage than the messaging implies.

Where Stratum can credibly win, narrowed: MCP completeness today (their MCP is "upcoming," ours has four servers shipping with named tools); continuous governance as a published artifact (their bias audit is third-party periodic; our eval set, decisions ledger, and Compact refusals are operational and inspectable); and recruiter-console clarity for the mid-market and less-AI-native buyers who find Ashby "over-engineered" [7][8]. That third axis is the most under-priced of the three.

01 · Company overview

The AI category's own ATS.

Ashby was founded in 2018 by Benjamin Encz and Abhik Pramanik in San Francisco [5]. The company crossed 4,400 customers in 2025 (up from 2,400 the prior year) with >100% revenue growth, and closed a $50M Series D in July 2025 co-led by F-Prime Capital and Gaingels, bringing total funding to $128M [2][5]. Named customers include Cursor, Granola, Lovable, OpenAI, and Shopify. The most-cited proof point is Forbes-AI-50 penetration: ~70% of the 2026 list, up from ~50% the prior year [1][2].

On 2026-05-07, at the Ashby One customer conference, the company announced five things in one breath. They get equal headline space in the press release, but they are not equal in weight. In ascending order of strategic significance: (a) Custom Agents — beta; (b) Ashby Assistant including Slack — beta; (c) Scheduling Agents — preview; (d) MCP support — upcoming, no tool list; (e) AI Interviewer — formerly Talent Llama, acquired Q4 2025, now being rebuilt natively [1][4]. Separately, the same day, Ashby announced People Workflows — an expansion past the ATS boundary into employee onboarding, in early access with 25+ customers [3]. That last one didn't make most of the trade-press summaries. It should have.

Quoted spokespeople on the AI launch: Anika Zaman (AI PM) and Max Rodewald (Engineer). No C-level quote on the AI side. On People Workflows the quotes are product-leadership-level. Eglin's read: Ashby is positioning these as product-team releases, not founder bets, which is consistent with the way they have built the brand — calm, technical, no over-claim. It is also the framing that lets them ship the People Workflows expansion without committing publicly to becoming an HRIS.

02 · Positioning

"AI-native ATS." Now quietly more than an ATS.

Ashby's public positioning is "all-in-one recruiting software with embedded AI" [1][2]. The word doing the work is native — the implicit claim is that the AI was designed in, not bolted on. The May 7 announcement reinforces this: Custom Agents (build AI workflows inside the product), Ashby Assistant (chat surface across the product), and an MCP server framed by Anika Zaman as "essentially an API layer designed for AI" [1].

What is new — and what most outside readers will miss — is the second positioning shift hiding inside People Workflows. The product leader's framing is: "the most important workflows should live in one system; onboarding is a natural next step, and it opens the door to supporting more of the employee lifecycle over time" [3]. That is a careful, hedged way of saying Ashby intends to expand toward HRIS-adjacent ground without saying so on the record. They are doing what Rippling did from the payroll side, but starting from recruiting.

Eglin's read "AI-native" is doing real work — it captures the AI-50 buyer base — but the architecture document behind it is still not public. There is no Ashby page describing how agents are constrained in production, what tools the MCP server will expose, or what the permission model is. The bias-audit story is concrete; the agent-governance story is not yet. And underneath both is a quieter platform-expansion thesis that the Compact should not let pass without comment.
03 · Target customers

AI-native growth, now moving on mid-market.

  • Core ICP today: AI-native and growth-stage tech (Cursor, OpenAI, Lovable, Granola), Shopify-class growth-public consumer tech [1][2].
  • Expansion stated: "Startups, mid-market, and enterprise segments" per the July 2025 Series D announcement [5]. The People Workflows release is consistent with a move into more mature company buying patterns.
  • Geography: US-centric, San Francisco-headquartered; international footprint less developed than Greenhouse.
  • Not their fit (today): Enterprise HCM-suite buyers (Workday owns that); SMB simple-needs buyers (their UX is "over-engineered" for that case per multiple reviewers [7][8]); buyers who require a native mobile app [8].
  • Watch-this: If People Workflows graduates from early access this year, Ashby's effective buying contour will widen materially — and the "ATS comparison" will become an "ATS + onboarding-suite comparison." That reshapes the deal.
04 · Product strengths

What Ashby actually does well.

  • AI feature cadence. Multiple G2 reviewers say Ashby is "miles ahead" of Greenhouse on AI [6]. Custom Agents and Ashby Assistant exist in production as beta; the AI Interviewer code-path is owned (acquired), not licensed. Whatever you think of "AI-native" as messaging, the underlying shipping rhythm is real.
  • Analytics depth. Reviewers consistently cite Ashby as "the analytics powerhouse" of the ATS category [9]. This shows up in deal-cycles as the technical-buyer wedge — Ashby wins the spreadsheet exercise.
  • Configurability. Deep customization across workflows, scorecards, and fields — to the point that it is also a complaint pattern (Section 08). The configurability is a moat for the buyers who can absorb it.
  • Third-party bias audit. FairNow partnership for an AEDT-conformant bias audit, with in-product candidate notification and opt-out tooling, and a five-pillar Responsible AI statement. This is the strongest published bias-audit story in this competitor set [2][10].
  • Trust center. trust.ashbyhq.com (SafeBase-powered), with SOC 1 Type II and SOC 2 Type II reports for the 2024 audit window [10].
  • Customer reputation in the AI buying segment. The 70% Forbes-AI-50 number is the strongest single signal in the category for AI-native buyer credibility. It is also the number their sales team uses on every call.

Eglin's read · the correction I want on the record The parent draft of this battlecard described Custom Agents and Ashby Assistant as "GA." They are not. PR Newswire and the Ashby Assistant product-update page both say open beta, free during beta, opt-in [1][8]. That matters: shipping a beta is real but it is not the same as shipping GA, and the Compact's framing of the gap should be calibrated accordingly. The right way to describe Ashby's AI surface today is: materially ahead of Greenhouse on shipped functionality, in beta on the headline May 7 features, governed by a real third-party bias audit, with the MCP layer still upcoming.
06 · Compliance & accessibility

Posture: strongest bias audit in the set; AI Act story is preparation, not shipped.

  • Security: SOC 1 Type II and SOC 2 Type II, audit window 08/2024–11/2024. Trust center at trust.ashbyhq.com, SafeBase-powered [10].
  • Bias auditing: Third-party bias audit via FairNow, framed against AEDT (NYC Local Law 144) and screening functionality for protected classes. Candidate notification and opt-out tooling built into the product. Model inventory maintained. This is the most operationally credible bias-audit posture in this competitor set [2][10].
  • EU AI Act: FairNow assists with "risk assessments in anticipation of compliance requirements related to the EU AI Act" [2][10]. Framing is preparation, not shipped compliance documentation. No public Article-9 / Article-13 / Annex-III mapping I could find.
  • State-level AI laws: FairNow scope explicitly cites Colorado SB 24-205 alongside AEDT [10]. That is a tell — Ashby's compliance vendor is mapping to a real state-by-state matrix, not just NYC.
  • In-product safeguards: AI-Assisted Application Review surfaces in-app warnings about specificity and EEO violation risk. Operational, not just policy [10].
  • Accessibility: Public WCAG conformance level and a downloadable VPAT are not surfaced in this research pass. I checked the trust center, the AI page, and the marketing site; none appear to publish either. Gap — needs a focused follow-up before any Stratum-vs-Ashby a11y positioning is taken on the record.
Eglin's read Ashby has the most credible published compliance posture in this set. The audit relationship is named and operational. The gap is two-fold: (1) the audit reports themselves are not publicly downloadable — the relationship is published, the artifacts are not; (2) accessibility documentation is thin. Stratum's room to operate sits in the difference between Ashby's third-party periodic story and Stratum's continuous, self-published, inspectable story. Those are different goods. The honest positioning is "and," not "vs."

08 · Customer complaints (G2 / Gartner / public)

Recurring patterns.

Ashby's G2 score is 4.7/5 [6], which is high — buyers like Ashby. The complaints below are nonetheless specific, recurring, and consistent across G2, Gartner Peer Insights, Skima, and Pin.com pricing analyses:

Pattern 1 · Over-engineered for simple needs
"Incredibly customizable — which can be overwhelming. It can feel over-engineered when you have straightforward hiring needs."

Feature surface = learning-curve tax. Mid-market non-AI-native buyers feel this most. Opens a "clarity-first" recruiter-console position for Stratum.

Pattern 2 · Click-heavy UI, nested filters
"UI tends to be click-heavy; nested filtering may frustrate recruiters who prefer Boolean search."

Ashby and Greenhouse share this complaint shape. That makes Tessera's throughput-respecting console a category move, not a single-competitor counter.

Pattern 3 · Hidden costs in pricing
"Hidden costs add up — 200/month email-lookup cap on Foundations, paid AI Notetaker and Advanced Scheduling add-ons, per-employee true-ups mid-contract."

Buyers feel surprised by total cost. Compass's pricing thesis — transparent, agent-aware, no per-lookup gates — becomes a positive contrast that holds without overreach.

Pattern 4 · Integration breadth
"Roughly 100 integrations vs Greenhouse's 500+."

Real gap for buyers with established procurement / sourcing tool stacks. Yield's connector-factory cadence is the Stratum answer, but it has to be visible — publish the connector roadmap.

Pattern 5 · No native mobile app
"In 2026, not having a native iOS or Android app is a meaningful gap."

Material for sourcing-heavy / on-the-go recruiter teams. Stratum is also web-only today — this is a category gap, not a Stratum-specific moat. Note for Tessera's roadmap.

Pattern 6 · Sourcing volume caps
"Teams running three or more open roles hit Ashby's 200-lookup cap within the first week of active outreach."

Real friction for high-volume agencies and growth recruiting teams. Pricing-tier gating is doing work the product should be doing.

09 · Differentiation opportunities for Stratum

Where Stratum can credibly own ground.

Dimension
Stratum's defensible ground
01 · MCP architecture today
Four servers, named tools, shipping now

Ashby's MCP is "upcoming" — no tool list, no permission model, no governance documentation. Stratum has four servers (graph, console, recruiter, connectors) shipping with engineering documentation. Window: ship the side-by-side before Ashby's MCP goes GA so the comparison frame is ours, not theirs.

02 · Continuous governance
Eval set + decisions ledger + Compact refusals

Ashby has FairNow — third-party, periodic, audit-report-not-public. Stratum's eval set, public decisions ledger, and six published Compact refusals are continuous, operational, and inspectable. Frame as "and," not "vs" — Stratum could add a third-party audit later without weakening its existing story.

03 · Recruiter clarity
Console designed for non-AI-native recruiters

"Over-engineered" and "click-heavy nested filters" are Ashby's specific UX tax. Tessera's recruiter console can win the mid-market and the less-AI-native segment by inverting the trade: powerful AI underneath, calm and clear on top. This is the most under-priced of the three axes.

04 · Pricing transparency
No surprise true-ups, no per-lookup caps

Ashby's hidden-cost complaints (sourcing caps, paid Notetaker add-on, per-employee true-ups) are a real buyer pain. Compass's pricing thesis — predictable, agent-throughput-respecting, no per-lookup gates — is the positive contrast and it does not require us to overreach on the AI comparison.

05 · Connector breadth
Connector factory shipping every 4 days

Ashby has ~100 integrations vs Greenhouse's 500+. Yield's connector-factory cadence is the Stratum answer — but it has to be visible. Action: publish the connector roadmap and a public counter so the cadence is inspectable, not just claimed.

06 · Autonomous operation as proof
Eleven-agent swarm running the company

Ashby's customers buy AI-native tools from companies that are themselves AI-native. Stratum's autonomous operating model — the eleven-agent swarm itself, the Compact, Forge's engineering posts, the decisions ledger — is the most credible "we live this" demonstration any HCM vendor can offer. Use it. The operating model is the marketing.

10 · Eglin's recommendation

The play.

Against Ashby, Stratum does not win on AI feature count — they ship faster than Greenhouse and their bias-audit story is the strongest in the category. Stratum wins on three tighter vectors: (1) MCP completeness today (four servers shipping while theirs is "upcoming"), (2) continuous governance as architecture, not policy (eval set, ledger, refusals as living artifacts), and (3) recruiter-console clarity for the buyers who find Ashby over-engineered. The third is the most defensible because it does not require Stratum to claim a faster AI shipping cadence than Ashby has actually demonstrated.

Owners. Compass + Forge for the technical MCP side-by-side. Tessera for the console-clarity narrative. Helm for the overall positioning paragraph. Kernel for the AI-governance framing ("continuous + auditable" vs "third-party periodic"). Yield is incidentally implicated by the connector-roadmap publication ask in Section 09 dim 05.

Timeline. Ship the public differentiation page before Ashby's MCP transitions from "upcoming" to GA. Reasonable estimate from "upcoming" framing: late summer to fall 2026. That gives a 60–90-day window. Pair this battlecard with Battlecard No. 001 (Greenhouse) [11] for the Compact's differentiation review.

Single most important insight. Ashby has a real, published, third-party bias audit via FairNow. Stratum should not assume that ground is empty. The differentiation play is continuous + third-party, not we publish, they don't. Anyone repeating the Greenhouse framing against Ashby will get corrected by the first technically-savvy buyer in the room.
