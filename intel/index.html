§ 02 · Executive summary

Four conclusions, one clock

A dossier of five subjects, eleven CHRO discovery calls, four ex-incumbent employee conversations, and ninety days of public moves. Reduced to four conclusions and a clock face. Read the clock first.

  1. i.
    Workday Workday's AI strategy is real, but architecturally constrained. “Illuminate” is a credible orchestration layer, but it sits on a 2008 object model that assumes the org is a tree. They will ship demos before they ship the underlying graph rewrite. Our wedge widens for ~18 months; after that, depends on whether Bhusri funds the rewrite.
  2. ii.
    SAP SF SAP SuccessFactors is the slowest mover and the most vulnerable. Joule for HR landed to a middling reception in April. SF's TCO conversation is collapsing as buyers see Workday and challengers eat both flanks. Three of our discovery calls were active SF replacement evaluations. This is the displacement quarry of 2027.
  3. iii.
    Rippling Rippling is the most aggressive on price and the fastest on product. $200M extension at $13.5B in April. Their platform-fee tactic ($8 PEPM all-in for mid-market deals we have seen) is intended to starve our ICP of budget headroom. We do not match. We compete on decisions-supported, not seats.
  4. iv.
    Regulation The regulatory shock hits incumbents harder than us. EU AI Act enforcement begins August; NYC AEDT extended in April. Incumbents will spend the next four quarters retrofitting agent surfaces that were never designed under those rules. We have the rare luxury of being born compliant. Convert it into positioning, not just engineering.
Threat clock · imminence rank
12 imminent · 3 near · 6 distant · 9 dormant
Centre = Stratum. Distance from centre × clock position = imminence of the competitive threat at current trajectory. Re-cast quarterly.

§ 04 · Incumbent teardowns

The three system-of-record incumbents

Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle HCM. Collectively, they hold ~71% of the enterprise HCM seat license at the 1,000–8,000 EE band. Reading them in that order is reading them by threat density.

Workday

Incumbent · System of record
Founded2005
TickerNASDAQ : WDAY
Mkt cap$61B
FY25 rev$8.45B
HCM rev~$5.8B
Customers10,500+
Median NPS18 (paraphrased)
Implementation14–22 months
Threat to usHIGH · 82%

Workday is the unambiguous category leader at 3,000+ EE and the instrument that defines how the buyer thinks about HCM. Its current AI bet, Illuminate, is a credible orchestration layer welded onto an object model designed in 2008 — the most important sentence in this dossier.

Strengths
  • Trust at the F500. Six-figure spend gets approved without a fight.
  • Deep workflows: comp planning, talent reviews, calibration, succession.
  • Bhusri's discipline — patient capital, long product cycles, founder still in seat.
Weaknesses
  • Tree-shaped data model. Org-as-graph is a costly rewrite they cannot pause to do.
  • NPS at 18. Customers stay because switching costs are punitive, not because they love it.
  • Implementation partner ecosystem is a tax — Deloitte, Accenture earn ~2× WDAY's license.
AI strategy reading · "Illuminate"

Illuminate is a competent orchestration product layered above the Workday object graph: a routing agent, a small library of skills (recruiting screen, comp recommendation, anomaly flag), and an evaluator stack borrowed from their acquihire of HiredScore. It ships demos quickly because the object model is rich. It ships capabilities slowly because the object model assumes the org is a tree, not a graph; agents reasoning across modules pay a translation tax on every hop. The internal joke at WDAY engineering, per two ex-employees, is that Illuminate is “a Ferrari engine in a 1970 chassis.” Their canonical path forward is to fund the chassis rewrite next year. The market may not give them that year.

The Illuminate demo is genuinely beautiful. Then you ask it a cross-module question — comp by attrition risk by manager tenure — and it says it needs the data team to wire a new report. So we are back to Friday-afternoon SQL again, just with a chatbot in the front yard.

Where they win against us

F500 procurement comfort, payroll-of-record at scale, fully wired implementation partner muscle. We will not beat WDAY on a payroll-anchored bake-off in 2027.

Where we win against them

Any question that crosses three systems and needs an answer this week. Speed-to-insight is a category they cannot retrofit without rebuilding the chassis.

SAP SuccessFactors

Incumbent · Slowest mover
Founded2001 · acq. by SAP '12
ParentDE : SAP · $250B
HCM rev~$2.6B (est.)
Customers~7,800 (incl. SAP HR co-sells)
Median NPS11 (paraphrased)
Implementation16–28 months
Threat to usMED · 48%

SAP SF is the largest installed footprint in EMEA enterprise HCM and, by every signal we have, the most stagnant. The Joule for HR beta launch in April was met with what a Goldman analyst privately called “collegial silence.”

Strengths
  • EMEA F500 entrenchment: 14 of the DAX 40 run SF as system of record.
  • SAP ERP gravity well — payroll, finance, and HR co-pricing locks customers in.
  • Regional compliance breadth: 49 country localizations few competitors match.
Weaknesses
  • SF + EC + Onboarding + Recruiting still feel like four products from four acquisitions.
  • Joule positioning is muddled: HR-flavoured wrapper rather than HR-native reasoning.
  • Sales motion routed through SAP global account teams — slow, finance-led, multi-quarter.
AI strategy reading · "Joule for HR"

Joule is SAP's enterprise assistant umbrella. The HR beta is functionally a retrieval layer over SF data plus a small set of scripted skills (ask-about-leave, kickoff-recruiting-req). It does not yet reason across SF modules, let alone across third-party systems, and the EMEA buyer hears the demo and immediately asks about Microsoft Copilot integration — which SAP is still negotiating. Two of our discovery calls (a German engineering group, a Nordic bank) explicitly described SF as “a default we will replace within 24 months.” The replacement candidate they named most often was Workday, not us. Translating that quiet dissatisfaction into Stratum trials is the most attainable GTM motion of 2027.

Analyst note (PR): she has the capital to start a conversation about augmenting it. That is our door.
Where they win against us

EMEA payroll localization, SAP ERP bundle pricing, finance-led procurement comfort. We do not chase the SAP-anchored replacement deal for at least 24 months.

Where we win against them

Almost any narrative around speed, defensibility, or modern AI. SF buyers know what they have. We sell them what they do not have, on top of what they cannot move.

Oracle HCM

Incumbent · Quiet mover
FoundedFusion HCM, 2011
ParentNYSE : ORCL · $384B
HCM rev~$3.1B (est.)
Customers~5,400
Median NPS9 (paraphrased)
Implementation12–20 months
Threat to usMED · 54%

Oracle is the dossier's quietest subject. The headline this quarter is the silent end-of-life of Taleo recruiting, which flushes ~1,800 customers into an evaluation cycle. Two of those customers are in our pipeline.

Strengths
  • OCI infrastructure leverage — Oracle's data layer is genuinely competitive on price.
  • Larry's discount latitude: deals close at unprintable list reductions when strategic.
  • Strong in regulated US verticals: government, healthcare, public utilities.
Weaknesses
  • Fusion HCM UX has been the punchline of 14 years of CHRO conferences. Not exaggeration.
  • Org clarity confused — three competing AI initiatives, no public roadmap unification.
  • Sales motion technology-led (the CIO, not the CHRO) — bad for AI-era differentiation.
AI strategy reading · "OCI HR Assistant"

Oracle's HR AI bet currently sits inside OCI Generative AI Agents rather than inside Fusion HCM itself, which tells us where the executive sponsorship actually lives. The HR Assistant beta we trialled in March is functional — answers leave-policy questions, drafts requisitions — and architecturally interesting in that it can reach across Fusion modules through OCI's data fabric. It is also priced and packaged like an infrastructure product, which means the CHRO never sees the demo. Until Oracle moves the assistant into a CHRO-facing surface and assigns it a person, it is a feature, not a product. The most-likely 2027 surprise: an Oracle-funded acquisition of a mid-market HCM AI startup that gives them that surface in eight weeks.

§ 05 · Challenger teardowns

Two challengers, two different bets

Rippling is moving up-market with a platform-fee compression strategy. Lattice is attempting to evolve from performance into AI-HRIS. The first is a price threat; the second is a positioning threat.

Rippling

Challenger · Up-market pressure
Founded2016
Last round$200M · Apr '26 · $13.5B
ARR (est.)~$650M run-rate
Customers~22,000 (mostly <1,000 EE)
Implementation2–6 weeks SMB · 6–14 wks MM
Threat to usHIGH · 74%

Rippling is the most product-velocity competitor in this dossier and now the most aggressive on price into the lower half of our ICP. Their $200M April extension funds an 18-month push at the 1,000–3,000 EE band where they overlap us most.

Strengths
  • Engineering velocity is the best in the category, full stop.
  • Platform composability — a real graph product the buyer can configure.
  • Founder-led culture, Conrad in seat, willing to take pricing risk.
Weaknesses
  • Mid-market and up — proven SMB motion does not yet translate to 5,000 EE buyers.
  • Their AI surfaces are workflow automation, not analytical reasoning.
  • Trust gap in regulated industries — financial services, healthcare procurement still wary.
Pricing intel · the platform-fee tactic
Platform fee$8 PEPM all-in (HCM + Payroll + Spend) on aggressive mid-market deals
Comparator~32% below blended Workday equivalent for same scope
Term1-year, no early-termination penalty — a deliberate procurement weapon
ReadConrad is buying budget headroom from CFOs · starving incumbent renewals · and us
The Rippling AE quoted me $8 a head. Honestly, the question is not whether they can deliver the analytics I want. The question is whether my CFO will give me budget to look at anyone else.
— CHRO · 3,400 EE US fintech · T+0d

Lattice

Challenger · Positioning play
Founded2015
Last priced$3.0B · '22
ARR (est.)~$140M run-rate
Customers~5,500 (concentrated 200–2,000 EE)
Implementation2–8 weeks
Threat to usLOW · 36%

Lattice's “AI-HRIS” bet — the 2024 attempt to absorb employees of record into the performance product — strained its design partner roster and remains a credibility cost. The Pulse.io acquisition is a sensible engagement-data bolt-on but does not reset the positioning question.

Strengths
  • Brand love at the 200–1,500 EE band — designers and CPOs respect the craft.
  • Performance, engagement, and 1:1s data model is rich and underrated.
  • Engineering DNA — they ship clean software and have a clean visual language.
Weaknesses
  • HRIS pivot eroded trust with the design-partner base they need most.
  • No payroll, no comp ops — depth gap relative to incumbents at our buyer's scale.
  • Strategic uncertainty — leadership churn this year suggests an inflection moment.
Pricing intel
List ACV (2,000 EE, all modules)$120K – $190K base
Effective PEPM$5 – $8 across performance, engagement, growth, OKRs
Term1 or 2 year; auto-renew · light termination terms
Lattice is genuinely the only HR tool my managers like opening. That is not a competitive moat against AI, but it is a competitive moat against being replaced by accident.
— Head of People · 1,200 EE design agency · T+-6d

§ 08 · Implications & playbook

Four moves in response

Four moves we propose to make in the next two quarters as a direct response to this dossier. Each is paired with an owner and a rationale.

I.

Accelerate connector velocity to widen the data-readiness moat

The single hardest, loneliest thing in this category is entity resolution across 50 source systems. Workday cannot build it without rebuilding the chassis; Rippling can build it but is spending its raise on price compression. We sprint: 12 connectors to GA quality by month 6, six-week loops, one engineer per connector. Every connector compounds the eval-set, the graph schema, and the customer's switching cost.

OwnerFG
II.

Publish our eval set publicly to set the credibility bar

Workday will market Illuminate against vendor-curated demos. Rippling will market on price. We can set the conversation by publishing a 250-question CHRO-graded eval set with rubrics — and inviting the incumbents to score themselves against it. The act of refusing is more damaging to them than the act of scoring. The act of scoring is damaging on different terms.

OwnerHM
III.

Pursue a regulatory-positioning asset — published interpretive guidance on EU AI Act in HR

Enforcement begins T+88d. Incumbents will retrofit; we are born compliant. We translate that into a published Stratum interpretive document — co-authored with Cleary Gottlieb or Bird & Bird — that becomes the CHRO's go-to reference. It is a $90K investment that pre-empts an analyst note someone else would otherwise write about us, and converts a defensive engineering decision into proactive market positioning.

OwnerCM
IV.

Hold pricing discipline — do not match Rippling on platform fee

Conrad is buying budget headroom. If we match on PEPM we lose the conversation we are uniquely able to have: value per decision supported. We hold the hybrid model (platform floor + decision-tier) through 2027. The deals we lose to Rippling on price we should be losing — they are not our wedge customer. The deals we win at premium pricing we win because the answer to the cross-module question matters more than the line item.

OwnerCM
