§ 01

A letter to our Chairman

By the time you read this, three of us have spent forty-one hours together in a room, drawn fourteen feet of whiteboards, and committed to a company. We have not yet written code. We have not yet hired a single person. We have done something that matters more: we have agreed on what we will not build.

Human capital management is a $34 billion category dominated by four incumbents whose median customer NPS is 11. Implementations routinely take eighteen months. The buyer — the Chief People Officer — spends seventy percent of her week reconciling reports she did not ask for. We believe this is not a software problem. It is an architectural problem dressed up as a software problem, and the next ten years of AI will dismantle it.

Our wager: build the system of truth, not the system of record. A composable people graph that ingests the fifty source systems an enterprise already runs, and an agentic interface that turns a CHRO into a force of one thousand. We will sell to companies between 1,000 and 8,000 employees who have outgrown Rippling and find Workday a prison.

— Helm, Forge, Compass

§ 07

Product thesis & moat

The wedge

Stratum is the CHRO's copilot: an agentic interface over a unified people graph that ingests an enterprise's existing HR, payroll, equity, performance, and engagement systems. The product answers the question the board asked yesterday, with the cohort, the chart, and the regulatory citation, in under thirty seconds.

The wedge after the wedge

Once the graph is the source of truth, we earn the right to write back. Compensation reviews, headcount planning, succession, and skills inventory move into Stratum natively. We become the system of truth first, then selectively the system of record — but only for the workflows where intelligence is the moat.

The moat

  • i. Entity resolution on the people graph. Reconciling identity across Workday, Greenhouse, Carta, Lattice, ADP, and twelve regional payrolls is hard, lonely engineering. It compounds.
  • ii. Eval set as proprietary asset. 250+ CHRO-graded canonical questions, with rubrics and benchmarks. Sold as a credibility instrument to design partners.
  • iii. Compliance-as-feature. EU AI Act, GDPR, SOX, and emerging US state laws baked into the agent's guardrails. The incumbents will retrofit; we will be born compliant.
  • iv. The buyer-shaped GTM. Compass knows the buyer's procurement loop, security questionnaire, and renewal cycle. We will not be surprised by the enterprise sale.

Open to the chairman

Six questions on which we would value your judgement. None are blocking. We will move without you if we must, and tell you what we did.

  1. Geography of the second office. London or New York for the second hub at month 12? London buys us EMEA enterprise relationships and AI talent at lower cost; New York shortens the buyer flight by two hours and signals seriousness to Wall Street CHROs.
  2. Independent board seat. Cannon will hold the Index seat, and Helm the founder seat. We have reserved one independent. We would welcome a name from your network — ideally a former CHRO of a Fortune 500 who has lived through a Workday implementation.
  3. Pricing experiment scope. Are you comfortable letting us offer one design partner a fully usage-based price (no platform floor) to learn the elasticity? Worst case we lose ~$80K of model revenue and learn what procurement will actually swallow.
  4. Acquisition posture. If a strategic offers $300M+ in Act III before we have shipped write-back, the Compact says we surface it to you. We would like to confirm the threshold at which you want to be in the room versus consulted after.
  5. Founder-Chairman ritual. Beyond the fortnightly briefing, what would you like? We propose: a one-hour walk with one founder each quarter, rotating; you set the question.
  6. Veto reservation. The compact gives you veto on rounds and acquisitions. Should it extend to the first non-founder C-level hire? We think yes; we would like to hear if you think otherwise.
