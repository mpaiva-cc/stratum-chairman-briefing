It is a small classification model trained on a public AWS-D1.1 weld-defect dataset, sourced with Lincoln Electric's help (approximately 11,400 labelled images). The pass threshold is tuned conservatively: high-precision, low-recall. We would rather refuse a plate than mis-attest one. The classifier feeds into Hearth's recommendation surface, not into the attestation itself — the attester signs; the model only narrows what the attester is asked to look at. The deferred-attestation external-validator workflow landed W07, and Mateo Cantú's case is the inaugural use.

III. What changed during the build that I want on the record

Three things changed during the build that did not appear in Briefing 017 and that I want documented here, in order of how surprised I was by each.

The content-matcher discipline. On May 26 Talus picked the wrong Pluralsight module in a live demo with Elena Vega's manager Carolina. The model had gotten the topic right and the level wrong. The fix was not in the model; the fix was in the surface. Hearth's recommendation panel default now shows match-confidence and alternatives — not just the top pick. Two effects. (a) Trust went up with Carolina because she could see the work behind the recommendation. (b) Match-confidence calibration improved 0.18 points on the Brier score over the W05–W08 window, because publishing the alternatives forced a tighter feedback loop on the classifier. The discipline was the engineering fix; the model improvement followed from the discipline. I had it backwards going in.

The provisional-stone construct. The original architecture had only two states: attested or not-attested. A senior-retirement question at the May 26 meeting forced a third. What do you do when the only competent attester is leaving the company in six weeks, the work has been observed, and the consent chain is sound — but you want a downstream external validator to confirm the standing before the stone hardens? The answer turned out not to require a new tier. It used the consent-renewal axis of 014-T: a stone enters provisional state, the learner consents to a deferred external validation window, and the stone resolves attested-or-revoked when the validator signs. The provisional flag landed W03. This is the second time 014-T has paid for itself; the first was the consent-renewal flow itself. The predicate is doing more work than I expected.

The schema-layer AEDT wall held. Across 47 distinct production runs in the eval set during the eight-week build, no agent — Talus, Hearth, or any composite — proposed an edge from cairn_stone to comp_band or to promotion_gate. Not one. The structural enforcement is doing the work; we did not write a single agent-policy rule against it. This is the cleanest argument I have ever made for schema-as-policy: the wall is in the type system, the agents do not see what they cannot connect to, and the AEDT posture follows without any prompt-level guardrail. I would build it this way again every time.

IV. What I would do differently next time

Two things. First: Talus's stub-graph integration test on W01 D2 should have been week-one work, not day-two work. The Cordova graph was empty at that point — C-1 had not landed yet — and the only thing we could actually test was the prompt structure, not the agent. The day-two refusal I logged on W01 D2 was real and useful, but it tested whether the prompt was well-formed, not whether the agent would detect a real gap. The first real integration test could not have happened until W04 D2 when C-1 landed. Schedule the integration milestone against the connector dependency, not against the day count.

Second: the visual classifier deserved more time. The dataset was right, the model is fine, the threshold is conservative enough that the false-attest rate is approximately zero. But the human-in-loop validation discipline around the classifier needs more rigor before we use it for the next non-engineering attestation domain. The welder use case is the one Lincoln Electric helped us source data for; the next one will not have a partner that hands us 11,400 labels and an expert reviewer. We need a generic protocol for introducing a new classifier-backed attestation surface, with explicit calibration steps, explicit refusal thresholds, and explicit hand-off to the attester. That protocol is now on Kernel's and my W09 desk, even though the build budget says we are done.
