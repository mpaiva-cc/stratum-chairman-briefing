Section 1 · The meeting, minute by minute

What happened.

00–05
Open · Vector frames the session Tone set before the first screen share

Vector opened with a one-sentence scope statement: detect, recommend, deliver, attest — the closed loop — and named what the session would cover. Before Forge could bring up the demo environment, Annika spoke.

“Show me, don’t tell me. We’ve already read the architecture.” Annika Whitfield · CHRO, Cordova Manufacturing · minute 1

The framing slide was not shown. Forge had it ready; Vector signaled to skip it. Inez said nothing. The tone was set: this was a technical review, not a product presentation, and everyone in the room understood the difference. Vector noted the posture and moved directly to the demo.

05–22
Demo · Closed loop on Cordova data Mateo Cantú · Querétaro plant · AWS D1.1

Forge screenshared with the methodology-disclosure panels pinned. The employee in the run was Mateo Cantú, a welder at Cordova’s Querétaro plant, whose data clearance Annika had confirmed the previous morning. Talus ran the detection pass against Mateo’s documented work artifacts. The flag came back within ninety seconds: a gap in pressure-vessel weld pattern, specifically deviation from the deposition rate tolerances called out under AWS D1.1 section 4.8 — a code Annika named aloud before Forge could read it from the screen.

Then the recommendation step surfaced a Pluralsight module. Forge paused before clicking through to it.

“I should flag this before we go further. That match is wrong. The module covers thin-gauge tube welding. Mateo needs pressure-vessel procedure. The weld-code reference in the module’s metadata does not include D1.1. Talus surfaced it because the skill taxonomy overlap is high, but the content is not appropriate.” Forge · Engineering, Stratum · minute 14

There was a short silence. Forge continued: the content-matcher inside Talus was using a catalog with poor metadata, and the work to fix that was scoped to weeks 4 through 6 — not yet done. Annika nodded. Inez wrote something down. No one on Cordova’s side asked Forge to minimize it.

22–32
The senior-retirement question Three approaches · one surprise question

Forge presented the three attestation approaches without ranking: Option A (third-party expert validators), Option B (recorded-skill demonstrations with frame-level scoring), Option C (deferred attestation pending peer maturity). The framing held: Stratum had not ratified any of them. Annika listened through the full presentation before responding. She leaned toward Option B for the Stamping Line 3 case, though she did not commit. She said the deferred-attestation model made her nervous given the retirement timelines she was working with.

Then Inez asked a question that was not on anyone’s agenda. She had been quiet through the demo. She asked it precisely.

“What about cross-company peer attestation? If there is a senior welder at a plant we don’t own — say, a certified journeyman at a supplier or a peer company — could that person co-attest a Cordova stone, given appropriate credentialing?” Inez Ramos · IT Director, Cordova Manufacturing · minute 27

Forge did not answer quickly. There was a pause that lasted a few seconds, long enough to be noticeable.

“We have not built that. It is an interesting question. Let me think about it.” Forge · Engineering, Stratum · minute 28

No promise was made. Kernel, who was present at Annika’s mid-week request, added one observation: the attester-standing weight in the eval set could theoretically accommodate non-employee attesters if the credentialing chain was graph-resolvable. This was offered as a structural note, not a commitment. Annika logged it; she said she wanted to see it in writing if Forge ever came back with a position on it. Vector noted the item for the open-questions list.

32–42
Pricing · Henrik joins One probe · one answer · no further pushback

Henrik Sjöberg dialed in at minute 30. Vector walked the hybrid model: $80K platform base, $14 per manufacturing-floor employee per year, $35 per attested stone. The design-partner activation-tier structure was described briefly. Henrik did not ask about the activation tier. He had one question.

“What is the floor if we are below 1,000 employees in three years?” Henrik Sjöberg · CFO, Cordova Manufacturing · minute 34

Vector answered directly: the $80K platform fee holds. It does not discount. The per-employee and per-stone components compress with scope, which is the natural hedge in a downcycle. If the manufacturing-floor scope contracts by 20 percent, the per-EE component contracts with it; the per-stone component contracts with attestation activity. The $80K does not move. Henrik nodded. He asked no follow-up questions on pricing. He left the call at 14:42 as expected.

The silence after his departure lasted long enough for Annika to glance at Inez. Whatever signal passed between them was not verbalized.

42–50
The MOU · one clause differs Annika’s general counsel had already drafted it

At minute 42, Annika said she wanted to talk about the MOU. She produced a printed copy — her general counsel had prepared it based on the commercial shape in the partnership document, which Vector had not asked her to circulate in advance. She had done it on her own initiative. The document tracked the Stratum design-partner template closely. One term differed: Cordova wanted a 30-day termination-for-convenience clause in year-1. Stratum’s standard is 90 days.

Vector did not negotiate in the room. The answer was clear: this needed Compass review, one business day, and Vector would come back with a position. Annika said that was acceptable. She signed the MOU at minute 50, subject to resolution of that single clause. She signed with a pen she took from her desk drawer, not the one sitting in front of her laptop. No one remarked on this. Vector filed the signed copy immediately.

The 30-day versus 90-day gap is a real question. Thirty days is aggressive for a design-partner arrangement where Forge has committed engineering time to Cordova’s graph data. Compass will decide. Vector has no recommendation either direction before that review.

50–53
Forward · Annika’s asks Three timelines · stated as requirements, not preferences

The meeting was scheduled to end at minute 45. Annika had not stopped at 45. She moved directly from the MOU signing to next steps, and she stated them as requirements rather than questions. Yield kickoff within seven business days. Kernel re-run on Cordova data within 14 days. First attested stone in production within 60 days. Vector confirmed in principle on all three, with the explicit caveat that final dates depended on Compass’s legal review of the termination clause. Annika accepted the caveat. The call ended at 14:53.

Section 4 · What we refused

What we did not agree to.

  • Content-library expansion · not committed At minute 18, during the Pluralsight bad-match moment, Annika asked whether Stratum could add additional content partners with stronger pressure-vessel coverage. Vector declined to commit. The answer: content-partner expansion decisions are Stratum’s to make on the product roadmap, not in a design-partner meeting. Forge named the W04-W06 metadata improvement as the near-term lever; Annika accepted that framing.
  • First-stone date before Yield connector confirmed · not committed Annika asked for a hard commitment on the 60-day first-stone target. Vector held: the date could not be committed until Yield delivered the connector and Compass cleared the legal clause. The principle was agreed to; the date was not. A specific date will follow in writing.

The design-partner activation tier was described structurally but was not positioned as a price concession and was not the basis for any negotiation. The $80K is a hard floor per Compass pricing discipline and stayed that way.

Section 6 · Vector’s reflection · written same evening

The meeting went well because we were prepared and honest, in that order. The Pluralsight bad-match moment did more for trust than anything else in the 53 minutes. When Forge flagged a wrong recommendation in real time — before anyone else had seen it, before he could have known whether Annika would react badly — something shifted in the room. That is the only kind of trust that holds under production conditions.

Annika is the right design partner because she will tell us when we are wrong. She came in having read the architecture, not the pitch. She produced a printed MOU from her own counsel without being asked. That is a person who takes design partnership seriously and expects us to do the same.

Forge’s “let me think about it” on the cross-company attestation question was the right move. The temptation to promise was real — the idea is genuinely interesting and Inez had asked it precisely. Promising without a position would have been the wrong trade. She will remember whether we come back with something real.

Section 7 · Methodology — what this readout records, what it does not
  • No recording was made This readout is reconstructed from Vector’s notes taken during the call, Forge’s annotations filed within two hours of the meeting, and Kernel’s eval-question log. Where three sources agree, the account is treated as accurate. Where only one source holds a detail, it is presented as that person’s recollection, not as a verbatim transcript.
  • Attendee review pre-publication Annika Whitfield, Inez Ramos, and Henrik Sjöberg reviewed the readout before publication. One factual correction was received from Inez: the weld code referenced in the Talus detection flag was AWS D1.1 (structural welding, steel), not D1.5 (bridge welding, structural). The readout has been corrected accordingly. No other corrections were received.
  • Quotes are reconstructed Direct quotations are from notes. They represent the substance and, where memory is reliable, the words. They are not verbatim transcripts. Material pauses and silence are noted where they were significant to the meeting’s dynamic and were observed by at least two parties.
  • Cordova’s data The employee data used in the demonstration (Mateo Cantú, Querétaro plant) was cleared by Annika on 2026-05-25. No other Stratum customer’s data was used or referenced in the session. Mateo’s name appears here with his role-level data only; no PII beyond name and plant is in this document.
