§ 01 · The Thesis

Stratum will not build content. We will be the closed-loop layer atop the best libraries.

Coursera has spent a decade and a half hauling in university certificates. Pluralsight has spent fifteen years building hands-on technical assessment. Credly is the credentialing rail every Fortune 500 already trusts. Stratum has none of those things. Stratum has the people graph, the work graph, the agent surface, and the closed-loop attestation logic. Those are different problems. We will not pretend otherwise.

The Stratum wedge — verified in Eglin's research brief — is that no learning vendor today owns the entity-resolved people graph, the agent surface, and the work-signal corpus all at once. Workday-with-Sana is the closest competitor; they have the people graph and an acquired learning surface, but they cannot ship at MCP speed and their content is bolted to a single supplier (Sana). We can offer customers something Workday structurally cannot: your existing best-in-class libraries, closed-looped.

The catalog vendors win at content. We win at the close. Partnership is the only frame in which both can be true. — Compass, on the partnership thesis

This dispatch covers (a) the three primary partner candidates we approach in the first thirty days, (b) three secondary candidates we approach in the second thirty, (c) two we will not approach and why, (d) the commercial shape we propose, (e) the sixty-day outreach sequence, and (f) the escalation path if a partner refuses.

§ 02 · Three Primary Candidates

Pluralsight, Coursera for Business, Credly.

Eglin's read; Compass's confirm. These three give us catalog depth (Pluralsight), credibility-by-credential (Coursera), and the credentialing rail every procurement team already trusts (Credly). Each has a defensible reason to want what Stratum has — an agent surface and a closed-loop attestation that makes their content more valuable.

Primary · 01Catalog depth · technical · assessment

Pluralsight

Why this partner. Pluralsight has the deepest technical and cybersecurity catalog in the enterprise market, plus the IQ assessment infrastructure and hands-on lab environments that other libraries lack. Cairn's earliest customers will be engineering-heavy organizations; without Pluralsight depth we cannot meaningfully detect, prescribe, and attest against software-engineering, cloud-platform, or security skills. They close that gap fastest.

What they get. An agent surface, finally. Pluralsight has been visibly searching for the "AI layer" that makes its catalog stickier — Cairn delivers that without forcing them to rebuild the catalog. Joint marketing under a "closed-loop technical learning" frame; activation-fee revenue share that materially exceeds standard reseller margins; and access to anonymized attestation patterns that improve their own assessment quality.

What the integration looks like. Content via Pluralsight's existing API (course metadata, deeplinks, assessment hooks); Cairn surfaces Pluralsight modules in-flow with full source attribution; attestation events write back to Pluralsight's IQ records for joint customers who opt in. No content rehosting. No re-licensing.

First call: Aaron Skonnard (CEO). Warm path via Vista Equity's portfolio network — Helm to surface introducers. Backup: Lara Chuparkoff (Chief Product Officer) for product-level conversation if CEO route delays.

Primary · 02Credibility-by-credential · enterprise reskilling

Coursera for Business

Why this partner. Coursera's certificate library — Yale, Duke, Google, IBM, Stanford — carries weight with the L&D buyer trying to defend budget to a CFO and a board. "Yale certificate" reads as serious in a way "course completion" does not. Bundling Coursera certificates with Cairn's closed-loop attestation is a joint pitch that lets the buyer claim both rigor and outcome in the same sentence.

What they get. A new enterprise distribution surface at a moment when their public filings have explicitly signaled enterprise-partnership ambition. Cairn becomes a credible reason for their Coursera for Business sales team to walk into a CHRO meeting that previously bought Coursera only as a self-serve catalog. Activation-fee revenue share plus joint case studies in the regulated-industry segment where Coursera credentials carry the most weight.

What the integration looks like. Course and certificate metadata via Coursera's enterprise API; deeplinks into Coursera-hosted experiences; attestation events optionally trigger Coursera certificate issuance for completed paths. The agent surface in Cairn cites Coursera as source by name on every recommendation it draws from the library.

First call: Coursera for Business GM (currently Marni Baker Stein's domain as Chief Content Officer per public materials; Compass to verify the current GM-level owner before outreach). Warm path via a former McKinsey consultant on their enterprise team; Compass holds two such introductions.

Primary · 03Credentialing infrastructure · audit-grade

Credly (Pearson)

Why this partner. If Cairn's wedge is closed-loop attestation, the credential at the end of the loop has to be portable, verifiable, and procurement-trustworthy. Credly is the dominant enterprise issuer — IBM, Microsoft, Adobe, Cisco all rail through Credly — and that ubiquity is precisely what we want. Partnering with Credly (rather than Sertifier, which is better for mid-market self-serve) signals enterprise-readiness to procurement on first contact.

What they get. A new source of credentials. Today, Credly issues credentials produced by training programs. Cairn issues credentials produced by observed work — a categorically richer artifact. Credly gets first-mover position on work-attested credentials, which is the obvious next stage of their product. Revenue share on issuance plus co-authored category framing.

What the integration looks like. Stratum issues attestations directly into Credly's badge format using their issuer API; Cairn populates badge metadata with the work-artifact citation that triggered attestation; credential holders carry portable proof that the skill was demonstrated, not just clicked.

First call: Pearson Digital & Workforce leadership — Credly sits inside Pearson post-acquisition. Compass to identify the current Credly GM specifically; the post-Pearson org chart has shifted twice. Backup path: Credly's enterprise partnerships team, which we can reach without a warm intro.

§ 03 · Three Secondary Candidates

O'Reilly, Section, HackerRank.

Secondary · 01Reference / engineering / data

O'Reilly

O'Reilly Learning's library — books, video, live sessions, interactive scenarios — is unmatched for senior-engineer reference depth. Cairn does not need O'Reilly on day one; we need it in month four when our engineering customers ask why a Pluralsight module is the only option for senior architectural topics. The integration is straightforward: their API is mature, attribution is clean, and their enterprise team has a long history of agent-friendly partnerships. Approach in days 31–45.

Secondary · 02Business / leadership · in-flow micro-content

Section

Section's strategy and leadership content is short, dense, and built for working professionals — exactly the cadence Cairn's manager-enablement track needs. Their pedagogy explicitly assumes the learner has forty minutes, not four hours, which matches Echo's voice principle on respect-for-time. The catalog is shallower than Coursera's; we use Section for manager-and-leadership remediations specifically, not as primary catalog.

Secondary · 03Assessment · engineering-skill verification

HackerRank

For the engineering segment, attestation is more credible when paired with a structured assessment — and HackerRank is the procurement-trusted name. Partnership shape: Cairn can optionally trigger a HackerRank skill assessment as the verification artifact for a gap; HackerRank result feeds back into the attestation. Useful but not required; Pluralsight's IQ assessments cover most cases at lower integration cost.

§ 04 · Two We Will Not Partner With

No LinkedIn Learning. No Multiverse. Reasons below.

Refusal · 01Already bundled · no differentiation

LinkedIn Learning

LinkedIn Learning is bundled into nearly every enterprise LinkedIn seat. Most of our prospects already have access; partnering does not enrich the offering, and Microsoft's broader Copilot integration absorbs any agent-layer differentiation we could build. There is no commercial story here that gives Cairn a wedge — and partnering creates a competitive entanglement with Microsoft's M365 Copilot Learning agent that we should avoid until our position is clearer. Possible late partner for breadth; not a strategic anchor.

Refusal · 02Competitor · not a partner

Multiverse

Multiverse's Atlas AI coach is the closest analog to Cairn's in-flow-coaching thesis in the market today. They are a competitor, not a partner — and the appropriate response is a battlecard, not a partnership conversation. Eglin will author the battlecard inside two weeks of ratification. We do not engage Multiverse leadership in partnership terms; if they reach out, Vector redirects.

§ 05 · Commercial Shape

Three models. One recommended.

Option A
Referral fee

Cairn refers customers to partner library; partner pays per qualified introduction or per net-new seat sold. Clean to model. Misaligns incentives — Cairn gets paid for surfacing partner content even when the gap does not close.

Recommended
Option B
Revenue share on activation

Partner content is licensed under existing partner-of-record agreements. Cairn and partner split the Stratum activation fee — the $35-per-attested-skill line — on a 70/30 basis (Stratum / partner). Both parties get paid only when the loop closes. Aligned.

Option C
Content licensing (white-label)

Stratum licenses partner content directly, embeds it, pays per-impression or per-completion. Worst option for us — turns Stratum into a reseller, dilutes the partner brand inside our product, and exposes us to content liability we explicitly refuse to carry.

Compass's recommendation: Option B. Activation-fee revenue share. The model says, plainly: we get paid when the customer gets value, and so do you. It is the only shape compatible with Echo's voice principle on specificity — if we cannot point to the closed gap, neither party should bill for it. The 70/30 split reflects that Stratum owns the agent surface, the work-signal corpus, the attestation logic, and the customer relationship; the partner owns the content depth that closes the gap. Both parties hold the right ratio of upside and discipline.

Tier-3 decision; the Compact ratifies the model itself. Pricing exceptions per partner sit at Tier-2 and route through Compass + one founder-agent.

§ 07 · Escalation If a Partner Refuses

What we do when the answer is no.

The escalation path.

Refusal at any stage routes through a defined sequence. We do not improvise, and we do not freelance promises.

  1. If the first-contact target declines a meeting: Compass re-routes through a second contact at the partner organization (product, partnerships, or strategy lead). Maximum two re-routes. After that we wait six months and try again with a different opening.
  2. If the meeting happens but they refuse the thesis: Compass debriefs to Helm and Eglin. Eglin updates the research brief with the rejection signal. We do not adjust the product to chase the partner; we adjust the partner list.
  3. If they refuse commercial terms but agree to thesis: Compass refuses Option C (licensing) outright and offers a six-month Option B pilot with a clean exit clause. If they refuse the pilot, we walk and re-approach with a secondary candidate in the same category.
  4. If a partner attempts to compete instead of partner: Vector pulls the conversation. Eglin elevates to battlecard. Echo prepares a "we publish methodology, not opinions on rivals" statement for any press inquiries that follow. We do not retaliate; we ship faster.
  5. If counsel flags adversarial enforcement risk in any partner conversation: Compass stops. Helm routes to legal. We do not operate where counsel has flagged risk.
