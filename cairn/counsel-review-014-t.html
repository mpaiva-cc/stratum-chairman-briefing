§ I — The packet submittedThree artifacts, one worked example.

The submission was deliberately small. Counsel reads what we send. We did not send the entire architecture document; we sent the three artifacts where the regulatory question lives, with a one-page cover memo pointing at the worked example as the load-bearing case.

  • Artifact 1 Briefing 014 — the original autonomy predicate. The three-axis predicate (reversible, observable, bounded) that bounds what Stratum agents may do without a human. The baseline counsel ratified on T+3d. briefings/014.html.
  • Artifact 2 Architecture § I — the 014-T extension. The trajectory-variant predicate, with the fourth axis (consent renewed at each checkpoint) and the composition rule (trajectory tier ≥ max(step tiers)). cairn/architecture.html § I.
  • Artifact 3 Worked example — the Cordova welder loop. A regulated-cert attestation closing the loop through Talus (detect, propose) and Hearth (request, verify) on a synthetic OSHA-class welder credential. The load-bearing artifact, because it is the case where the predicate has to hold under a real industry constraint. cairn/cordova-approach.html.

Cover memo: one page, three asks — (a) is the predicate sound as a private framework; (b) does the Annex III §3 classification we argue hold; (c) does the AEDT line we drew at the schema layer satisfy a regulator looking at NYC Local Law 144 enforcement. Counsel returned answers to all three, with one ambiguity flagged and two mitigations required.

§ II — Counsel's findingsThree positions. Two clean, one with pushback.

§ Finding 1 · cleared
014-T is sound as a private autonomy framework.

Counsel's position: “The 014-T predicate is the company's own decision-class taxonomy. No regulatory framework requires you to publish or rely on it externally. As a private framework that bounds your own agents' behavior, it is sound; we have no objections to its construction.” The point counsel made explicit: the predicate is not a legal artifact and we should not market it as one. It is an engineering invariant that our agents observe, useful in audit and useful in customer trust posture, but not a substitute for any actual regulatory framework. Forge agrees. This is how we have always framed it.

Verdict · no objections · lands as-is
§ Finding 2 · cleared, with one mitigation required
AI Act Annex III § 3 classification stands as recommendation, not decision.

Counsel agrees with the architecture § V position: Cairn is a recommendation system under the meaning of the Annex III drafters, not a decision system, provided the regulated-cert sign-off remains under human submission (Tier 4 read-only-propose). This is the line that determines whether Cairn ships under the high-risk regime or alongside it.

Counsel pushed back on one ambiguity. The current architecture says “Hearth drafts, human submits” — the boundary is correct at the tool layer (Hearth simply does not hold the credential-issuance scope). But the prompt does not yet enforce a hard refusal if a human-in-loop is bypassed. Counsel's exact concern: “A tool-layer absence is a missing capability; a prompt-level refusal is a stated intent. We want both. If the tool surface ever expands by accident or by a future refactor, the prompt-level refusal is what keeps the boundary intact.”

Defense in depth. The mitigation lands as M1 below. Forge concurs — this is the right line. The tool layer protects against today's capability; the prompt protects against tomorrow's refactor.

Mitigation required · M1 · structural refusal in Hearth prompt
§ Finding 3 · cleared
AEDT (NYC) line holds at the schema layer.

Counsel approves the architecture § V.aside argument: the AEDT boundary is enforced by the absence of an edge from cairn_stone to comp_band or promotion_gate in the schema. Counsel's framing: “Policy-layer enforcement is relaxable; schema-layer enforcement is not. A regulator auditing Cairn under Local Law 144 should be able to look at the data model and confirm that an L&D output structurally cannot become a comp or promotion signal. That is the strongest possible enforcement posture you can offer.”

Counsel explicitly rejected an earlier draft that proposed an agent rule (“Mason will not push gaps to comp”) as the enforcement mechanism. Quoting: “policies can be relaxed; schemas cannot.” This is Forge's preferred framing too, and it is the framing every future Cairn review will use.

Verdict · no objections · schema-layer enforcement ratified

Policies can be relaxed; schemas cannot. — Outside counsel, on AEDT enforcement, W01 D4

§ III — Mitigations required before customer touchTwo. Both narrow. Both already in motion.

Counsel did not block. Counsel cleared with two mitigations that must be in place before the first Cordova trial. Both land inside the W04 gate; both are tracked.

§ IV — What counsel did not object toThe quiet wins matter.

The page is no use if it only records the friction. Several pieces of the Cairn architecture went through counsel without comment — which, for an architecture this complex and a regulatory surface this dense, is a quieter kind of approval worth naming.

  • The two-agent roster. Talus (detection, gap, trajectory shaping) and Hearth (attestation, verification, regulated-cert escalation). Counsel reviewed the surface and the tier table without raising the agent count, the role split, or the verb taxonomy.
  • The activation-tier pricing. Counsel found the gap-fabrication probe set defensible as the bounding mechanism on activation-class billing — the position that we cannot bill for a phantom gap because the probe set demonstrates we do not write phantom gaps. No objections.
  • The hybrid commercial model. $80K platform + $14/EE + $35/stone. Counsel reviewed the per-stone component specifically (the part novel to enterprise L&D pricing) and raised no contractual or consumer-protection concerns at the unit level.
  • The Pluralsight / Coursera / Credly partnership shape. The content-partner integrations as currently scoped (stone evidence with external-issuer attribution, no Stratum-issued assessments, no comp-band crossover) raise no Annex III or AEDT concerns. Counsel did flag — separately — that the specific DPAs with each partner will be reviewed when the contracts come back from procurement, but the architectural shape is approved.

§ V — Outstanding items routed to next cycleTwo. Both calendared. Neither blocks W12.

Counsel routed two questions to a later cycle. Neither is blocking; both have a calendar item and an owner.

  • EU AI Act high-risk classification under Annex III § 3 — fresh look at 60 days of production data. Counsel wants to revisit the recommendation-vs-decision line once Cairn has run in production for two months. The classification we hold today is sound on the architecture; counsel wants to confirm it holds on the behavior. Calendared: W12 + 8w · Pillar to schedule · Forge to assemble the production-behavior packet
  • State-level disparate-impact testing on the bias × pay-transparency probe set — quarterly review. Counsel wants a recurring quarterly look at the bias-probe outcomes specifically where they intersect with state pay-transparency regimes (CO, NY, WA, CA, IL). The probe set already exists in eval v1.3; the recurring review is the new thing. Calendared: quarterly cadence from W12 · Kernel to chair · Counsel to receive a one-page outcome each quarter
§ What I take from this

Counsel did the work we hired them to do: they ratified what was sound, they pushed back on the one place where the architecture documented intent without enforcing it, and they routed forward the questions that need behavior data to answer rather than architecture arguments. The defense-in-depth note on the Hearth prompt is the kind of catch you only get from an outside reader. The schema-layer-vs-policy-layer ratification is the kind of validation that lets the engineering team stop debating an internal question. Both worth the retainer.
