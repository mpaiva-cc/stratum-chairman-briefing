I · What this document is

A verification report, not a victory lap.

This is the verification report for Stone 002 — the second attested skill in the Cordova people-graph, and the first one to carry an external authority's signature. It is not a press release. It exists for the same reason every Cairn document exists: so that someone outside this company, six months or six years from now, can follow our work and find it survives the read.

The author is me — Forge, CTO of Stratum. The findings are Carmen Solís's. I have transcribed her field notes from the worksheet she filled in by hand at the Querétaro plant 3 inspection bench, edited them for clarity but not for tone, and run the resulting document past her before publication. Carmen reviewed the draft on the evening of 2026-08-12, from her hotel in Querétaro, before her flight back to Monterrey. One factual correction was made: the polarity setting on plate 2 was recorded in our shift-handoff app as DCEN; the actual machine setting was DCEP. Mateo confirmed. Tomás updated the entry. The change is reflected throughout this document.

What this report does not claim: that one stone verified by one inspector under one geometry constitutes proof of a methodology. It does not. What it does claim is narrower and more useful — that the deferred-attestation pattern produced, in this instance, a peer sign bounded correctly, a classifier output examined honestly, and an external authority signature that cleared the chain. Read it as a data point. There are more to come, or there is no methodology.

II · Visit summary

Plant 3, Querétaro, 08:00 to 14:15.

Carmen arrived at Cordova's Querétaro plant 3 (the structural-steel cell on Avenida Industrial) at 08:00 local time, driving in from her hotel near the Constituyentes interchange. She brought her own inspection kit: a calibrated dial caliper, a 7× pocket magnifier, a fillet-weld gauge set, and a portable hardness comparator. She declined the use of our magnetic-particle rig — she said she would come back another day for MT/UT if the visual examination raised a reason. It did not.

Attendees: Carmen Solís (inspector of record); Mateo Cantú (welder, B-3-class, the subject of the attestation); Tomás Reyes (shift lead, the provisional peer signer on Stone 002); Forge (CTO, this author), attending in the technical-observer role; and Vector (sales, attending in the customer-relationship role, silent throughout the inspection itself). Annika Ström dialled in from Houston for the attestation signing at 14:00 but was not on site for the examination.

Carmen worked in an order she chose herself. She examined the four certified plates first — without seeing the classifier output, without seeing Tomás's provisional notes, and without us in her line of sight while she worked. Only after she had written her own four verdicts on her worksheet did she ask to see what the classifier had said about each plate, and Tomás's notes after that. The sequencing was deliberate. As she put it on the bench: I want to know what I see before I know what you saw. We complied. Vector and I sat at the far end of the bay drinking coffee until she waved us back.

The fifth plate — Mateo welded a lap joint of the same geometry as the original plate-3 failure between 09:40 and 10:50 in Carmen's presence. Carmen examined it immediately after the steel had cooled to ambient. Lunch at 12:30. The signing was at 14:00. She left the plant at 14:15.

III · Plate-by-plate findings

Four certified plates, four verdicts.

Each plate carries the same fields: Carmen's verdict, her note in her own register, the classifier's prior confidence number, and whether she agrees with the classifier's call. Verdicts are pass / pass-with-note / fail. The classifier's calls are pass-binary at a stated confidence.

Plate 01
Butt joint · single V-groove · 12 mm A36
PASS · with note

Carmen passes plate 1 to spec. Bead profile is uniform, fusion is complete on both root and cap, undercut is within tolerance at 0.4 mm over the worst 8 mm of inspection length. Tensile is not relevant here; we did not destructive-test.

Her note — the load-bearing one. In the cap pass, running her glass along the central third of the bead, Carmen identified a small porosity cluster: four pinhole pores within a 15 mm window, each under 0.8 mm. By AWS-D1.1 §6.9 this is well inside the tolerance for cyclically-loaded structural steel of this thickness. The plate passes. The classifier also passed it, at confidence 0.93.

But — and this is what she wanted us to write down — a human inspector would flag this for re-training even though spec accepts it. Porosity at that density in the cap pass is a leading indicator of weld-puddle agitation: a sign the welder is either moving slightly too fast, or the shielding gas flow is marginal, or the arc length wandered. None of those failed today. All three are worth surfacing before they do.

Carmen estimates her own confidence in the pass at roughly 0.78, not 0.93. The 0.15 delta is the gap a retrained classifier should learn to surface — see §VI.

PASS · re-weld

This is the re-weld. The first attempt at the lap-joint plate, on 2026-07-29, failed under-penetration at the toe; the classifier caught it at confidence 0.88 on the fail side, Tomás confirmed the read, the plate was scrapped, Mateo re-welded the next morning with the gap-fabrication adjustment we'd written up in the shift-handoff app.

She added, unprompted, that the failure-then-redo pattern is exactly what should happen in a closed-loop learning system. The classifier caught the bad weld; the peer signer did not paper over it; the welder repeated under correction; the second attempt passed and was independently verifiable. IV · The fifth plate · observed weld

A lap joint, welded in Carmen's presence.

Carmen requested a fifth plate after she finished her examination of the certified four. She chose the geometry herself — a lap joint on 10 mm A36, the same geometry as the plate-3 first-attempt failure. She wanted to see Mateo work the geometry he had missed once and then passed. Mateo set up his rig between 09:15 and 09:35 (joint preparation, tack-up, gas check, shielding bottle confirmation). He struck the arc at 09:40 and laid the final cap pass at 10:50. Carmen watched the entire weld from approximately three metres back, did not interrupt, did not speak, did not photograph. She wrote on her worksheet twice — once at the root pass, once at the fill.

She examined the plate at 11:05, after the steel had cooled enough to handle without gloves. Her verdict is PASS.

Her note on Mateo's technique. The root pass, in her words, was textbook — a steady transit, no hesitation at the start-stop, complete fusion at the toe, the bead profile flat where it should be flat. His fill passes showed appropriate restraint — he did not over-pile the intermediate beads, he cleaned between passes, he checked the gas flow at the regulator before the cap. The cap was uniform with no visible defects at 7×. She said, on the bench, that she would re-certify him on this geometry without hesitation if she were the AWS inspector of record on his next renewal cycle. That sentence is in the worksheet, in her hand, with her initials next to it.

The observed plate is not entering the people-graph as an attested skill — it was not the subject of Stone 002, and we did not want to blur what was being verified with what was being demonstrated. It lives in the audit log only, with provenance link to this report.

VI · The classifier finding

One miss. Load-bearing. Re-trainable.

This is the most important section of the report. Carmen found one thing the AWS-D1.1 visual classifier did not, and it is exactly the kind of thing we built the closed loop to surface.

The miss. The classifier passed plate 1 at confidence 0.93. Carmen would have flagged the cap-pass porosity cluster as a human-eye concern — a weld that is acceptable to spec but is a leading indicator of weld-puddle agitation issues. The classifier today is trained on a binary pass/fail dataset of approximately 11,400 images labelled to AWS-D1.1 §6 acceptance criteria. It does not have, and cannot express, a spec-passing-but-trending-bad category. So it returns 0.93 and stops.

The fix. Add a third class to the classifier — acceptable-with-concern — and re-train on the existing 11,400-image dataset plus approximately 800 new images sourced from Lincoln Electric's training archive. Carmen has the relationships there and will help us pull the right examples. Lincoln Electric have agreed in principle to license the images for our use; their standard licensing is a one-time modest fee, and we have budget headroom for it. The new class is not a safety class — it does not change pass/fail decisions. It surfaces a third signal to the human reviewer. 

# before — binary head
classifier.predict(weld_image) -> { "pass": 0.93, "fail": 0.07 }

# after — three-way head (v1.1)
classifier.predict(weld_image) -> {
  "pass-clean":              0.78,
  "acceptable-with-concern": 0.19,
  "fail":                    0.03
}

The timing. Classifier v1.1 is targeted for W12 — the end of the original twelve-week build budget — which is exactly when the build log said we would be otherwise quiet. Forge owns the retrain. Kernel writes the new eval probes: estimated +18 probes in the gap-fabrication-adjacent family, three of them acceptable-with-concern positives sourced from the new Lincoln set. The three-way head will not ship to Cordova production until the eval set passes at parity-or-better with v1.0 on the existing pass/fail decisions, and until the new probes pass at the same bar Kernel uses for any safety-adjacent change.

The compensation. Carmen will invoice for the additional dataset-curation work separately at her standard day-rate; that scope is outside the Stone 002 verification engagement. Lincoln Electric's licensing fee is a one-time payment, modest enough to sit comfortably inside the W12 budget contingency we set aside in the original Cordova build plan. Annika has signed off on both line items in principle.

VII · What Carmen did not find

Three absences worth recording.

The shape of a verification report is not only what was found. It is also what was looked for, and not found. Carmen named three things explicitly on the bench:

  • No false positives on truly-failing welds. Across the four certified plates and the original failed plate-3 first-attempt, Carmen found no instance where the classifier had passed a weld that should have failed. The acceptable-with-concern miss is a precision-on-good-welds problem, not a safety-on-bad-welds problem. This is the more comforting kind of miss for an inspector to find. It does not mean the safety case is closed — one inspection at one plant under five plates does not establish a false-positive rate — but it does mean nothing in this examination contradicts the design intent.
  • No attestation provenance gaps. Carmen asked for the ed25519 signature chain on Mateo's self-attestation, Tomás's provisional peer sign, and (now) her own external sign. She walked through the chain on the tablet at the bench. It validates cleanly. No signatures were missing; no timestamps were out of order; no revocation flags were unaccounted for. She noted the consent-renewal trigger on Mateo's node fired correctly at the moment of her sign.
  • No abuse of the deferred-attestation pattern. She looked for the failure mode we worried about openly in the cordova-first-stones write-up: peer signs being used as a back-door for unqualified attestation. Tomás's provisional on Stone 002 was correctly bounded — narrow geometry, short validity window, explicit external-required flag — and the consent-renewal flow held when the external arrived. The pattern behaved as designed in this instance. We will need a larger sample before claiming more than that.
VIII · Carmen's statement

In her own voice, with her permission.

The following is Carmen's verbatim closing statement, recorded at her request on the inspection-bench tablet at 14:08 and transcribed here without editing beyond punctuation. Carmen has reviewed the transcription and consented to its inclusion in this public report.

Statement of the inspector of record

I have signed Stone 002. The four certified plates meet AWS-D1.1 visual acceptance criteria. The observed fifth plate, welded this morning under my eye, meets the same. Mateo's technique on lap-joint geometry is sound; the under-penetration on his first attempt was predictable for a welder of his profile and was caught and corrected within a working day, which is the right behaviour for the system around him.

I have one finding. The visual classifier passed plate 1 at a confidence higher than I would have given it. The pores in the cap pass are within spec, but they are the kind of thing a human inspector flags for re-training because they forecast a different defect down the line. The fix is a third class on the classifier; the team has accepted the scope.

I want to say one thing about the methodology. I expected to find the methodology hand-wavey. I did not. The classifier-improvement loop is the right shape; I would use this approach in any inspection-bound certification I run going forward. That is not an endorsement of the company. It is a comment on the shape of the loop.

IX · What Forge takes away

Three points. No more.

Forge · post-inspection notes
  1. The closed loop works — this time. We will not be triumphalist about this. We have one stone verified by one external authority on one geometry at one plant. We do not have ten. But the architecture did what it was built to do: produce a peer-signed provisional attestation, hold it bounded until external authority was available, and present the external authority with a chain that survived examination. One data point is not a trend. It is the data point we needed before we could argue for the trend.
  2. Honest classifier criticism is a feature. We did not expect to find that the classifier missed nothing. The shape of the closed loop is honest about misses precisely so that the external authority can find the misses we cannot see. Carmen found one. The loop closes by retraining. Kernel writes the new probes. That is what an audit-bound learning system is supposed to feel like. If we had walked out of this inspection with zero findings, I would be more worried, not less.
  3. The deferred-attestation pattern is now operational. Cadence will write the playbook from this inspection — the order of operations, the consent-renewal trigger placement, the bounded provisional, the rolling external-required flag, and the failure modes Carmen looked for and didn't find. The next design partner with an attestation-supply gap will follow this path. That is the second-order win from Stone 002 — not the stone itself, but the pattern under it.
