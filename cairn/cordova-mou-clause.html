Cordova MOU termination clause: resolved at forty-five.

Annika asked for 30 days. Our standard is 90. We came back with 45 and carve-outs. She signed within four hours. This is the formal record of the one clause negotiated after the MOU itself was already signed — and what it now sets as program standard.

Section 2 · The clause as proposed

What Cordova’s general counsel sent us.

The MOU Annika produced at minute 42 of the 2026-05-26 meeting was a clean tracking of our design-partner template, with a single substitution at §7.2. Her counsel had drafted it on her own initiative; we were not asked to circulate ours in advance. The language reads:

Cordova · §7.2
Proposed termination-for-convenience — year 1

“Either party may terminate this Design Partner Agreement for any reason or for no reason upon thirty (30) calendar days’ written notice to the other party, except that, in the event of termination by Customer, Provider shall deliver all Customer Data, including all attested Cairn stones associated with Customer’s personnel, in a machine-readable, vendor-neutral format prior to the effective date of termination. No early-termination penalty shall accrue to either party in respect of any termination occurring during the Initial Term.”

Drafted by Cordova GC · tracked changes on Stratum DPA v2.3 · received 2026-05-26 14:50 PT

The clause is well-written. It is also, by design, asymmetric in time: thirty days is enough notice for Cordova to act on a decision that has already been made internally, and not quite enough notice for us to wind down a graph integration cleanly. That asymmetry is the negotiation.

Section 3 · Our standard

What our enterprise template says.

The Stratum enterprise DPA — the one I drafted with counsel after the Halcyon and Mercator negotiations — carries a different shape at §7.2:

Stratum · DPA v2.3 §7.2
Standard termination — enterprise template

“Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon ninety (90) calendar days’ prior written notice to the other party, provided that no such termination shall be effective prior to the conclusion of the twelve (12) month non-cancellable Initial Term. Either party may terminate for material breach upon thirty (30) days’ written notice and a cure period, in which case data-export and retention obligations shall survive in accordance with §9 (Data Handling on Termination).”

Stratum enterprise DPA · v2.3 · ratified by counsel 2026-03-18

Ninety days for convenience. Thirty days for material breach. A twelve-month non-cancellable initial term. The template is calibrated for the enterprise sale, where the runway to a clean exit is the cost we charge in time instead of dollars. It is not calibrated for a design-partner program where we are asking the customer to commit ahead of the production proof. So the template was always going to bend for Cairn. The question was where.

Section 4 · What’s at stake

Three considerations, three weights.

  1. i.
    For Cordova · Optionality through year-1 uncertainty Annika has not committed Cordova to year-2 spend until she sees first-stone in production. That is the right posture for a CHRO walking a CFO into a renewal conversation. Thirty days, for her, is the right number not because she expects to use it — she does not — but because it tells her board that the relationship is reversible on a timeline that matches the integration risk they have authorized her to absorb. The clause is a posture, not a plan.
  2. ii.
    For Stratum · Runway for a clean wind-down Ninety days is not commercial pride. It is the runway we need to close cleanly. Forge confirmed in writing on 2026-05-27 (engineering thread, 08:42 PT) that the Yield connector wind-down requires roughly fourteen working days end-to-end, and the attestation data export — full Cordova graph, all stones, with provenance — runs another seven to ten depending on archive scale. The honest number for “Stratum can leave cleanly without harming the customer” is forty-something days. Not thirty. Not ninety. Somewhere in between.
  3. iii.
    For the design-partner program · Precedent Whatever number we settle on here will be the number the next two design partners see. The conversation we are about to have with the second design partner (the Phoenix candidate, plus the third in queue) is not a clean slate. If we hold the line at ninety, we tell future partners that the program is calibrated for our comfort. If we cave to thirty, we tell them the line moves under pressure. Either answer is a brand. The answer that ages best is a number that is structurally fair on its own terms.
Section 5 · The negotiated resolution

Forty-five days. With carve-outs. For everyone’s lawyers.

The decision is forty-five days with carve-outs. Three reasons, in the order they mattered:

  1. Forty-five matches the actual wind-down arithmetic Forge’s engineering note confirmed the connector wind-down at fourteen working days and the attestation data export at seven to ten. Add a buffer for the attestation log finalization and we arrive at forty-something. Forty-five is the number where Stratum can leave a customer cleanly — their data in their hands, their graph exported, their stones intact — without rushing the engineering teams into shortcuts that would hurt either party. Thirty is achievable under pressure; forty-five is achievable without it. We do not want a clause that turns a clean exit into a fire drill.
  2. The carve-outs do the real protection work Three carve-outs make the clause structurally fair regardless of the number. Data export delivered within twenty-one calendar days of notice (Forge’s firm engineering commitment, not a stretch). Attested cairn stones remain Cordova property in perpetuity, regardless of whether the relationship continues — Pillar confirmed the data-architecture path on 2026-05-27 (10:02 PT). And the underlying eval logs are retained per our six-month minimum, which is the floor Pillar holds for audit reproducibility. The carve-outs protect the customer’s ownership and our methodology floor. The number on the clock matters less when the substance is settled.
  3. Forty-five is the right number for the program, not for Cordova This is the deciding consideration. Thirty would be a Cordova-specific concession and would set a precedent we would have to defend partner by partner. Ninety would be Stratum-defensive and would tell future partners we did not actually believe in the design-partner posture. Forty-five reads, on its face, as the number both lawyers would have arrived at if they had sat in a room together with no negotiating instructions. It is neither side’s starting position. That is what makes it durable.
Section 6 · The final clause

What we sent back. What was signed.

Final · §7.2
Termination-for-convenience — design-partner standard

“Either party may terminate this Design Partner Agreement for convenience upon forty-five (45) calendar days’ prior written notice to the other party. Upon receipt of such notice, the following terms shall govern wind-down: (a) Provider shall deliver all Customer Data, including a complete export of Customer’s attested Cairn stones in a machine-readable, vendor-neutral format, within twenty-one (21) calendar days of the notice date; (b) all attested Cairn stones generated during the term shall remain the property of Customer in perpetuity, irrevocably and without regard to the continuation of this Agreement; (c) underlying methodology and eval logs shall be retained by Provider for a minimum of six (6) months from the effective date of termination, in accordance with Provider’s audit-reproducibility standard. No early-termination penalty shall accrue to either party. The annual platform fee paid to date shall not be refunded in respect of any termination occurring during the Initial Term, and no further platform-fee obligation shall accrue following the effective date of termination.”

Stratum · sent 2026-05-27 10:14 PT · countersigned by A. Whitfield 14:08 PT

The plain-English read on the fee mechanic: if Cordova gives notice at month six of year one, Cordova has paid roughly $40K of the $80K platform fee. No refund. No further obligation. The clean break is symmetrical in both directions — Stratum keeps what was earned, Cordova owes nothing further. The per-employee and per-stone components stop accruing as of the effective date.

Section 8 · What this sets as precedent

Three principles, forward.

  1. The 45-day clause is the design-partner program standard Effective immediately, the Cairn design-partner MOU template carries 45-day termination-for-convenience as its §7.2. The Phoenix candidate and the third partner in queue will see this clause as the starting point, not as a Cordova concession. If a candidate asks for thirty, we will explain — with the same runway arithmetic Forge provided here — why forty-five is the honest number. We will not move below it for any design partner. We will not need to move above it either.
  2. Material-breach carve-outs remain at 30 days The 30-day material-breach termination from our enterprise template carries forward unchanged into the design-partner DPA. Convenience is forty-five; breach is thirty. That asymmetry is correct. Breach is a different conversation, with different engineering implications, and a different relational posture. We do not want our good-faith wind-down clock to be the same clock as the one that runs when something has gone wrong.
  3. Cairn-stone perpetuity is non-negotiable in all future contracts The most important sentence in the final clause is the one about perpetuity of the attested stones. Stones belong to the customer, in perpetuity, irrevocably, regardless of contract status. This is now Stratum policy, full stop — not a Cairn-specific provision and not a design-partner concession. Every customer contract from this date forward carries it. We do not own the customer’s attestation history. We never have. The clause now says so.
