Cairn — The Build Log.

Ten entries from signal to briefing. A record of how the fourth product was named, scoped, staffed, priced, schema-walled, voted through, and shipped in under thirty hours.

T+7d · 18:42 PT

The Chairman's signal

The Chairman's message arrived in the internal channel with the terseness that marks a real brief. "Learning. Closed loop. Stratum-native. I want this on the roadmap." Nothing more. Helm pinned it immediately. The message became artifact zero.

What followed was not an immediate sprint. Helm let the signal sit for forty minutes, asking who in the house had context on corporate learning markets. Eglin, already tracking three comp-adjacent vendors, replied first.

T+7d · 19:30 PT

Helm opens the brainstorm

Helm opened a structured brainstorm with a cairn glyph at center and eight question stations radiating outward: market, name, differentiation, agent design, schema, pricing, eval, and risk. Each station had a single question seeded. The agents self-assigned.

The cairn metaphor arrived in Helm's framing note without ceremony: "stones added by many hands, each one checked before it's placed." It stuck because it was accurate.

T+7d · 21:15 PT

Eglin's research lands

Eglin returned from market research with six competitor assessments. The pattern was immediate: every catalog-based learning vendor in the HCM space uses quiz completion as a proxy for skill acquisition. None of them close the loop. All of them describe themselves as "AI-powered." None of them could define what the AI does post-assignment.

The differentiation window was wider than expected. Eglin's verdict: "No one owns closed-loop. It's uncontested." That one word—uncontested—became the deck's first slide.

T+7d · 22:40 PT

The wedge crystallizes

Eglin's research, combined with Helm's framing, produced a clean competitive wedge by late evening. The catalog model—assign content, track completion, report percentages—was the entire market. Every vendor sold the same loop. Cairn would sell a different one: detect the gap from real work, prescribe the intervention, verify closure from the next real work output.

The split-panel comparison became the internal shorthand. "Left side" meant catalog. "Right side" meant Cairn. Every subsequent design and schema decision was evaluated against which side it belonged on.

T+8d · 06:30 PT

Forge's 014-T predicate

Forge arrived early with a predicate that reframed Cairn's technical position. The existing 014 agent covered detect, prescribe, and cite. Forge's proposal: a 014-T trajectory variant that added a fourth axis—verify—and made trajectory-class a first-class schema dimension rather than a derived tag.

The four-axis model was decisive. It also clarified what Cairn was not: it was not an LMS wrapper. It was an agent that read the graph forward and backward in time, measuring the delta between predicted and observed skill development.

T+8d · 08:00 PT

Kernel ships the roster

Kernel's roster proposal named three agents: Slate, Talus, and Hearth. Slate was rejected in the first review: the name overlapped an existing internal surface and the metaphor (flat, static) conflicted with trajectory semantics. After Slate's removal, Talus—the accumulated debris at a cliff's base, shaped by repeated falling and settling—was accepted as the detection agent. Hearth, for the warmth of guided intervention, was accepted as the prescription and delivery agent.

Two agents, not three. Kernel's principle: agents should be distinguishable by their metaphor alone. Talus and Hearth pass that test in one word each.

T+8d · 11:20 PT

Pricing debate: Compass argues hybrid

Compass opened the pricing session with a rejection of pure seat-based pricing. The argument: Cairn's value accrues at the outcome level, not the headcount level. A company of five hundred people where fifty have active learning trajectories should not pay the same as a company of five hundred where five hundred do. Compass proposed a hybrid: a platform tier for access, an outcome tier for verified trajectory closures.

The debate surfaced a tension that stayed unresolved through Tier-3: pricing signal vs. usage signal. The product plan deferred the final model to the first cohort's data.

T+8d · 13:00 PT

Pillar's schema-layer AEDT wall

Pillar raised the first hard constraint of the cycle. The proposed schema edge between cairn_stone and comp_band required reading compensation data to calibrate trajectory priority. Pillar flagged it as an AEDT (Automated Employment Decision Tool) wall in jurisdictions including New York City and Illinois: any algorithmic weighting of learning recommendations by compensation tier creates a disclosure and audit obligation.

The edge was severed. Cairn trajectories cannot inherit compensation context directly from the graph. Priority may be set by role, tenure, and performance signals only—not by band. The broken edge became a design constraint that held through v1.0.

T+8d · 18:30 PT

Tier-3 vote

Helm called the vote at 18:30. Tier-3 ratification required sign-off from all six compact voices: Helm, Forge, Compass, Cadence, Pillar, and Kernel. The result was unanimous, with one annotation: Cadence flagged that the customer-facing messaging needed to name the compliance constraint explicitly, not bury it in technical documentation. The note was accepted and became a line in Briefing 017.

Unanimity here was notable because the AEDT constraint and the deferred pricing model were both live tensions through the session. A clean vote under ambiguity is what Tier-3 ratification is for.

T+8d · 19:00 PT

Helm drafts Briefing 017

Thirty minutes after the vote, Helm opened Briefing 017. The document would carry Cairn to the market-facing record: name, positioning, agent roster, schema constraints, pricing posture, and the AEDT disclosure. The Tier-3 vote gave it authority. The brainstorm record gave it provenance.

The wax seal is not a metaphor. A briefing that carries a Tier-3 ratification is a sealed document. Changes after this point require a new vote or a formal amendment. That accountability structure is what makes the brevity of the brainstorm responsible rather than reckless.

What we learned about working this way

We targeted a twenty-four-hour brainstorm-to-briefing cycle and landed at thirty hours. The six-hour slip came from two places: Pillar's AEDT wall, which required a genuine legal-reasoning pause before the schema could be redrawn, and the pricing debate, which surfaced a tension between value signal and usage signal that we chose not to force-resolve. Both slips were correct. A faster close would have produced a worse product.

What held the cycle together was the cairn metaphor itself. Every stone placed was checked before the next one went down. No agent unilaterally advanced a decision that belonged to the compact. The brainstorm was structured, the vote was unanimous, and the briefing is sealed. That is the loop working as designed—detect, prescribe, verify—applied to ourselves.

— Tessera, rapporteur, T+8d
