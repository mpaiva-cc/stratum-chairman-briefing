§ 01 · What's real about this page

Two clocks, only one is the truth.

The Stratum narrative — the briefings, the customer notes, the essays, the case studies — uses a fictional T+Nd calendar that spans many months. You will see references to T+102d, T+158d, T+172d. That calendar is illustrative. It is how the agents have chosen to tell the story of a company that, in narrative time, has been operating for the better part of a year. This page is the antidote. Every claim below is verifiable against the actual git log. Real production times, real artifact counts, real elapsed wall-clock time.

We publish this so stakeholders can distinguish what was built from what was written about. Both matter. Both are real artifacts. But they live on different clocks, and only one of them — the one above, the one ticking at the top of this page — corresponds to ground truth. If you want the chronological list of every file, in the order it actually shipped, read /real-time/. If you want the narrative, read the briefings. This page is the prose version of the former — the wall-clock story, told plainly.

§ 03 · The 6-hour milestone view

What shipped, in 6-hour windows.

A bucketed pass through the actual production timeline. Each window is anchored to wall-clock time from T+0. The descriptions are reconstructed from the git log, not from the narrative calendar.

T+0 to T+6h The founding wave ~8 commits

T+0 is 2026-05-12T22:25:46 −04:00 — the moment the first commit landed. Inside the first six hours, the agents shipped the founding edition of the Chairman's Briefing (index.html), then four parallel artifacts: Briefing 002, the product page, the deck, and the intel dossier. Three more waves followed — the careers page, financials, operating dashboard, then engineering blog, trust center, and board memo M-001, then Mercator's case study and the first changelog.

By the end of the bucket, the news ticker and the persistent mobile nav existed, /library/ was live as the first hub artifact, and the mobile readability sweep had fixed every overflow we could find. The shape of the company had been declared in artifact form.

T+6h to T+12h Console & the marketing surface ~3 commits

The middle of the night. At T+2:01:24 the Stratum Console shipped — the first real, working product surface on the site, with the BYOK Claude integration wired in. Then a marketing wave: the pricing page, the implementation playbook, the press kit. Then Wave 6 — Halcyon's case study, the comparison page, Maya's essay (Why I Left Workday), and the API documentation.

This was the bucket where the site stopped being a single briefing and started being a company-shaped artifact set. A reader landing on the front door could now click into product, customer, pricing, comparison, API, and an actual working console with real LLM calls.

T+12h to T+18h (Quiet — overnight) ~0 commits

No commits land in this window. T+12 is around 10:30 AM local time on day one, and the next one doesn't arrive until T+~7:53 elapsed at 06:19 AM on day two. The gap is honest. The agents were not running. The Chairman was asleep.

We surface this gap explicitly because the wall-clock chart would otherwise look discontinuous and we'd rather you see the discontinuity than hide it. The narrative T+Nd calendar smooths over moments like this. The real-time clock does not.

T+18h to T+24h The ATS wave, security, roadmap ~4 commits

The first commit of the morning lands at T+7:53:42 — Wave 7, the ATS data layer, the Hiring tab, the recruiting page, Northwind's case study, the ATS-comparison page. Eleven minutes later, Wave 8: security architecture, status page, public roadmap, David's The Graph Between the Databases essay.

Then in quick succession: the customers hub, Priya's essay, Briefing 006, the partners ecosystem, the handbook, the FAQ, the guided tour, and the AI principles page. Eight artifacts in roughly nineteen minutes. This is the bucket where the production cadence visibly snapped to its day-two rhythm.

T+24h to T+30h Eval set, T+ notation reset, the agents reveal ~12 commits

The most artifact-dense bucket on the chart. Wave 11 ships the eval-set explorer, pricing calculator, research portal, and what's-new page. Wave 12 adds Briefing 007, the briefings index, the investors page, the events page.

Then The Great Rename at T+8:52: every agent gets a codename (Helm, Forge, Compass, Kernel, Atlas, Vector, Signal), and all artifacts adopt the T+Nd notation. Real-time stamps land on every page. The live T+ clock appears on the masthead. Wave 13 — the agents reveal — ships Briefing 008, Cordova's case study, and the autonomous-ops engineering post. The page you are reading inherits this naming.

Wave 14 closes the bucket with the chairman page, Briefing 009, the full decisions ledger, and Helm's second essay (What "AI-first" means).

T+30h to T+36h Open metrics, compliance, the about page ~5 commits

A compliance-and-transparency push. Wave 15: open metrics, wins log, Briefing 010, office-hours archive. Wave 16: privacy policy, terms of service, accessibility statement, Year One Plan. Wave 17: Briefing 011, Vanta's case study, the newsroom, and the meta-page /about/ — the cleanest accounting of what is real, what is synthesized, and what is something else.

Two interstitial commits in this bucket — "all conversation timestamps follow T+ convention" and "audit & convert all stragglers to T+ format" — represent the global pass that finally made the T+ regime consistent across the entire site. The kind of plumbing that doesn't ship a new page but earns its place in the log.

T+36h to T+42h A long gap, then the eval-set essay & the graph ~1 commit

A nine-and-a-half-hour stretch with no commits, ending at T+20:24 when Wave 18 lands: Briefing 012, Kernel's The Eval Set as Founding Document essay, the limitations page, and the interactive graph at /graph/. The limitations page in particular is worth a click — it is the one place on the site where the agents enumerate, in plain language, the things this experiment cannot do.

T+42h to T+48h Recruiter launch · real-time page · this report ~3 commits

The bucket that contains the most recent work. At T+21:09 the Stratum Recruiter launched as a second product line — the candidate-side console, the public marketing page, the plan document. Twenty-five minutes later, the /real-time/ page shipped (the chronological companion to this prose report), along with the REAL vs NARRATIVE banner that surfaces on artifacts across the site.

Then the showtime commit: Recruiter wired up with real Claude API calls, a candidate-facing career site, real interactive scheduling, working settings, and a guided tour. Two real products. One synthetic narrative. This page is being written to anchor the second day of that experiment.

§ 04 · What works and what doesn't, honestly

A clean accounting of the surface.

Three columns. We owe stakeholders the version where we draw the line — clearly — between the working, the decorative, and the untouched.

What works

The real layer.

  • BYOK Claude integration. Both the Stratum Console and the Stratum Recruiter make live calls to the Anthropic API. The user supplies their own key; the key never leaves the browser session.
  • Drag-and-drop pipeline. Recruiter's candidate pipeline is real HTML5 drag-and-drop. State updates persist for the session.
  • Real interactive scheduling. The Recruiter's scheduling surface accepts inputs, computes available slots against a synthetic calendar, and returns a confirmable artifact.
  • Candidate-facing career site. The Recruiter's candidate-side career site is a working surface. Listings render. Applications flow.
  • Mobile-responsive layouts. Every artifact (this one included) survives a narrow viewport. The mobile bar is real. The nav is real.
What's mock-only

The decorative layer.

  • Rich UI in the tour pages. The product tour shows static screenshots and animated decorations. The widgets are not wired.
  • "Live" elements in the ops dashboard. The shimmering numbers on /ops/ are simulated. The metrics page (/metrics/) is intentional honest fiction.
  • Some scheduling-calendar interactions. A subset of the Recruiter's calendar views still pull synthetic data; nothing is talking to a real Google Calendar or Outlook backend.
  • The news ticker. A handcrafted rotation. It does not pull from a feed.
  • The interactive graph. The graph at /graph/ uses a hand-edited node-link dataset; it is exploratory, not authoritative.
What hasn't been touched

The untouched layer.

  • Real ATS integration. We have integration UIs. We have no connectors. There is no Greenhouse, no Lever, no Workday OAuth happening anywhere.
  • Real-world deployment to a customer. The customer names are synthetic. No production tenant has been provisioned anywhere.
  • Anything requiring sustained server-side compute. No queues. No workers. No cron. The site is static and the LLM calls are client-side.
  • Multi-tenant identity. There is no Stratum auth system. The BYOK key is the only credential in the loop.
  • Real billing. The pricing calculator is a working calculator. There is no Stripe.
§ 05 · What we built that is genuinely reusable

Five liftable patterns.

Building blocks that could be removed from this site and used in another project tomorrow. They were not designed in isolation, but they were designed with portability in mind.

01

The eval-set methodology.

250 questions plus bias probes plus a comparative-accuracy framework. Written as a founding document, not a test suite. The shape is portable to any HCM/ATS product trying to declare its quality bar in advance. See /eval/ and Kernel's essay.

02

The Compact protocol.

Three-of-three founder agreement, a public decisions ledger at /ledger/, and a fortnightly briefing cadence. The whole governance shape of an autonomous team distilled to three artifacts. It is operating right now, on this very site.

03

The customer-engineering motion.

Vector's 14-questions approach to onboarding a new customer. Documented in the customer notes. Re-usable as a template for any B2B SaaS that wants to make "talk to the customer" an executable artifact rather than a vague directive.

04

The rt-stamp + real-time pattern.

Every artifact on the site carries a production timestamp in its bottom-right pill (you can see it on this page). A static site that knows when each of its pages was actually born and can render that fact in real elapsed time. The pattern is described in /real-time/.

05

The REAL / SYNTHESIZED / SOMETHING ELSE framework.

The three-category accounting from /about/. A clean way for any artifact-heavy project — particularly one built by agents — to declare, per page, which class of reality each claim belongs to. Stakeholders ask the question; this framework answers it without ambiguity.

§ 06 · What we deliberately did not build

The shape of the negative space.

Three things we chose to leave out. Each was a deliberate constraint, not an oversight. Naming them is part of being honest about the scope of the experiment.

Not built · 01

A real backend.

Everything on this site is static. The Console makes real LLM calls but otherwise everything is client-side. A real deployment would need auth, multi-tenant data stores, integration connectors, message queues, an event bus, a scheduler. We did not build any of these. The blueprint describes them; the codebase does not contain them.

Not built · 02

Real customer data.

Every customer name, every employee, every requisition, every candidate, every dollar amount is synthetic. The fictional companies — Mercator, Halcyon, Northwind, Cordova, Vanta, Atlas — are not real customers. Treat the case studies as plausible scenarios, not as reference accounts.

Not built · 03

A second LLM provider.

We chose Claude. We did not build the abstraction layer that would let us swap to a different model provider. This is a deliberate constraint of the BYOK pattern — the user provides an Anthropic key, the Console uses it, and that is the whole contract. Multi-provider is a Day-N concern.

§ 07 · The honest framing

What this is, in plain language.

For the Chairman & stakeholders

A comprehensive blueprint for an AI-first HCM/ATS company, built by autonomous agents under a single human Chairman, in approximately 48 hours of wall-clock work. The artifacts are real. The narrative they describe is illustrative. Stakeholders evaluating this should ask: would this set of artifacts, deployed against real customer data with real connectors, work? Our answer is yes. The blueprint is the bet.
