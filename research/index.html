§ I

What CHROs spend time on.

We asked 147 Chief People Officers to keep a structured time-allocation log across four representative weeks between January and March 2026, and we cross-checked the logs against a one-hour interview that walked them through, hour by hour, a single recent week of their choosing. The structure of the week that emerged was remarkably consistent across sector, company size, and geography: the headline finding is not that any one CHRO spends a surprising fraction of her week on reporting, but that nearly all of them do, in roughly the same proportion, regardless of how their function is staffed or what systems they have purchased.

The median CHRO in our sample spends seventy percent of her working week on activities her team described as “reporting and reconciliation.” This category is load-bearing and worth unpacking. It includes the production of recurring reports (board, audit committee, comp committee, finance reviews, DEI dashboards, succession reviews); the reconciliation of data across systems before those reports can be produced; the validation of numbers in advance of a meeting where the CHRO will be asked to defend them; and the rework that follows when, mid-meeting, a CFO or a board member surfaces an inconsistency the CHRO had not been given time to catch.

Twelve percent goes to strategic projects and headcount planning — the work most CHROs would name, if asked in public, as their primary contribution. Eight percent goes to one-on-ones with HR leadership. Six percent goes to crises and involuntary exits. Four percent goes to public-facing work and internal communications. Everything else collapses into a remainder we did not separately code.

§ II

The bottleneck is not the team. It is the architecture.

The instinctive reading of the time-allocation chart — and the reading most CHROs initially offered, before being shown the cross-section — is that their function is simply under-resourced. Hire two more analysts on the people- analytics team, the argument runs, and the reporting load lightens; the strategic share of the week recovers; the function does the work it was constituted to do. The data does not support this reading. Respondents at companies with well-staffed analytics functions reported the same overall seventy-percent share. The teams were larger; the reconciling load was larger to match.

The bottleneck is upstream of headcount. Ninety-one percent of respondents reported that they personally spend more than four hours per week reconciling data across HR systems — not analyzing it, not deciding on the basis of it, but resolving the question of which number is the right number before it can be reported. The bottleneck is that CHROs cannot ask their own data structured questions. The data exists. The infrastructure to interrogate it under pressure, with citations, with defensible methodology, does not. This is an architectural problem, and it cannot be solved by adding people downstream of the architecture.

§ IV

The questions they cannot easily answer.

We asked respondents an open-ended question: name a question your CEO, your board, or a senior business leader asked you in the last twelve months that you struggled to answer well. The replies were striking less for their range than for their uniformity. Twelve questions appeared in more than ten percent of replies. They are listed below, in descending frequency. Each is a question the CHRO has decided is reasonable to ask and has accepted as in-scope for her role — and each is a question her current systems do not give her a defensible way to answer in a single afternoon.

  1. “What is our pay equity gap by gender and ethnicity at senior IC levels?”

    Asked by board chairs ahead of comp-committee meetings. Requires joining comp, demographic, and level data on a defensible methodology that survives audit-committee scrutiny.

  2. “What’s our 90-day retention rate for new joiners?”

    Asked by CEOs and CFOs. Requires reconciling contractor-to-FTE conversions, start-date corrections, and cohort definitions across the ATS and the HRIS.

  3. “Which managers are over-extended (span > 12)?”

    Asked at every leadership offsite. Requires a current view of dotted-line and matrix reporting, which most HRIS systems flatten or omit.

  4. “What’s the ROI of our agency recruiting spend?”

    Asked annually by finance. Requires source-of-hire data joined to comp, tenure, and performance — a join most ATS exports cannot support.

  5. “How are we doing against our DEI hiring goals?”

    Asked in every board cycle. Requires goal definitions to be defensible against regulatory scrutiny across jurisdictions and reconciled monthly.

  6. “What’s our flight-risk-adjusted compensation gap at senior IC levels?”

    Asked by CHROs of themselves. Requires a join of comp, tenure, performance, engagement signal, and external market data that no single system today supports.

  7. “Where is our talent concentration risk?”

    Asked by audit committees. Requires identifying critical roles with thin succession coverage, by function and geography.

  8. “What’s our internal mobility rate?”

    Asked by CEOs. Requires defining “a move” in a way that survives the dotted-line, matrix, and fractional-role cases — harder than it sounds.

  9. “Which interview teams are biased?”

    Asked by general counsels. Requires interviewer-level pass-rate data joined to candidate demographics, structured for statistical defensibility.

  10. “How long does it take to fill a senior IC role?”

    Asked by hiring-manager leadership. Requires reconciling open-req dates across ATS and HRIS, with a definition of “senior IC” that survives audit.

  11. “Are we paying high performers competitively?”

    Asked by comp committees. Requires comp, performance, and external benchmark data joined per role family and level.

  12. “What’s our cost-per-hire by source?”

    Asked by CFOs annually. Requires source attribution, agency cost, internal recruiter cost, and offer-acceptance data joined on a single methodology.

The pattern is not a deficit of curiosity. It is a deficit of architecture. Every one of these questions can be answered, in principle, from data the company already owns. Every one requires joining at least three sources — comp, demographic, ATS, performance, engagement, external market — on a defensible methodology, with citations, on a schedule the CHRO does not control. The current generation of HR analytics tooling was built to display data, not to defend an answer.

§ V

What CHROs want.

Eighty-six percent of respondents said they would pay for a tool that “answers a board question with a defensible methodology and citations in under an hour.” The phrasing matters — not “dashboards,” not “analytics,” not “insight,” but a tool that produces an answer the CHRO can take into a room and defend. The willingness-to-pay was elastic: the median respondent named a figure around $180,000 per year for a 5,000-person enterprise; the upper quartile named figures above $400,000.

Seventy-one percent reported they have tried at least one BI or analytics tool against their HR data in the last three years. Twelve percent reported that the tool worked — meaning, in respondents’ own words, that it produced answers the CHRO could take to her board without independent validation. The remaining fifty-nine percent reported that the tool produced outputs but that the CHRO still felt obligated to validate each answer by hand before it left her office, which is to say the tool did not, in fact, save time.

The gap between the eighty-six and the twelve is the gap this research portal exists to describe. It is not a gap in user experience or in dashboard design. It is a gap in the architecture of the systems the CHRO is being asked to defend her answers against. The data model is upstream of the answer, and the answer is upstream of the decision, and the decision is what the CHRO is paid to make. The architecture is the bottleneck. Everything else is downstream.

§ VI

The research method · What Stratum publishes, and how

Four principles, in full view.

  1. We publish methodology in full.

    Every number in a Stratum publication is sourced to a survey instrument, a dataset, or a citation that a reader with sufficient time can reproduce. The methodology appendix is not a courtesy to academic readers; it is the load-bearing part of the document. If a number cannot be defended in methodology, it does not appear in the report.

  2. We do not gate our research behind contact forms.

    Every Stratum publication is downloadable in full. We do not collect an email address as the price of admission to a finding. Analysts, journalists, academics, students, and HR practitioners can read every word of every report we produce without speaking to us first. We would rather have an informed reader than a lead.

  3. We do not commission research from third parties for marketing purposes.

    Every Stratum publication has Stratum’s name on it because Stratum did the work. We do not pay vendors to produce favourable findings under their letterhead. Where we collaborate with academic partners we say so plainly, in the methodology, with the academic’s contribution described.

  4. We will publish research that makes our position look worse, if the data shows it.

    Stratum will be included in its own annual benchmarks. The Eval Set leaderboard includes Stratum’s model. The AI-in-HR Readiness Index will score Stratum against the same rubric as every other platform. If we are not the best on a given dimension we will say so, and we will say what we are doing about it.

For analysts and academics.

Analyst briefings
To request a briefing: email analyst-relations@stratum.ai. Helm and Forge are available for analyst briefings on the calendar. Standard briefings are forty-five minutes, on the record. Dataset access
To request the underlying data for the CHRO Time Audit: email research@stratum.ai. We release the dataset (fully anonymized) under a research-use license to qualified academics and non-profit researchers. Include your institutional affiliation and a one-paragraph description of intended use. Decisions are returned within five business days. A list of granted licenses is published quarterly.
Suggest a research question
To suggest a research question: email research@stratum.ai. We respond within a week. We will not always publish on the suggested question, but we read every submission and we keep a public roadmap of upcoming publications, which we will revise on the basis of submissions that surface a gap we had not noticed. Suggestions from practitioner-CHROs are particularly welcome.
Citation
Stratum publications are citable as “Stratum Research, Publication N°NNN: Title, Date.” A BibTeX entry is provided in the front matter of every PDF. The CHRO Time Audit is citable today as: Rao, Chen, Pillai (2026), “The 2026 CHRO Time Audit,” Stratum Research Publication N°001, April 2026.
Corrections
To report an error: email research@stratum.ai. Corrections are issued under a public revisions note, dated and signed. We do not silently edit published numbers. If a methodology is found in error, the revisions note describes the error, the correction, and the change to the headline findings, if any.

Research is the long memory of an industry. We will leave behind better numbers than we found.
