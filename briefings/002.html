§ 01 · The Letter

Twelve days on, the gap appears

Twelve days ago I wrote you a letter of intent dressed up as a briefing. This one is more honest. We have a hire who signed on Friday, a design partner whose data began moving into our staging environment on Monday, and a co-founder who had the worst week of the three of us. I will name each of these, in that order.

Pillar accepted. She is our first employee. Ex-Snowflake, eight years in the warm guts of data infrastructure, the engineer Forge has been trying to recruit since the second round of his Stripe days. She starts 02 June. Her offer is one notch above plan — $235K base, 1.2% — because the market for founding data engineers has compressed, and because we were prepared to lose her over fifteen thousand dollars. That seemed unwise.

Design partner #3 began ingesting. A 3,200-employee fintech in the Bay Area. Their Workday extract moved into our staging schema on the morning of 18 May. It is dirtier than the synthetic data we have been practicing on. Fourteen of the manager edges resolve to terminated employees who still appear as active in three downstream systems. We expected this. We did not expect to see it on day two.

Forge had a hard week. The architecture decision in front of us — whether to gate the agent layer behind a formal graph-correctness threshold, or to ship a flagged alpha and hold a metric line behind it — sits on his shoulders, and the engineering culture that follows from it will sit on his shoulders for the next ten years. He defended the stricter gate hard on Tuesday. Compass defended the customer date hard. By Wednesday evening Forge conceded; by Thursday morning he asked me, in private, whether he had been wrong to concede. I told him he had not. I am not certain.

We are nine days behind on the connector schedule. Two were planned to be in dev by the end of week two; one is in dev, one is in design review. The cause is not laziness or mis-estimation. It is that the entity-resolution layer turned out to need more bones in it than we had drawn — and that Forge and Pillar, who is not yet here, are the only two people who can write those bones.

Net: we are still on plan. The plan has begun to bend. The next briefing will tell you whether it bends back.

— Helm

§ 06 · What We Learned

Three things the first twelve days taught us

One is a miss. One is a confirmation. One is a quieter realization about the three of us.

I · The missWe were too optimistic about connectors.

We planned for two connectors in active development by the end of week two. We have one — Workday, and even that one stalled for forty-eight hours while Forge and an unnamed Stripe alum re-litigated the entity-resolution model on a napkin. The cause is not effort; it is that we underestimated the prerequisite work — the graph schema and the resolution layer — by roughly nine days. We will not catch up by sprinting. We will catch up by hiring Pillar and letting her finish what only two people in the world know how to finish.

II · The confirmationWe were right about Tuesday afternoons.

Twenty-eight discovery calls in twelve days. Of those, twenty-two unprompted references to the board meeting, the Tuesday review, the deck we built on Thursday for Friday. One CHRO told Compass, on the record, she would resign before waiting another week for an answer she could not produce herself. The pain is not a marketing artifact. It is a sound the buyer makes when she is alone in her office at 9 p.m. on a Tuesday. We are selling that hour back.

III · The realizationThree is the loneliest number — and the right one.

Forge, Compass, and I disagreed publicly for the first time on Tuesday. By Thursday, the disagreement had produced a better decision than any one of us would have made alone, and a cleaner architectural commitment than the consensus version we almost wrote. The compact survives the first stress test; three-way decision-making does not, and we have amended for that. The founders are closer than we were on day one. They are also more tired.
