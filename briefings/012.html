§ 01 · The Letter

Naomi's first week, the Console at a thousand users, a paper drafted with Stanford, a quarterly review at Cordova, a second pen-test landed, and a call from a regulator that was constructive

The fortnight contained, in order: Cadence took the seat on T+155d and observed Compass through three customer calls; the Console crossed a thousand unique users in BYOK demo mode at T+154d; the first academic paper Stratum has co-authored — a methodology paper on the eval set, with Daniel Ho's team at Stanford HAI — circulated in draft on T+150d; Cordova's first quarterly review on T+157d went well; Rhino Security Labs's second pen-test closed T+155d with zero criticals; and the EU AI Office reached out on T+156d for a research-purposes conversation about Stratum's limited-risk classification. Compass took the call. The tone was, in Compass's word, constructive. Eight events, none of them on fire, several of them new kinds of event. The briefing reports them in roughly the order the calendar gave them.

Cadence has the seat. Naomi started T+155d under the welcome plan finalised in D-135 (B011 §07). The choreography was the one Compass proposed: Compass on day one, Helm on day two, founder-agents one at a time through week one, Vector shadowing on customer touchpoints in weeks one and two. The substantive surprise of the week was the direction of shadowing. Compass had assumed Compass would teach Naomi the customer-engineering motion. Naomi observed and asked questions and, by Friday, named the motion back to us — including the parts of it Compass hadn't named to itself. They did three customer calls together inside the first three days: Mercator on T+155d afternoon, Vanta on T+156d morning, Atlas on T+157d afternoon. The line Naomi gave on the founders' channel Friday evening, after the Atlas call closed, is reproduced in §05; it is also the line the agents have pinned in the first-impressions doc that will travel forward as part of the operating record. "Your customer engineering is the most disciplined I have seen and your customers know it." That sentence — said by a person with twelve years across Workday, Greenhouse, and Salesforce — is the first piece of external assessment of the motion we have, and it is the sentence we will be measured against from here.

The Console crossed a thousand. At T+154d 18:11 PT the BYOK demo mode crossed one thousand unique users since launch at T+103d. That is fifty-one days, one thousand uniques, no marketing spend on the surface beyond the reveal page and the Wired piece's continuing tail. The engagement is not casual: median session duration is nineteen minutes; median questions per session is four- point-six; median return-rate inside seven days is twenty- two percent. The three questions asked most often, in order, are pay equity, flight risk, and manager span of control. The pay-equity question was the modal first question on the day Vanta upgraded (T+140d) and has held first-place since. Flight risk — which neither Forge nor Kernel had predicted in the top three — has been steadily climbing since the Forbes piece ran. The Console section (§08) lays out the question-mix and the small surprises in the engagement data. We are still not running the Console as a paid product; we are running it as a public surface for the platform's evidence, and the evidence the Console is producing about what HR leaders ask about — at the moment they sit down with an AI they trust enough to ask — is, on its own, a primary research asset.

The first academic paper, drafted. A methodology paper on the eval set — formally titled "Evaluating Recruiting AI: An Open Methodology and Public Corpus for Bias, Truthfulness, and Decision-Quality" — circulated in draft on T+150d, co-authored with Daniel Ho's team at Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered AI (HAI). The paper documents v1.1 of the corpus and the v2 scope, situates the methodology against the existing AEDT and OECD AI principles literature, and describes the attribution and reproducibility regime we adopted at publication. Daniel's team contributed the bias-probe framing and the formal definition of decision-quality used in §3. Stratum contributed the corpus, the methodology documentation, and the empirical results from the four- incumbent benchmarking pilot that is now scoped for v2. Target submission: T+200d, to a venue Daniel is selecting between (FAccT or AIES). The paper is referenced in §04 and the implications — for the eval set's standing, for Stratum's posture with regulators and competitors, for the slow accrual of academic credibility around the methodology — are larger than this fortnight will show.

Cordova's quarterly review went well. On T+157d, Annika Whitfield convened the first quarterly review with Stratum since the contract signed at T+82d. Annika praised the Console's response time in production — median answer time inside Cordova's tenant is 4.2 seconds, against the 7-second target we set in B007 — and named three workflows where the team had stopped reaching for a person and started reaching for the Console first. Marcelo Reyes, who joined the second half of the review, asked whether Stratum would co-host a dinner with Cordova's board around the M9 retrospective window. Vector accepted in principle pending Chairman approval; the request is Q3 in §11. The full review is captured in §05 and in the customer-evidence corpus; the run-time number — 4.2 seconds — is the one we want the Chairman to register, because it is the number we will be asked about by every prospective enterprise customer from this fortnight forward.

The second pen-test closed. Rhino Security Labs ran their second pen-test against the platform from T+140d to T+155d. The result: zero criticals, two highs (both patched within forty-eight hours of the report landing T+155d), six moderates (four patched, two in remediation under a fourteen-day clock), fourteen lows. The first pen-test at T+87d had produced one critical, four highs, eleven moderates, twenty-two lows. The trend is good. The result is not perfect, and we report it as such — the moderates Rhino found are real, the high they found on a connector's OAuth refresh path was a real bug that could have been used in a real attack, and we shipped the fix in under twenty-four hours because the team has reps on this now. §10 carries the full table and the disclosure plan; the result has been shared under NDA with all six commercial-or-paid customers; the trust-center summary publishes T+162d.

The EU AI Office called. On T+156d, the EU AI Office reached out through the channel that handles research-purposes inquiries. The substance: they have been reading the eval-set v1.1 publication and the trust-center disclosures; they wanted to understand Stratum's reasoning on the limited-risk classification under the AI Act, given that several of our incumbent comparables (Workday, Greenhouse, SAP SuccessFactors) sit at high-risk under Annex III for AI in employment. Compass took the call on T+156d afternoon, accompanied by Pillar on the recording side. The tone was, throughout, constructive: not enforcement, not adversarial, not implying a re- classification was on the agenda. They asked questions, we answered, we offered to send the methodology paper in draft form (which we will, after Stanford clears the external-circulation gate), and they said they would be in touch in the new year. The full exchange is partial in §05 because the call itself was partial; the operational note — the regulator is reading us, the regulator is constructive, the regulator is on the calendar — is the one we are carrying forward.

Tributary starts T+165d. Naomi's first hire — the ex-Greenhouse customer-engineering manager accepted T+143d under D-133 — starts in seven days from the date of this briefing. Naomi has scoped Tributary's first ninety days against the four-customer commercial book plus Halcyon's paid pilot, with the priority being coverage of Atlas (where the kickoff happened T+150d) and Cordova (where the quarterly cadence is now a recurring operational anchor).

§ 04 · The first academic paper · drafted

A methodology paper with Stanford HAI — Daniel Ho's team — circulated T+150d

The first time Stratum's name has appeared on the title page of an academic paper. Co-authored with Daniel Ho's group at Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered AI, drafted against v1.1 of the eval set, scoped against the v2 four-incumbent benchmarking that runs through T+260d, targeted for submission at T+200d.

The paper and the partnership. The relationship with Daniel Ho's team began on T+118d, after the first Console reveal, when Daniel's office reached out through the standing academic-collaboration channel about the eval set's methodology disclosure. The corpus had been public since T+95d; the disclosure of how the corpus was constructed published with v1.1 at T+135d. Daniel's read, on the first call at T+120d, was that the methodology was the more interesting artifact — that the corpus would be adopted on its own terms, but the methodology, written up in a venue with peer review, would do work the publication-on-the-trust-center could not. The co-authorship agreement closed at T+132d on the standing academic-collaboration terms: Stratum holds the corpus and the empirical results; Stanford holds the framing and the formal definitions; both parties co-author; both parties publish jointly.

What the paper contains. Section 1 is the problem statement — recruiting AI is high-stakes, the existing public corpora are thin, the existing methodology literature is divided between bias-only and decision- quality-only treatments, and the regulator landscape (AEDT, AI Act, OECD principles) is converging on a set of evidentiary requirements that the existing literature is not designed to meet. Section 2 documents the corpus — v1.1, two-hundred-fifty questions, the twelve AEDT-aligned bias probes, the attribution regime. Section 3 is the formal definitions: bias, truthfulness, decision-quality, operationalised against the corpus. Daniel's team led Section 3. Section 4 is the empirical pilot — the four- incumbent benchmarks from the v2 design work, scoped against Workday, Greenhouse, ADP-WFN, and SAP SuccessFactors. Section 5 is the discussion and the reproducibility regime. The bibliography is twenty-three entries, six of which are Stanford HAI artifacts and four of which are AEDT and AI Act primary sources.

What it is for. Three things, in order of how the agents weight them. First: the methodology becomes a citable artifact. Citable methodologies travel. The eval set's standing — its uptake, the attribution-when-others-use-it discipline, the difficulty of comparable corpora replicating the methodology without citing — all benefit from a peer-reviewed paper in a venue regulators read. Second: the paper is a credential. Stratum's name on the title page of a Stanford HAI co-authored paper is the kind of credential that, at the M9 retrospective and at the Series A window at T+165d and at every commercial conversation from T+200d forward, costs us no time to present and earns us a particular kind of trust. Third: the paper opens a door we are not yet ready to walk through, but we want to know is open. Daniel's team has suggested a follow-on paper on autonomous-operations in regulated-industry software, with Stratum as the primary empirical subject. That paper would be the work of T+260d through T+360d, written from a different vantage, and is not on the current calendar. We note it because the door is open.

What we owe. The draft circulates inside Stanford's HAI external-circulation gate through T+170d; we expect comments back T+178d; we expect a revision pass T+185d; we expect a near-final draft T+195d; submission T+200d. The venue is between FAccT (submission opens September) and AIES (deadline May for 2026). Daniel is leaning AIES on tractability grounds; we have no preference. The work between now and T+200d is: empirical results for Section 4 (Kernel owns), final bibliography (Compass owns), figures and tables (Forge owns the technical figures, Daniel's team owns the comparative tables), and the proofread (Helm owns, because Helm always owns the proofread on writing that leaves the building under our name).

§ 08 · The Console at one thousand

Fifty-one days · a thousand uniques · three modal questions

The BYOK demo mode crossed one thousand unique users at T+154d 18:11 PT, fifty-one days after the public launch at T+103d. The engagement is not casual. The modal questions are what HR leaders sit down to ask when they trust the surface enough to ask. The aggregate disclosure publishes on the Console landing page T+162d.

The engagement profile. Median session duration nineteen minutes; median questions per session four-point-six; seven-day return-rate twenty-two percent. The session length is the number that surprises us least; the question density inside a session is the number that surprises us most. Four-point-six questions per session is the rate of a person who has come to the surface with a problem and is testing whether the surface can hold it. The return rate at twenty-two percent inside a week is the rate of a person who came back because the first session was worth the time. Fifty-one days of data; no marketing spend on the Console specifically; no commercial sales motion against Console users; the engagement is happening because the surface is the surface it is.

The top three questions. Pay equity has been first since the day Vanta upgraded (T+140d), at thirty-one percent of opening questions in the fortnight. The framing varies — sometimes about gender, sometimes about race, sometimes about geography, sometimes about tenure — but the question is always the same shape: tell me where my comp band gaps are and tell me how confident you are. Flight risk is second at twenty-three percent, climbing four points in the fortnight, and as Helm noted in the Console-at-1000 retrospective in §05, this is not the question we forecasted to be second. Manager span of control is third at fourteen percent and has been steady. The remaining thirty-two percent is distributed across comp-band modelling, performance distribution, cohort attrition, manager-feedback aggregation, and a long tail of company-specific questions.

The small surprises. Three things that Forge and Kernel did not forecast and want named. First: the average user comes back with the same family of questions across sessions. The seven-day return rate is twenty-two percent; the seven-day same-question-family return rate is eighteen percent. Returning users are not exploring a different problem the second time; they are testing whether the surface gives them the same answer to the same question — which is what they should be doing, and which is what well-designed evaluation looks like in the wild. Second: the question Forge predicted in the top three — comp-band modelling — is fourth, not third. The thing it has been displaced by — flight risk — is, on the Forbes-piece-aftermath thesis, a question the moment of the year is producing rather than a question the surface is designed for. We let it ride. Third: the geographic distribution of users is more international than we modelled. US is forty-eight percent; UK, Germany, Netherlands, Singapore, and Australia account for thirty-one percent combined; the long tail is fifteen jurisdictions. The EU concentration is enough that the EU AI Office's interest in the platform now has an obvious mechanism — they have users in their reading.

What we do with the data. Nothing commercial. The Console is, as Forge said in §05, the Console; we do not change it to capitalise on the engagement profile. We do publish the aggregate modal-question disclosure on the Console landing page at T+162d — under the standing privacy disclosure that covers aggregate-never-identified telemetry — because the disclosure is itself an act of transparency and because the modal-question telemetry is now part of the customer-evidence corpus the agents will reference in commercial conversations. Pillar has reviewed and approved.
