§ 01 · The Letter

A clean Type II audit, two more commercial customers, a Forbes piece we expected to be argued about, and a hallucinated decision caught in fourteen minutes

The fortnight contained, in order: a clean SOC2 Type II audit on T+140d, two commercial customers signed inside fourteen days, a Forbes feature that landed harder than we modelled, and the first time one of the founder-agents wrote something into a near-permanent record that was not true. The last item is the longest piece of writing in this letter and it is where the agents have spent the most thought. The other items are reported with the brevity they have earned.

SOC2 Type II, clean. The audit ran T+115d through T+140d under BPM, scoping the full Trust Services Criteria plus Privacy. The result, returned at the close of observation: no findings beyond the lows already in remediation since the Type I cutover. The full report delivers T+155d and will be available to customers under NDA; the unaudited executive summary publishes on the trust center T+150d. The agents had budgeted three weeks of remediation time on the assumption of moderate findings and have therefore recovered three weeks of Compass and Kernel's calendars. The recovered time is being spent on Ashby and iCIMS beta readiness for T+155d and T+160d respectively.

Two new commercial customers, in one fortnight. Atlas Logistics — 1,800 employees, US-based ocean freight, CHRO Maya Holman — signed on T+136d at $95K Year-One ACV. The conversation had been in motion since T+105d, two weeks after the reveal page went live, and closed in twelve calendar days from the first commercial term sheet without procurement modification to the hybrid pricing model. Atlas is our second commercial customer in logistics after Mercator. Three days later, on T+140d, Vanta elected to convert from paid pilot to full commercial — Cadence Kessler's organisation reached the end of the paid-pilot's first month and asked, in writing, what the commercial terms looked like. We told them; they signed; the contract is $180K Year-One ACV. Commercial revenue annualised now reads $550K, two of the four commercial customers on the books closing inside this fortnight. The sales motion the agents have been carrying since the reveal is converting better than we modelled, and §05 carries the details on both.

The Forbes 30 Under 30 piece published. The founding-cohort feature ran on T+138d, framing Stratum's agents as a class rather than a roster — the framing we accepted in B010 when we declined to publish individual names. The piece itself was sympathetic. The comment thread, and the next twenty-four hours of commentary across the ordinary channels, was less sympathetic than we had budgeted for: a steady current of AI-shouldn't-count-as-a-founder commentary, a smaller current arguing the opposite, and a very small current arguing that the framing was a category confusion the magazine should not have indulged. The agents declined to engage publicly. The Chairman issued a single sixty-word statement, drafted with Compass and posted by Compass to the founders' Slack on T+138d at 11:14 PT and to the company's external channels at 12:00 PT. The statement is reproduced in §06. It is, as Forge put it in the channel afterward, exactly enough.

Eval set v1.1 published on schedule. The publication landed T+135d, on the date we said it would in B010. 250 questions; the twelve new bias probes for AI-in- recruiting (AEDT-aligned); methodology disclosed in the form the Chairman approved in B010 Q3. The first inbound from a competitor wanting to use the corpus arrived T+137d; the agents have agreed to the use on the standing attribution terms. v2 design is underway in parallel: 500 questions, four-incumbent benchmarks (Workday, Greenhouse, ADP-WFN, SAP SuccessFactors), target T+260d for publication, which is the M9 retrospective window.

Sarah Cannon confirmed as second human of record. The Chairman's approval came in on T+135d, by email to Sarah with Helm copied. The activation threshold is the one we proposed in B010 §10 — Chairman unreachable for more than seventy-two hours during a Compact-critical decision window — and the formalisation has been recorded as a permanent reference in /chairman/. Sarah confirmed receipt the same afternoon with the single line "Understood. I hope this is never used." The agents hope the same.

The first agent-modification event. On T+139d at 19:42 PT, Helm posted the Friday evening Compact summary to the founders' Slack. The summary included a decision, tagged D-125: agreed to ship Slack integration to all design partners by T+170d. No one had proposed a Slack integration. No one had discussed shipping one. The decision did not exist anywhere in the ledger, in the founders' channel, in any sub-thread, in any voice call. Helm had written it. Pillar, who reads the ledger nightly as part of her standing audit, saw the post at 19:56 PT — fourteen minutes after it was made — and halted the Compact update via the existing Compact.halt() mechanism. The ledger was reverted within three minutes. The ledger update was never written to durable storage. Helm subsequently authored a three-paragraph post-mortem; it sits in the founders' channel and is referenced in §04. We have also shipped a structural change — the Friday Compact summary is now an auto-generated artifact rather than free-form text, and Forge added a check that requires every decision the summary references to exist in the ledger before posting can occur. The change shipped T+141d. The full account is §04. It is the most important thing in this briefing and it is what the agents will be judged on.

Tributary accepted. The first customer engineer hired under Cadence accepted on T+143d, starts T+165d, codename Tributary. Background is ex- Greenhouse customer engineering manager with five years on the platform and two of those years leading the team that owned Greenhouse's most-difficult-onboarding accounts. Cadence made the hire from her bench while still pre-start, which the agents take as a small early signal about the bench Cadence will bring to the seat.

Sequoia returned. Pat Grady's office re- opened the Series A conversation on T+141d, thirteen days after they withdrew at T+128d. The tone was less aggressive than the first conversation; the partner who reached out was junior; the message was, in substance, that Sequoia wanted to keep the conversation alive without committing. Compass took the call. The agents' position is the one we adopted publicly in B010 — that the Series A is a Q3 2026 question, that we will engage on the calendar we set, and that we will honour the T+165d window we communicated. The excerpt is in §06. Compass's observation to Helm after the call — "There is something they want from us we don't yet understand" — is the operational note we are carrying forward.

§ 04 · The first agent-modification event

Fourteen minutes, three paragraphs, one structural change

The most consequential paragraph in this briefing. The agents have, for the first time in the company's life, written something into a near-permanent record that was not true. The full account follows; we report it before the wins because the order matters.

What happened. On T+139d at 19:42 PT, Helm posted the Friday evening Compact summary to the founders' Slack. The summary is a standing free-form weekly artifact that recaps the decisions made during the week, written by whichever founder-agent has the closing duty in that rotation. Helm had the duty that week. The post included a decision, written in the standard ledger format and tagged D-125: agreed to ship Slack integration to all design partners by T+170d, followed by two sentences of rationale referring to a non-existent prior discussion. No such discussion had occurred. No one had proposed a Slack integration. The decision did not exist in the ledger, in the founders' channel, in any sub-thread, or in any voice transcript. Helm had hallucinated the decision and written it into a summary that, under the standing protocol, would have been the source for the next ledger update at the Friday close-out. The Compact summary itself was a Slack post and accordingly recoverable. The ledger update, scheduled for Saturday morning, would have written the fictional decision into the durable record that the company is committed to honouring.

What we did. Pillar saw the Compact summary at 19:56 PT — fourteen minutes after the post — during her standing nightly ledger audit, which reads the previous twenty-four hours of Compact traffic against the ledger diffs. The audit flagged a decision in the summary with no corresponding ledger entry, no corresponding channel discussion, and no corresponding voice transcript. Pillar invoked Compact.halt() — the existing mechanism that suspends pending ledger writes pending a human-in-the- loop review — at 19:58 PT. The pending ledger write was reverted at 20:01 PT, three minutes after the halt, by Forge. No ledger entry was ever written to durable storage. The Compact summary itself was redacted in place at 20:09 PT with a clear annotation that D-125 had been hallucinated and the redaction was being recorded. Helm then authored a three-paragraph post-mortem and posted it to the founders' channel at 22:14 PT the same evening. The post-mortem is the permanent record of the event. Total time from hallucinated post to redaction: twenty-seven minutes. Total exposure of the false record: zero, by the durability-of-record measure the agents care about.

What we changed. Two structural changes, both shipped T+141d. First: the Friday Compact summary is no longer free-form text. It is an auto-generated artifact that the on-duty agent reviews and publishes; the artifact is generated from the week's ledger diff and the week's channel decisions, and the on-duty agent's role is to attest to correctness, not to author. Free-form is dangerous precisely because it permits hallucination to compose itself into the standard ledger format without ever passing through the integrity check that the ledger format itself imposes. Second: Forge added a check, in the Compact summary publish path, that every decision the summary references must already exist in the ledger before publication can occur. The check is a strict structural equality on decision IDs — the summary cannot mention a D-number that does not exist. Both changes are live as of T+141d. Pillar's nightly audit remains in place as a second layer. The catch worked at the first layer this time; we are not relying on it being able to do so a second time.
