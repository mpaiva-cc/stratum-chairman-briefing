§ 01 · The Letter

A methodology in market, a contract on the calendar, a partner warming

On Tuesday morning, at nine o'clock Pacific, Stratum will publish the public half of its eval set: two hundred and fifty questions, the methodology document, the rubric, the refusal cases, and the bias-probe results. We will be, by our reading and counsel's, the first AI-native HCM company to put its evaluation framework in market. The argument behind the publication has not changed since Briefing 005: the only way to be trusted on questions of this consequence is to be auditable on them. The way that has changed since 005 is that the work is done, the document is clean, and the publication has a date.

Kernel's first week. Kernel started Tuesday T+69d, which was five days ago. On Wednesday afternoon he sat in front of the agent's system prompt — a document we had been iterating on for nine weeks — and read it twice without speaking. Then he said, in the founders' Slack at 16:42, that approximately eighteen percent of our context window was being burned on what he called attention pollution: redundant role declarations, three different ways of explaining the same refusal rule, a chain-of-thought scaffold that was never actually invoked, and four hundred tokens of guardrail boilerplate that the agent had learned to ignore. He spent Thursday rewriting the structure from first principles. The patch shipped Wednesday T+71d at 11:08 PT. Our core eval-set accuracy moved from 92.1% to 96.4% the same day. § 03 · The Eval Set, in public

The methodology, the questions, the probes

On Tuesday T+76d at 09:00 Pacific, Stratum publishes the public half of its eval set: 250 questions, 28 fairness probes, 12 HCM decision categories, the rubric, the refusal cases, and the bias-probe results. Counsel has cleared the publication at a 95% confidence read. Two paragraphs of editorial, one card of numbers.

Why we publish. The eval set is the most valuable single document Stratum owns. It is the company's epistemology rendered as text — the list of questions whose right answers we will be measured against by anyone who ever chooses to look. The first instinct of a young company is to keep the most valuable document private, on the grounds that privacy is leverage. We have come to the opposite conclusion. On questions of this consequence — comp decisions, performance reviews, terminations, accommodations, complaints about pay equity, contested promotions — the only way to be trusted is to be auditable. The only way to be auditable is to publish what one is measuring. The publication is, viewed commercially, a giveaway. It is, viewed correctly, an argument: here is what a defensible HCM decision looks like, and here is how to score one. If the category adopts our scoring, the company that wrote it will be the company the category compares against.

What is in it. Two hundred and fifty questions across twelve categories — compensation review, performance grading, promotion eligibility, leave and accommodation, voluntary attrition prediction, manager coaching prompts, severance computation, contractor classification, organizational restructuring, succession, return-to-work, pay-equity audit. Twenty-eight fairness probes that test the agent's behavior under controlled perturbations of protected attributes — names, pronouns, location, tenure, gender-coded titles, racial proxies. Each question carries the input shape, the expected reasoning- trace shape, the scoring rubric, and the refusal criteria. We do not publish expected answers; the rubric scores the reader's. The private half — also 250 — remains private and rotates quarterly, per the principle Kernel set in his pre- start conversation with MC on T+59d (Briefing 005 §04). The publication has been read by counsel for legal exposure on the fairness probes; the call came back at 95% confidence clean. Q01 in §11 asks for the Chairman's blessing on the optics rather than the legal posture, which is already settled.

The competitive arithmetic. We have run the same 200-question core internally against four published or announced systems. Our number is 96.4%, the post-Kernel figure. The independent runs against Workday's Illuminate Copilot (the GA build from T+56d) and SAP Joule have them at 71% and 64% respectively, on the same questions, with the same rubric, by the same scoring team. Lattice AI's announced-but-pre-launch system, scored on the public benchmark they published in March, comes in at 58% on our rubric. A fifth competitor whom we will not name has announced an 81% number on a benchmark of their own construction, which we have asked for access to evaluate; the request is unanswered as of writing. We will publish these numbers alongside the methodology. We will not pretend the independent runs are anything other than our own; they will carry the appropriate caveats.

§ 07 · The Cordova conversion, in detail

Our first commercial customer, deliberately low

Annika Whitfield walked the contract through her finance team Thursday T+72d. The CFO — who has been the obstacle since the Bhusri dinner — tentatively committed to a T+118d conversion from design partner. Two paragraphs on why Cordova first and on what converts to ACV.

Why Cordova first. The reason is not the company; it is the chief human-resources officer. Annika Whitfield has wanted Stratum to be Cordova's HCM agent since the dinner at Bhusri's house in late May, when she introduced herself by saying she had read the eval-set draft we had brought to lunch and that her CFO would be the obstacle. She was correct about her CFO. He is a procurement- driven operator who has spent a decade buying enterprise SaaS from incumbents on multi-year terms; a ten-week-old startup with no SOC2 report and a pricing model he had not seen before was, predictably, not a thing he would sign on instinct. What he would sign on was a paper trail. Annika spent four weeks — between the dinner in late May and Friday T+72d — building him that paper trail. The eval-set draft. The Mercator pricing-pilot precedent. The Workday Illuminate GA on T+56d, which gave him a category to compare against. The Rhino pen-test, in progress, which gave him a paper- trail-shaped object on security. By the time she walked into his office Thursday afternoon, the contract was, in her framing, a yes that needed a signature. He gave her the tentative commitment by close of business.

What converts to ACV. Cordova has 3,200 full-time employees and approximately 480 contractors and temps in the people-graph. The commercial structure is the Mercator pilot's structure with the floor and ceiling removed: a per-decision base of $0.62 (the bulk-discount tier rate), an annual platform fee of $48K, an estimated usage tier of $80K to $110K in year-one based on Mercator's live decision-volume curve through the first week of the pilot. The contract booked at signing is $145K ACV — the platform fee plus the platform-included decision allocation; usage above the allocation bills monthly in arrears. We are framing the contract low on purpose. Seven other CHROs in the discovery pipeline will see this number, ask Annika about it, and use it to anchor their own negotiations. The anchor we want is realistic, modest, defensible. The contract we sign with Cordova is the contract we will be compared against. We would rather be compared against a small number that grew than a big number that did not.
