§ 01 · The Letter

The company has texture now

The last briefing was about a plan beginning to bend. This one is about what the bend made of us. Four weeks in, the company is no longer an outline filled with intentions; it has surfaces — a connector, an alpha milestone, an employee on her first week, a partner I will fly to see on Thursday because their sponsor was promoted out of our deal. I will name each in turn.

Asha's first week. She started on 02 June at eight in the morning and by Thursday lunch she had found a bug in our graph schema that none of the three of us would have found in a quarter. The bug is small to write and large to live with: the contractor identity model treats temp-to-perm transitions as new entities, breaking tenure analytics. A worker hired as a contractor in March and converted to a full-time employee in May appears in our graph as two people with no edge between them — and therefore as a person with zero months of tenure on the day she should have been counted toward retention. We were going to ship this. Asha caught it in thirty-six hours. The cost of her offer paid for itself before her badge worked at the door.

The first connector hit alpha on 04 June. Five days late against the bent plan, four days earlier than the briefing-002 trajectory would have predicted, which means we have started to recover. Workday HR Core is now reading live data from design partner #3 behind their firewall, in read-only mode, with no agent attached. It is not a product. It is a quiet brown box that resolves 3,412 employees, 47 contractors, and 11 service accounts into a coherent people graph. I have left a longer note about it in §03; I will spare you the lyric here.

A fourth design partner signed — and not the one any of us would have predicted. Northwind Studios is a creative agency, 1,200 employees, headquartered in Brooklyn and Lisbon, with the kind of org chart that does not look like the kind of org chart our pitch deck draws. Their COO called Priya from a layover in Newark and asked if we would let her sign before we changed our minds. We said yes. We are leaning into this one — for reasons I will get to in the dinner readout, because they overlap.

The Halcyon situation. Our second design partner — Halcyon Health, 4,400 employees, the CHRO who was the most enthusiastic person on every call we had with her — has been promoted to Chief People & Strategy Officer with a seat on the executive committee. Wonderful for her; a problem for us. Her COO, Reuben Marsh, inherits the Stratum project along with three other things he did not ask for. I do not know him yet. I will Thursday. §07 has more.

The Bhusri dinner happened on 04 June. Six CHROs, three of whom we knew, three of whom we did not. Two new design-partner conversations started before the cheese arrived. One of those — Annika Whitfield, at a 5,200-employee manufacturer called Cordova — is worth flagging, and §08 does. David remains, in private, slightly unconvinced we should have gone. He was right to be cautious; he was, I believe, wrong on the merits. We can talk about it.

Net: the company has texture now. Plans converted into objects you can touch. One of those objects is a wobble, and the wobble is honest, and we will handle it. The other objects are better than I dared write to you on the 24th.

— M. Chen

§ 03 · The First Connector

A quiet brown box, behind a customer's firewall

On 04 June, at 11:43 PT, the Workday HR Core connector resolved its first production schema into our staging graph. It is not a product. It is the first surface the company has.

Alpha, for us, means something very specific. It means read-only — we do not write anything back to Workday. It means behind the customer's firewall — the connector runs in their VPC, not ours. It means no agent — the copilot surface is not attached to this data and will not be for sixty days. And it means manual schema mapping — Asha and David sat in a room on Wednesday for six hours mapping fourteen fields by hand, the last three of which involved opening a Workday support ticket none of us had hoped to file in 2026.

On Thursday morning, less than thirty-six hours after her badge worked at the door, Asha pulled a coffee from the kitchen and said, quietly, that she thought our contractor identity model was wrong. The example she walked David through was the temp-to-perm transition: an engineering contractor at the fintech partner, converted to FTE on 14 May, who in our graph appeared as two distinct people with zero edges between them. Tenure analytics break, she said. Span of control breaks. Anything that pivots on retention breaks. We are going to need to write a resolver and a backfill and we need them both before any agent sees this data. David did not argue. He asked when she could have it written. She said Wednesday. She means this Wednesday.

What shipping the first connector felt like, in honesty, was less euphoric than I had expected and more procedural. There was no champagne. David and Asha took a screenshot of the record count and put it in a Slack channel called #graph-things that has six members and no emoji reactions. Priya was on a call with Northwind. I was reviewing a contract redline. The cheers came later, at dinner, and they were the right shape. We had earned the next ten weeks of work, not the next ten months of confidence.

§ 07 · The Halcyon Situation

Our most enthusiastic sponsor was promoted out

Worth its own sub-section because it is the first real test the company has of whether early enthusiasm survives organizational change. Risk, not loss. Tracked, not panicked.

Halcyon Health is a 4,400-employee specialty health system headquartered in Phoenix, two campuses in California, one in Reno. They signed as design partner #2 on 09 May. The sponsor was Deepa Anand, then CHRO — by some distance the most enthusiastic person on every call we had with her, the one who introduced us to the others at the Bhusri dinner, the one who once said, on a Wednesday, that she would build her board deck on top of our copilot the day we shipped it. On 02 June, Halcyon's CEO promoted Deepa to Chief People & Strategy Officer with a seat on the executive committee. We are happy for her. We are also less covered.

The Stratum project has been re-assigned to Reuben Marsh, the COO, who inherits it along with their Epic upgrade, a new Phoenix bed-tower programme, and a labour action at one of the California campuses. He is, by reputation, more skeptical than Deepa, more measured, and more allergic to vendors than to problems. None of that is bad. None of that is what Deepa was.

The honest read: this is a risk, not yet a loss. Deepa remains an advocate at the executive level — she has volunteered, in writing, to attend the first Marsh meeting alongside me — and the Halcyon data flow is not in production, so we have not lost ground we have built. The cost is calendar. Marsh will want a different conversation than Deepa did, and that conversation will start in his office, not ours. I am flying to Phoenix Wednesday evening and meeting him Thursday at 11 MST. I will listen for an hour. I will speak for thirty minutes. I will leave him with one decision to make this month and nothing else. If we lose the partner, we lose it in the open, with a paper trail; if we keep them, we keep them because Marsh chose to, not because Deepa lobbied for it.

§ 08 · The Bhusri Dinner — Readout

Six CHROs, two new conversations, one name to flag

Held at Aneel's home in Atherton on the evening of 04 June. Three we knew; three we did not. Maya and Priya attended; David did not, by his own choice, which I supported and continue to.

The format was Aneel's: dinner at seven, two rounds of introductions, no slides, the briefing left in a folder by each place setting for whoever wanted to read it on the way home. Three of the six were people we had been trying to reach since week one — Marisol Cheng at a 9,000-EE retailer in Texas, Devon Park at a regional bank in the Carolinas, and Annika Whitfield at Cordova, a 5,200-employee specialty manufacturer in Ohio. The other three were friends of Aneel's whose orgs we had not mapped. None of the six wasted our time; two of them, before the cheese course, started conversations that have since produced calendar invites.

The standout was Whitfield. She arrived having read the brief, which I did not expect, and she asked one question of David that I have been turning over since: do you let the agent decide which question is the question, or do you make me phrase it? (We let the agent decide; she nodded once and said "good".) She offered, unprompted, to be design partner #5 if we wanted her — to which I said we did but we would not say so until after a second conversation, because we have learned that the first conversation is the easy one. The second is on the calendar for 16 June.

"I've sat through fourteen demos like the one you described. The thing that's different is that you're not selling me a product — you're selling me back hours I haven't had since I made VP." — Annika Whitfield, CHRO · Cordova · 04 Jun, after dessert

On the David question: I want it on the record that he was right to be cautious about the optics of taking Aneel's hospitality this early, and that I think, on the merits, the evening produced value the cautious read would not have. The cost was one evening of our time and one evening of his hospitality, and the surface area now includes two conversations we would not otherwise have started. Aneel has offered to do a second dinner in early August, smaller, more regional. We are saying yes. We will be more selective.

§ 09 · New Decision, queued

An unbudgeted hire, in service of the wobble

Surfaced today because it is the first thing the compact has not cleanly answered for us. A soft test, by design.

Queued · Decision 034 · pending Chairman
We are considering a hire that is not in the month-12 plan: a founding customer engineer, specifically to anchor the Halcyon transition with Marsh and to be the human face of the connector work to design partners #2 through #5. Target offer: $200K base + 0.5% equity; start late June if the candidate (one we already know, ex-Looker forward-deployed) is available. The reason we are surfacing this rather than just doing it is the compact: non-routine spend above $150K, and a deviation from the agreed M12 hiring sequence, both require Chairman visibility. We can act on our own authority and brief you after — but we would rather not. We would rather know whether you want this to clear the full compact-review channel, or whether a written nod by Tuesday is sufficient. The question, in other words, is not about the hire. It is about how the compact wants to handle a hire that the compact did not anticipate.
§ 10 · Open to the Chairman, III

Three questions for this fortnight

The first is the queued decision above. The second is a soft branding ask Aneel made on the evening. The third is an awkward little one from Madrid.

  1. The unbudgeted customer engineer. See §09. The narrow question: are you comfortable blessing this on a written nod by Tuesday, or would you prefer it goes through the formal compact-review channel? The broader question — which is the one we actually care about — is whether the compact should have a light-weight lane for hires of this shape, and if so, what its ceiling and form should be. We do not need an answer to the broader question this week. We need an answer to the narrow one by Tuesday evening.
  2. The Information. Aneel has offered to introduce us to Berber Jin's colleague at The Information for a profile piece — small, founder-led, embargoed until our first commercial contract. Priya is split: she likes the reach and dislikes the timing. David is firmly no. I am cautiously yes, but only if the piece is held until at least one connector is at beta and one partner is willing to be named. Your read?
  3. The design-week budget. Tomás has asked, in writing, for a small $12K design-week budget to fly to Palo Alto from Madrid for five days before his start date of 23 June, observe the team, sketch a v1 of the answer surface, and leave with a recommendation. He has offered to eat the airfare himself; we have refused. The Compact's threshold is $25K so we are under it, but the cleanest read of the spirit is to surface it. We are surfacing it.
