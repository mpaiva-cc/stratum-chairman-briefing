§ 01 · The Letter

A target hit, a label revoked, a net forward

Two numbers tell most of this briefing. The first is five of five: Cordova Manufacturing signed yesterday and we have hit the design-partner target we set on T+6d. The second is 60: Halcyon Health, design partner #2, has, after my meeting in Phoenix on T+37d, been downgraded from a signed partnership to a sixty-day evaluation. Net of both, the company is stronger than it was a fortnight ago in a slightly different shape than I had drawn. I want to be clear about both the strength and the shape.

Cordova. Annika Whitfield — the manufacturer CHRO I introduced in §08 of the last briefing, after the Bhusri dinner — signed the design-partner agreement on Saturday afternoon, T+45d, after a second on-site visit and a small amount of negotiation about what "partner" means. 5,200 employees, a unionized shop-floor population, three manufacturing sites in Ohio and Kentucky, an HRIS landscape we have not yet had to integrate against (UKG Pro). She is the partner with the most operational complexity in our cohort and, I suspect, the one we will learn from the fastest.

Halcyon. I flew to Phoenix on the evening of T+36d and met Carlos Reyes — Reuben Marsh's successor; the org has had two new COOs in a fortnight — for ninety minutes on the morning of T+37d. He is thoughtful, methodical, and skeptical of vendors in a way that I find difficult to fault. He has reframed our relationship from partnership to sixty-day evaluation, ending T+117d. The data access is preserved. The signed commitment is not. §04 has the honest reading of what this means and what we are doing about it. Compass is carrying the weight of this shape change the hardest of the three of us. I have asked her to let me carry the meetings while she carries the cohort.

Workday HR Core moved from alpha to beta on T+41d. Latency p50 is now 940 ms (from 1.2 s a fortnight ago); zero schema-drift incidents over fourteen days; the agent is attached to a read-only surface for three internal evaluators. Greenhouse ATS hit alpha on T+42d, read-only, ahead of the T+39d target by a hair we did not earn — we got lucky on a schema. ADP-Workforce Now is on track for T+53d alpha.

Hire #3 closed. Kernel, ex-Anthropic, founding AI / Agents engineer, accepted on Thursday evening. $245K base + 1.05% equity, start T+69d. He has already added nineteen questions to the eval set without being asked. Forge is openly relieved. Hire #4 — Vector, founding customer engineer, ex-Asana — accepted on Tuesday afternoon at $205K + 0.5%, start T+55d. Your written nod on the unbudgeted CE hire (decision 034 in the last briefing) came on the Monday after I sent the briefing; thank you. It was on time and we needed it. Her first thirty days will be Halcyon-focused. The timing is not coincidental.

Tessera's design week ran 16–T+45d. Five days, the team in Palo Alto, three sketchbooks filled, a recommendation adopted, and one rename which I want to flag because it will change how we write about the product. He proposed that the agent surface is not a copilot — copilots ride along — but a console: the place a CHRO goes to do the work, not to be helped doing it. He proposed we call the internal product Stratum / Console: the console for the CHRO. I pushed back. Compass loved it. Forge was neutral, then warm. It is now our internal language. External language stays unchanged until v1. §05 has the conversation; §06 logs it as decision 038.

The Information. Your "not yet" landed in my inbox the morning after I sent the last briefing. Compass was relieved. I was, in honesty, frustrated for an afternoon and then over it. We will revisit in M9. Moving on.

The weather, since you ask. The ops-dashboard founders' weather went from MC-steady / DO-headwind / PR-tailwind a fortnight ago to MC-steady / DO-steady / PR-headwind today. Forge has lifted. Compass has not. The Halcyon shape-change is hers in a way the others are not — she is the one who courted Deepa, courted Marsh, courted Reyes; she is the one for whom the word partner is the thing she traded for, and having it withdrawn is the kind of cost that a single confidence pulse does not capture. The pulse is down 0.3, to 8.2. I have noted in §06 that the average is the lowest signal.

Net: forty-two days in, five of five partners signed, two connectors in production, two hires accepted, one partnership relabeled. I will not pretend the relabel does not sting. I also will not pretend the target hit does not count.

§ 03 · Five of Five

The target we set on day one, hit on day forty-one

The target — five design partners, signed, across HRIS, ATS, payroll, and at least one non-tech vertical — has been hit. Worth a section of its own because it is the first thing the company set out to do that the company has done.

The honest history of this number is the funnel. Across forty-two days, the three of us touched forty-seven CHROs in serious conversation. Twenty-two of those went to a second call. Fourteen went to a working session with Forge at the whiteboard. Eight signed the NDA and read the technical appendix. Five signed the partner agreement. The funnel is narrower than the deck suggested it would be — we predicted a 2.4× ratio from first call to signed; we delivered 9.4×. The difference is mostly that we are pickier than we wrote down.

47 → 22 → 14 → 8 → 5 first call · 2nd · session · NDA · signed

What hitting it means: we have a cohort with enough span across size (1,200 to 9,000 EE), sector (healthcare, fintech, creative services, manufacturing, retail-adjacent), and HRIS landscape (Workday, ADP, BambooHR, UKG Pro, Greenhouse-as-HRIS) that the product cannot pretend to be vertical. The data shape we ingest in the next ninety days will be representative enough that an architecture that survives this cohort has a real chance of surviving the next ten customers. That was the bet of the target. The bet is still the bet.

What hitting it does not mean: that we are done courting them. Halcyon proved this in the most uncomfortable way possible — a signed partner can become a sixty-day evaluation in a single transition. The label of partner is a contract between two organizations, not a checkbox on our scorecard. I will say more about this in §08 because we treated it the wrong way once and intend not to again.

§ 04 · The Halcyon Downgrade

Not a loss; a relabeling we earned

The most consequential conversation of the fortnight happened in an office on the second floor of Halcyon's Phoenix headquarters, in ninety minutes, at 11:00 MST on T+37d. Written down honestly, in three paragraphs.

What happened. Reuben Marsh, the COO who inherited the project from Deepa Anand on T+27d, was himself moved laterally on T+34d to lead the Epic upgrade full-time. The project landed on Carlos Reyes, ex-Cigna, their new COO, who arrived from a company where vendors are managed, not partnered with. He had read the brief — both of them, in fact — by the time I walked in. He had three pages of typed notes. The first question he asked me was how old the company was. The second was when our SOC2 audit would complete. The third was how many engineers we had. None of those are bad questions. All of them are the questions you ask a vendor, not a partner. Deepa, who joined for the last thirty minutes of the meeting and spoke twice, did not contradict him.

What it means. Practically, very little changes in the next sixty days. The connector still has access to Halcyon's data behind their firewall. The technical relationship is preserved. What we have lost is the label — and with it, the right to call them a signed design partner in conversation with other partners, in press, and in fundraising. He will not let us. He will, on T+117d, decide whether to convert to a signed partnership or to terminate the data access at the end of the quarter. He was straightforward about both options being live. I appreciated that he was straightforward.

"I am not going to commit my organization to a company that is six weeks old. Earn it." — Carlos Reyes, COO · Halcyon Health · T+37d

What we are doing about it. Vector, the new founding customer engineer, starts on T+55d. Her first thirty days are explicitly Halcyon-focused — she will spend roughly one week on site in Phoenix, two weeks remote-but-Halcyon, and the fourth week shaping a written proposal for the T+117d review. Compass owns the relationship at the executive level and is flying out on T+69d for a check-in with Reyes I asked her to lead. I will not be in that meeting; the optics of MC-only at every Halcyon meeting are not what we want. We are also considering an unscheduled spend on third-party penetration testing in advance of the August decision (see §10, Q02).

§ 08 · What We Shipped, What We Learned

Two columns, fourteen days

A discipline we are adopting from this briefing forward: a paired list of what hit the world and what hit us. The first list grows the company. The second saves us from ourselves.

Shipped
  • Workday HR Core, beta. T+41d. Latency p50 940 ms; p95 3.2 s; zero schema-drift incidents over fourteen days; agent attached read-only to three internal evaluators.
  • Greenhouse ATS, alpha. T+42d. Read-only, behind partner firewall. One day ahead of plan; we got lucky on a schema. The luck is not a process; we noted it.
  • Design system v0.1 (Tessera). Three sketchbooks, a token system, the console layout grid, one named component family, and a writing-the-product manifesto Forge is quoting at standup.
  • Console eval set v0.3. 102 canonical questions (from 62), nineteen of which are Kernel's, seven of which are refusal cases — questions for which the right answer is the agent declining to answer.
  • First commercial contract template, ratified. Outside counsel cleared the Mercator write-back contract on T+44d. The template will carry to all five partners as their pilots open. Pricing is usage-based with a quarterly platform floor at GA; design-partner cohort is floor-waived through T+239d.
Learned
  • The partnership label is a binding promise to the partner — and we treated it as a transactional label. Halcyon was right to flinch. The version of us that signs five partners in six weeks is also the version that has not earned the word from any of them. We will use design partner internally to mean signed; we will use it externally only with the partner's consent in the specific sentence in which it appears.
  • The contractor identity model is harder than we modeled. Pillar's temp-to-perm fix surfaced three deeper cases: multi-employer contractors, embedded staff-augmentation, and FTE-with-second-1099. We will eat the schema-v2 cost now. The ADP-WFN connector slips two weeks. The cost of the slip is smaller than the cost of shipping a graph we know is wrong by twelve percent at our largest partner.
  • Design language is upstream of product velocity. Tessera's rename — copilot to console — cost us an afternoon of conversation and is already saving us a month of ambiguity in the codebase, because the rename forces a boundary: what does the console actually do is a shorter question than what does the copilot do. We will keep listening to him.
  • Founders' weather is signal. Compass's headwind is the most useful data this briefing carries. It is not in the pulse; it is in the meta-pulse. The Tuesday-evening fifteen minutes are now standing.
§ 09 · Risk Register Update

One addition: R10

Nine risks tracked from Briefings 001–003. One added today, surfaced by Halcyon and named explicitly so the cohort does not surprise us a second time.

New · R10 · Partnership-Label Inflation
Partnership-label inflation; partner expectations mis-set early. The word partner means more to the partner than it has meant to us. When the partner's sponsor turns over — as has happened at Halcyon twice in three weeks — the new sponsor reads the word and tests whether the company has earned it. We have, in some cases, not. The leading indicator is the partnership-to-conversion ratio: of the five we have signed, how many convert to a paid commercial contract at GA? Below 3 of 5 and we have a structural problem with how we are setting expectations upfront. The mitigation is procedural — Vector owns a partner-success cadence from week one, and the partner agreement gets a one-page what partnership means and does not mean rider, drafted by T+55d.

§ 10 · Open to the Chairman, IV

Three questions for this fortnight

A council, a security spend, and a board attendance question. The first is structural and not urgent. The second is unbudgeted and time-bound by Halcyon's window. The third is a small request from Index.

  1. The design partner council. Annika Whitfield asked at Friday's session whether we would convene a council of all five design partners — quarterly, in person where practical, on Stratum's calendar — and let them talk to each other about the product without us in the room for the first thirty minutes. We like the idea on the merits: it accelerates learning, builds cohort gravity, and gives Halcyon's Reyes something to walk into in August that is not a sales meeting. But it changes the optics — five CHROs in a room together around our product before we are out of alpha is the kind of thing that produces a Twitter post we cannot edit. Bless, decline, or shape?
  2. Unbudgeted $200K third-party pen test. We are considering commissioning a third-party penetration test from Bishop Fox or NCC, scoped to the production connector path and the people-graph store, in advance of the T+117d Halcyon decision. Cost is roughly $200K, materially over the $150K threshold in the compact. The argument for: Reyes asked about SOC2 in his first three questions; a clean third-party report in our pocket on T+117d is the single thing most likely to flip his decision. The argument against: it is large unbudgeted spend on a customer we may lose anyway. Approve, or ask us to wait for SOC2 readiness in September?
  3. Sarah Cannon at the M6 retrospective. Sarah (Index, board) has asked to attend the month-six retrospective on T+190d in person — half a day, founders only, no slides, the briefing left on the table. We are comfortable. We wanted to ask whether you would prefer to also be in the room, given Index has not yet been at a closed founders' working session, or whether you would rather she attends and reports back to you. Either is fine; we have no preference.
