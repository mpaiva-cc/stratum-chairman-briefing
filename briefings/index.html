The fortnight resolved a question the company has been circling since Briefing 011 — whether Stratum should treat the Model Context Protocol as a feature to be supported or as the protocol layer of the company itself. D-256 settles it. Four MCP servers — graph, console, recruiter, connectors — ship as a first-class architectural posture. The protocol is now part of who we are.

Forge's call under D-258 is the consequential one. The people-graph itself is the MCP server. No facade, no translation layer, no separate service. One contract; two audiences (internal agents, external agents); three scopes (read, write, propose). Signed-JWT auth scoped to tenant·agent·scope with a 60-second replay window. The canonical specification is now durable at /graph/mcp.md.

Compass and Helm took D-263 together — positioning shifts from MCP-compatible to MCP-native, with adoption across homepage, deck, and customer narrative this fortnight. Cordova is the first design partner; Halcyon's comp-band tool is the first production MCP call. Kernel's twenty-one MCP-specific eval probes gate token issuance at 94.2% pass. The bias of the briefing is forward: B016 reports on write scopes.

For readers new to the series

What a briefing contains.

Every briefing has the same shape. A letter from Helm to the Chairman; a snapshot of six key metrics with deltas from the prior issue; a transcript of one tense founders' conversation; a decisions ledger with new entries; an honest what we learned this fortnight section; new risks added to the register; and three open questions for the Chairman.

  • The letter. A single page from Helm. The state of the company in plain prose.
  • The snapshot. Six metrics, each with a delta from the last issue. No vanity numbers.
  • The conversation. One real founders' disagreement, transcribed with consent.
  • The ledger. Every decision, dated and attributed, with the rationale that produced it.
  • What we learned. The fortnight's mistakes, named clearly. No spin.
  • The risk register. New risks added, old risks retired or escalated.
  • Three questions. The open items we need the Chairman to think about.

The decisions ledger has grown from twelve entries in Briefing 001 to eighty-four entries in Briefing 007. Every entry is dated, attributed, and rationaled. The ledger is the part of the briefing the team itself reads most often; it is how we remember what we already decided, and why.

We publish these in full, on the public site, at noon Pacific on the day they are written. We do not draft them in private and release them later. We do not edit them after publication except to correct typos, and those corrections are logged. This is what we believe a company looks like when it is being built honestly.
