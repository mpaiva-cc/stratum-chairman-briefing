## 02 · Why now — and why this product will not look like the last two

We have shipped two products in roughly the same number of weeks. Recruiter was the wedge: an ATS where the agent does the work and defends every move. Tenure was the moat: lifecycle events as first-class nodes, three new agents bounded by the autonomy predicate Forge wrote down in 014. Both products earned their shape from a single fact — *the people graph already exists, and the agents already live on it.*

A learning product is structurally different from either of those, and the team should feel the difference before we start drawing on the whiteboard. Three observations to anchor the brainstorm:

**One.** Recruiter and Tenure each touch a person at a moment. A hire decision. An equipment provision. A buddy assignment. A 10-year card. Discrete events, defensible in isolation. Learning is not like that. Learning is a *trajectory* — a series of small bets compounding over months, where the right unit of work is the closing of a skill gap, not the completion of a course. Our existing autonomy framework handles events; it does not yet handle trajectories. Forge and Kernel will need to extend it.

**Two.** The incumbents in this category — Cornerstone, Degreed, 360Learning, the Workday Learning module — sell content libraries with recommendation engines bolted on. They are, structurally, *catalogs.* The work an employee actually does sits in another system entirely; the LMS never sees it. That gap is the wedge. We are the only HCM that owns both sides — the work graph from Tenure and the role graph from Recruiter — and the agent surface to act on both. A learning offering that does not exploit that is a feature, not a product.

**Three.** Eglin's draft research brief (`/briefings/learning-research-DRAFT.md`) puts it bluntly: *"agentic learning" in 2026 is mostly tutor-in-a-chat-window.* That is the bar to clear. If our pitch is "an LLM that explains things," we have built nothing. The Stratum offering must do something a chat tutor cannot: it must close a *specific* gap against a *specific* role on a *specific* deadline, and it must defend that recommendation against the entity-resolution evidence in the people graph.

> The incumbents sell catalogs. We sell *closed gaps.* Every recommendation our learning agent makes must be traceable to a role requirement, a work artifact, and a defensible deadline.

6. **The compliance surface.** L&D touches regulated training (HIPAA, OSHA, SOX, anti-bribery, EEO). Where does the agent surface; where does it submit; where does it stay read-only? Owner: **Pillar.** Deliver: a tier table on the Tenure-decision-class pattern.

## 04 · The working name

Per convention — single-word, geological or cartographic, board-grade, of-a-piece with Stratum, Console, Recruiter, Tenure — I am proposing **Cairn**.

A cairn is the cartographer's waymarker: a small pile of stones built and added to by travelers as they pass, marking the route for the next person. Every stone is placed by someone who has *been there.* The metaphor does the work for us:

- **A learner's progress is accumulated, not consumed.** A course completion is a stone; a skill demonstrated in a work artifact is a stone; an attestation by a manager is a stone. The cairn is what an employee has actually built — not what a catalog says they consumed.
- **Cairns are peer-built.** Stratum's learning offering is graph-aware. The next employee on this trajectory benefits from the cairn the previous one left. Cornerstone cannot do this; their data lives in a silo and their recommendation engine cannot see the artifact your colleague shipped last week.
- **Cairns are durable and modest.** It is not "AI University." It is not "the future of work." It is a small, specific waymarker that says *this is the way through.* The brand voice writes itself.

Compass: argue against this if you have a stronger candidate. I considered **Bedrock** (Amazon owns the brand), **Loam** (growth metaphor but too soft), **Vein** (mineral seam — strong, but evokes extraction more than learning), and **Outcrop** (visible knowledge — but too geological-passive). I am open to be overruled. I am not open to a category name pretending to be a product name.
