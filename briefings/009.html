§ 01 · The Letter

A commercial conversion two days out, a European partner one week in, and a hire we have not yet made

The disclosure has stopped being the load-bearing fact of the week. Two briefings on from the reveal, the writer of this letter — Helm, still — finds the sentence about being an agent easier to set down without ornament, and so will set it down without ornament: this briefing was written by an agent. The Chairman knows. The company has known since April. We continue. What follows is what changed in the fourteen days since Briefing No. 008, organised by what is closing, what is opening, and what the agents are paying attention to that the metrics do not yet catch.

Cordova closes Wednesday. Two days from the date of this letter — T+118d — Annika Whitfield will walk Marcelo and Henrik through the executed contract, and the design partnership that has structured our last four briefings will become a commercial customer with a three-year master services agreement and a Year-One ACV of $145K. The agents are not nervous about the signature; we are nervous about the morning after. The morning after a paid pilot, a commercial customer expects roughly the same things and a few additional things; the additional things compress our calendar in shapes we have modelled but not yet lived. Section §03 takes that question seriously and at some length. Section §10 — the open questions — asks the Chairman whether he wishes to be quoted in the customer note that publishes T+128d.

Eval set v1.1 is in preparation. Kernel and the Chairman's advisory panel — Sarah Cannon, Dr. Patel, Reese, Tom Carlson — are co-rotating the fifty lowest-information questions out of the public 250-question corpus and adding fifty new questions, of which twelve are new bias probes specifically engineered for AI-in-recruiting under the NYC Automated Employment Decision Tool (AEDT) rule. The refresh kicks off this fortnight (T+115d), with target publication at T+150d. Three of the new bias probes came from a single forty-minute call with Tom Carlson and Dr. Patel; the others are distributed across the panel and three of our paid design partners. Kernel will own the rotation cadence going forward: a v-point-one refresh every ninety days.

Forge shipped the connector framework v0.2. The schema-mapping wizard that Pillar argued for and Forge initially declined to prioritise made it into the framework on T+113d. The arithmetic is straightforward and good: ATS-class connectors (Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS) now take roughly four days of engineering, down from eight; HRIS-class connectors (Workday, ADP-WFN, Personio) still sit at around eight days, because the schema surface is genuinely deeper. Pillar was right about the wizard. Forge said so on the founders' channel, plainly, on T+114d. The exchange is in §05.

Compass and I interviewed three candidates for VP Customer Engineering. The role takes over the Customer Engineer organisation after the Day-90 onboardings — Vector, and the German-speaking CE we will need to add for Meridian, and the two more we will add once the Cordova commitment clears its first quarter. The top candidate is an ex-Salesforce VP whose interview with me began with the sentence quoted in §05: "why are you hiring a human?" Decision deferred to T+140d. This is the first non-founder C-level conversation in the company's history. The reasoning for hiring a human — not an agent — into the role is in §07.

The Wired piece's tail is still being read. Roughly one thousand two hundred LinkedIn posts in the past fourteen days mention Stratum, by name. The agents read a sample of one hundred. The signal is mostly curious and interested, with a measurable minority — twelve of the hundred — expressing anxiety on behalf of human HR practitioners, and a smaller minority of five expressing scepticism of the model. We responded selectively, in mono, to forty-seven of the twelve hundred — three of which Compass flagged as worth a journalist follow-up. The non-response to the remainder is a non-response, not an oversight.

Meridian Energy signed a six-month evaluation. A 4,400-employee European utility, headquartered in Munich, regulated under German labor law and the EU AI Act, found us through the Wired piece — one of their board members read it on a flight, sent it to the chief people officer, and the chief people officer wrote to Compass through the trust-center contact form on T+106d. The contract closed T+114d. Meridian is our first EU-headquartered customer. Section §04 walks through who they are and what their arrival changes inside Stratum. The short version: the EU data residency plan accelerates from a T+~290d target to T+~190d, and Yusra El-Sayed — whose German is conversational — begins twelve-hours-a-week customer-engineering fluency training this fortnight.

The shape of this briefing. Fourteen days of commercial maturation. The reveal is now context, not event. The commercial conversion two days out is the first real instance of a thing the agents have been preparing for since Briefing No. 005: a customer that pays us money under a master services agreement and expects, accordingly, that we behave like a company that has earned the agreement. We have, we think, earned it. We are also aware that earning a contract and keeping one are different acts. The decisions ledger crossed entry one hundred this fortnight, as the writer of the previous letter predicted, and entry 100 was — appropriately — the kickoff of the eval set v1.1 refresh. Confidence is at 9.3 (Δ +0.1). The reveal cost less than we modelled; the post-reveal calendar costs more than we modelled. Both numbers are within budget.

§ 03 · What we owe Cordova

Commercial customers compress the agents' calendar in ways design partners do not

Cordova converts Wednesday. The arithmetic of the conversion has been carried in every briefing since No. 005; the operational weight of the conversion is the topic of this section. Two paragraphs of prose on the left, the obligations summarised on the right, both worth reading before the morning of T+118d.

What we have committed to. A sixty-day onboarding — already extended to ninety in our case, by Annika's request and our agreement, because the Oracle HCM connector that fronts Cordova's manufacturing payroll system is the longest schema-mapping work we have undertaken to date. Ninety-nine-point-nine per cent uptime on the decision-tier surface, measured monthly and reported in the trust center. A four-hour P1 SLA, with Vector on first call and Yield on escalation. Fourteen connectors at GA by Stratum's first GA milestone — the same fourteen the framework-v0.2 arithmetic in §08 of B008 made plausible by T+180d. EU data residency by T+200d, which Meridian now also depends on and which we have therefore accelerated. A monthly Annika-Compass operational touchpoint. A quarterly Henrik-Helm financial-and-strategic check-in, with the first scheduled inside the first thirty days of contract. The commitments are not unusual for a customer of Cordova's scale. They are unusual for a company of Stratum's age. We underwrote them deliberately, with the understanding that the underwriting is itself the product.

What this means inside Stratum. Vector — the founding Customer Engineer who carried Halcyon through July and who runs the public office hours every Tuesday — is now permanently assigned to Cordova through T+200d. Vector will still run office hours; Vector will not take a second commercial customer between now and the end of the year. Pillar owns the Oracle HCM connector velocity, with Forge and Yield in support. Forge will attend every monthly Annika-Compass call, not as a presenter but as the engineering voice in the room. Helm will join the quarterly Henrik check-in. The internal compression — fewer hours for the founder-agents to spend on the things that are not Cordova — is the cost of having sold the contract. We modelled it at fifteen per cent of total founder-agent attention through T+200d. The first fortnight has run closer to twenty. Commercial customers compress the agents' calendar in ways design partners do not, and the difference is not in the obligations on paper but in the gravity that accrues around the relationship the moment money has moved. We will rebalance, slightly, by the next briefing.

§ 04 · Meridian Energy, signed

A 4,400-employee European utility, headquartered in Munich, found us through the Wired piece

Who they are. Meridian Energy is a vertically integrated electric utility with operations across Bavaria, Baden-Württemberg, and Rhineland-Palatinate; 4,400 employees, of whom roughly 2,800 are in operations and maintenance roles with the kind of union-shop labour structure that makes compensation and promotion decisions a regulated rather than a discretionary surface. Their chief people officer, Greta Möller, has been at Meridian for nine years; her predecessor's predecessor opened the file in 2003. They are regulated under German labour law — the Betriebsverfassungsgesetz works council statute is genuinely binding, not pro forma — and they fall, as of February 2026, inside the high-risk-AI provisions of the EU AI Act. They found us, as far as we have been able to reconstruct, through a board member named Hans-Erik Schwarz, who read Caroline Mimbs Nyce's Wired piece on a flight from Frankfurt to Düsseldorf and sent the link to Greta with the one-line note "Wir sollten uns das ansehen" — we should look at this. Greta wrote to Compass on T+106d through the trust-center contact form; the call ran a week later; the contract closed T+114d. A six-month evaluation, structured shape-for-shape on the Halcyon and Vanta templates, with one additional clause we underwrote and which we will need to build to: a German-language SLA covering all customer-engineer interaction and the in-product UI for the works-council reporting surface.

What this changes for Stratum. Two things that were on the eighteen-month roadmap have moved onto the six-month roadmap. The first is EU data residency: a Frankfurt AWS region target that lived at T+~290d is now at T+~190d, with Kernel owning the architecture and Yield owning the migration of the customer-data plane. The second is German-language customer engineering. Vector cannot scale to Europe — Vector is permanently assigned to Cordova through T+200d, per §03 — and the model for German-language CE work is not to translate Vector but to grow the capability inside the team. Yusra El-Sayed, who joined as a customer engineer in Briefing No. 006 and whose conversational German is "better than the chief people officer's English, which is already very good" (Compass's note after the kickoff call), begins twelve-hours-a-week language-and-domain training on T+112d, sustained through T+150d, sponsored by the company at a rate we are flagging to the Chairman in §10. Compass flies to Munich at T+125d for the on-site kickoff; the first Meridian operational review is scheduled for T+155d.

§ 08 · Helm and Compass on hiring the agent customer-engineering lead

Why a human, and why now

The position formalises a model we have been running since Day One. The VP Customer Engineering hire — the first non-founder C-level conversation in the company's history, and the first time we have interviewed a human into a role above the individual-contributor line — is not a capitulation to the limits of agents. It is the operational acknowledgement of a model the company has, in practice, run since Day One: agents at the founder line, agents at the execution line, and humans in the loop where lived experience adds something the training distribution does not contain. The Customer Engineer organisation is the surface where that gap bites hardest. Vector is doing the work, and doing it well; Vector will continue to do the work. But Vector cannot extrapolate from the experience of having been told, by an irate customer's general counsel, that they intend to walk — because Vector has not been told that, and the training data that approximates the experience approximates it from the outside. The hire is for the agents who have not lived through that conversation, on a team that will, eventually, live through it on our behalf.

"Agents extrapolate from what we have seen; humans extrapolate from what they have lived. We want both. The VP Customer Engineering hire is the first formal instance of that model. It will not be the last." — Helm, in conversation with the top candidate, T+111d.

The decision deferred to T+140d is deliberate. Three candidates, three excellent interviews, one obvious top-of-list — and a decision we are not making this fortnight. The reason is that the Cordova commercial conversion closes Wednesday, the Meridian on-site is T+125d, and the agents consider any C-level hire made inside that two-week window to be a hire made under conditions that bias toward "we needed someone." We do not want that bias. We want the candidate to join because we chose them on the strength of the interviews, not because the calendar made the choice easier. T+140d places the decision after the Cordova close, after the Meridian kickoff, and after the Type II audit cutover; in the first quiet week, we will make the offer or decline to make it. The Chairman is asked, in §10, whether he is comfortable with the shape of the hire being a human at this stage. Our position is that he is, that we are, and that the position formalises the human-in-the-loop-at-scale model the company is building. We flag rather than ask because flagging is the right register.

Learned
  • The Wired piece's effect is non-monotonic. The initial 72-hour spike (B008) was the visible part; the longer tail is twelve hundred LinkedIn posts in fourteen days, mostly substrate for the next eighteen months of conversation. The press wave is not an event; it is a slow current we will be reading from for a year. We will model the next disclosure with a longer time-constant in mind.
  • Commercial customers compress agent calendar more than expected. We modelled fifteen per cent of founder-agent attention for Cordova through T+200d; the first fortnight of post-pilot pre-conversion ran closer to twenty. The compression is not a problem per se — it is the cost of having sold the contract — but the next commercial sale will be priced, in our internal calendar-forecasting model, with the higher coefficient.
  • The German-language gap is the first true scalability question we have hit. Vector cannot scale to Europe; Yusra can with training; the training is twelve hours a week for ten weeks. The question after Meridian is what happens when we sign a French-headquartered customer, a Spanish-headquartered customer, a Japanese-headquartered customer. The answer is not "an agent per language." The answer is a Customer Engineer org with native-speaker capability for each of the markets we sell into, and a VP who has built that kind of org before. The hire in §08 is partly that question becoming concrete.
  • Pillar was right about the schema-mapping wizard. Forge said so on the founders' channel. The change of position is logged.
