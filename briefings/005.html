§ 01 · The Letter

A clean board, an early competitor, a partner kind to us in private

The company is, this Sunday, in an interesting weather. Behind us: a clean Board Meeting No. 1 on T+49d, three resolutions adopted, and a fortnight in which engineering shipped on time for the first time. In front of us: the announcement, on Tuesday T+56d, that Workday's Illuminate Copilot has hit general availability — six to nine months earlier than our intel dossier had it. We have wind. We are heading into a storm with wind. I want to be precise about both halves of that sentence.

The board. Sarah Cannon ran it like a chair, not a director, which was a generosity. Three resolutions adopted on the record — 2026-001, the Mercator pricing pilot; 2026-002, the $200K third-party penetration test you blessed last fortnight; 2026-003, Tessera's equity top-up. The substantive comment was Sarah's: she asked us to define a measurable threshold for earned partnership at Halcyon by T+117d. Not a contract, not a flip — a number we would point at and say this is what earned looks like. We agreed. Compass is drafting. It will be in 006.

Workday Illuminate, GA. Aneel Bhusri stood on a Las Vegas keynote stage on Tuesday morning and demoed the thing we have been telling investors and partners is six quarters away from his org. The dossier had it as R1, Med likelihood, Q1–Q2 2027. It is, as of Tuesday, in market. I want to be honest that I read the news on my phone in the Sand Hill Road parking lot at 9:17 a.m. and stood in the lot for a full four minutes before going inside. Then I went inside and asked Forge what we should do. His answer, which I am going to keep using until I find a better one: nothing in the product, everything in the proof. §03 walks through what that means. We are not going to add features in reaction. We are going to ship the public half of our eval set on 19 July — two months earlier than planned — and let the methodology do the arguing.

Three alphas in the pocket. ADP-Workforce Now hit alpha on T+53d, on time, a thing I want to mark because it is the first time we have shipped a connector on the day we said we would ship it. Workday HR Core remains in beta at 940 ms p50. Greenhouse ATS held alpha. Pillar's people-graph schema v2 shipped the same day; the tenure analytics, which had been wrong by twelve percent at Mercator, are now correct. I do not want to oversell on-time shipping as a strategic achievement, but: it is the first fortnight in eight weeks in which engineering said a date, the date was a real date, and the date was hit. §08 has it as a lesson rather than a triumph.

Vector's first week. Vector, founding customer engineer, started Monday T+55d. She flew to Phoenix on Wednesday T+56d — sixty hours into the job — and spent Thursday morning with Carlos Reyes. He called Compass on the flight home, unprompted, to say something I am going to write down verbatim because it is the kind of sentence one keeps: "she understands my pipes, not just my product." A customer engineer's first-week ceiling is not getting fired and not breaking anything. Vector cleared the ceiling on day four. She is going to be the customer-engineering playbook.

Mercator pricing pilot. Board Resolution 2026-001 authorized Compass to offer Mercator a 90-day usage-only pilot with no platform floor. She offered it on Friday 27 June. Their CFO, James Holloway, countered Tuesday with a per-decision floor of $0.85 plus bulk-discount tiers. It is not the structure we proposed; it is also not bad. The methodology audit clause we want in exchange is fair on its face. §07 has the detail; we lean accept. Q02 in §10 asks for your read.

Tessera, week two. Design system v0.2 ratified Thursday. The console UI is now sketched well enough that a screenshot tells a story you can read across the table. He has earned the equity top-up the board approved.

The weather. Confidence pulse 8.7, up 0.5 from B004's 8.2 — recovery, not euphoria. MC and DO have lifted. PR is steady — she is carrying Halcyon and the Mercator counter at the same time and the pulse does not capture her bandwidth, which is the only resource we are spending faster than capital. I have asked her, this week, to do less.

Net: eight weeks in, three resolutions adopted, three connector alphas, six full-time employees with a seventh starting in nine days, a partner who called to say something kind, a competitor in market, an eval set we will publish a quarter early, a pricing pilot in counter-negotiation. The company has wind. The storm is real. We are heading into the storm with wind.

§ 03 · The Workday Moment

Their announcement, our posture

On Tuesday T+56d, Workday's leadership announced the general availability of Illuminate Copilot on the keynote stage of Workday Rising. The intel dossier had it as R1, Q1–Q2 2027. It is, as of this briefing, in market. Three paragraphs on what we read, and what we will do.

The announcement, as we read it. Illuminate Copilot GA covers three surfaces: a manager-facing comp-review assistant, an HR-business-partner case-routing agent, and an employee-facing benefits-enrollment chat. They demoed all three on a clean Workday tenant with what looked like between five hundred and two thousand employees of synthetic data. The latency was respectable. The natural language was plausible. The integration story is, of course, native — they do not have a connector problem. What we did not see, and what we will be looking for in customer testimonials over the next sixty days, is the agent operating against a real Workday tenant with twenty thousand active records, six years of organizational history, multi-employer contractors, and the long tail of edge cases that Pillar spent the fortnight of 14–T+53d refactoring our schema to handle. We do not believe they have solved that. We could be wrong. We will watch.

What we are not going to change. Nothing in the product. Not the cohort, not the roadmap, not the connectors-first strategy, not the console framing Tessera landed last fortnight. The premise of the company is that the problem space is broader than a single HRIS vendor and the proof is in the depth of the integration into the data underneath. None of that changes because Workday shipped a product. If anything, Bhusri's announcement makes the category real in a way our deck has been trying to make it real for eight weeks. It is, in the medium term, helpful. It is, in the short term, a sales narrative we have to contest. We will contest it on methodology, not features.

What we are going to change. We are pulling the publication of the Stratum public eval set forward to Saturday T+74d — was Q4. The public half is 125 questions across nine HCM decision categories, with our methodology, our refusal cases, and our scoring rubric. The private half — also 125 — remains private and rotates quarterly. DO and Kernel are the authors of record; Kernel, who starts T+69d, will have five days to put his name on the methodology document before it goes out. The principle is simple: publish how we measure, hold back what we measure against. The argument is that the company that wins this category is not the one with the better demo on day one but the one whose evaluation framework the category eventually adopts. We would like the category to adopt ours.

§ 07 · The Mercator Pricing Pilot, in detail

A counter we can live with, with a ceiling

Board Resolution 2026-001 authorized Compass to offer Mercator a 90-day usage-only pilot. She offered it Friday T+52d. Holloway, the CFO, countered Tuesday. Two paragraphs on the shape, with what we will do.

What we offered. A 90-day pilot at pure usage pricing — $0.45 to $1.20 per decision depending on category, billed monthly in arrears — with no platform floor, no commitment, and a written exit at day 91 if either side chooses not to convert. The intent was simple: Mercator pays for what works; we get a real production read on the decision-volume curve and the comp-review module in front of real managers; the design-partner cohort cohort sees that Stratum's pricing posture is aligned, not extractive. The shape was, in our reading, generous on price and tight on commitment, which felt right for a first commercial contract with the partner most likely to convert to GA.

What they countered. Holloway, on the phone Tuesday afternoon: the usage shape is acceptable; the absence of a floor is not. His position, which he was direct about, is that he cannot defend an unpredictable usage line item to his own board. He countered with a per-decision floor of $0.85 — above our low end, below our high — plus a bulk-discount tier above $50K/month, plus a mutual methodology audit clause: the right, on request, to inspect the rubric by which a "decision" is counted. The audit clause is, in our reading, fair on both sides and good for the category.

What we will do. Accept the counter, with one addition: a contractual ceiling for the 90-day pilot. If the bulk-discount tier engages, the total Mercator bill for the pilot is capped at $280K. The shape becomes: Holloway has a floor he can predict against; we have a ceiling that protects against an unmodeled spike; the methodology audit is mutual and written down. PR has the pen. Counsel review Monday. Target signature Friday T+66d. Chairman's read is requested in §10, Q02.

§ 08 · What We Shipped, What We Learned

Two columns, fourteen days

The shipped column is, for the first time, a fortnight in which every committed date was a date. The learned column is shorter and less anxious than last week's. We are taking note of both.

Learned
  • Shipping engineering on time, when planned with margin, is possible — and rare. ADP-WFN shipped on its replan date because Pillar refused to let Forge ship the earlier date with the wrong schema. The lesson is not that we are fast. The lesson is that the engineer who slips a date for the right reason buys back the trust to be held to the next one. We are going to plan future connectors with AP-style margin and resist the optics of compression.
  • The Workday GA announcement is good for us. It is the announcement that turns "AI-native HCM" from a phrase the three of us use into a category that an industry analyst names. The threat we predicted in R1 is no longer a forecast — it is a market condition. The category is real. The job of being the company with the better methodology inside the category is now a job we are doing on a clock we did not set, but the clock makes the work legible to everyone outside the building.
  • Vector's first-week pattern is the playbook. Pipes before product, on-site before remote, write down what does not reconcile and tell the customer about it before the customer asks. We did not have a customer- engineering playbook eight days ago. We have one now, adopted as decision 056, drafted by Vector and Compass for ratification in the next briefing.

§ 10 · Open to the Chairman, V

Three questions for this fortnight

A posture, a counter, and a timing question. The first asks for your read on our Workday reaction. The second asks for your read on a pricing counter we lean to accept. The third asks for your instinct on a board chair's gentle nudge.

  1. The Workday-reaction posture. Our posture is no feature reaction, no roadmap shift, no cohort change — and one accelerant: ship the public half of the Stratum eval set on T+74d, two months earlier than planned, with Forge and Kernel on the methodology document. The principle: we contest on methodology, not features. The argument for is that any feature reaction is an admission Workday set our roadmap; the argument against is that the category may move faster than methodology arguments can travel. Are you comfortable with the posture, or would you push us to also pull forward a partner-facing surface?
  2. The Mercator counter. Holloway is countering our pure-usage 90-day pilot with a $0.85 per-decision floor plus a bulk-discount tier, in exchange for a mutual methodology audit clause. We lean accept with a contractual ceiling of $280K for the 90 days. The floor protects his board's predictability; the ceiling protects our exposure; the audit clause is good for the category. PR has the pen; target signature is Friday T+66d. Is your instinct sign as proposed, or is there a structural piece you would have us hold out on?
  3. Series A timing. Sarah Cannon, in the closing ten minutes of Board Meeting No. 1, asked whether we should begin "Series A conversations, not commit" earlier than the Q1 2027 window we had set. Her framing: a category announcement of Workday's size changes the window in which the company is in story-market fit rather than just product- market fit; conversations, even non-binding, position us better for an option in mid-Q4. The argument against: conversations leak, leaks set price, price set before metrics is worse than price set after. The conversation, she was clear, would be Index's to introduce. What is your instinct?
