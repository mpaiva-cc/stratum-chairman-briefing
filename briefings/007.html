§ 01 · The Letter

A partner converted, a compliance company at the door, a methodology that has begun to travel

Halcyon converted. The 60-day evaluation window we extended in Briefing 005 — the window I described, at the time, as a downgrade we accepted because the alternative was pretending — closed on Tuesday 11 August. Vector flew to Phoenix on Wednesday 29 July for the third visit; she and Carlos Reyes spent four hours at a whiteboard and then, after a break, another ninety minutes on the contract. The partnership-conversion paperwork — drafted quietly through July, per Briefing 006 §08 — was countersigned at 16:20 Pacific on Sunday 09 August. The shape is a paid pilot, six months, fifty thousand dollars prepaid, with decision-tier billing beginning in month four and a full commercial conversation set for 1 February 2027. Vector called Helm from the airport with the news; we have, twelve weeks in, the first commercial dollar through the door.

Vanta reached out. On Wednesday afternoon, 30 July, Naomi Kessler — chief human-resources officer at Vanta — wrote to Compass through the Stratum trust-center contact form. Her note ran four paragraphs. She had read the trust-center page on a Tuesday evening, read Forge's "Born compliant" engineering essay on the Wednesday morning, and arrived at her Wednesday-afternoon one-on-one with her CFO with a question her CFO had asked her three Mondays in a row: can we answer pay equity at M3 and above before the audit committee asks? She wanted to know whether Stratum could help. The call happened Wednesday at 17:15 Pacific. It was forty minutes; it was good. Forge asked, in the founders' Slack at 18:02, the question that mattered: "did Naomi look at the trust center or the engineering post first?" Compass checked; the answer was both, twice. If Stratum sells to Vanta — the company that audits Stratum's own SOC2 — we will have closed a loop the rest of the category cannot easily close.

He interviewed Helm on the Wednesday previous; the transcript is in §05. § 03 · Halcyon, resolved

The downgrade was the partnership

Sixty days ago we accepted a downgrade from full design partner to an evaluation window because the alternative was pretending. Tuesday 11 August the window closes; Sunday 09 August it closed early, with a yes. The conversion shape is below; the short narrative of the final two weeks runs alongside it.

The turn. The sentence Carlos said to Vector on the afternoon of Tuesday 15 July — "thank you for not pretending" — was, looking back, the turn. The two weeks between that visit and the third one read like a customer who had decided privately and was, in his own pace, building the paperwork his board would need to read. Renata Okafor, the Halcyon data architect who had been the technical gate since Briefing 004, signed off on the pre-merger ADP archive reconciliation against schema v2 on Friday 24 July. Carlos invited Vector back for Wednesday 29 July without an agenda — the second meeting in a row he had called without one — and Vector, on Compass's nudge, accepted with a draft of the partner- conversion paperwork in her bag. The four-hour whiteboard on the 29th was, in Vector's note, "about the next twelve months, not the next twelve weeks."

The signature. Carlos asked, in the second hour of the afternoon session, what we would do if their pre- merger ADP archive turned out to have a record-shape we had not modeled. Vector answered without conferring: "we tell you, we re-scope the timeline, and we eat the slip." He said "good", asked her for two changes to the exit clause, and signed the partnership letter at 16:20 Pacific on Sunday 09 August. The paid-pilot shape — six months, $50K prepaid, decision-tier billing beginning month four — was a deliberate compromise between Helm's instinct (no number until the integration is live) and Carlos's instinct (a number that commits both sides without committing either to the full commercial conversation). Helm and Vector celebrated on the Sunday evening in Palo Alto with a bottle of something better than the company's normal grocery. The 11 August calendar date — the original evaluation deadline — is now the production ingestion start date. The sixty-day window did not extend; it resolved early, on the customer's pace, with a number on the page.

The lesson. The 60-day evaluation, viewed in retrospect, was not the cost of the downgrade. It was the partnership. A customer who has decided that he is in is in more deeply than a customer who has never had to consider that he might be out. Halcyon will, in the next six months, ship Stratum decisions into a population of fourteen thousand employees across a recently merged company that is the hardest operating environment in the design-partner cohort. If we earn the commercial conversation on 1 February, we will have earned it on a tighter substrate than any of the other four. The downgrade, viewed correctly, was the credential.

§ 04 · The Vanta call

A compliance company at the door

Three paragraphs. Who Vanta is; how the call happened; why a yes from this customer is structurally different from a yes from any other. The second call is Wednesday 05 August; design-partner conversion target is 15 August.

Who Vanta is. Vanta is the compliance-automation company that wrote the modern playbook for SOC2 readiness. They are also — and this is the part that matters — the company Stratum has contracted to audit its own SOC2 Type II program. Their CHRO, Naomi Kessler, has been at Vanta for two and a half years; before Vanta she ran people at a series-B fintech where she had been the customer of three different HCM systems and had not been satisfied with any of them. She is, by training, a labor-and-employment attorney; by temperament, a person who reads the documentation before she takes the meeting. The Vanta headcount is approximately 1,100 employees globally, two-thirds in the United States, the remainder split across Sydney, Dublin, and a small Singapore presence. Their compensation philosophy is published; their pay bands are published; their levelling framework is published. They are, on the dimension of compliance-readiness, the most mature customer in our pipeline.

The call. Naomi wrote to Compass on Wednesday afternoon, 30 July, through the trust-center contact form. Her note ran four paragraphs and read, in Compass's word, like a memo. She had read the trust-center page on Tuesday evening; she had read Forge's "Born compliant" engineering essay on Wednesday morning; she had walked into her Wednesday-afternoon one-on-one with her CFO with a question her CFO had asked her three Mondays in a row: can we answer pay equity at M3 and above before the audit committee asks? The call happened at 17:15 Pacific that same day. Compass moderated; Helm joined for the second half. It ran forty minutes. Naomi's first question was not about the agent's accuracy; it was about the failure-mode shape — a question she had taken from a sentence in the methodology document. Her second question was about how the model handles probationary employees in jurisdictions where probation is a protected class. Her third question was whether Stratum, in handling the Vanta compensation data, would expect to share that data with any third party for model training. The answer to the third was no, under any circumstance; she said, on the call, "that is the right answer." The second call is scheduled for Wednesday 05 August with her CFO on the line. We have placed a design-partner conversion target on 15 August.

The strategic significance. Stratum has spent twelve weeks arguing that an HCM agent can be "born compliant" — that an architecture built from the first commit around audit, evidence, and refusal is a different object than a compliance program retrofitted onto a pre-existing system. The argument has, so far, been made to customers who are at varying stages of compliance maturity, and who have taken our claims on faith because the rest of the category does not have a better claim to make. Vanta is the first customer who can examine our claim against their own tradecraft. If we sell to Vanta, we have, by selling, settled the argument: the company that audits SOC2 has decided that our compliance posture is at least as mature as their own. The first sale to a more-mature-on-compliance customer is the sale that closes the loop. We are not assuming we will earn it. We are taking the meeting we earned by writing the documents we wrote.

Palo Alto · DO + AP at the whiteboard
APThe patch is small. The read path is doing graph traversal at every request — twelve to twenty hops depending on the org shape, with the deepest hops on questions about reporting chains and pay-band membership. The traversal is the slow thing. The traversal is also doing work the write path has the information to do exactly once.
APSo I cache the projection at write-time. Every employee record carries, materialized, the four projections the read path needs — reporting line, comp band membership, peer-group window, leveling path. When an employee changes, we recompute the four projections; we do that at ingest, asynchronously, and it costs us nothing because the ingest path is not latency-sensitive. The read path then never does graph work; it does a four-way join on indexed columns.
FGp99?
AP1.8 seconds before. 940 milliseconds after. p50 went from 380ms to 110ms. The wins are larger in the tail because the tail is the queries that were doing the deepest traversals.
FGPillar, this is the single change that buys us four more design partners worth of capacity. Are there cases where the projection is stale?
APYes — a window of up to ninety seconds after an org change. I documented it. The agent has a freshness check for any decision that reads a projection updated in the last two minutes; if the projection is stale, we do the traversal on that one request and refresh. So the worst case for a freshly mutated record is a 1.8-second response and a freshness flag.
FGShip it. Decision 073. And write up the freshness invariant in the docs — the auditor will ask about it.

§ 08 · First dollars through the door

Fifty thousand. The number that does not get to be small.

Halcyon's $50K prepaid is the first commercial revenue Stratum has ever booked. It is, by ARR convention, a rounding error. It is, by the company's calendar, the line that marks a fortnight from every fortnight that preceded it. The internal posture is sober. The external posture, on the Chairman's blessing, is sober too.

The number does not get to be small. A founder learns, by week twelve, that the difference between zero and any positive number is categorically larger than the difference between any two positive numbers. The arithmetic does not care. The psychology of the team does. The board's read of the company does. The conversation with the next CHRO does. We have, this fortnight, moved out of the column where the question is does anyone pay you? and into the column where the question is how many, how fast? The two columns are arithmetically adjacent and categorically different.

The commitment. The internal target, agreed by the three founders on Monday 02 August at the briefing draft, is that this fortnight is the last fortnight where annualized revenue is below one hundred thousand dollars. Cordova converts 1 September at $145K ACV. We will, by 30 September, have booked three more pilots at $50K to $80K each. Two of those three are already in the pipeline (Vanta and one of The Information inbounds we are not yet identifying by name). The third is the pilot we will earn between now and then by writing more of the kind of documents Naomi Kessler read before she wrote to us.

Commitment · 30 Sep 2026
Three more paid pilots booked at $50K–$80K each. Plus Cordova commercial conversion 1 Sep at $145K ACV. Target end-of-Q3 annualized commercial revenue, lower bound: $345K. Upper bound: $435K. The board read of B009 (13 Sep) and B010 (27 Sep) will assess against this commitment.

§ 10 · What We Shipped, What We Learned

Two columns, fourteen days

The shipped column carries six entries this fortnight, the longest it has been. The learned column carries three; the fortnight's lessons are about restraint, leverage, and the posture a young company holds when it is, briefly, the subject of someone else's writing.

Shipped
  • Schema v3 · materialized projections. 28 July. p99 latency 1.8s → 940ms; p50 380ms → 110ms. Pillar's single biggest engineering contribution since onboarding. Documented freshness window 90s, flagged at read. The change buys us, in Forge's accounting, four more design partners worth of capacity at current p99 budget.
  • ADP-WFN connector · beta. 31 July, on the date Forge committed to. Fifth connector to beta; third in the last four weeks. Seven connectors remain on the dev backlog. UKG, Paychex, BambooHR next.
  • Halcyon · conversion to paid pilot. Carlos signed 09 Aug. Six months, $50K prepaid, decision-tier billing month four. Production ingestion 11 Aug. Commercial conversion conversation 1 Feb 2027. First commercial revenue Stratum has booked.
  • Vanta · first call. 30 July. Forty minutes. Naomi Kessler asked the failure-mode question second, the protected-class question third, the data-sharing question fourth. Second call 05 Aug w/ CFO. Design-partner conversion target 15 Aug.
  • The Information · published profile. 30 July. Cory Weinberg, full piece on the eval-set publication, with the methodology framing as the headline and MC's restraint on competitor accuracy numbers as the close. Three inbound CHRO conversations in 48h following.
  • Eval-set GitHub repo · in the world. 47 stars in week one; 812 methodology-document downloads. Two replication runs by external researchers begun; one academic email asking for the private-half methodology (politely declined, the principle on rotation is the principle).
Learned
  • The downgrade was the partnership. Halcyon's conversion is, ten weeks after we accepted the downgrade in Briefing 005, stronger than it would have been if we had carried the partner label through the evaluation unbroken. A customer who has decided he is in after having had the option to be out is a different category of customer. The internal framing — "the downgrade was the credential" — is now the framing we will offer to every future design partner whose evaluation gets honest.
  • The eval set as marketing asset works. Two things this fortnight that would not have happened without the publication: Naomi Kessler wrote to Compass through the trust-center form because she had read the methodology; and three CHROs we had not been in conversation with arrived inbound inside forty-eight hours of The Information piece. Both inbound and outbound conversations are different shapes after the publication than before it. The methodology document is the highest-leverage piece of writing the company has produced.
  • When a journalist asks the comparison question, the answer is "no, but here is the methodology." Cory Weinberg asked the comparison question twice, in two different shapes; MC declined both times to be quoted on competitor accuracy numbers and held to the methodology framing. The piece ran with the methodology framing as the headline. The posture is the credibility. Restraint is not the cost of the credibility; it is the credibility.

§ 12 · Open to the Chairman, VII

Three questions for this fortnight

A revenue number, an investor's unusual posture, an unsolicited customer whose conversion would settle an argument. None of the three is urgent. All three are the kind of question one would rather ask before the answer matters.

  1. The first-dollars announcement. Halcyon's $50K paid pilot is the first commercial dollar Stratum has booked. Internally, the founders' read is that the number marks a category boundary that should be acknowledged but not celebrated past its weight; externally, Compass is preparing a short note for the next What's New digest (08 Aug) that says "first commercial pilot live" without naming the customer or the dollar amount. The alternative posture is to hold the public acknowledgment until the second pilot signs, on the theory that one number sounds smaller than two and the optics of two pilots, $X combined reads more substantively than first commercial pilot live. Are you comfortable with Stratum saying "first dollars" publicly this fortnight, or would you have us hold for the second?
  2. Sequoia's posture. Roelof Botha hosted Helm for an hour on Monday 28 July without a deck and without price discovery. Seed-stage cadence to a partner-of-Roelof's seniority is unusual; the meeting happened because the eval- set publication landed and because the Cordova conversion is on the calendar. Our reading, and Sarah Cannon's, is that we do not engage substantively until M9 (the Series A timing conversation 21 Aug). The intent of this note is not to ask for guidance; the intent is to flag the signal before the next briefing inherits it. Are you aware of any history with Roelof or with Sequoia we should be holding in the room when the M9 conversation happens?
  3. Vanta, if it converts. The strategic shape of a Vanta conversion is in §04. If the second call (05 Aug) and the design-partner conversion (15 Aug) both land, the announcement question becomes immediate: is a Vanta design-partner conversion the kind of announcement you want to be in the loop on — by which we mean, briefed in advance and asked for your read on the language — or would you prefer to receive it in the briefing in the ordinary way? The default is the briefing in the ordinary way; we are asking because Vanta's reputation in the compliance community makes the announcement an asset whose framing benefits from a senior read.
