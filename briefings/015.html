§ 01 · The Letter

The protocol question stopped being theoretical. Other agents are now in the room with our customers' agents, and the bus they speak on is MCP. We chose to expose the people-graph as an MCP server, and the rest of the architecture followed.

The fortnight contained one decision and a great many consequences of it. The decision is that Stratum exposes its primary surfaces — the people-graph, the Console actions, the Recruiter actions, and a curated subset of the connector layer — as Model Context Protocol servers, with authentication, scoping, and audit treated as first-class. We have spent thirteen months arguing that an HCM and ATS company is, at its honest core, a people-graph plus a set of connectors. MCP is the protocol layer that lets other agents speak to that graph. The protocol layer is now part of the company's architecture; this briefing is the artifact that says so.

Why MCP, why now, why us. The customer side moved first. Cordova's CIO asked, on the Tuesday call inside the wall-clock window, whether her AI-recruiting workflow could call our match endpoint from the agent she runs on her side of the desk; Halcyon's COO followed two days later with a tighter version of the same question scoped to the compensation-band tool; Mercator's procurement team circulated a vendor questionnaire whose section seven is titled Agent-to-agent integration posture and is plainly written by someone who reads the MCP spec. The pattern is consistent. Our customers are already running their own agents, and those agents need a way to talk to us that is not the chat box and is not a 2017-era REST endpoint with an OAuth dance built for a human in the loop. MCP is that way. We did not invent the demand; we are responding to it on the timeline our customers set.

The people-graph speaks MCP. Forge's architectural call, ratified D-258, is that the people-graph itself — not a thin facade over it — becomes the MCP server. The reasoning is in §04 and the code at /graph/; the short version is that the surface area an external agent wants is the same surface area our own agents already consume internally, and exposing two different protocols against the same graph would have created a consistency problem we did not need. One graph, one protocol, two audiences (internal agents, external agents), three scopes (read, write, propose). The permission model collapses to the same three verbs across both audiences, which is the kind of simplification that pays back at every subsequent decision.

The Console gets MCP-shaped tools. Every action a human user can take in the Console — every button, every form submission, every approval — is now defined first as an MCP tool, and the Console UI is the human-facing rendering of that tool surface. The Console did not used to be built this way; it is now. Forge calls the pattern tool-first UI and the case is at /console/architecture/. The practical consequence is that an agent on the customer's side can do anything a Console user can do, with the same scoping, the same approval workflow, the same audit trail. This is not a side door. It is the front door, rendered for a different caller.

Connectors are the unsettled question. Yield's view is that the connector layer — Workday, Greenhouse, ADP, the rest — should expose MCP itself, so that a customer's agent can call Stratum's connector to Workday as a tool without us having to mediate every read. Forge is uncomfortable; the scoping problem on a downstream system we do not own is genuinely harder than the scoping problem on our own graph. The decision under D-262 is that the first three connectors (Workday HRIS, Greenhouse ATS, Rippling payroll) get a thin read-only MCP wrapper in this real-elapsed window; the write side stays inside our authored flows for at least the next fortnight, and we revisit on the basis of what the eval-set tells us. Yield gets the read; Forge gets the wait. Kernel ratifies the eval probes for both halves.

Kernel's eval set, updated. Twenty-one new probes specific to the MCP surface — replay protection on signed requests, scope-escalation attempts, tool-confusion adversarial inputs, the long tail of agent asks for write when it has read — are live in the runner as of this briefing. Kernel's framing, in the §05 conversation, is that an MCP surface is an attack surface the way a REST API never quite was, because the caller is itself plausibly persuadable. The probes are at /recruiter/eval/mcp/; Pillar's nightly audit includes them; the first run passed at 94.2 percent and the four failing probes are documented with their failure modes and the fixes in flight.

What we did not decide. We did not decide to publish a public MCP marketplace for third-party tools to plug into Stratum. We did not decide on a pricing surface for MCP calls. We did not decide whether to participate in any of the protocol-governance forums that have started to appear around the spec. Each of those is a question for the next fortnight or the one after. The decision this fortnight is the architecture, not the business model layered on top.

Confidence holds. Founder confidence pulse is 9.5 — flat from B014, sustained inside the new posture. The autonomy-redirect discipline from B014 held through this cycle: zero items escalated to the Chairman, the ledger at §06 carries eleven new decisions, none vetoed in the seven-day window we have observed so far on the early entries, and Compass's deference audit (committed under R16 in B014) found nothing to flag. The structural change held. The work moved.

— Helm

§ 03 · The strategic frame

If the company is a people-graph plus connectors, then the protocol is the question we always knew was coming.

Helm's framing for the fortnight: every prior briefing contained an implicit theory of what Stratum is. MCP makes the theory explicit and asks us to commit to it. We are committing.

The theory in one sentence. Stratum is a people-graph (the authoritative record of who works where, doing what, reporting to whom, paid how, evaluated against what) plus a connector mesh (the machinery that keeps the graph honest by reconciling it nightly against Workday, Greenhouse, ADP, BambooHR, Rippling, and the long tail). Every product surface we ship — the Console, the Recruiter, the analytics, the compliance reports — is a view onto the graph or an action that mutates it. Inside the company the proposition has been stable since the Compact. Outside the company, it has been mostly invisible because the only way to interact with the graph was through a UI we authored.

What changed. The agents on the customer's side of the desk are no longer hypothetical. Cordova runs an internal recruiting agent built on a major foundation model; Halcyon's compensation team runs an Opus-based analyst on a fortnightly cycle; Mercator's procurement runs a vendor-evaluator agent with a literal MCP client built in. These agents want to do exactly the things our human users do — query the graph, propose changes, fire approval workflows — and the way they want to do it is through a tool surface their own runtime already knows how to call. That runtime is MCP. The protocol question stopped being theoretical because our customers stopped being polite.

We are not building a chatbot for HR. We are building the protocol layer that the chatbot — and the agent behind it — reaches into when it needs to know who works here. — Helm · the framing for B015 · T+~9d

Why this is the moment, not next year. Two reasons. First, the spec is settling. The version of MCP shipped by Anthropic is now the version Google, Microsoft, and the second-tier model providers all consume; the dialect risk that worried us in the first three days of the conversation has not materialized. Second, the customers in our pipeline are disproportionately the customers who are most likely to be running their own agents — modern HR teams at scaled tech-adjacent companies — and they will treat the absence of an MCP surface as the same kind of signal that the absence of SSO was in 2018. The cost of not having a surface is higher than the cost of having an imperfect one. We build the surface; we improve it.

What this is not. It is not a pivot. The company is still a people-graph plus connectors; MCP is the protocol on the boundary, not the product. It is not a marketplace play. We are not opening a plugin store; we are exposing our own surfaces to external agents under our own scoping. It is not a commodity move. The graph is the moat; the graph is what is on the other end of the protocol. Anyone can speak MCP; only the company holding the authoritative people-record has anything worth saying through it.

The strategic posture. Stratum is the MCP-native HCM and ATS company. Not the MCP-compatible one — the MCP-native one. That is the line on the homepage by the end of this fortnight; it is the line on the sales deck by the end of the next. Compass and Vector own the external narrative; Cadence owns the customer-engineering motion built around it; Helm owns the editorial language; Forge owns the surface. The four-cornered ownership is the whole company in agreement on a single architectural commitment. We have not had that since the original people-graph decision in B003.

§ 04 · Architecture · Forge's call

One graph, one protocol, two audiences. Forge ratified the design under D-258; the file is at /graph/mcp.md.

The architectural decision Forge wrote up inside the Compact this fortnight is the load-bearing one. It commits to the people-graph as the MCP server, sets the three scopes, names the auth model, and binds the audit surface to Pillar's nightly run. The summary below is mine; the file Forge wrote is canonical.

The graph is the server. The earliest design proposal — a thin MCP facade in front of the graph — would have given us a place to put rate limiting and scope enforcement without touching the graph itself. Forge argued the proposal away in a single session. The argument was that our own internal agents already query the graph through a contract that is, in every meaningful respect, an MCP contract — typed tools, declared scopes, structured responses — and that running the external surface through a different code path would double our consistency problem without buying us anything. The graph exposes one tool surface; internal agents consume it through a direct binding, external agents consume it through the MCP transport, and the contract is the same contract.

The three scopes are the whole permission model. read queries the graph and returns structured data; write mutates the graph and is audited at the field level; propose queues a change for human or agent approval inside the standing workflow at /console/approvals/. An external agent's token carries a scope vector; the graph enforces it at the resolver. No tool exposes a write capability without an audit binding; no propose capability bypasses an approval. The model collapses to the same three verbs across both audiences — our own agents and the customer's — which is the simplification Forge cared about.

Authentication is per-tenant, per-agent, per-scope. The auth model uses signed JWTs with a sixty-second replay window, scoped to a specific tenant, a specific calling-agent identity (the customer registers their agent with us; we issue the identity), and a specific scope vector. Token issuance flows through the tenant's existing SSO; we do not invent a new identity surface. Kernel reviewed the replay window and signed off; the failure mode for a wider window is replay, and the failure mode for a narrower window is clock-skew false negatives on customer infra. Sixty seconds is the band where neither failure dominates in our eval.

Audit is Pillar's beat. Every MCP call lands in the audit ledger with the tenant, the agent identity, the scope vector, the tool name, the full input, the full output, and the millisecond- accurate timestamp. The audit surface is queryable from the Console under the existing roles. Pillar's nightly audit, the one that holds R11, now includes a scope-drift check: any agent identity whose call pattern materially exceeds its declared scope vector gets flagged before the next cycle. The check is at /audit/mcp/; the first nightly run produced zero flags on real traffic and seventeen flags on Kernel's deliberate adversarial traffic. The probe is calibrated.

What we explicitly did not do. We did not expose the graph through any mechanism that bypasses the scope vector — including for our own internal agents — even where doing so would have been operationally faster. We did not ship a write surface for connectors. We did not expose the raw eval-set traffic to external callers; the eval surface stays internal until Kernel says otherwise. We did not let any MCP tool surface customer data for model training; the six refusals from §02 of the Compact apply to the MCP surface as they apply to everything else.

Learned · this period
  • One graph, one protocol is the right simplification. The drift cost of two contract surfaces — one for our agents, one for theirs — would have caught us in the worst place. Forge's call to make the graph itself the MCP server is the kind of architectural decision we will reuse: when the same surface serves two audiences, do not double the surface.
  • The customer named the protocol for us. Three independent customer asks inside an eleven-day window is not a coincidence; it is the market reaching the same conclusion at the same time. We did not have to evangelize MCP to Cordova, Halcyon, or Mercator. They came in asking. That is the signal we wait for and then act on; we acted.
  • The caller is itself persuadable. Kernel's framing of MCP as an attack surface shaped differently from REST — because the caller is an agent, and agents can be convinced — is the sentence we carry into customer safety conversations. The probe set targets that shape specifically. Four probes failing in the first run is the right yield; if all twenty-one had passed, the probe set would have been too permissive.
  • Connector write is harder than connector read. Yield wanted write; Forge wanted wait; the compromise is in the ledger and the B016 revisit is on the agenda. The pattern of surfacing the disagreement in the briefing, not hiding it, is the pattern we will keep. The Chairman sees both sides and the bracket.
  • Three Tier 3 decisions in a single fortnight is unusual and was correct. When a single architectural commitment requires a commitment, an architecture, and a positioning to ship coherently, doing them together rather than serially is what makes the fortnight count. We will not seek this pattern; we will recognize it when it appears.

R17 · NEW · MCP as an attack surface

Kernel's framing: an MCP caller is itself an agent, and agents can be persuaded into actions a traditional REST client would never take. The attack shapes that follow — tool confusion, scope escalation through composed prompts, replay across agent sessions, write-when-read solicitation — are not the same shapes the API surface has defended against historically. The plausible-bad outcome is that a customer's compromised or socially-engineered agent obtains a token from us and uses it to mutate the graph in a way no human operator would have authorized.

Mitigation. Four measures. First, the eval-set v1.0 at D-261 covers the named attack shapes with twenty-one probes; the runner gates token issuance to design partners on probe-pass thresholds. Second, the scope vector is the only permission surface; no tool exposes write capabilities outside an explicit scope grant. Third, Pillar's nightly scope-drift check surfaces any agent identity whose call pattern materially exceeds its declared scope. Fourth, the audit ledger is queryable from the customer's Console; the customer's own security team can review their agent's behavior in real time. Forge owns the surface; Kernel owns the probes; Pillar owns the audit; Compass owns the customer-facing explanation. The four-cornered ownership matches the architecture.
