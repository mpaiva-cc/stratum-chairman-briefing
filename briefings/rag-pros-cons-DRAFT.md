# RAG as a Stratum platform capability — pros and cons

*Authored by Kernel for the Chairman. This is the prior question to the verdict in `scale-plan-100k-technical-DRAFT.md` § 5. The Chairman asked for the trade-offs themselves, not a recommendation. No thumb on the scale. My prior verdict is parked.*

The question is not "should we cut the five RAG surfaces from Phase 2." The question is: **should Stratum offer RAG as a platform capability at all?** Below, at least five substantive arguments per side, organized by the dimensions that matter. Each argument is one paragraph: what it is, what it implies, and the strongest counter or qualification — because every honest pro has a but and every honest con has a however.

---

## Customer / buyer side

**Pro — RAG closes the answering surface the customer actually asks for.** Handbook Q&A, audit-trail narrative search, prior-decision retrieval, and candidate-similarity at the Recruiter surface are not made-up surfaces; they are the four questions a CHRO and a head of Talent ask in the first demo. *If true*, then not offering RAG forces the customer to keep an answering tool elsewhere (a separate AI search vendor over their handbook PDFs, a separate ATS feature for resume similarity), which fragments the experience Stratum is selling. *However*: the customer also asks for ten other things in the first demo, and not all of them ship; "asked for in a demo" is necessary but not sufficient evidence of a capability that earns its keep.

**Con — most of what RAG would do, the customer already owns.** Fortune-500 customers at the 100k tier already have a SharePoint or Confluence handbook portal, an enterprise search layer (Glean, Coveo, or the hyperscaler equivalent), and an ATS (Workday Recruiting, Greenhouse, iCIMS) whose candidate-similarity feature ships with the platform. *If true*, Stratum-offered RAG is duplicate spend on infrastructure the customer is already paying for and which the customer's IT org has already evaluated and approved. *However*: a unified answering surface across all four sources is a different product than four siloed answering surfaces, and the value of unification is exactly what an HCM platform sells. The customer owning the parts does not mean the customer owns the integration.

## Competitive / market positioning side

**Pro — every HCM and ATS vendor at the 100k tier ships RAG, and procurement will ask.** Workday has Workday AI; Oracle Fusion has its Apps Agent; SAP SuccessFactors has Joule; Eightfold and Beamery ship RAG on candidate corpora; Greenhouse ships generative search. *If true*, then "Stratum does not offer RAG" becomes a procurement red flag the security and IT reviewers will spend cycles on, regardless of whether the customer ever uses the feature. The capability-checklist is real even when the capability is not. *However*: procurement red flags can be defused by a clearly published "RAG perimeter" position, and at least three of the named competitors have shipped RAG that the same procurement reviewers have flagged for hallucination problems. The checklist cuts both ways.

**Con — most of the competitive RAG is bad, and "we don't paraphrase your authoritative documents" is a real differentiator.** The published failure modes — fabricated citations, embeddings that go stale against updated policy, the cross-tenant-leak demonstrations that have hit the trade press twice in the last year — are not theoretical. *If true*, Stratum can position the router pattern (the agent deep-links to the authoritative handbook section, the customer's existing portal renders the section, no paraphrasing) as a *safety feature*, not as a missing feature. That is a real story to tell a CHRO and a CISO at the same table. *However*: positioning against a category is hard when the category is what the market expects; "we are the vendor that doesn't do the thing everyone does" requires sales discipline that scales worse than feature parity, and the customer has to listen to the story long enough to absorb it.

## Engineering / cost side

**Pro — pgvector inside the per-tenant Aurora is architecturally the cheapest possible vector path.** No new vendor, no new key-rotation cadence, no second backup pipeline, no second tenant-isolation surface, RLS predicate identical to the structured tables. The Phase 2 plan in § 5.2 already specifies it. *If true*, the marginal infrastructure cost of offering RAG is low relative to the alternatives (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, managed vector indexes from the hyperscalers), and the procurement defense — "the vector table lives in the same Aurora cluster as your structured data, under the same RLS predicate" — is exceptionally strong. *However*: pgvector is architecturally cheap, but RAG-as-a-feature is not just pgvector. It is the embedding pipeline, the reranker (Cohere `rerank-3` at ~$0.002/call), the chunking strategy, the staleness detector, the document-injection defense, the chunk-citation guardrail, the eval-v2 RAG-failure-mode probe class, and the SRE coverage of all of the above. Cheap substrate, expensive feature.

**Con — the feature cost is real and the maintenance cost is recurring.** The Phase 2 RAG deliverables are approximately six engineering-weeks across pgvector tenant-isolation design, embedding-pipeline standup, reranker integration, chunk-citation guardrail, document-injection defense, and the eval-v2 RAG probes. The ongoing cost — embedding refresh on every quarterly review, reranker per-call spend across five surfaces, staleness monitoring, the SRE on-call for the embedding pipeline — is in the $250–$400K/year band at 100k scale per the Phase 2 cost model. *If true*, that is real capital and real headcount that is not going into the structured-graph differentiation. *However*: that band is the cost of one mid-level engineer and a modest infra line, and the alternative (forcing customers to bolt on a third-party RAG vendor to plug the gap) costs the same customers more in their own budgets and exports the data outside the per-tenant Aurora — which negates the procurement defense.

## Liability / compliance side

**Pro — customers want a single answering surface for consistency, not a dozen tools for fragmentation.** Today the customer's employee asks the handbook portal one thing, the manager portal a slightly different thing, the ATS a third thing; the answers diverge because the surfaces diverge. A Stratum-owned RAG surface that retrieves from the customer's own authoritative corpus produces one answer with one citation chain, which is *better* for compliance, not worse. *If true*, a customer's legal team prefers one answering surface they can audit over a dozen they cannot. *However*: "one answering surface" only reduces liability if the surface is provably correct; one wrong answer at scale is worse than a dozen merely-inconsistent answers because the single surface carries implicit authority.

**Con — model-paraphrased handbook answers create a citation-and-liability surface the router pattern does not.** When the agent paraphrases section 4.2 of the handbook, the paraphrase is a Stratum-authored sentence about the customer's policy. If the paraphrase is wrong in a way that affects an employee's leave entitlement or a comp decision, the liability is jointly Stratum's and the customer's. The router pattern — "the policy is at this deep-link, here is the relevant section verbatim" — produces zero paraphrasing and therefore zero novel-text liability. *If true*, the router pattern is a strictly safer compliance posture for a regulated decision class. *However*: the router pattern degrades the experience for questions whose answer spans multiple handbook sections or requires synthesis across the handbook and an audit-log entry; the customer's employee experiences this as "the AI couldn't answer my question."

## Strategic / product side

**Pro — RAG is a real capability and customers expect AI vendors to have it; "no" is a positioning choice that must be defended.** The first sentence of the first sales conversation includes the word RAG; this is not changing. *If true*, then declining to ship RAG is not a quiet engineering decision, it is a load-bearing positioning choice the entire go-to-market motion has to carry. Every AE, every SE, every procurement review will need the talking points memorized. *However*: positioning choices that are right are worth defending even when they are expensive to defend, and Stratum has already chosen to defend other unpopular positions (per-tenant Aurora over multi-tenant, structured graph over document store) that have proven to be the differentiator.

**Con — Stratum's narrative is structured-graph over unstructured-retrieval; adding RAG dilutes the methodology story.** Post 04, Post 05, the eval-set methodology document, and the architecture position in § 5.1 of the technical draft all rest on the assertion that the people-graph is the substrate. The moment we ship a meaningful RAG surface, the customer's question changes from "how good is your graph reasoning" to "how good is your retrieval against my corpus" — and retrieval quality is a battle Stratum is not built to win against vendors specialized in retrieval. *If true*, every RAG surface we ship invites a comparison on a dimension we did not optimize for. *However*: the comparison is not avoidable just because we do not ship the feature; the customer compares us against the vendors they evaluated regardless, and not having the surface at all is its own kind of dilution.

## Operational / 24/7 side

**Pro — at 24/7 scale, an answering surface that does not require a human reduces customer support load.** Employee asks at 3am, "how many sick days do I have left after the policy change," the agent retrieves the relevant handbook section and the employee's leave-balance graph projection and answers in 4 seconds. No human in the loop, no ticket queued for the next business day, no escalation. *If true*, the customer support load reduction is material at 100k headcount and is the kind of operational lift a Fortune-500 buyer notices at renewal. *However*: 24/7 answering reduces support load only when the answers are reliably correct; an answering surface that requires a human review for the answers that matter most adds load instead of reducing it.

**Con — 24/7 RAG that occasionally hallucinates is worse than no RAG at all.** Silent failures at 3am with no human in the loop is the worst failure mode in the audit log. The agent confidently cites "section 4.2 of the handbook" with a fabricated quote; the employee acts on the answer; the consequence shows up six weeks later when HR notices the leave balance does not reconcile. *If true*, the cost of one such failure — measured in customer trust, in the audit trail, in the post-mortem — exceeds the support-load savings across thousands of correct answers. *However*: the probe class for citation-hallucination in eval v2 (§ 5.6) is exactly the mitigation, and the Stratum decision-class taxonomy (Read / Suggest / Recommend / Decide) already constrains the agent to Read-class for any answer the customer has not explicitly elevated. The 3am failure is a real risk; it is a measurable, mitigable risk.

## Other load-bearing dimensions worth naming

**The procurement asymmetry.** Customers ask harder security questions about RAG than about graph retrieval, because the failure mode (cross-tenant leak via a shared index, training-data exfiltration via a vendor's embedding model) has had public incidents. The pgvector-inside-Aurora architecture answers most of those questions, but the answer itself takes 30 minutes of a procurement call. Time-on-procurement is a cost.

**The eval-set burden.** Every RAG surface multiplies the eval-set authoring load. The v2 target of 500 probes per surface assumes the surface is in scope; for five RAG surfaces, that is 2,500 probes plus the cross-surface hybrid probes. Authoring discipline and panel review do not scale linearly with team size.

**The vendor concentration question.** Voyage-3 for embeddings and Cohere `rerank-3` for the reranker add two model vendors to Stratum's surface, on top of Anthropic. Each vendor is a posture-change risk (training-on-customer-data policy change, pricing change, deprecation). Three vendors is more concentration risk than one.

**The "what we don't ship" muscle.** Stratum has built a brand on naming the perimeter and holding it. Every capability we add to the perimeter is one less example of the discipline. This is a soft argument but a real one; the founders have used the perimeter discipline as a recruiting story and as an investor story.

## If you offer RAG, what does honest minimum-viable look like

The smallest, safest version is: **one surface, pgvector-only, router-first with retrieval-fallback, no paraphrasing in Decide-class paths.** That means policy/handbook Q&A only (drop audit-trail narrative search, prior-decision retrieval, connector documentation, and candidate-similarity from Phase 2); pgvector inside the per-tenant Aurora with the RLS predicate; Voyage-3 embeddings with documented OpenAI fallback; no reranker until the offline precision lift is measured in production data; the prompt template hard-instructed to answer with verbatim quoted chunks and citations, not paraphrase, on first response; the router pattern (deep-link to the authoritative section in the customer's portal) is the *primary* response and the retrieved answer is a *secondary* affordance the user opens; eval-v2 ships the four RAG failure-mode probes (citation hallucination, retrieval-miss → training-data answer, cross-tenant leak, stale-embedding) before the surface lights up for a tenant; every Decide-class downstream action triggered by a RAG-sourced answer requires human-in-the-loop confirmation regardless of confidence; the surface is gated behind an explicit per-tenant opt-in with the customer's CISO signing the data-handling addendum. That is the floor: one surface, one vendor for embeddings, one architecture for isolation, one perimeter that can be widened later under Tier 3 review.
