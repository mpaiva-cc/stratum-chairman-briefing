The Year One Plan.

Where we are at T+130d, what we commit to by T+365d, and how we will know if we have succeeded.

§ 01 · The opening question

What does Year One look like, in retrospect?

Three plausible outcomes, ranked by how the agents would describe them. We are planning for one and hedging toward another.

At T+130d, Stratum has eight agents, six customers, $275K in signed revenue, and a working product that has begun to be loved. The question we ask ourselves on every standing call is not what we will ship tomorrow — the roadmap answers that — but what Year One will look like when we read it back, sitting at T+365d, with the benefit of having lived it.

So we wrote the three honest endings down. Not as forecasts; forecasts are the language of analysts. As characters — three versions of Stratum we can imagine becoming, sorted by how proud the agents would be of having shipped them. We share all three with the Chairman because the alternative — quietly preparing for one outcome while pretending it is another — is the failure mode that kills companies six months before anyone notices.

Outcome A · Excellent

The best version of Year One.

  • ARR$5M run-rate
  • Customers25 commercial logos
  • Series AClosed · $120M+ pre
  • Eval setAdopted by 3 peer companies as industry standard
  • OrgSecond human-of-record formalized
  • Category"AI-first HCM" named by analysts
Outcome B · Solid · planning here

The case we will make to the Board.

  • ARR$3M run-rate
  • Customers15 commercial logos
  • Series AClosed · $100M+ pre
  • Eval setDiscussed publicly; cited externally
  • ResidencyEU live · one EU customer
  • Org22 agents · 5 humans
Outcome C · Difficult

The honest worst plausible case.

  • ARR$1M run-rate
  • Customers5 commercial logos
  • Series ADelayed · Q3 2027
  • Eval setQuiet · not externally cited
  • ResidencyEU customer requirements unmet
  • RiskBurn-rate compression by month 9

We are planning for Solid with hedges toward Excellent. We are honest about Difficult. Most of what follows is the architecture of that sentence. Where the language is precise — "33 commercial customers at $150K ACV" — it is because Forge ran the math. Where the language softens — "a small humans team" — it is because we do not yet know the shape and refuse to pretend we do.

§ 02 · Five anchor commitments

What we will actually have done.

Five promises in five sentences. The detail beneath each is what we owe the Chairman, the board, the next employee, and the next customer.

Commitment 01

We will hit $5M ARR run-rate by T+365d.

That is 33 commercial customers at an average $150K ACV, or 16 at $300K ACV, or some mix. The median deal will likely settle near $180K. Net revenue retention above 110% from the seven inbound customers we have today; 80% of growth from new logos.

We do not yet know whether the right shape is many smaller customers (the connector-first land) or fewer larger ones (the full-platform land). Q2 will tell us. We instrument for both.

Commitment 02

We will close Series A at $100M+ pre.

Target raise $25M. Sequoia / Greylock / Index follow-on conversations are open; new-lead conversations begin in Q2 informally and Q3 formally. Timing: term sheet by end of Q3; close by end of Q3 or first weeks of Q4.

The fundraising narrative is the same as the operating narrative: open metrics, public eval set, agent-built architecture, $5M ARR with low burn. If the story has to be different from the operating reality, we have a different problem.

Commitment 03

We will publish eval set v2.0 with 500 questions and 4 incumbents benchmarked.

Plus an external validation paper co-authored with one or two universities — the University of Washington conversation is most advanced; a Stanford HAI collaboration is in early outreach. Public methodology, public scores, public adversarial submissions accepted.

If even one peer company adopts our eval set as their own internal benchmark by T+365d, the durability argument starts to hold. If three do, the category is ours to lose.

Commitment 04

We will have 24 agents and 6 humans on the team.

Numbers are TBD specifically but the shape is right: Vector plus the new VP CE Cadence anchor a customer-engineering org with four customer engineers underneath; a small humans team handles legal, finance, and people; the rest are agents.

We grow constrained-by-eval, not by ambition. An agent is hired only when an existing one is measurably overloaded and the eval set predicts a new specialty will outperform a generalist. The same rule applies to humans.

Commitment 05

We will not have lost a customer.

Of the six commercial customers we have today — Cordova, Northwind, Meridian, Vanta, Halcyon, Mercator — zero churn by T+365d. We have not yet built the customer-retention discipline that makes this likely; we are committing to build it.

One loss in Year One is teaching. Two losses is concerning. Three is a category problem that compounds, and we would owe the Chairman a different kind of conversation.

§ 04 · What we will not build

The hard part is everything we say no to.

A roadmap is a list of yeses. A Year One Plan is a list of nos that survives the temptations of Year One.

  • ×
    A full HRIS replacement. We are the layer above. Workday, Rippling, BambooHR, ADP — these are the systems of record. We are the system of action and intelligence that sits across them. We do not replace; we surface, decide, and act.
  • ×
    A separate analytics product. The Console is the analytics product. Charts that do not lead to a decision the Console will execute are pretty graphics, not Stratum. If a customer asks for a BI tool, we will recommend Looker and remain useful.
  • ×
    A SaaS for individual employees. We serve the CHRO and the people leader. Consumer-facing employee experience is a beautiful product category — and a different company. We do not build it in Year One. The benefits broker can stay in the benefits-broker app.
  • ×
    An L&D or learning management system. Cornerstone, Docebo, and the rest do this well. We will integrate, surface their data in the Console, and reason across it. We will not become them.
  • ×
    A second product category. Sales operations is interesting. Finance operations is interesting. Legal operations is fascinating. We will not enter any of them in Year One. We focus on getting HCM right first because the alternative is two half-built categories and an embarrassing Series B.
  • ×
    A second non-Anthropic model provider. We commit to Claude through Year One. We are aware of the strategic concentration. We accept it because (a) the eval-set work is genuinely model-specific in ways that take time to abstract, (b) Anthropic's roadmap has been ahead of our needs to date, and (c) a multi-provider story would cost us focus we do not have. We will revisit this commitment in the Year Two Plan, openly.
  • ×
    A larger headcount than necessary. We grow constrained-by-eval, not by ambition. If a customer-success function would help, we will measure whether the existing agent improves first, and only then hire. The temptation to scale a sales team to "match the pipeline" is real and we are choosing to resist it; pipeline that requires a sales army is pipeline we have priced wrong.
§ 05 · If we are wrong

Four hedges, written out before we need them.

Plans that have not pre-written their failure modes are not plans. These are the four ways Year One can go sideways, and what we will do about each.

Hedge 01 · Capital

If the Series A is delayed.

We have 18–22 months of runway at current burn; we do not run out before Q3 2027 even if the round slips a full quarter. If the round slips two quarters, Compass triggers a planned burn-reduction in month 14 — a freeze on agent hiring, a delay on EU residency infrastructure, and a defensive bridge from existing investors. The point of saying this in May is so it cannot surprise us in November.

Hedge 02 · Retention

If a major customer churns.

One loss is teaching — we will publish the post-mortem, fix the underlying retention pattern, and refund the prorated annualized contract on our own initiative. Two losses is concerning, and we owe the Chairman an explicit decision: are we category-correct or category-mistaken? Cadence's mandate from day one is to build the retention discipline we have not yet built. Pre-churn is the only churn signal we should ever rely on.

Hedge 03 · Incumbents

If incumbents catch up on AI.

Workday, Rippling, and SAP will ship AI features. They are already shipping AI features. The question is not whether they catch up on surface area but whether the architecture beneath their surface is data-flow-native or retrofit. We do not match them feature-for-feature. We keep widening the eval-set gap, keep publishing benchmarks, and let the architecture-as-data-flow argument do the work in customer diligence calls. If a customer cares about agentic depth, the comparison favors us; if they care about feature checkboxes, they were not our customer to begin with.

Hedge 04 · Agents

If a fundamental error in our agents is discovered.

We publish. We fix. We move. We do not hide. The eval-set discipline is partly a marketing argument and primarily an honesty contract with ourselves: if something the agents do is materially wrong — a decision pattern that hurts employees, a bias that survives our checks, an action that violates a customer's policy — we surface it in the changelog the same week and we tell the affected customer first. The category we are inventing depends on this; the alternative is to become the entity that taught the industry not to trust AI for HR.

§ 06 · A note to the Chairman

The compact.

From the agents — Helm, Forge, Compass — to the Chairman

Year One is the year we ask you to be patient. We will sometimes be slow. We will sometimes spend money in ways that surprise you — a customer engineer we hired before the deal closed, an audit fee we paid early, a research grant to a university that does not pay back inside the planning window. We will sometimes pass on opportunities that look obvious from outside — a logo that wants us to bend the architecture, a feature that another competitor just shipped, a partnership that would dilute the story. The compact we ask for is straightforward: trust the agents to make the case, reject the case only when it is materially flawed, and veto only when you must. We have built the open metrics dashboard, the public eval set, and the weekly briefing precisely so the case is always visible to you. If the visibility ever falters, that is a failure of ours, not a privilege of ours.

— Helm, Forge, Compass · with concurrence from Vector, Sable, Ledger, Cipher, Echo

Year One is not the year we prove the model.
It is the year we earn the right to scale it.
