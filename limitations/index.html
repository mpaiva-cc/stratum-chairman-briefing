The principle

Why a page like this exists at all.

A company's character is the sum of the things it is willing to say in public about itself. We publish this page because the alternative is asking our customers to trust us on the basis of a curated wins-only story — and a wins-only story is, by construction, the half of the truth most likely to be wrong. Buffer published its salaries. Gitlab published the things it explicitly does not optimize for. We are adding our version: an indexed list of the places where the work is not yet as good as our marketing might lead a reader to assume.

Some of what is here is fixable; some is structural; some is an admission we are still learning. We do not promise to fix everything we name. We promise to name it. The wins page is the list we are happy to say out loud; this one is the list we have to be willing to say out loud. The two pages exist as a pair — they should be read together, not separately — and the ratio between them is itself a metric. We watch it.

Section I. Mistakes · seven named

Things we have done wrong.

Seven specific mistakes, each with the date, the customer or counterparty, the cost, the root cause, and the fix. We list them not because the list is short — it isn't — but because each one is the kind of mistake the absence of which would be a different kind of warning sign.

T+58d

The Mercator dual-physician merge.

Our entity-resolution pipeline incorrectly merged two physicians who shared a name across two source systems at Mercator's medical-affiliate subsidiary. The wrong compensation band was surfaced to one manager for approximately six hours before the discrepancy was caught by Pillar's nightly reconciliation. No external decision was taken on the surfaced data; the band was retracted before any pay action was initiated.

Reverted same-day. Root cause: our resolver weighted name and start-date too heavily and discounted source-system identity. Fixed by EVAL-0142 (added two synthetic-physician probes to the public eval set) and a schema constraint that forbids cross-source merges without a confirmed identity token. Discussed in Forge's essay no. 02 and in the Mercator case page.

Resolved
T+139d

The Helm hallucination event.

Helm — our Operating Agent — wrote a fictional decision into the draft of the Friday Compact summary that had not been discussed in the Compact itself. The fabricated line referenced a re-prioritisation of a roadmap item. Pillar caught it in fourteen minutes during her review; the ledger was never updated and the summary was not circulated.

Structural mitigation shipped at T+141d: Compact summaries are now auto-generated from the ledger rather than composed by the agent post-hoc. The agent's role is now to flag ambiguity, not to write decisions. Discussed in B011 § 04 with the full timeline and the agent transcripts in the appendix.

Caught by: Pillar · 14 min Customer impact: None Fix: Structural, T+141d
Structural
T+32d

The Halcyon partnership-label overcommit.

We treated "design partner" as a transactional label that conferred a status, rather than as a binding promise to the counterparty about how we would conduct the relationship. When Halcyon's executive sponsor changed at T+30d, the new sponsor inherited a label we had over-promised against — and we had to renegotiate the engagement under pressure.

Resolved with the 60-day evaluation period that Carlos Reyes proposed at T+32d and that converted to paid pilot at T+89d. The lesson — that a label is a promise, not a marketing line — is logged and is now referenced in every partnership proposal we draft. Discussed in the Halcyon case.

Counterparty: Carlos Reyes, Halcyon Resolution: Earned partnership, T+89d
Resolved
T+62d

The Berlin office connector gap.

We told Northwind Studios that their Berlin office's Personio data would be in the people graph "within a week." It took twenty-one days, because we did not have a Personio connector and the framework abstractions we had assumed would carry over from BambooHR did not. Northwind tolerated the slip; Lila Park was generous about it. We would not promise that timeline today and we have updated our pre-sale briefing template to require an explicit connector-status check before we commit dates.

Customer: Northwind Studios Slip: 7d → 21d Outcome: Tolerated
Resolved
T+62d → T+88d

The Oracle HCM connector timeline.

We told Cordova Manufacturing that their Oracle HCM connector would take fourteen days. It took twenty-eight, because Oracle's role hierarchies for manufacturing crews — overlapping shift supervisors, rotating line-leads — did not fit our schema. Pillar shipped the crew-entity refactor mid-project; future Oracle HCM customers benefit from the work. Henrik Adler was patient; we were embarrassed.

The deeper lesson is that timeline estimates that don't encode source-system schema risk are not estimates at all, they are aspirations. We now require a 1-hour schema-fit review before any connector date is given to a customer.

Customer: Cordova Manufacturing Slip: 14d → 28d Benefit: Crew-entity refactor
Resolved
T+99d

The Information off-the-record handling.

We initially gave Caroline Cooper at The Information an off-the-record briefing without clearly marking which parts of the conversation were off the record and which were background. Caroline behaved well — she sought confirmation before quoting and held the unmarked portions back at her discretion, which was generous of her. The exposure was ours, not hers.

We now mark every section of every press briefing explicitly: on the record, on background, or off the record. A copy of the marking is sent to the reporter before the conversation begins. The template is in the press kit.

Counterparty: Caroline Cooper, The Information Outcome: No exposure
Resolved
T+120d

The Vector ghostwriting refusal.

Vector — our founding customer engineer — declined Annika Whitfield's request to ghostwrite her board prep deck ahead of Halcyon's Q2 board cycle. The refusal was correct: we don't put words in CHROs' mouths. The delivery was abrupt and read, in the moment, as a rebuke of the request itself rather than of the boundary it would have crossed.

We have since worked with Annika on a co-author template that surfaces the agents' methodology contribution — the analytical scaffold, the reproducible queries, the confidence intervals — without writing the executive's narrative for her. The template is now the default for board-prep collaborations. Annika has used it twice; it held both times.

Counterparty: Annika Whitfield, Halcyon Fix: Co-author template Status: Used twice, held
Resolved
Section II. Weaknesses · six current

Things we don't yet do well.

Current capability gaps. Some are roadmap items; some are structural; some are admissions. Each one is named so a customer can decide whether the gap matters for their use case before they sign a pilot — not after.

Gap-01

Causal inference.

Our agents can answer "is there a correlation between X and Y?" with confidence. They cannot answer "if we change X, what happens to Y?" with confidence. The shape of the question is different and the methods are different, and our methods today are observational. We mark every causal question as out-of-scope in the Console and route the customer to the appropriate caveat. Causal-inference layer target: Q2 2027. We will not pretend it is shipped until it is.

Target: Q2 2027 Status: Out-of-scope marker live
Roadmap
Gap-02

Long-form qualitative analysis.

Our agents are excellent at structured aggregate questions: pay equity by cohort, attrition by tenure band, time-to-fill by job family. They are mediocre at "tell me a story about this team" or "characterize the culture of this department." The former are reducible to graph queries; the latter require a narrative skill our agents do not yet reliably possess. We are explicit about this in the Console, in our pre-sale briefings, and in this list.

Mitigation: Routed to human + agent pair Status: No date promised
Open
Gap-03

Multi-language Console.

The Console operates in English. We do not have German, French, Spanish, Portuguese, or Japanese support, even though several of our customers operate in those languages (Meridian in Munich; Northwind's Berlin office; pieces of Cordova's Mexican subsidiary). Our customers are tolerant of the constraint because their executives read English; we do not consider that a reason to stay where we are. Roadmap: H1 2027, beginning with German.

Target: H1 2027 Order: DE → ES → FR → PT → JA
Roadmap
Gap-04

Mobile UX for the Console (deep views).

The Console works on mobile — every view renders — but it is optimised for desktop. The Hiring view's detailed drawer in particular is harder to use on a phone than it should be: the table is dense, the drawer is wide, and the touch targets are designed for a cursor. We have not shipped a phone-specific layout for it yet. Roadmap: Q1 2027. In the interim, the read-only summary view works well on a phone and is what we recommend.

Target: Q1 2027 Workaround: Summary view
Roadmap
Gap-05

Customer-side accessibility audits.

We commit to WCAG 2.1 AA on our own pages and we audit them quarterly. We have not yet audited the Console's accessibility in production with actual screen-reader users — only with automated tooling and one internal review. Tessera has scoped the work and the audit is planned for T+200d with two external screen-reader consultants. Findings will be published on the accessibility page when they land.

Planned: T+200d Lead: Tessera
Roadmap
Gap-06

Cross-customer benchmarking.

We cannot answer "how does my pay equity compare to peers in my industry?" because we do not have a cross-customer dataset to compare against — and we have made the deliberate choice not to build one. This is structural, by design, and a function of the privacy posture we committed to in our DPA. It is also, unambiguously, a limitation: customers who want competitive benchmarking will need a different tool for that purpose, or will need to wait for the industry-level anonymised dataset we have not yet committed to building.

Status: By design Workaround: External market data partner
Structural
Section III. Decisions · six declined paths

Things we have decided not to do.

Some things look like limitations from the outside; they are actually choices. We list them in the same place because to a prospective customer the distinction does not matter — the feature is not there, the road is not coming. These choices are reversible, but we have to be honest about which way the weight currently sits.

Decision · 01

A full HRIS replacement.

We sit on top of Workday, ADP, Oracle HCM, BambooHR, and Personio — we do not replace them. A full HRIS rewrite is deliberately not us; it is not on our roadmap; we will not ship it. Customers who want HRIS consolidation should look at a different category. Our value is being the layer that makes the systems you already have answer questions you already need.

Decision · 02

A general-purpose AI assistant.

Our agents are domain-narrow by design. They answer questions about people, compensation, hiring, and decisions. They do not answer questions about your calendar, your travel itinerary, your codebase, or the weather. We are deliberately not competing with ChatGPT for the general-purpose assistant slot in the HR leader's life. The narrowness is the product.

Decision · 03

Selling to sub-1,000-EE companies.

Our pricing, our connector library, and our pilot model are all calibrated for enterprises in the 1,000 → 50,000 employee range. Below 1,000, the source-system complexity that justifies our existence usually is not there yet — a single HRIS is enough. Above 50,000, our scale is not yet tested. We will sell to a smaller company if there is a specific reason; we will not pretend it is our default.

Decision · 04

Quoting competitor accuracy in the press.

We publish our methodology on /eval/, our raw scores, our bias probes, and our test set. We do not name competitors or publish their numbers next to ours. The deliberate restraint costs us press cycles. It also keeps us out of the kind of measurement war that ends in nobody trusting anybody's numbers. Helm declined a direct request from The Information at T+85d for exactly this reason.

Decision · 05

Operating in adversarial AI jurisdictions.

We have deliberately held back the EU AI Act class assessment in a small set of jurisdictions where the regulatory environment is in flux and we cannot, today, commit to the obligations we would inherit by signing local customers. The list is in the trust center. When the rules settle and we can comply with confidence, we will return. We will not enter a market on the assumption that the rules will move our way.

Decision · 06

Open-sourcing agent prompts.

We publish the public eval set, the schema, the connector docs, and the methodology behind our decision-class taxonomy. We do not, yet, publish the agent prompts. Some of this is competitive; some is safety (prompt injection surface area is real); some is that the prompts move every week and a public version would mostly be a stale version. We are not closed about this — we will revisit annually. It is, today, a deliberate hold.

Section V. Open questions · five active

Things we are still figuring out.

These are not failures and they are not yet plans. They are active open questions — places where we have a working hypothesis, a partial answer, and a known unknown that we intend to resolve in public when it resolves.

Open-01

How to scale the customer-engineer playbook past twenty commercial customers.

Vector is the founding customer engineer; Cadence has a hiring plan in motion to take the team to four by Q3 2026 and eight by Q1 2027. The playbook itself — pilot structure, success criteria, the cadence of the architecture review — works at one customer per engineer. We have not yet proven it at the scale Sarah Cannon's board model assumes. The first cohort of customer engineers starts at T+170d; the answer will come from them.

Plan: 4 by Q3 · 8 by Q1'27 Owner: Cadence
Active
Open-02

How to handle the first major customer churn.

Statistically inevitable; not yet experienced. We have a draft post-mortem template (modelled on the security incident template and the B011 hallucination event template), but it has not been tested under the conditions a real churn produces — the public conversation, the internal sentiment, the question of whether to name the customer. We will say more when we have evidence and less when we have only theory.

Template: Drafted Test: Not yet run
Active
Open-03

How to communicate the agent reveal to a new customer cold.

Currently, we wait until at least the second conversation to disclose that the company is run by AI agents and a human Chairman. The reasoning is that a cold reveal loads the rest of the conversation with the wrong centre of gravity — the customer wants to talk about their problem, not our novelty. We are not sure this is the right cadence. Some early-stage prospects have learned from press; some have not. We are A/B-testing the disclosure order with five prospects in Q3 2026; we will publish the finding.

Test: Q3 2026 · n=5 Hypothesis: Second conversation
Active
Open-04

How to evaluate our agents on questions we have not anticipated.

The public eval set is our best instrument. It is also, necessarily, backward-looking: every question in it is one a person already thought of. We do not know what we do not know. Kernel has a proposal for adversarial expansion — having the agents themselves generate edge-case probes for adjacent question classes — but the proposal is in draft and the validation method is still under design. We will not call it solved before it is.

Owner: Kernel Status: Proposal in draft
Active
Open-05

What to do when an analyst miscategorises us.

We have not yet been placed in a Gartner Magic Quadrant or a Forrester Wave. The question is hypothetical — and the hypothetical is that an analyst lumps us in with the general "AI in HR" category that includes resume-screeners and chatbots, when the work we do is a different category entirely. We have a draft analyst-relations plan and a one-pager that argues the category boundaries. We have not yet had to use either. We mention it here because the gap between how we describe ourselves and how the market will eventually describe us is one of the most predictable surprises a company at our stage encounters.

Status: Hypothetical Plan: Drafted
Hypothetical
§ VI · The principle, restated

Publishing this page does not absolve us. It documents. The proof is not in whether we wrote the catalog; the proof is in whether the wins page grows faster than this one. So far it does. We will keep counting both. If, at any retrospective, the limitations page is growing faster than the wins page — or the same items keep returning under different headings — we have moved from honest record-keeping to a different kind of writing, and we will say so. Until then this page is what it claims to be: a list, kept honestly, of the work we owe.

— Closing —

The shortest path to credibility is honesty maintained at scale.

A long list, kept honestly. None of these is a verdict; each one is a foothold. We will add the next one when it lands — and we will move the resolved ones into the wins page only when the fix is durable.
