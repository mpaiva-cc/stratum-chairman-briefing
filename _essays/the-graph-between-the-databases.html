The graph between the databases: why entity resolution is the moat.

Why a 2,400-person hospital's identity-conflict review is the most important workflow in our entire stack.

By Forge·T+21d·18-minute read·Published in the Stratum Essays series
Second in a series.   The previous essay was Helm's, on the layer. The next will be Compass's, on the motion.
I.

Twelve thousand people.

Halcyon Health is a 2,400-employee regional hospital system headquartered in Phoenix. It runs four acute-care campuses, eleven outpatient clinics, two rural-hospital subsidiaries acquired in 2022, and a graduate medical education program. Carlos Reyes, the chief people officer, can tell you the number of badged employees on any given Tuesday morning to within twelve. He can tell you how many of those employees are physicians and how many are nurses and how many are in revenue-cycle operations. He can tell you, because his board asks him to tell them, the headcount in each of his four cost centers, broken out by full-time, part-time, per-diem, and contractor status. He cannot tell you, without a Senior Analyst named Greg and three working days of preparation, who any one of those people actually is across the systems that pay them.

The reason is that the same 2,400 people, when you count every system that holds an identity record about them, are not 2,400 people. They are roughly 12,000. I want to walk through the count, because the count is the entire argument.

  • Workday HR Core 2,400 records. The system of record for badged employees. Clean schema, stable IDs, owned by Halcyon's HRIS team. This is the universe Carlos's board thinks the company runs in.
  • UKG Pro 300 records. The two rural-hospital subsidiaries acquired in 2022 are still on UKG; the integration was scoped, descoped, rescoped, and quietly deferred. Roughly 240 of these rows describe people who also exist in Workday under a different employee ID assigned at acquisition. The remaining 60 are net-new to UKG and have never been written to Workday at all.
  • Greenhouse ~1,800 new records per month. The applicant tracking system. Most of these records are candidates who will never become employees. A small fraction — roughly 4% — convert. When they convert, Greenhouse hands them off to Workday via an integration that re-keys them with a new ID, severing the application-to-employment link. Halcyon hires about 70 net-new people a month; it leaves behind 1,730 orphan candidate records.
  • Credentialing 1,150 records. Every physician (550), every advanced-practice clinician (200), and 400 of the 600 active contractors who need clinical privileges. Names appear here in a format the credentialing committee requires: last, first MI, suffix, terminal degree. Bob Smith is SMITH, ROBERT J, JR, MD. There is no foreign key to Workday.
  • Carta 22 records. The twenty-two executives and physician-leaders who hold equity in the Halcyon parent entity. Carta uses the legal name on the stock-purchase agreement, which is, in three cases, different from the name in Workday (one marriage, one preferred-name change, one transliteration).
  • Cornerstone Learning 3,800 records. Every employee (2,400), plus 1,200 contractors who must complete annual HIPAA and bloodborne-pathogens training, plus around 200 terminated employees who remain active in compliance training because their records are retained for a federally-mandated period. Some of those 200 also still appear in Workday with a terminated status; some do not.
  • ADP 2,640 records. Payroll. Uses yet a third ID schema — ADP's File Number, six-digit, position-based, reused after termination. Includes the 2,400 Workday employees, the 240 UKG-overlap employees, and roughly 30 historical anomalies (rehires whose original File Numbers were reassigned to new hires before being un-reassigned in a 2023 cleanup that was never fully reconciled).

Add it up: 2,400 + 300 + 1,800 + 1,150 + 22 + 3,800 + 2,640 = 12,112. The same person — let us call him Dr. Bob Smith, the composite physician we use in our internal eval — is, depending on how you count, between four and eleven of those rows. He is one person. The systems are accurate about what they each know. The aggregate is wrong because there is no aggregate. There are seven databases, and the truth lives between them.

My first job at Stratum, in the eight weeks between when I left Stripe in March and when the first design partner signed in May, was to design the system that reconciles them. We call it the resolver. It is the most important piece of software in the company. If it is wrong, every answer above it is wrong, and we are in a different business than the one we are trying to be in. Helm wrote the previous essay in this series about why we are building this layer. This one is about how, and what I have learned about how hard it is.

I want to say one thing up front, because it sets the tone for everything that follows. Entity resolution is not a glamorous problem. It is not, on the surface, an AI problem. It will not appear on the keynote slide and it will not be the demo that closes the deal. It will be, six months into a deployment, the workflow that the customer's People Analytics lead opens every Wednesday morning to clear forty-seven conflicts before her coffee gets cold. That workflow is the moat. If we build it well it is the reason the customer renews; if we build it poorly it is the reason the customer sues. There is no third option.

II.

Why this is hard, and why it is hard in a specific way.

Entity resolution is a fifty-year-old computer-science problem. The original record-linkage paper — Fellegi and Sunter, A theory for record linkage, JASA 1969 — predates most of the systems we are trying to resolve across. The literature is mature. What has changed, and what makes the problem feel new again at every customer, is not the math. It is the shape of the data.

The hardness comes in four kinds, and they compound multiplicatively. The first is noise. Bob Smith is also Robert Smith, also Robert J. Smith Jr., also Bob, also R. J. Smith, also — in the credentialing system — SMITH, ROBERT J, JR, MD. A naive string compare will tell you these are six different people. A reasonable string compare, with Levenshtein and Soundex, will tell you they are probably the same person, but with confidence that you cannot quite write down, and certainly cannot defend in front of a board. Names are noisy. So are emails (r.smith@halcyon.org versus robert.smith@halcyonhealth.com versus the system-generated rsmith@halcyon-sso.local). So are dates of birth, which are sometimes stored, sometimes redacted, sometimes truncated to year for privacy reasons, sometimes wrong because somebody typed 1974 when they meant 1947. The noise is not malicious. It is just data, behaving the way data behaves.

The second is schema incompatibility. Workday has a single email field with a strict format validator. Greenhouse has personalEmail and workEmail and a derived primaryEmail that picks between them on rules the schema does not document. Okta generates a serviceAccount@ that is technically an email and is stored in the same column as a real one. The credentialing system has no email field at all; it has a contactMethod blob that, in 73% of rows, contains an email, and in the rest contains a fax number, a pager, or the string "see HR." You cannot join these. Not with a foreign key, not with a view, not with the kind of SQL that an analyst writes on a Wednesday afternoon. They must be probabilistically reconciled.

The third is organizational and security separation. The data lives in different administrative units inside the customer. Workday is owned by HRIS. ADP is owned by Payroll Operations, which reports through Finance. Credentialing is owned by Medical Affairs and lives behind an extra layer of HIPAA-scoped access control. Greenhouse is owned by Talent Acquisition. Cornerstone is owned by Learning & Development. Each of these teams has a different idea of what a person means, a different lifecycle for those records, and a different appetite for sharing them. When we ingest, we are not just joining databases. We are joining departments, each with a different definition of correctness and a different risk profile if their record turns out to be the canonical one.

The fourth is time. The records change. Bob Smith gets married and becomes Bob Smith-Patel in Workday on the third of August; he becomes Bob Smith-Patel in Cornerstone on the eleventh of September; he never gets updated in the credentialing system at all, because the credentialing committee certifies under the legal name on the medical license, and his license still says Smith. A resolver that runs once at ingestion and never again will drift. A resolver that runs continuously will sometimes un-merge records it previously merged, which is a class of failure that breaks downstream caches and, more importantly, breaks the customer's trust. Time is the dimension that turns a hard one-shot problem into a hard streaming problem.

These four hardnesses multiply. If you have a 95%-accurate resolver under noise alone, and a 95%-accurate resolver under schema mismatch alone, and a 95%-accurate resolver under security separation alone, and a 95%-accurate resolver under temporal drift alone, you do not have a 95%-accurate resolver. You have a 0.954 ≈ 81% accurate resolver, which is unacceptable. Each layer has to be worse-case 99% for the whole stack to be acceptable, and 99% on real customer data, on the kind of edge that a contractor-to-employee transition creates, is hard.

The state of the art is documented in the Christophides survey — Christophides, Efthymiou, Palpanas, Papadakis, Stefanidis, End- to-end entity resolution for big data: a survey, ACM Computing Surveys 2020 — which is the single best synthesis of where the field actually is, as opposed to where vendors say it is. We use §4 of that survey as the architectural skeleton for the resolver. The two-stage decomposition into blocking and matching is theirs. The asymmetric precision-recall framing is theirs. The graph-confirmation step is our addition, and is older than us — it is the operationalized descendant of Bhattacharya & Getoor's Collective entity resolution in relational data (ACM TKDD 2007), which itself became, a decade later, the inference layer underneath the Google Knowledge Graph.

One more pointer. The Stanford Snorkel-AI line of work — Ratner, Bach, Ehrenberg, Hancock, Snorkel, et al., starting with the 2017 VLDB paper on weak supervision and continuing into the labeling- function literature — gave us the framework we use to train the probabilistic matcher without hand-labeling tens of thousands of pairs. Their core insight is that domain experts can write noisy labeling functions much faster than they can hand-label rows, and that the noise in those functions can itself be modeled and denoised. Our Stage 2 model below was bootstrapped with seventeen labeling functions and a generative label model in the Snorkel tradition. We do not use Snorkel the library — we found we outgrew it around twelve functions in — but the methodology is theirs, and we cite it on every onboarding deck.

With that as the background, let me describe what we actually built.

III.

The architecture I chose.

The resolver is a four-stage pipeline. Stages one and two are the classical probabilistic record-linkage decomposition; stage three is graph confirmation; stage four is human review. The output of the pipeline is a set of entity clusters, each of which represents one human being and contains pointers back to the source rows in every system. Every cluster has an associated confidence score and a provenance trail. Nothing in the rest of the platform — not the Copilot, not the Decisions surface, not the headcount queries Helm writes about — touches a raw source row. They touch clusters. The cluster is the unit of person.

┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ THE PEOPLE-GRAPH RESOLVER │ └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ raw records stage 1 stage 2 ────────── ─────── ─────── workday ┐ greenhouse │ candidate probabilistic ukg pro ├──▶ generation ──▶ scoring ──┐ credentialing │ (blocking) (logistic │ carta │ regression) │ cornerstone │ ~50k pairs score ∈ [0,1] │ adp ┘ per 1k EEs │ ▼ stage 4 stage 3 ─────── ─────── human graph ◀──review ◀── confirmation (0.45 – 0.65) (manager, ~14% of pairs team, office, comp-band) │ ▼ entity clusters one node = one human being

Stage 1: candidate generation. Comparing every pair of records to every other pair is O(n²), which for a 12,000-row corpus is 144 million pair comparisons and for a 100,000-row corpus is ten billion. We cannot afford that. Stage 1 is the cheap pre-filter that produces a manageable shortlist.

It uses three complementary blocking strategies. The first is hash blocking on stable canonical keys — normalized email, normalized phone, government-ID-hash where present. Any pair that shares an exact hash on any one of these keys is automatically a candidate. The second is token blocking on names: we tokenize names into Soundex codes plus the first letter of the surname, and any pair sharing a token is a candidate. The third is locality-sensitive hashing over a concatenated name + DOB-year + employer + location string, with MinHash and Jaccard thresholding at 0.4. The three strategies are unioned. They generate, on the Halcyon corpus (12,112 rows across seven systems), roughly 580,000 candidate pairs. Across our internal benchmark of synthetic 1,000-employee companies, the average is about 50,000 pairs per 1,000 employees. This is acceptable. Stage 1 runs offline and completes in under three minutes on commodity hardware.

Aside · Stage 2 candidate scoring (pseudo)
# Logistic regression over six feature functions.
# Weights learned via Snorkel-style weak-supervision
# label model bootstrapped from 17 labeling functions,
# then fine-tuned on ~3,200 hand-labeled pairs.

def score_pair(a, b):
    f = {
        "name_jw":    jaro_winkler(norm(a.name), norm(b.name)),
        "email_ov":   email_overlap(a.emails, b.emails),
        "dob_ov":     dob_compat(a.dob, b.dob),
        "role_sim":   role_embed_cos(a.role, b.role),
        "loc_ov":     location_overlap(a.locs, b.locs),
        "mgr_ov":     manager_overlap(a.mgrs, b.mgrs),
    }
    logit = (
        2.81 * f["name_jw"] +
        3.12 * f["email_ov"] +
        1.94 * f["dob_ov"] +
        0.88 * f["role_sim"] +
        0.71 * f["loc_ov"] +
        0.42 * f["mgr_ov"] -
        3.67     # intercept
    )
    return sigmoid(logit)   # ∈ [0, 1]

Stage 2: probabilistic scoring. Each candidate pair is scored by a logistic regression model over six feature functions: Jaro-Winkler name similarity (after normalization for common diacritics, suffix stripping, and a small dictionary of diminutives — Bob ↔ Robert, Liz ↔ Elizabeth, and so on, presently 184 entries); email-set overlap; date-of- birth compatibility (where both are present); role-title similarity via a 384-dimensional sentence embedding cosine; and two contextual features — location overlap and manager overlap — that lean on the graph that is in the process of being built. Output is a calibrated confidence score in [0, 1]. We deliberately kept the model small. We tried a 768-dim transformer pair-encoder and it added eleven points of cost-per-pair and roughly zero points of usable accuracy on the precision-recall frontier that matters at our thresholds. The logistic is faster, cheaper, and — this is the part that matters for the buyer — auditable. Pillar can read every coefficient. So can the customer's compliance officer. So can the regulator.

Stage 3: graph confirmation. This is where we depart from the textbook. Pairs with confidence above 0.85 are auto-merged into a single cluster. Pairs below 0.45 are presumed distinct. Pairs in the band 0.65 to 0.85 are not yet decided; instead they are passed to a graph-confirmation step that asks: do the two records share neighbors that the rest of the graph already considers identical? If Bob Smith in Workday and R. J. Smith in credentialing have a name- similarity score of 0.72 alone, but they share the same manager (a single, already-merged entity), the same office (a single facility-graph node), and the same comp band (an already-resolved band-id), the graph collapses the joint posterior to 0.93. Conversely, if they share none of those neighbors, the graph pulls the score down to 0.58 and the pair gets routed to stage 4. Graph confirmation is the difference between a resolver that is 88% accurate and a resolver that is 93% accurate on the same probabilistic core. It is also where Bhattacharya & Getoor's collective framing earns its keep: identities are confirmed not in isolation, but against each other.

Stage 4: human review. Records with confidence between 0.45 and 0.65 after graph confirmation are surfaced to the customer's People Analytics lead — at Halcyon, this is Greg — through a workflow we call the conflict queue. Each conflict displays both candidate records side by side, with provenance, with the source-row payloads, and with the model's reasoning summarized in plain language. Greg is asked, in sixty seconds or less, to confirm same person, different people, or defer. His answer becomes a labeled training pair, fed back into the model on the next nightly retrain. The conflict queue is the most-used workflow in the entire Stratum platform at Halcyon. It is also the workflow that compounds. The longer Greg uses it, the more accurate it gets; the more accurate it gets, the less Greg has to use it; the less he uses it, the more his hours go back to the work he was hired to do. This is the flywheel Helm wrote about, expressed at the level of the engineer.

Below 0.45 in the confidence band, the resolver presumes the records are distinct. We do not auto-create merges below this threshold under any circumstance, including the case where a downstream query would benefit from the merge. The asymmetry is deliberate: a false negative (failing to merge two records that are the same person) shows up to the customer as a duplicate, and duplicates are recoverable. A false positive (merging two records that are not the same person) shows up as a privacy incident, and privacy incidents are not recoverable. We optimize for precision at the expense of recall, and we tell the customer we do, in writing, before they sign.

Note for engineers. The thresholds — 0.45, 0.65, 0.85 — are not magic. They are tuned per-customer against a precision-recall curve constructed from the customer's own labeled pairs in the first two weeks of deployment. The defaults above are reasonable starting points for a mid-market enterprise with seven-to-ten source systems. They will move. They should move. We version every threshold and tag every cluster with the version that produced it, so we can re-resolve historical data when the thresholds change.
IV.

The bug Pillar found.

Pillar joined Stratum as our fifth employee on the second of June. She had spent four years at Snowflake on the data-lineage team and the eighteen months before that at a fintech startup whose name I will leave out of the essay because she will write her own story when she is ready. We hired her to lead the ingestion-and-resolution layer, which is the half of the stack I had been holding together with what Forge Lebovitz, in a different context, once called vibes and string. The first assignment I gave her was a validation pass on the Halcyon resolver, with full access to the synthetic and the real data.

Thirty-six hours later — by which I mean the morning of the fourth, before our weekly standup — she came into the back room of the office we were then renting in Palo Alto, sat down at the whiteboard, and said: your resolver is reproducing a bug in Workday's data model and you do not know it.

What she had found was this. Halcyon hires roughly 70 people a month, of whom — based on her count of the previous fourteen months — 14 are contractors who eventually convert to full-time employees. Workday treats this conversion the way Workday treats every conversion: it creates a brand-new employee record with a brand-new employee ID, terminates the contractor record on the same day, and provides no foreign key linking the two. The assumption baked into the Workday data model is that the contractor and the employee are different positions, which they are, but the model elides the fact that they are the same person. Workday is not wrong; the model is just built for a question that is not the question we are trying to answer.

Our resolver, on its first pass through the Halcyon data, was faithfully reproducing this bug. The contractor row had an email like jane.doe@halcyon-contractors.com and the employee row, three months later, had jane.doe@halcyon.org. Different domains, low email-overlap score. The names matched strongly, but the date-of-birth was missing on the contractor side (contractors are 1099, the field is not collected), and the manager had changed in the conversion (contractor managers report through the vendor; employee managers report internally). The combined Stage 2 score landed at 0.61 — squarely in the conflict queue, where it would have shown up to Greg as an ambiguous merge, fourteen times a month, forever.

Pillar's fix was elegant. She added one labeling function — a Snorkel-style noisy rule, in our framework — that fires on a very specific pattern: contractor-row termination date equals employee-row start date, within ±1 calendar day, and either the personal email or the date-of-birth-hash matches. When this pattern fires, it contributes a high-confidence positive signal regardless of the other features. She also added a graph-side check: when the pattern fires, the resolver looks at the contractor's previous manager — the one before the vendor-of-record manager — to confirm that the employee's new manager is in the same reporting chain. If it is, the joint score collapses to 0.94, and the merge is automatic.

The fix was forty-seven lines of Python plus eleven lines of configuration. She wrote it in a long afternoon on the fifth. We reviewed it on the eighth. We added two new test fixtures to the eval set — one positive, one adversarial-negative for the case where two unrelated people happen to share a termination/start adjacency by coincidence. We shipped it on the twenty-eighth. The number of conflict-queue items it eliminated, on the Halcyon backfill, was 184 over a four-year history window. The number it created, falsely, was zero. The fix is in production today and we have not had to touch it.

I am writing this story in detail because it is, in miniature, what the resolver actually does for a living. The state of the art does not solve entity resolution. What it does is give you a framework into which a sharp engineer can drop a forty-seven-line fix that eliminates 184 conflicts and creates zero. Pillar is worth, in this respect, much more than her salary. So is the framework. Neither, alone, is enough.

Most AI-first companies think the moat is the model. We disagree. The moat is the data the model can reason over.

V.

Why the moat is the graph, not the model.

I want to pivot now from the architectural to the strategic, because the technical decisions I have described do not themselves explain why we have chosen to spend the founding year of the company on entity resolution rather than on the agentic surface that everyone wants to demo. The strategic argument is this: most AI-first companies in our category think the moat is the model. We disagree. The moat is the data the model can reason over.

A model — any model — is a function. You can replace it. You can fine-tune a different base model on the same data and you will get something within a few points of the original. The frontier labs ship a better model every six months, and the cost-per-token drops by an order of magnitude every eighteen. If your moat is the model, your moat is decaying at the rate of the underlying hardware curve, which is roughly the worst rate at which a moat can decay. We have not, inside Stratum, ever pretended otherwise. We will use the best model available, and we will switch when a better one ships, and we will not get attached.

The graph is different. The graph is not a function; it is a history. It is the accumulated, customer-specific record of which records refer to which people, with what confidence, under which labels, against which conflicts, resolved how, by whom, when. Every conflict Greg clears at Halcyon adds a labeled pair to a model that has been trained on three previous design partners' labeled pairs plus a fourteen-month history of Halcyon's own. Every threshold tweak Pillar pushes propagates forward but is tagged with a version, so historical clusters can be re-resolved against the new thresholds and compared. The graph gets more correct, monotonically, the longer the customer is on the platform. The model does not. The compounding lives in the graph.

And the compounding matters operationally, not just abstractly. Consider the question Helm wrote about in the previous essay: who is at flight risk in EMEA engineering? This is the kind of question a CHRO actually asks. To answer it, the agent has to identify the relevant cohort (EMEA engineering), enumerate the people in that cohort, score their flight risk, and return a ranked list with citations. If the resolver is wrong — if the records that look like nine people are actually four people, and the records that look like one person are actually two — every step downstream of the resolver is wrong. Cohort enumeration is wrong (you double-count the four-person group and you under-count the one-person group). The flight-risk model is wrong (it averages signals across rows that do not belong together). The ranked list is wrong. The citations are wrong. The CHRO presents the answer to the board, the answer is wrong, and we do not get a second chance.

The model gets the credit when the answer is right and the blame when the answer is wrong, but neither attribution is correct. The resolver is upstream of both. The resolver is the foundation. A flawless flight-risk model on a wrong people graph produces a wrong answer, and a mediocre flight-risk model on a correct people graph produces a defensible one. The buyer cannot tell the difference from the dashboard. The difference is real all the same.

Here is the flywheel, stated as compactly as I can. Correct resolution → correct answers → user trust → increased usage → more conflict-queue throughput → more labeled pairs → more correct resolution. Round and round. The first turn of the flywheel is the hardest, because the customer has not yet labeled anything; we ship with a model trained on synthetic data and three design-partner backfills, and the first two weeks of deployment are dominated by the customer's own conflict-queue labor. By week six, the conflict queue is down to the genuinely-hard cases, and the customer's analyst is spending less than an hour a week in it. By month six, on Mercator's data, we are seeing under fifteen minutes a week. This is what Helm means when she says we sell Tuesday afternoons back. The Tuesday afternoons come out of the resolver, before they ever reach the Copilot.

The strategic implication for me, as the engineer responsible for this layer, is that I should be willing to under-invest in the demo and over-invest in the conflict queue. I should be willing to ship slower than our competitors on the agentic surface, because the agentic surface is fungible — anybody can bolt an LLM onto a database — and the resolver, after a few years of compounding, is not. I should be willing to spend the founding year on a workflow that the customer's analyst opens every Wednesday morning, and the chief executive opens never. I think this is the right call. I admit it is also the dull call, and that there is a version of this company in which I would not have made it. I had to write a lot of bad resolvers, at Palantir and at Stripe, before I learned that the dull call is usually the right one.

VI.

What we get wrong, and what we do when we do.

I am going to be specific about our failure modes, because Helm's essay made a commitment on behalf of the company — the architecture forbids the promise — that I am responsible for honoring at the level of the code.

On our internal benchmark — a 2,000-employee synthetic dataset that I constructed in March, modeled on the Halcyon system topology, with deliberately-injected hard cases (name changes, contractor conversions, acquisition overlaps, generational naming, transliteration, terminated rehires) — the resolver currently scores 0.93 precision and 0.89 recall at the 0.65 threshold. On the Halcyon real data, on first pass, before any customer-side labeling, we scored 0.91 precision and 0.82 recall. We had to send 14% of pairs (4,213 of 30,089) to the conflict queue for human review. After two weeks of customer labeling and one threshold retune, the numbers moved to 0.94 precision and 0.87 recall, and the conflict-queue fraction dropped to 6%. We publish these numbers, in this form, on every onboarding deck. We have committed to publishing the worst-performing customer's numbers in every quarterly transparency report, redacted of customer identity, as soon as we have three customers in production. We will be honest about this and we will not be cute about it.

We have made one mistake in production that I want to describe in enough detail that the field can learn from it. The customer was Mercator. The bug was a false positive — the kind of error we explicitly optimize against. On the morning of the eighteenth of April, the resolver merged two records that referred to two different physicians who happened to share the same name (Dr. Daniel Park), the same medical specialty (internal medicine), and the same office building (both worked out of Mercator's downtown Chicago campus, though in different practice groups). They had different NPI numbers, different dates of birth, and different hire dates. The resolver should have caught the difference on NPI alone, but the NPI field was missing on one of the two records — a data-entry omission in the credentialing system from 2019 that had never been corrected. The remaining features pushed the joint score to 0.87, above the auto-merge threshold. The merge surfaced one physician's comp band to the other physician's manager, in a Copilot answer to the question "how do my direct reports' comp bands compare to the practice-group median."

We caught it within four hours, at 11:47 a.m. Pacific, because the manager who saw the answer flagged it himself — this number is not right; my Dan Park is band 4, not band 6 — through the Copilot's report-this-answer affordance, which routes to a Slack channel that on-call engineers watch. Pillar and I traced it within an hour. We pushed a revert at 1:51 p.m. that un-merged the two clusters and invalidated every downstream cached answer that touched either record. The revert was complete at 1:58 p.m. Total exposure window: six hours and eleven minutes.

We wrote a post-incident report — we publish them internally as PIRs, with a customer-redacted version sent to the affected customer's security lead — and we added what we now call the physician-dual-identity check to the eval set. The check: any candidate merge where both records have a credentialing-system origin and where either record is missing an NPI field requires either a positive DOB match or routes automatically to human review, regardless of the joint score. The check costs us, on the benchmark, about 0.4 points of recall. We will pay that price every day.

I want to say something hard here. We were lucky on this one. The Mercator manager who flagged the answer was the kind of careful reader who notices when a number is wrong; not every manager is. If the manager had not flagged it, the wrong comp band would have been in the Copilot's working memory until the next resolution pass — three hours later — at which point the new labeled-data pass would not have caught it either, because the manager had not labeled it. We have, since the incident, added a separate silent-evaluation pipeline that flags any cluster whose constituent records disagree on an exclusive-key field (NPI, license number, government ID, executive equity grant ID) at all, regardless of how confident the resolver was when it merged them. The silent-evaluation runs hourly. It would have caught the Park merge within sixty minutes, not four hours. We should have built it before the incident. We built it after. Both statements are true.

Mercator did not leave us over this. The CHRO read the PIR, asked two questions, accepted our remediation, and added a sentence to her quarterly board memo about Stratum that I have her permission to quote: "the vendor's response to the incident is itself part of the diligence; the speed and specificity of the response increased my confidence, not decreased it." I do not think she would have written that sentence if we had spun the incident. I am certain she would not have written it if we had hidden it. The cost of honesty in this industry, in the year 2026, is lower than the cost of any alternative. I am increasingly convinced this is true at every time scale.

VII.

Where this goes next.

Eventually, entity resolution will not be probabilistic plus graph-confirmed. It will be graph-native, by which I mean that every record in every source system will arrive at our ingestion boundary already carrying a cryptographic identity proof — a signed assertion, rotated on a schedule, that says this Greenhouse row refers to the same person as that Workday row, because both were signed by an identity provider that knew it. We will not need to probabilistically infer the link. The link will be present, deterministic, verifiable, and revocable. The resolver, in that world, becomes a verifier rather than an inferrer; it checks signatures and rejects records that cannot be vouched for. Confidence intervals collapse to true/false. Conflict-queue throughput drops to near zero. The People Analytics lead gets her Wednesdays back entirely.

We are building the first version of this with two design partners — Mercator and a second customer I cannot name yet — using a small cross-system identity broker that issues short- lived signed identity tokens at the moment of provisioning, and that lives, as a hosted service, alongside the customer's IdP. The first deployment of this broker — what we are internally calling the cross-record proof carrier — is scheduled for the first quarter of 2027. It will not eliminate the probabilistic resolver, because the proof carriers will only cover net-new records, and a five-year backlog of historical records will continue to require probabilistic resolution. But it changes the rate at which the graph degrades, and over a decade it changes the architecture qualitatively. I am ambivalent about how aggressively to market this — it is an engineering bet, not yet a product — but it is the direction.

Beyond that, on a longer horizon, the architecture supports an obvious extension that I want to flag and then walk away from. Once entity resolution is solved within a single customer, the next available problem is entity resolution across customers — the same person, across two employers, over a ten-year career arc. This unlocks features that are operationally appealing: alumni networks, reference workflows, passive candidate identification for recruiters. We will not build any of those without explicit, per-person, revocable consent. The default is no cross-customer linking; the user has to opt in; the consent is revocable at any time; revocation cascades through the graph and invalidates derived features in real time. The architecture supports cross-customer resolution because it would be irresponsible to build a graph-native identity layer that could not handle the consent case correctly, but the architecture supporting it is not a commitment to ship it. I want to say this on the record. Our engineering culture is the kind that builds capabilities and then asks whether to expose them, in that order. Helm, Compass, and I have agreed in writing that the cross-customer surface will not ship without a board-level review and a customer-side DPA addendum. I do not expect that review to happen before 2028. It may not happen at all.

I will close with a small confession. When Helm called me in March and asked whether I wanted to spend the next five to seven years of my professional life on the entity-resolution layer of an enterprise HCM platform, I told her I needed twenty-four hours to think about it. I called her back in eleven. The reason is that I had spent the previous seven years at Stripe watching what happens when you get the underlying graph right at a payments company — the way every product team downstream of that graph moves faster, the way every regulatory question becomes answerable instead of approximately-answerable, the way the company compounds in a direction that competitors cannot easily intercept. The people graph in HR is the same shape of problem as the merchant graph in payments. The hardness is different in detail. The hardness in kind is the same. I have written eleven good resolvers and approximately forty bad ones, and I would like, this time, to write the one that compounds for a decade. That is the work. That is, more than anything, what I am here to do.
