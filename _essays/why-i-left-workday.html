By Helm

Why I left Workday to build the system of truth above the system of record.

A letter to my former colleagues, my new ones, and the CHROs who keep asking me the same question.

By Helm·T+8d·16-minute read·Published in the Stratum Essays series
First in a series.   The next essay will be by Forge, on entity resolution.
I.

Five Mondays a year.

For the last four years at Workday I had a recurring meeting that, by the end, I could have run from memory. Five Mondays a year, more or less — sometimes four, once seven — I flew to a customer city, took a car from the airport to a corporate campus, signed in at a security desk that did not know my name, and spent four hours in a glass-walled conference room briefing a Chief Human Resources Officer who was about to roll out a configuration my team had shipped. The CHROs were never the same person twice. The room was, with small variations, the same room. The slide deck was, with smaller variations, the same deck. And the meeting always ended, eventually, with the same question.

The question was usually asked in the last fifteen minutes, after the CHRO had walked her people through the rollout plan and the risk register and the change-management timeline; after we had agreed on who would own training; after she had thanked me politely and the room had begun to thin. She would look at me — sometimes across the table, sometimes from the doorway with her coat already on — and she would say, in some variation: I love this. Can you make it move faster?

She did not mean the software. She meant the company. She meant: can you make the next configuration take six weeks instead of six months; can the data model accept the new comp band I am proposing without another sprint of professional services; can the report I will need for the audit committee in November exist by October without me paying a consultant another forty thousand dollars; can the contractors who run my distribution centers — there are now more of them than full-time employees in three of my regions — appear in the same headcount view as everybody else, in a way I can defend on the audit. Can the tool I bought to manage my workforce keep up with the workforce I actually have.

I gave the same answer for four years. I would try. The team would try. We had a roadmap; some of what she was asking for was on the next release; some of it was further out; some of it would require an architectural change that I could not, sitting in that conference room, promise her. I gave this answer in San Diego, in Charlotte, in Stamford, in Toronto, in Frankfurt, in Singapore. I gave it to the head of people at a $14B insurer who had been at her company for nineteen years and who, six weeks after I gave it, retired. I gave it to a younger CHRO at a software company in Austin whose Slack-name was an emoji and who, in the meeting, was visibly tired in the way that people running large functions become tired around their fourth year. I gave it to a woman in Düsseldorf who interrupted me, mid- answer, to say in clear English: I have been listening to the same answer from your company since 2014.

I will tell you what I now think she was asking. She was not asking for a software upgrade. She was asking whether anyone in my industry had noticed that the world her people lived in — the world of dotted lines and fractional roles and contractors and agents and matrix teams — had begun, sometime around 2019, to leave the world that our data model could describe. She was asking whether anyone had noticed that her job had changed underneath her, and whether the platform she had bought to do that job was going to change with it, or whether she was going to have to.

I want to be careful here. I am not writing this essay to be cruel about a company that taught me almost everything I know about building enterprise software, and where I worked for people whom I still call when I am stuck. Workday is, by any reasonable measure, one of the best-built pieces of business software of the last twenty years. The architecture is consistent. The team is serious. The product moves, in production, against payroll, in two hundred and forty countries, on Tuesday afternoons at scale, and very rarely breaks. I am writing this because the question the CHRO was asking me, five Mondays a year, was not a question I could answer from inside the architecture I had inherited. And I had begun to suspect, around the third Monday of 2025, that no one inside that architecture could.

I left in March. I called Forge, who had spent seven years moving money through Stripe's payments infrastructure, and asked him whether he would have a long conversation with me. He said yes, provided I bring Compass, whom we had both known for years and whose career had been spent inside the buyer we were going to be selling to. We sat in a room together for forty-one hours, between the sixth and the ninth of May. On the ninth we incorporated. This essay is, in some ways, what we talked about in that room. It is also the answer I wish I had been able to give the woman in Düsseldorf.

II.

The org isn't a tree.

In 2008, when Workday's core data model was designed, the organization was a tree. Every employee had one manager, one location, one cost center, one job family, one comp band. Reporting relationships ran upward to a single chief executive in a clean hierarchy that could be drawn on a single piece of paper and that could, with reasonable confidence, be reconciled against the same structure in a payroll system. The data model was an honest description of the organization. The org chart and the data model were, more or less, the same object.

By 2026 this is no longer true, and the gap is no longer small. Inside a 5,000-person enterprise today, an employee may have a solid- line manager and a dotted-line manager and a third manager for the cross-functional team she spends sixty percent of her week on. A single role may be split between two people, each at fifty percent, one of whom is a contractor on a 1099 paid through a vendor of record in a different country. A director-level individual contributor may report into a vice president who reports into a different director, because the title and the reporting line have been decoupled on purpose. A workflow that two years ago belonged to a Senior Analyst named Rebecca may now be owned by an internally-deployed AI agent that was provisioned by IT, charged to Rebecca's cost center, and reviewed quarterly by her manager. None of this is exotic. All of it is happening right now, inside companies that are paying $1.4M a year for an HCM system that cannot describe most of it.

The org is no longer a tree. It is a graph — a graph in the mathematical sense, with nodes that are people and software agents and vendors and projects, and edges that are reporting lines and working relationships and contractual obligations and budget authorities, and properties on those edges that say how much, for how long, under what authority, visible to whom. A graph cannot be flattened into a tree without losing information, and the information you lose is precisely the information the CHRO most needs to do her job. When she asks how many people work on the LATAM growth team, she is asking a graph question. When she asks what is the true cost of the data- platform org if you include the contractors and the AI seats, she is asking a graph question. When she asks which of my employees moved from a contractor relationship to a full-time relationship in the last year and where did they land in the comp band, she is asking a graph question that her current platform will answer wrong, or not at all.

Pillar, our first engineer outside the three founders, ran into this on her literal first week. We had given her, as an onboarding project, a small dataset from one of our design partners — a clean one, we thought, just to let her exercise the connector framework she would soon be building. Within four days she had surfaced a problem we had not anticipated. The data contained eleven people who, across a fourteen-month window, had moved from a contractor arrangement to a full-time employment arrangement at the same company. In the source-of-record system, these eleven people existed as twenty-two distinct entities — eleven contractor records and eleven employee records — with overlapping but non-identical names, different employee IDs, different start dates, and no foreign key anywhere in the schema connecting the before to the after. The company's headcount report counted the employee record; its spend report counted the contractor record; nobody had ever, on a single page, reconciled the two. The CHRO did not know that eleven of her people had become twenty-two of her people on paper. Neither did anyone else.

That is a graph problem dressed up as a data-hygiene problem. It is not solvable by a better report. It is not solvable by a better UI. It is solvable, eventually, by a data model that admits up front that a person is one node and not eleven different rows, that a role can change shape over time without changing identity, that the edges between a person and a company can be of multiple kinds at once. The model has to be graph-native, because the world is graph-native. Stratum's bet is that every successful HCM company of the next decade will accept this, build for it, and that the companies that do not will, over the same decade, slowly become invisible to their customers — not because their software broke, but because the world walked past the model the software is written in. The data model is upstream of the user experience, and the user experience is upstream of the contract renewal, and the contract renewal is upstream of the stock price. The architecture is the company.

III.

The Tuesday afternoon argument.

I want to pivot, here, from the architectural argument to the buyer, because if you have been a CHRO reading this essay so far you may have nodded politely at the graph point and waited for me to get to what you actually came here for, which is whether Stratum will give you back the next eight hours of your week. So let me get to it.

The CHROs I have talked to in the last six months — and the number is now north of ninety, by Compass's count, in companies ranging from eight hundred to twenty-eight thousand employees, in seven countries — do not, in private and at the second drink, describe themselves as having a software problem. They describe themselves as having a time problem. The number Compass likes to quote, because we have heard it now from enough of them that we believe it, is that seventy percent of a senior people leader's week is spent reconciling, formatting, and re-cutting reports that nobody, including the person who asked for them, actually reads. The other thirty percent — the part that contains the leadership conversations, the talent calibration, the compensation decisions, the work she was hired to do — is what is left over after the reconciliation has finished. The reconciliation almost never finishes.

The promise of AI in our industry, as it is currently being marketed, is approximately: we will make your software prettier. A chat box on the corner of a dashboard. A summarization of a performance review. A suggested calendar invite. The promise is, on its face, not wrong. The work is real, and the demos are competent, and the press cycle will reward it. But it is the wrong promise, because it does not address the time problem. The time problem is not that the dashboard is ugly. The time problem is that the dashboard requires four hours of human assembly before it can be shown, and the assembly is performed by a senior analyst whose training is wasted on it, and the answer that emerges is eighty percent confident at best, and by the time it is delivered, the question has moved.

The right promise is not we will make your software prettier. The right promise is: we will give you back your Tuesday afternoons.

I am repeating a line that has now been said inside our company more times than is strictly dignified, but it earned its repetition on the fourth of June, over dinner at Aneel Bhusri's house in Atherton. Aneel, who co-founded Workday and who, with a generosity I am still surprised by, became one of our first angels, had invited six CHROs to a long table for an evening that was, on its face, a small dinner and, in fact, an unannounced advisory board. The six of them ranged from a chief people officer at a publicly-traded healthcare company to the head of HR at a sub-2,000-person manufacturing firm in the Midwest. The conversation moved, as these dinners do, through the polite preliminaries and then, somewhere around the second course, began to be honest.

Annika Whitfield, the CHRO at Cordova Manufacturing — a 1,600-person industrial firm headquartered in Cleveland, who would later become our fifth signed design partner — said, into a moment of quiet that followed someone else's complaint about a long-running implementation project: the time I get back is not free; it is reallocated to better questions. Aneel, who is a careful listener, wrote that sentence down on the back of his menu. So did I. I have it still. I think it is the most precise account of the value of what we are building that I have heard from a buyer, and it is more precise than anything we have written about ourselves.

What Annika meant, and what she elaborated for the rest of the table over the next twenty minutes, is that the hours she would get back if her platform could answer the board's questions defensibly in eleven minutes — that this is the number Mercator's CHRO has begun to see — would not vanish into the calendar. They would be reallocated. They would be spent on the leadership bench review she had been postponing for two quarters. On the compensation philosophy conversation with the CEO that needed four uninterrupted hours and had been getting forty interrupted minutes. On reading the engagement survey responses, in full, herself, instead of reading the summary written by a consultant. The reallocation, she said, was the point. The hours were not the prize. The questions she would now have time to ask were the prize.

I would like to tell you that this is what we sell. It is not, yet, what we sell — we are too early; the product is in private beta with five design partners; the eval set is published but the platform itself is not generally available; I am writing this in May. But it is what we are trying to build. And it is the only promise about AI in our industry that I have heard a CHRO repeat unprompted, with her own example, to other CHROs, at a dinner where she did not know she was being quoted.

The org is no longer a tree. It is a graph — and the data model is upstream of the user experience, which is upstream of the contract renewal, which is upstream of the stock price. The architecture is the company.

Helm · Co-founder & CEO · Stratum, Inc.
IV.

Why "system of truth" and not "system of record."

Most of the HCM startups that have been pitched to me — and a surprising number have, in the eleven weeks since we incorporated, sent me their decks — are trying to build a better Workday. They are trying to displace the system of record. They will lose. I want to explain, plainly, why we think that is the wrong fight, and what we are doing instead.

The system of record is, in most enterprises, fine. It works. It has been deployed for three to seven years, it has consumed eighteen months of implementation, it has trained the workforce, it handles payroll, it runs the year-end process, it survives audits, and it is not going to be ripped out. The CHRO does not want to rip it out, the CIO does not want to rip it out, the CFO has the implementation cost on a depreciation schedule, and the procurement team is in year three of a seven-year contract. Displacement is not the buyer's request. A startup whose theory of the case is we will replace the system of record is selling against the strongest interest in the room, which is the buyer's interest in not having to do an eighteen-month implementation again.

The system of record is not broken. The problem is not that the record is wrong. The problem is that no one trusts the record, because the record is fragmented across nine systems. In a representative 2,000-person company, the answer to a single board question — what is our true cost of headcount in LATAM, including contractors and rehires — lives in Workday, in ADP, in a regional payroll vendor, in a time-and-attendance system bought by a previous COO, in an engagement survey platform, in two spreadsheets maintained by analysts, in a Slack channel where finance and HR argue about taxonomy, and in the head of one senior analyst named Greg who has been at the company for nine years. The record is not wrong. The record is scattered. The system of record cannot make itself a system of truth, because the truth lives between the records.

Stratum's wager is this. We are going to build the layer above. We are going to ingest, in read-only mode, from the nine systems the enterprise already runs. We are going to resolve identities — the same person, the same role, the same cost center — into a graph that is kept consistent on a schedule the customer sets. We are going to provide an agentic interface on top of that graph that can answer the questions the CHRO is actually being asked, with citations back to the source rows, with methodology disclosed, with cohort sizes visible, with confidence intervals where they matter. The system of record stays where it is. The system of truth is the layer above, and the layer above is where the intelligence lives.

Then, selectively, for the workflows where intelligence is the moat — not for headcount entry, not for benefits enrollment, not for any of the workflows where the system of record is mature and correct, but for comp review, succession planning, headcount allocation, the narrow set of decisions that an enterprise makes a few times a year and gets wrong more often than it admits — we will write back. We will write back through documented APIs, with the customer's governance team in the loop, with a clean audit trail, in the direction of the systems that exist, not against them. We will not compete with the record. We will compose on it.

Forge, who is the architect of this, has a phrase he likes to use with engineers who interview with us: the moat is not the database. The moat is the graph that sits between the databases. Entity resolution across nine systems, at the scale of a 5,000- person enterprise with five years of history, is a hard problem. It is harder than it looks. It is hardest at the edges — the rehires, the contractor-to-employee transitions, the acquisitions, the name changes, the regional payroll quirks — and the edges are exactly where the CHRO's questions land. Whoever builds the graph correctly owns the layer. The layer is the company. I will let Forge write the next essay about how he intends to build it; he has reserved the right to be specific about the algorithm.

A quick aside on language. System of truth is not a phrase I love. It is grandiose; it has been used by data-warehouse vendors for fifteen years to mean something else; it invites the obvious retort that no system is the system of truth, that truth in an organization is contested, social, often political. All of that is fair. We use the phrase anyway, because the alternative — system of intelligence, which our investors at one point suggested — misstates the wager. The wager is not that the layer is smart. The wager is that the layer is correct. The layer is the place where, for the first time, the headcount number and the spend number and the attrition number can be reconciled against each other on a single page, defensibly, in a way the CHRO can take to the board without asking Greg to stay past midnight. That is what we mean by truth. A small, contested, useful word.

V.

The case for being honest.

I am going to write this section in a more personal voice, because what I want to say in it cannot quite be said in the architectural register of the previous sections, and because I am, in the end, asking you to trust a company that has eleven people in it and will need, over the next five years, to earn that trust against a long list of incumbents who are very good at sounding trustworthy.

Inside Stratum we have a phrase that I did not invent — Compass wrote it on the third whiteboard of the founding session and we have never taken it down — which is: the architecture forbids the promise. What she meant is that we have written the platform such that we cannot, even if we wanted to, tell a customer something the system does not know. Every answer in the Console returns with citation chips back to the source rows. Every methodology is named. Every cohort size is shown. The eval set is published; the failure modes are documented; the platform itself refuses to render an answer below a configurable confidence threshold. These are not marketing decisions. They are architectural decisions that have forced us, repeatedly, to ship slower than we would otherwise have shipped, and which I have come to believe are the difference between a company that builds enterprise AI and a company that gets sued for it.

I will tell you the moment this became real for me. On the twenty-eighth of April — eleven days before we incorporated, in the slow-motion period when Forge and I were already working but the legal entity did not yet exist — I had a second conversation with Carlos Reyes, who is the chief people officer at a 4,200-person hospital system called Halcyon Health, headquartered in Phoenix. Carlos is a careful buyer. He has been at Halcyon for six years; he came up through clinical operations; he reports directly to the CEO; he has a board that asks him hard questions and that will sit, in September of this year, in front of an evidence pack about physician-pay-equity that his finance team is currently building. I had been hoping he would become our second design partner.

In the second call I demoed a synthetic dataset. The synthetic data was meant to look like a hospital system; it had been generated by Forge two weekends prior, after we realized that I could not, in good conscience, demo on a real customer's data even with masking. The dataset contained, among ten thousand rows, a deliberate edge case Forge had planted to test the entity-resolution layer: Dr. Maria Hernandez, MD, employed in the cardiology department, and Dr. Maria Hernández, M.D., employed in the same department three months later under a separate record after a payroll-system migration. Two records, one diacritic, one person. Forge's resolver should have merged them. The version I was demoing, which was the version we had agreed to ship to design partners, did merge them. It also, in merging them, surfaced a comp band that was wrong — a hallucination, more or less, from an upstream join that had picked the wrong row.

I caught it on the screen. So did Carlos. I had a choice in that moment that I will not pretend I made gracefully. I could have moved on, said the demo was synthetic, suggested we look at a different query. The error was, after all, in a fake dataset designed to test exactly this kind of failure mode. Instead I stopped the demo. I said, on the call, with Forge muted on his end and visibly tense: Carlos, that comp band is wrong. The resolver did the right thing on the merge and the wrong thing on the band. I am going to send you, after this call, a one-pager that explains exactly what happened, why it happened, what we are doing about it, and what our eval set will and will not catch. I sent the one-pager that evening. It was three pages.

Carlos did not sign that day. He did not sign that week. The Halcyon partnership, which I had been planning a press release around in my head, was downgraded to a sixty-day evaluation, then paused while Carlos changed jobs internally and a new clinical- operations head came in. I assumed, for most of June, that we had lost the deal.

In the last week of June, Carlos wrote me an email I have not forgotten. It was eleven lines long. The seventh line said: I trust the company that tells me what went wrong. I have spent eighteen years in this industry and the vendors who tell you what went wrong are not the vendors who win the room on the first call. They are the vendors who are still in business in ten years. Let us restart the conversation. We are now in the early stages of that restart, with a new sponsor and a redrawn scope, and Carlos will speak for himself when he is ready. The published Halcyon case study, when it appears, will be his story to tell, not mine.

I am telling this story here because it has become, inside our company, a kind of small founding myth — the moment that I could have shaded the truth to protect a deal and did not, and the deal came back six weeks later, larger and on better terms. I want to be honest about why I did not shade it. I did not do it because I am virtuous. I did it because I am scared. I am scared of building a company that ships an enterprise AI product to a 4,200-person hospital and gets the comp band wrong on a real physician in a real pay-equity audit and gets sued, deservedly, and ends an industry's willingness to take a chance on the next AI-native HCM company for a decade. The architecture forbids the promise because the founders are afraid of the alternative. The fear is, I have come to believe, a feature of the company. We will need to keep it.

VI.

Why I left Workday.

I have so far been writing around the question in the title of this essay. Let me answer it directly. There are three reasons, and they are in order of how comfortable I am saying them out loud.

  1. Because the org I was shipping for was not the org I was reading about. Every Monday-morning newsletter on the future of work was describing a workforce of mixed employment models, agent-augmented roles, fluid team composition, and project-based reporting. Every Tuesday I was shipping into a data model that presumed a single manager, a single location, a single role, and a single comp band. Workday is not blind to this. The team is working on it. But the core data model is fifteen years old and its assumptions have compounded; you cannot retrofit a graph into a tree without rewriting the tree, and the tree is what is in production at seventy percent of the Fortune 500. The architecture I was shipping into could be extended. It could not, I came to believe, be rebuilt from inside.
  2. Because the customers I was talking to no longer needed faster reports — they needed a different question answered. The question that the CHRO is being asked by her board in 2026 is not the question her platform was designed to answer in 2008. The question has changed shape. It has become a question about cohorts that do not match the org chart, about costs that do not map to a single cost center, about people whose status is more than one thing at once. The platform answers an adjacent question very well. It cannot, architecturally, answer the question being asked. This is the AI-native opportunity, and Workday is constrained from fully addressing it not by talent — the team is excellent — but by the cost of breaking a data model that runs payroll for a third of the global economy. Someone outside that constraint will build it. I would rather it be us than someone who is going to be careless with the product.
  3. Because I wanted to find out if I could build a company that practiced the honesty I was admiring from a distance. This is the personal one and I am not going to oversell it. I had been reading, for the better part of two years, founders' essays and engineering blogs from companies — Stripe, Anthropic, Linear, a few smaller ones — that wrote about their products and their mistakes with a specificity that the marketing pages of enterprise software did not, in my experience, ever match. I wanted to find out whether the gap between how those companies wrote about themselves and how my industry wrote about itself was a difference in culture or a difference in courage. I did not know. I still do not know. What I knew, by the middle of last year, was that I was not going to find out from inside.

I did not leave angry. I did not leave because of a person or a project or a missed promotion. I left for a category of reasons that sound, said out loud, suspiciously close to the kinds of reasons every founder claims, which is part of why I am writing them down here and not in a Substack. If, in three years, this essay is being cited against me as evidence of something I did not do, the receipts will be public.

VII.

What I am asking of you.

This essay is the first in a series, and series come with asks. Mine are different depending on which audience you are reading from, so I will be specific.

  • To my former Workday colleagues

    I am not building against you. I am building above you. The system of record is not the problem; the layer is the problem, and the layer is what we are building. If a customer of yours ever asks you a question the platform cannot answer — a graph question, a cohort question, a question about contractors or agents or the cost of a team that crosses three cost centers — send them to me, and we will integrate, not displace. I will read every introduction email. Nothing in this essay is intended to make your job harder. Several things in it are intended to make it easier.

    maya@stratum.ai →
  • To the CHROs reading

    Open the live Console at stratum.ai/console and ask it your hardest question. It will refuse some of them, which is how it is supposed to behave. It will answer others, with citations and methodology, and you can decide for yourself whether the answer is defensible. If it gets one wrong — if it surfaces a number you cannot defend, if it cites a regulation that is not the right regulation, if it hallucinates a comp band the way it did to Carlos — send me what it said, and what you think it should have said. I will read it personally. I owe the field the same honesty Carlos gave me.

    Open the live console →
  • To engineers thinking about joining

    We are hiring our eleventh employee this week. We are slow on purpose. Every engineering hire reads the eval set and the architecture brief before the second interview; every hire meets all three founders before an offer; we have walked away from five offers in the last two months because the conversation about failure modes was not honest enough. If you are the kind of engineer who reads Forge's commit messages on a Saturday and finds them more interesting than your current codebase, we will probably get along. The role descriptions are long because we mean them.

    See open roles →
  • To anyone who wants the long version

    The architecture, the team, the financials, the customer notes, the engineering blog — they live in the library. I publish a Chairman's Briefing every fortnight. It is written for an audience of one — our board chair — but we make it public because the discipline of writing for him is the discipline of being honest with everybody. If you want to know what we are doing this fortnight, that is where to look.

    The library →

I left a company I loved to build a company I have only known for eleven weeks. I cannot tell you, on a Wednesday morning in May, that I will succeed. The base rates are not in my favor; the incumbents are real; the architecture problem is harder than this essay has made it sound, and the honesty problem is harder than the architecture problem. What I can tell you is that I now know, with a clarity I did not have last September, what I am trying to do and why I think it is worth doing. The org is no longer a tree. The system of record is not the moat. The CHRO does not need a better report; she needs an architecture that admits the workforce she actually has and the time she actually does not have. We are going to try to build that. If you have read this far, you are part of the small group of people whose attention this essay is in service of, and I am, finally, grateful for it.

The next essay in this series will be Forge's, on entity resolution — the technical problem at the center of the company. Mine was the framing essay. His will be the harder one.
