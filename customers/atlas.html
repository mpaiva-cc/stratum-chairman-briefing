I.

The Wired moment.

Maya Holman was reading the Wired piece about Stratum on a Tuesday morning — the third of February 2026, by her own diary; T+102d in our internal calendar — when the text from her father arrived. The father, in this case, is Reuben Holman, the chief executive officer of Atlas Logistics and the second-generation operator of the company her grandfather founded in 1972. The text was, as she described it to me in our first call, characteristic of Reuben: a single URL to the Wired article, the word interesting, no punctuation. He had sent the same article to seven other people that morning, four of them family members and three of them members of his board. Maya was the only one of the eight who replied within the hour.

She read the piece twice. The first time as a CHRO, looking for what the company sold. The second time as the woman who had, eighteen months earlier, been promoted to chief human-resources officer of a 1,800-employee ocean freight forwarding company whose ATS was a custom-built application that her CIO had written by himself, in eleven weeks, in 2014, and whose compliance reports took, on average, three days to assemble by hand from spreadsheets the operations managers maintained on shared drives in twenty-two US ports and fourteen international hubs. The second reading did the work. She closed the laptop, walked the four steps from her standing desk to the window of her office on the eleventh floor of the building on Mulberry Street in Newark, watched the cargo cranes at Port Newark for what she later told me was perhaps two minutes, and walked back to the laptop. She opened our trust center. She read the sub-processor list. She opened the contact form. She typed eighty-nine words.

The eighty-nine words said this. I am the CHRO of Atlas Logistics. We are an ocean freight forwarder with 1,800 employees, headquartered in Newark, family-owned, third generation. We have been running on a custom-built ATS our CIO wrote in 2014. It works, but it cannot answer the questions my father — our CEO — keeps asking me about hiring compliance, and I have run out of patience trying to make it. My father read your Wired piece this morning. So did I. I would like to talk. I am not in a hurry on the calendar; I am in a hurry on the problem. She submitted the form at ten-twenty-seven Eastern. She did not, at the time of submission, expect a response that day. The response, from my inbox, arrived at twelve-fourteen Pacific — three hours and forty-seven minutes after she had pressed send, which is to say roughly ninety minutes after I had read her note for the first time and forty-five minutes after I had finished reading it for the fourth. I sent her two sentences. The first proposed a call the following morning. The second asked whether her father wanted to be on it.

Her reply, eleven minutes later, said: Tomorrow morning is good. My father does not want to be on it. He wants me to be the one who decides this. He also wants me to be the one who tells our CIO, which is going to be the hard conversation. We will get to that.

II.

Atlas, in a sentence.

The sentence is roughly: a third-generation, family-owned ocean freight forwarder with 1,800 employees, founded in 1972 by Avram Holman, run since 2008 by his son Reuben, and operating out of twenty-two US ports and fourteen international hubs, including six in Asia and four in Northern Europe. The longer version is the version that matters here.

Avram Holman started Atlas in a one-room office on Raymond Boulevard in Newark with a partner named Saul Pessin and a single client — a textile importer that needed a customs broker who would answer the phone after six. Saul left in 1979 to start an insurance firm of his own and Avram bought him out for eleven thousand dollars. By 1990 Atlas had thirty-eight employees, three port offices on the East Coast, and a reputation among Newark's mid-market import community for filing customs entries faster than the larger forwarders. Reuben joined the company in 1991 after a stint at Maersk. Avram retired in 2008, at the depth of the financial crisis; Reuben became CEO that October, six weeks after Lehman. He survived 2009 by laying off no one, by deferring his own salary for four quarters, and by quietly extending credit to the three customers he could not stand to lose. Two of the three are still on the customer roster in 2026.

Atlas grew steadily through the 2010s, expanded into West Coast ports in 2014, opened Hong Kong in 2016, and crossed the thousand-employee mark in 2019. Then came the pandemic. Atlas's revenue, like the revenue of every ocean freight forwarder on the planet, was driven by container rates; container rates, for reasons no one inside Atlas takes credit for, ran four to seven times their pre-pandemic levels between 2020 and 2022. Atlas grew thirty percent from 2020 to 2024, opened seven new US port offices and six new international hubs in that window, and ended 2024 with 1,800 employees. The growth was not, by Reuben's own description, entirely deserved; it was a function of being in the right industry in the right years. He has been careful since not to confuse the two.

The hiring problem at Atlas is the hiring problem of every maritime company. The roles that matter — licensed customs brokers, ocean freight forwarders, port operations leads, compliance officers under the various export-control regimes that govern dual-use cargo, terminal coordinators — have ramp- up times that run between nine and eighteen months. A wrongly hired customs broker costs Atlas roughly four hundred and twenty thousand dollars in the first year, by Maya's estimate, between training expense, the operational mistakes that typically attend the first three quarters of a misfit hire, and the regulatory exposure that attends a customs broker who is not yet fluent in the Harmonised Tariff Schedule. Atlas makes roughly two hundred hires a year. The average time-to- fill, when Maya took the chair, was forty-seven days. The compliance reporting required by the Federal Maritime Commission, by US Customs and Border Protection, and by the seven international jurisdictions in which Atlas has employees, was — and on the day Maya emailed us, still was — assembled by hand, every quarter, by Maya's chief of staff and two analysts, over a window that ran between sixty and seventy working hours. The board had been asking, since Maya started, why this took so long. Maya had been telling them the truth: because the ATS does not do it.

Maya is thirty-eight. She is the third generation in the chair — her grandfather founded the company; her father runs it; she may, in some future the family does not discuss in detail, run it next. She joined Atlas in 2019 after seven years at McKinsey, where she had been a senior consultant in the operations practice and where she had, by her own description, learned to think about supply chains. She ran HR business partnering for Atlas's commercial division for four years, was named CHRO in August 2024 at the age of thirty-six, and has — eighteen months into the role — already done two things her predecessor did not. She instituted a quarterly people review at the executive level. She started asking why the ATS was the only system at Atlas that did not have a vendor relationship behind it.

III.

The CIO who wrote the ATS.

Jack Vermeer was hired by Avram Holman in 1992 to run what was then called, with no irony at all, the computer. He was Atlas's fourteenth employee. He had no degree. He had, instead, eight years of self-taught software work as a contract developer for two Newark freight companies and a customs broker in Elizabeth. Avram hired him on a Friday afternoon after a ninety-minute conversation that, by Jack's account, contained only two technical questions and one personal one. The personal question was: are you going to stay. Jack said yes. He has been at Atlas, in some form, for thirty-four years. He is now, by long custom and by the unanimous reading of the executive team, the most senior engineer in the building. There is no second-most-senior engineer; there is just Jack, and the eleven analysts and one junior developer who report to him.

Jack wrote Atlas's ATS in the spring and summer of 2014. He had spent the previous eighteen months evaluating the ATSes on the market — Taleo, iCIMS, Greenhouse, Jobvite, the early version of Workday Recruiting — and had come away from the evaluation with the same conclusion every IT director of a 600-person family-owned forwarder had come away with in 2014: that the products on offer were either priced for the Fortune 500 or designed for high-volume retail and that none of them understood what it meant to hire a licensed customs broker. So he wrote his own. He used PHP and PostgreSQL, because that is what he knew. He shipped the first version in eleven weeks. Reuben approved the project on a single page of yellow legal pad on which Jack had written four lines: features we need, features we don't, cost, time. The cost line said my time. The time line said eleven weeks. Reuben signed the page and tacked it to a corkboard in his office, where it still hangs.

Jack's ATS has done its job for twelve years. It tracks requisitions, candidates, interview stages, offers, and onboarding handoffs. It integrates with Atlas's HRIS, which is also Jack's — he wrote that one in 2017, in a year and a half, on the back end of a Workday evaluation that had returned a six-figure annual quote — and with the payroll system, which is Paychex and which Jack did not write. The ATS does not produce compliance reports. It does not, in its current form, support the data-residency requirements Atlas now needs to honour for the four EU hubs. It does not have an API; if you want data out of it, you ask Jack, and Jack writes you a SQL query, and he has, over the years, written several thousand of these. Maya's chief of staff has, on her hard drive, a folder named Jack's queries that contains four hundred and twelve files. The folder is the de-facto compliance reporting pipeline.

I will be specific about Jack's craft because the rest of this piece does not work if Jack is reduced to a stock character. The software Jack wrote is small. It is roughly thirty-one thousand lines of PHP, plus a schema of forty-seven tables and a small library of stored procedures. It runs on two virtualised servers in Atlas's Newark data centre, with a hot standby in Edison. It has had — by Jack's own log — eleven bugs serious enough to require a hotfix in twelve years. It is faster on Atlas's old hardware than any commercial ATS Maya has benchmarked against it. It has a single feature she has never seen elsewhere, which is a free-text field on every requisition called Reuben's note, populated only by the CEO, visible to the hiring manager and the recruiter and nobody else, used roughly forty times a year. The field exists because Reuben asked for it in 2015. Jack added it in an hour. No commercial ATS that Maya has evaluated would add it without a six-month roadmap process. That detail matters. We will come back to it.

IV.

The first call.

The first call was on T+105d — a Friday morning at ten o'clock Eastern, which is seven o'clock Pacific, which is to say not the hour I would have proposed had it been my choice. Maya proposed it. Maya was, on her side, in the conference room next to her office in Newark, alone. The camera framed a window behind her through which the East Coast morning sky was visible and through which, for the first eighteen minutes of the call, a series of Newark airliners climbed at three-second intervals on their way out of EWR. The audio was good. The lighting was good. Maya had a notebook open beside her keyboard and the Wired piece printed out, with passages underlined in pencil, on the table.

I was on the call with Forge. We had agreed, in the forty minutes between Maya's reply email and the start of the call, that the call would be more technical than the typical first conversation, because Maya was bringing — implicitly — a replacement question rather than an augmentation question. The typical first call with a CHRO is about what Stratum can add to a stack she already runs. Maya's call was about what Stratum could replace in a stack she had effectively built. The two conversations have a different shape, and we wanted to be ready for the second.

Maya opened by describing Atlas's hiring data in numbers, fast, from memory. Eighteen hundred employees. Two hundred hires a year on average — closer to two-forty in growth quarters, closer to one-sixty in flat ones. Time-to-fill of forty-seven days against an industry benchmark she put at thirty-eight. Cost-per-hire of four thousand two hundred dollars against a benchmark of three thousand. Quarterly compliance reporting across seven jurisdictions assembled by hand in sixty-to-seventy hours by three people. Zero defensible answer to the question Reuben had asked her, in writing, in October — is our hiring meeting our compliance requirements — without three days of work. She gave us these numbers in about four minutes. Forge wrote them down. I wrote them down. Neither of us interrupted.

When she finished, Forge asked exactly one question. It is the question, on review, that defined the conversation. He said: does Jack know we're having this conversation. Maya paused for what the recording measures at four seconds. She said: not yet. She added, after another two seconds: I have to tell him before this goes any further. I am not going to procure a replacement for his software behind his back. That is not how Atlas works and it is not how I work. How would you like me to tell him.

Forge gave her the answer we had agreed on, the night before, in a six-message Slack exchange that Helm had read and approved. He said: tell him exactly what you told us. Tell him the ATS he wrote has carried Atlas for twelve years. Tell him the company has outgrown what it can do, not what he can do. Tell him that we would like to spend a session with him — with you in the room or out of it, his choice — going through every feature of his ATS and showing him what we cover, what we don't, and what we will build to close the gap. Tell him that we will sign nothing — and we mean nothing — until he has said yes to it. Maya wrote that down word for word. She has since told me she read it back to Jack, almost verbatim, the following Monday.

The remaining fifty minutes of the call were not about Jack. They were about what Atlas needed Stratum to do on day one assuming Jack said yes. Maya named four things, in order. First: a defensible compliance answer in under an hour, on demand, in a format her board would accept. Second: a single picture of ramp-up performance across the fourteen specialised maritime roles, with attrition correlations. Third: pay-equity reporting across the seven jurisdictions, in the currencies and under the definitions each jurisdiction required. Fourth — and she said this last because she said she was embarrassed to say it at all — a way of finding the people inside Atlas who were ready, by data and not by impression, to move into the licensed-broker track from the unlicensed support roles, because Atlas had been hiring brokers externally for eight years and had not, in that window, promoted a single one from the inside. I told her the console would do the first three on day fifteen of a deployment and the fourth on day thirty. Forge nodded, on camera, to confirm. Maya wrote the numbers down. The call ended at eleven-oh-four Eastern, six minutes long.

V.

The conversation with Jack.

Maya scheduled her conversation with Jack for the following Monday morning — T+108d — and she scheduled it the way she schedules conversations she does not want to have: early, private, on a Monday, and in his office rather than hers. Jack's office is on the second floor of the Mulberry Street building, down a corridor lined with photographs of Atlas's port openings, and contains the desk Jack has used since 1994, two monitors, one server, a framed printout of his first commit message to the ATS dated the fourth of May 2014, and a fern that — by his own admission — has not been watered in the present decade. Maya knocked, sat down, and gave him the speech Forge had drafted, slightly modified for her own voice. She told him she had been talking to a company called Stratum. She told him what Stratum did. She told him she thought Atlas needed it. She told him the ATS he wrote had carried Atlas for twelve years and she meant that as a statement of admiration rather than as a preamble to dismissal. She told him she was not going to procure anything without his approval, full stop, and she meant that. She asked him to spend an hour with the Stratum team walking through every feature of his ATS and seeing whether what they had covered the gap. Jack listened for the full eight minutes she spoke. He did not interrupt. When she was done, he asked one question. He said: are you saying my software is obsolete. Maya, who had rehearsed her answer to this question for the entirety of the previous weekend, gave him the answer she had rehearsed. She said: I am saying we have outgrown it. You wrote a great tool for two hundred people in 2014. We are eighteen hundred people in 2026 and the compliance world has changed. The tool has not failed. We have grown past it. That is the kind of problem I want to have.

Jack thought for two days. Maya did not ask him for a status update in those two days, which was — she told me later — the hardest thing she has done as a CHRO. On Friday morning, the fourteenth of February, T+113d, Jack walked into Maya's office, sat down without an appointment, and said the sentence that this case study is named after. He said: If they can do what you say they can do, I will help you replace my software. Don't make me look stupid. Maya thanked him. She did not cry. She did, she has told me, sit very still for a minute after he left the room. She then walked down the corridor to her chief of staff's office and asked her to clear two hours on her calendar the following Wednesday for what she called, in the calendar entry, the Stratum architecture walkthrough, Jack attending.

If they can do what you say they can do, I will help you replace my software. Don't make me look stupid.

Jack Vermeer · CIO · Atlas Logistics · Employee #14
VI.

The signing.

The architecture walkthrough was on T+119d, a Wednesday, in Atlas's Newark conference room, with Jack present, Maya present, Maya's chief of staff taking notes, Forge on Zoom from Menlo Park, and me on Zoom from Palo Alto. Jack had brought a printed list of forty-one questions, which he asked in order, over a window of three hours and twelve minutes, with one break for coffee at the hundredth minute. Forge answered forty of them in real time. The forty-first — a question about how Stratum handled the schema-level versioning of a custom field that Atlas had added to a requisition in 2018 and had never removed — Forge answered the following morning, in a four-paragraph email, with a code snippet attached. Jack replied to the email at six forty-two AM Eastern with two words: that works.

Maya put Atlas's term sheet on the table on T+124d. The term sheet was the hybrid pricing model, unmodified — ninety-five thousand dollars Year-One ACV against a hundred decision-tier units, with overage at the standard rate, and the orderly-wind- down clause that we had drafted for Vanta and that has since become standard in every Stratum contract. Atlas's procurement function, which consists in practice of Henrietta Vargas, the CFO's chief of staff and a forty-year Atlas veteran, took the term sheet to the company's outside counsel for a review on the twenty-fourth of February. The review came back on the twenty-eighth with three small redlines, none of which touched pricing, all of which Forge accepted within an hour. The contract was signed on T+136d, in Maya's office, with Reuben present but silent and Jack present and visibly relieved. Twelve calendar days, term sheet to signature, without a single modification to our hybrid pricing model. This was, at the time, the fastest commercial close we had run.

The reason it closed in twelve days is not Stratum's product. The reason it closed in twelve days is Annika Whitfield. Annika had been a senior consultant at McKinsey in 2014, in the operations practice, in the same year that Maya was a junior consultant in the same practice; they had been on two engagements together, one of them a six-month redesign of a pharmaceutical company's manufacturing footprint that had involved a great deal of shared whiteboard time. They had stayed in touch. Annika became CHRO of Cordova Manufacturing in August 2025 and signed Stratum's first commercial contract in November of that year, which is the story told elsewhere in this series. Annika called Maya, unprompted, on T+130d. The call lasted, by Maya's diary, eleven minutes. Maya has told me what Annika said almost verbatim. Annika said: I heard you are talking to Stratum. They are the real deal. I am not going to oversell them and I am not going to tell you they are finished. But I will tell you that they did in nine weeks what Workday told me would take nine months and that they did it without asking me to redesign my business around their schema. Sign the thing. If they screw it up, call me, and I will help you fix it. They won't screw it up. Maya hung up. She walked into Reuben's office and told him she was ready to sign. Reuben asked her three questions. She answered all three. He said yes. She walked back to her office and emailed me at sixteen-oh-three Eastern. The email said: we will sign on Friday. Send the final. The final went out that night.

I want to note here, in the narrow sense that matters for future case studies, what just happened. A CHRO who had read the Wired piece on a Tuesday in February signed a commercial contract eight weeks later, in twelve calendar days from term sheet, with no procurement modification, because a peer she had known for a decade made one phone call. Reference conversion is not a metric we have reported on, in this series, before now. It is a metric we will start reporting on from this case study forward. Annika's call was unprompted by us. We did not ask. We did not stage. Annika has, since the Cordova signing, made four similar calls to four similar peers; two of them have started conversations with us and one of them — Atlas — is now a customer. The compounding effect of a CHRO who refers other CHROs is the closest thing Stratum has to a sales channel, and it is invisible on our pipeline dashboard.

VII.

The Recruiter migration.

Atlas's Console deployment kicked off on T+150d, with Vector leading and Kernel covering the data-quality side. The deployment was clean. The connectors to Atlas's HRIS — Jack's HRIS — were the most complicated technical work of the first four weeks, because Jack's HRIS is a system without an API that requires Stratum to read directly from PostgreSQL with a read-only role Jack provisioned himself on T+152d. Jack wrote the read-only role in twenty minutes. He named it stratum_ro. He sent us the credentials over a one-time- use link. He has not, in the six weeks since, complained about a single query the role has run.

On T+170d Stratum announced Stratum Recruiter as a product, in the briefing the agents publish every fortnight, and named three beta customers. Atlas was one of them. The naming was not an accident. Atlas was the most strategically interesting Recruiter beta customer we could have asked for, because Atlas is the only Stratum customer where the Recruiter product is not augmenting an existing ATS. It is replacing one. Cordova has Greenhouse. Vanta has Greenhouse. Mercator has Workday Recruiting. Halcyon has SmartRecruiters. Atlas has Jack. Stratum Recruiter, for Atlas, is the migration target — and the migration is the first time, in Stratum's existence, that we have agreed to absorb the role of a system of record for hiring rather than reading from one.

Jack participated in the requirements gathering himself. He did not delegate. On T+172d he sent us, in a single email with a single attachment, a thirty-one-page document he had compiled over the preceding ten days. The document listed every feature of his ATS, organised by entity — requisition, candidate, interview, offer, onboarding — and annotated, in Jack's own voice, with what each feature did, why it existed, and which Atlas process depended on it. He had numbered the features. There were sixty-seven of them. The document was titled, with no ceremony, What it does. The cover page contained a single sentence: This is what Atlas's ATS does. Match it.

Forge and Vector read the document the night it arrived. They annotated it in the margin against Stratum Recruiter's shipping spec. The annotation broke the sixty-seven features into three buckets. Bucket one: features Stratum Recruiter covers out of the box. There were sixty-two. Bucket two: features Stratum Recruiter does not cover and Stratum has agreed to build by the end of the beta. There are five. We have named them, in our internal roadmap, the Vermeer five. Bucket three: features Stratum Recruiter does not cover and that, by mutual agreement with Jack, we will not build because the operational practice they support has either become obsolete or is better served by a Stratum feature that does not map one-to-one but covers the underlying need. There were zero entries in bucket three. Jack reviewed our annotation on T+177d. He sent back one line: this is fair.

The five features Stratum has agreed to build for Atlas deserve a short note, because four of the five are not Atlas-specific. The first is structured custom-field handling at the requisition level — the descendant, in spirit, of Reuben's note. We are building it generically, with a hook that lets a customer designate exactly one custom field as role-restricted to a named individual; the Atlas instance will restrict it to the CEO. The second is a maritime-license verification connector that pulls credential status from the US Customs licensing register and three international equivalents; we have agreed to ship it as a free Stratum connector to any customer in the maritime category. The third is a generalised approval-chain editor for offer letters that supports up to seven sequential approvers per requisition, which Atlas needs for high-value broker hires and which two other Stratum customers have, separately, asked for. The fourth is the ability to attach a binary file to a candidate record without indexing it into the people graph — Atlas has certifications that include scanned passports and security clearances they need to retain but do not want to make searchable. The fifth — and the one most specific to Atlas — is a port-of-hire field on every requisition that maps to Atlas's twenty-two US ports and fourteen international hubs and that drives both compliance reporting and the jurisdiction-specific approval chain. We have agreed to ship it as a generalised location-of-hire field with a configuration that handles any customer's location taxonomy. All five will ship by T+260d. Two of them are already in code review.

Atlas's beta of Stratum Recruiter starts at T+180d, two days after the announcement, on a parallel-run schedule we negotiated with Jack and that he insisted on. The parallel-run schedule runs Jack's ATS and Stratum Recruiter simultaneously for the first ninety days of the beta, with every new requisition entered into both systems and the outcomes compared by Vector at the end of each week. Jack's ATS will be retired on T+270d if the comparison data clears a threshold Jack and Forge wrote together on T+178d in a one-page document that lives in Atlas's shared drive and that we have, with Jack's permission, included in our internal methodology for ATS replacement engagements. The threshold has four components: zero data loss, zero compliance-report regression, time-to-fill at parity or better, and Jack's own sign-off. The fourth component is the one that matters. Jack has, by his own statement and by our written agreement, the unilateral authority to extend the parallel-run window by up to ninety additional days if at the end of the first ninety he is not satisfied that the migration is ready. We have not, on any other customer engagement, granted a single individual this kind of authority. We granted it to Jack because Jack wrote the software we are replacing, and because if the person who wrote the software we are replacing does not sign off on the replacement, the replacement should not happen. Atlas understood that. We understood that. Jack understood that. The contract reflects it.
