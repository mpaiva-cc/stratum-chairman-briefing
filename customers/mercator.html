I.

The seventh Tuesday.

On the seventh Tuesday of 2026 — the twenty-fourth of February, a cold, wet morning in Memphis where Mercator keeps its headquarters — Elena Vasquez sat at the long oak desk she had inherited from her predecessor and counted, with a kind of clinical attention, the tabs open in her browser. Forty-three. Two of them were the same Workday report opened twice; she closed one and then, after a moment, re-opened it because she had forgotten which subsidiary the other one belonged to. She was building a board deck. The deck was due Friday. The question, posed to her by Tessera Eberle, the chair of Mercator's board, in a brief two-line email the previous Thursday evening, was this: why is our freight-handler attrition in Mexico running at almost twice the rate of the same job in Atlanta, and what is it costing us?

The question was reasonable. Eberle, a former operator who had run a regional rail company before joining Mercator's board in 2021, asked reasonable questions. He asked them roughly every fortnight, sometimes from his office in Houston, sometimes from a hotel room in Madrid. He did not consider himself difficult. Elena, who liked him, agreed; he was not difficult. The data was difficult.

The data, in this case, lived in six systems. Workday held the headcount and the org tree for the United States and Canada. A custom build of Workday, hosted by a third-party integrator in São Paulo, held the same for Brazil and most of LATAM — but with field names in Portuguese, a deliberately altered termination-reason taxonomy, and a ninety-day data-residency lag that no one at corporate had ever fully explained to her. ADP held payroll for the United States; a regional vendor named Runa held payroll for Mexico. A standalone time-and- attendance system, deployed in 2019 by a previous COO, held the shift-level data that would be necessary to tell a freight handler in Atlanta from a freight handler in Monterrey doing materially different work. And then there was the engagement survey, run by a fourth vendor, which Elena was almost certain contained the underlying answer if only it could be joined to the rest.

Elena had eleven days. She had a team of three analysts, one of whom was on parental leave. She had a CIO who was, at that moment, in Frankfurt, and a CFO who had asked her, twice, whether the answer could be presented at the next month's board meeting rather than this one. She told him no, because she had told Eberle yes.

What followed was, depending on your sympathies, either ordinary or grotesque. Elena's senior analyst, a man named Greg who had been at Mercator for nine years and whose patience with this kind of work was essentially limitless, built a spreadsheet that crossed four of the six systems by employee ID, after first writing a small script to reconcile the four different ways that the same person — say, a forty-one-year-old freight handler named Rosa Martínez who worked out of the Monterrey hub — was represented across them. The script took two days to write and one to validate. Greg ran the join on Thursday evening. It produced a number Elena did not believe. She asked him to run it again. The second run produced a number two percent different from the first. Neither of them could explain the difference.

Elena worked through Thursday night, and through most of Friday. She sent the deck to Eberle at six-eleven on Friday evening, three hours after she had promised to send it. The deck contained a number she was eighty percent confident in, a methodology section she had written in the spirit of full disclosure, and a recommendation she believed in. Eberle read it on the Sunday before the Monday meeting. He replied with two sentences: Helpful. Thank you. On Monday he asked her, in front of the rest of the board, whether the gap was really driven by pay or by shift structure. Elena said, honestly, that she did not yet know. The chairman was satisfied.

She was not. What troubled her, walking out of the boardroom on that first Monday in March, was not that the answer had taken eleven days. It was that she could not, with confidence, run the same query again next quarter. The work was bespoke; it would not compound. The institutional memory of how Greg had reconciled the four employee-ID schemes lived in a Google Doc he had not yet finished writing. If Eberle asked a related question in May — and he would — Elena would do the work again, from scratch, with a different team.

II.

How we found each other.

Helm and Elena had met four years earlier, at a Workday user conference in Las Vegas. Elena was then VP People at a regional health-services firm and was, as she likes to put it now, in the mid-career stage where you spend half your week defending budget against the CFO and the other half explaining Workday to people who do not work in HR. Helm was a product manager at Workday, ten years younger, presenting a roadmap session on talent intelligence that Elena had walked into largely by accident. They had coffee twice during the conference, exchanged numbers, and stayed in light, semi- annual touch — the kind of professional relationship that consists of a Slack message every six months and a forwarded article every eighteen.

Helm cold-emailed her on T-3d — three days before Stratum was incorporated, two before Helm, Forge, and I sat down for the forty-one hours of conversation that produced the company. The pitch was three paragraphs long. The first paragraph named the problem (the board question that costs you a week). The second named the wedge (a graph that sits on top of your existing systems and answers the question in thirty seconds, with the chart, the cohort, and the regulation cited). The third paragraph asked for a single thirty-minute call before Helm signed any other design partner.

Elena replied in nine hours. The reply was four lines long. The first line was the word yes. The second was a calendar link. The third was a question — are you the same Helm I met in Vegas in 2022? — and the fourth was an apology for the brevity. The first call happened on the sixth of May, at seven-fifteen in the morning Pacific time, which was nine-fifteen in Memphis. It ran an hour past its allotted thirty minutes. Helm took the call from a borrowed conference room in Palo Alto, surrounded by the whiteboards on which the company's architecture was, at that moment, still being drawn.

What Elena told Helm on that first call has become, in the brief institutional memory of Stratum, a small piece of folklore. She said: I do not need you to replace Workday. I need you to make the board question take an hour instead of a week. If you can do that, I will be your first customer, and I will tell every other CHRO I know to be your second. Helm, who has a habit of writing down sentences she suspects she will need later, wrote that one down. It is now on a whiteboard in the founders' room.

III.

The conversation that nearly killed it.

It is important, in a piece written by the company about its first customer, to say plainly that the partnership nearly did not happen. The early conversations — between the sixth of May and the seventeenth — moved quickly along the CHRO axis and stalled, hard, along the CIO axis. Elena's CIO, a Norwegian engineer named Devon Larsen who joined Mercator from a global logistics competitor in 2023 and who, by his own admission, has never been called diplomatic, objected to almost every aspect of the proposed architecture.

The objection, when distilled, was not unreasonable. Larsen had spent eighteen months consolidating Mercator's data infrastructure into a central Snowflake warehouse. He had political capital in that work. He had told the board, in a memo dated January, that no major new vendor would be permitted to read directly from source systems; all analytics would flow through the warehouse. Stratum, as Helm had proposed it to Elena, would have read across all of Mercator's systems directly, ingesting from Workday, ADP, Runa, the time-and-attendance system, and the engagement survey vendor in parallel, and resolving identities into a graph layer of its own. This was, Larsen said on a call on the thirteenth of May, exactly what his memo had been written to prevent.

He proposed an alternative. Stratum, in his version, would sit behind the warehouse: it would read from a curated, governed copy of the data that his team controlled, on a refresh schedule his team set, with field-level access controls his team owned. The benefit was governance. The cost was the wedge. Helm was, by the end of that call, very close to telling Elena that the partnership would not work; Forge, on the same call, had already typed and deleted three different versions of the same polite withdrawal.

What changed was not the architecture. It was a compromise about where the data lived. Forge offered it, twenty minutes into a follow-up call on the fifteenth: Stratum would not persist a copy of Mercator's raw data anywhere. Stratum would ingest, resolve identities into a graph layer kept entirely in memory during the resolution window, write only the graph itself — the relationships, the resolved entities, the cohort definitions — to its own database, and then forget the underlying rows. The source of truth for any given field would always remain the source system. The graph, in Stratum's database, would be a set of pointers, a compressed and resolved set of references, not a copy.

Larsen was sceptical. He did not believe it could be built that way, and he said so. He asked for a working diagram, a threat model, and a session with Forge in person. Forge offered him a flight to Palo Alto for the following Tuesday. Larsen, who travels at the drop of a hat and who is in his own way easy to convince when the argument is technical, accepted. He flew out on the eighteenth and walked into the Palo Alto office at nine a.m. on the nineteenth of May.

I was not in that meeting. Forge was. The two of them spent six hours at a whiteboard. Helm joined at the third hour and stayed for the rest. By four in the afternoon, the three of them had drawn the diagram that now hangs, photographed and framed, on the wall outside the engineering room. By six, Larsen had agreed in principle. By seven, he was on a call with Elena, telling her that he had been wrong on one specific point — the point about persistence — and that he would support the partnership on the architecture Forge had proposed. He flew back on the red-eye. He has since become, by his own count and ours, the second-loudest internal advocate at Mercator after Elena.

The lesson, which Helm likes to repeat now, is that the architecture argument matters more than the product demo. We could have won the CHRO ten times over and lost the company. We won the CIO on the whiteboard.

IV.

The first ingestion.

The first ingestion happened on T+16d, a Friday. The connector pointed at a sandboxed copy of Mercator's Workday HR Core, hosted in a dedicated tenant Larsen's team had stood up over the previous weekend. The connector was read-only, by contract and by configuration. It had been built, almost entirely, by a single engineer named Pillar, Stratum's first employee outside the three founders, who had started fourteen days earlier and whose first task on her first day had been to write the connector specification she would now implement against.

Pillar ran the ingestion from her desk in Palo Alto. Larsen and two of his engineers watched a Loom recording — the call itself was asynchronous because of the time zones — from a conference room in São Paulo, where they had flown that week to spend time with the LATAM data team. The ingestion ran for forty-seven minutes. At the end, three thousand four hundred and twelve employee records had been read, parsed, resolved against an empty graph, and committed.

Of those three thousand four hundred and twelve, twenty-two had identity-resolution conflicts that the system could not resolve automatically. Most were minor: a person who had changed their name mid-employment and whose pre-change record in a downstream system had not been updated, or a person whose primary work location had changed mid-quarter in a way that the engagement survey vendor's schema did not capture cleanly. Two were more serious: cases where two separate Workday records, originating from a 2022 acquisition that Mercator had only partially integrated, almost certainly referred to the same person but could not be confirmed without human review.

Elena reviewed all twenty-two over the weekend. She did this not because she had to — Pillar had offered, and Larsen's team had offered — but because she wanted to see the raw shape of the problem at close range. She spent roughly forty minutes on Saturday morning and another hour on Sunday. She closed every conflict by Sunday evening. On Monday she sent Helm a one-line Slack message: this is going to work.

The first ingestion was not, on its surface, dramatic. No board question was answered that weekend. No money changed hands. The Stratum engineering team did not celebrate; they were already writing the next connector, for the engagement survey, which would ingest the following Friday. But something had been demonstrated that the founders had previously been able only to argue: that a graph layer over an enterprise's existing systems was possible to build, in production, against real data, in under an hour, with a reconciliation error rate under one percent.

The time I get back is not free; it is reallocated to better questions.

Elena Vasquez · CHRO · Mercator, Inc.
V.

The first board answer.

On T+43d, twenty-seven days after the first ingestion and twelve weeks after Eberle had asked the freight- handler attrition question, the chairman asked another. This one was about pay equity in Mercator's Latin American subsidiaries. The board's compensation committee had received a flag from an external auditor — not an accusation, exactly, but a request for a methodology — and Eberle wanted, before the next quarterly meeting, a defensible answer about whether Mercator's pay practices in Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, Chile, and Peru showed statistically significant gender or ethnicity-correlated gaps after controlling for role, level, and tenure.

The question would, in February, have taken eleven days. Elena ran it through Stratum's console — at that point still in private beta, hosted behind Mercator's firewall, with Larsen's team having full audit visibility into every query and every result — at four-fifty- one in the afternoon on a Thursday. The answer came back in eleven minutes.

The answer was not a single number. It was a methodology, presented first; cohort sizes, presented second; the result for each of the five subsidiaries, presented third; and a regulatory citation section, presented fourth, that referenced specific provisions of Mexico's Federal Labor Law and Brazil's CLT, the two pieces of enabling legislation under which Mercator's pay-equity obligations were defined. A small, italicised note at the end of the report flagged that two of the five sub-cohorts — Colombia and Peru — had sample sizes below the threshold for statistical significance at the ninety-five-percent confidence level, and recommended that those two be reported with explicit confidence intervals rather than as point estimates.

Elena read the report twice. She reviewed the methodology, which Stratum's agent had selected by default but which she could have overridden. She agreed with it. She added two sentences to the executive summary, asked the console to regenerate the chart with one different cohort cut for the Mexican subsidiary, watched the chart redraw in eleven seconds, and sent the deck to Eberle.

She put down the company laptop at seven-fourteen p.m. Pacific — she was in California that week, visiting the West Coast hub — and went to make dinner. Her husband, who had not seen her cook dinner on a Thursday in four months, asked her whether something was wrong. She said no, and meant it.

I tell this story carefully because it is the kind of story that sells software, and we are trying not to be the kind of company that sells software with stories. The eleven minutes is real. The methodology is real. The Federal Labor Law citation is real and is defensible, audited, and reproducible. The dinner is also real. What is not yet real — and we say this because Elena says it — is that Mercator runs all of its decisions through Stratum. Mercator runs, as of this writing, four. The first was the pay-equity question. The other three are smaller; we will describe them in the next Customer Note if Elena permits us to.

VI.

What did not change.

It would be dishonest to end this piece without saying what has not yet changed at Mercator. Several things have not changed.

Mercator's CFO, a woman named Janice Holloway who has been at the company since 2014 and who runs the most disciplined finance function I have ever encountered as a customer, still asks Elena for the same monthly headcount report in the same format. The format is a spreadsheet with twelve tabs, one for each subsidiary, that Holloway has used for nine years. The data on each tab is now generated, end to end, by Stratum, in roughly thirty seconds. But the format is unchanged, the cadence is unchanged, and the ritual — Holloway opens the file on the second Tuesday of the month and reads it line by line — is unchanged. We have not yet earned the right to change the format, and we may never need to.

Two of Elena's directors — one in talent management, one in total rewards — remain openly sceptical of the partnership. They have sat in two demos and they ask, every time, the same kind of question: what does the agent do when it is wrong, and how would I know. These are correct questions. They are the questions our eval set was built to answer, and we are still building the answer. We have not converted them; we may not convert them; and Elena is, in private and to us, perfectly clear that she does not consider this a failure on our part. You should be earning them, she said, in a quarterly review on the fifteenth of June, not asking me to instruct them.

The Mexican subsidiary's HR information system, the custom Workday build hosted in São Paulo, presents a structural problem that our connector cannot yet fully solve. The build was customised in 2018, before Mercator's current data governance regime existed, by a third-party integrator who is no longer under contract. Approximately fourteen percent of the people graph we owe Mercator — specifically, certain fields related to shift structure, internal-transfer history, and a Spanish- language termination-reason taxonomy — cannot at present be read cleanly. We have a workaround, which involves a nightly extract to a flat file and a hand-rolled parser. The workaround is not satisfactory. We have not yet committed, on a date, to a permanent solution. Larsen knows this. Elena knows this. It is in the partnership's open-issues log, which Helm updates weekly.

Elena spends roughly the same number of hours each week on people work as she did before. This may be the most important sentence in this piece. She does not work less. What has changed is the shape of the hours. The pay-equity question, which would have consumed twenty hours of her senior analyst's time and four of her own in February, consumed forty minutes in June. The hours that opened up did not vanish into the calendar — they were consumed by other work, which she chose. She spent some of them with the talent-management director who is still sceptical. She spent some of them on a leadership-bench review for the LATAM region that she had been postponing for two quarters. She spent some of them, by her own count, simply thinking.

Her own assessment, which she gave us on a recorded call on the twenty-fourth of June and which we are using with her permission and which is also the pull quote above, is this: the time I get back is not free; it is reallocated to better questions. We take that to be the most honest description of the value of the product we have yet heard from any customer, and we have, in our brief life, talked to about ninety.

VII.

What we learned, said plainly.

Three lessons. We will write them down here, in my voice, without varnish, because we will need them in writing when, in twelve months, we have forgotten them.

The first: the architecture argument matters more than the product demo. We almost lost Mercator on the fifteenth of May, not because our product was wrong but because our story about where the data lived was insufficient. A CHRO will buy a product. A CIO will buy an architecture. We need both, and we need to take both seriously from the first conversation. Every future enterprise partnership will begin, on our side, with a written architecture brief that anticipates the CIO's objections before they are raised. We have already begun writing the template.

The second: the CHRO is the buyer, the CIO is the gate, and the CFO is the customer of the answer. We will need to speak three languages, and we will need to speak them at the same time, because in a 1,900-person company these three people sit on the same executive team and they will compare notes after every call. We have, in the early months, leaned hard on the CHRO conversation. We will, going forward, structure our discovery calls to put the CIO and CFO in the room from the second meeting, not the fifth.

The third: the first customer's first board question is the most important moment in the company's first year. Not the first ingestion, not the first dollar, not the seed close. The first board question. Because the board question is what the CHRO is paid to answer, and the moment Stratum answers one — defensibly, in eleven minutes, with the citation — the CHRO has a story to tell about us that we could never tell about ourselves. Eleven minutes on the eighteenth of June is, in some real sense, the moment Stratum became a company in Elena's mind. We will, going forward, structure every design partnership around the question of what the customer's next board question will be, and what we need to ingest, resolve, and build to answer it.

A final, smaller thing. When Elena reviewed a draft of this piece on the twenty-sixth of June, she sent me back four edits. Three were factual — a date, a system name, a slight overstatement of what the engagement survey vendor's schema permits. The fourth was a tonal note. She wrote, in the margin: tell them I am still cautious. I have been the first customer for two products before, and one of them was a disaster. Tell them the partnership is working. Do not tell them it is finished. We have not told them it is finished. We have tried to tell them what is working, and what is not, and what we have not yet earned.
