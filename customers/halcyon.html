Halcyon Health
The honest one

The week we lost the room, and the day we earned it back.

How a 2,400-person hospital system's sponsor transition tested everything we said we were — and why we are publishing this before the outcome is known.

I.

The day Avery got promoted.

On T+34d, at eleven-forty in the morning Pacific time, a Slack message from a name I had not yet learned to recognise arrived in our shared #halcyon channel. The sender was Olivia Brennan, chief of staff to Dr. Avery Mensah-Cole, who was then Halcyon Health's Chief Human Resources Officer and who had, twenty-nine days earlier, signed the design-partner letter that made Halcyon Stratum's second customer in name and our first in healthcare. The message was four lines long. The first congratulated Avery on her promotion to System Chief Operating Officer, effective immediately. The second noted, with the kind of operational grace that defines Olivia, that Avery would be transitioning her CHRO portfolio to her successor over the next two weeks. The third named the successor: Carlos Reyes, who had been Halcyon's COO and who would, until a permanent CHRO was hired, hold both seats. The fourth thanked us for our patience as the team realigned and offered a thirty-minute call to discuss continuity on Thursday.

Helm read the message first. We were in the founders' room — the small glass-walled corner at the end of the second floor of the Palo Alto office, with the whiteboard that still has, in Helm's hand from May, the architecture diagram from the night Forge convinced Mercator's CIO to let us read from source. She read it once, said nothing, and slid her laptop across the table to me. I read it twice. I read it again. Then I said, because there was nothing else useful to say: our sponsor just got promoted out from under us.

Helm did not respond immediately. She has a habit — those who have worked with her for any length of time will recognise it — of holding a moment until the moment is finished. She held this one for what I later, with the help of a Loom recording running on the desk, measured to be eleven seconds. Then she said, in a voice that was the voice she uses when she has finished thinking but has not yet started speaking: that is the second-best thing that could have happened.

I asked her what the best would have been. She said: if Avery had stayed. The second-best, she said, was that Avery had been promoted up rather than out. Her champion was still inside the building. Her champion was now one level higher. The third-best was that we had Avery in our debt for a month while she handed over a portfolio she had not, herself, finished defining, and the worst was somewhere below that, and we were not yet in worst territory. Helm is, among the founders, the one most likely to count outcomes before she reacts. She finished counting and went to find Forge.

What I did not know at eleven-forty-one Pacific on the ninth of June, and what we would learn in the seventy-two hours that followed, was this: Avery's promotion had been in motion since March. Her successor had been chosen since the second of June. The design-partner letter she had signed on the eleventh of May had been signed in full knowledge that she would, by the time the first connector went live, no longer be the executive responsible for the project. She had not told us. She had not, by her own account given to me on a recorded call on the twenty-third of July, intended to deceive. She had intended to give us the relationship and then transfer it cleanly. The transfer is what this piece is about.

II.

Who Carlos Reyes is.

Carlos Reyes is fifty-three years old, the son of two pharmacists who met in Ponce and raised him in suburban Hartford, and he has spent twenty-three of his working years inside large American health insurers. Twelve of those years were at Anthem, where he rose from a regional claims-operations analyst to a divisional vice president responsible for the Mid-Atlantic block of business. The next ten were at Cigna, where he ran the operations function for a commercial book that, by the time he left in early 2024, comprised somewhere north of eleven million covered lives. He came to Halcyon in March of 2024 as Chief Operating Officer, recruited by the system's CEO with the explicit mandate to bring health-insurance operating discipline to what was, at that point, a hospital system run mostly by clinicians who had been promoted into management.

He is, by every account I have collected and by my own observation across nine calls and two on-site meetings, the kind of operator who reads the prepared materials and then asks for the spreadsheet behind them. He has, on his Halcyon office wall, a framed copy of the first operations memo he wrote at Anthem in 2007. The memo is one page long. Its central sentence, which he quoted to me without prompting on our third call, reads: most of the enterprise software we buy is sold on a promise and delivered on a discount. He believes this sentence in the way other people believe in proverbs. He has, in twenty-three years, watched it prove itself often enough.

I am writing about Carlos at length because the rest of this piece will not make sense without him. The story of Halcyon, from the ninth of June onward, is the story of a sponsor we did not choose meeting a company he did not choose to inherit and deciding, on his own timeline, whether we deserved the room. He is courteous in conversation, scrupulous in correspondence, and almost completely uninterested in product demonstrations. He read our website twice before our first call. He had read, by the time we spoke on the twelfth, two of our published Chairman's Briefings end to end. He had not run our agent.

The first call was on the tenth of June, a Wednesday, at eight a.m. Eastern time, which is five a.m. Pacific. Helm took it alone from the kitchen of her apartment in Mountain View. Carlos took it from his office in Charlotte. The call lasted thirty-eight minutes. He asked five questions. None of them were about features. He asked, in this order: how long the company had existed; how much capital we had raised and from whom; who, specifically, would be accountable for Halcyon's implementation if something went wrong on a Saturday night; what our SOC 2 timeline was; and whether we had, in writing, a clause that would allow Halcyon to exit the partnership at any point without penalty other than what had already been delivered. Helm answered each in turn. The honest answer to the first was thirty-four days. The honest answer to the fourth was that we had begun the SOC 2 readiness engagement but did not yet have a report. Carlos thanked her for her honesty and asked to meet in person.

What I have come to understand about Carlos, in retrospect, is that Avery's promotion did not hand him an opportunity. It handed him a problem. He had inherited a vendor relationship he had not approved, with a company younger than the period between his Halcyon anniversary and the date of the meeting, against a backdrop of nine separate HR systems his predecessor had been planning to consolidate and that, in his view, were not the kind of mess one solved by adding a tenth thing on top. He was not hostile. He was not even, as Vector would later observe, sceptical in the unproductive sense. He was doing his job. His job was to protect a 2,400-person non-profit hospital system from a category of mistake he had spent two decades watching other large institutions make.

III.

The Phoenix conversation.

The in-person meeting happened on the twelfth of June, two days after the first call. It was held in a windowless conference room on the third floor of Halcyon's regional administrative office in Charlotte — not Phoenix; Phoenix was where Vector later went; but the configuration was the same: a long table, four chairs, a whiteboard that had been wiped imperfectly and still bore the faded outline of a capacity-planning grid from the previous occupant. Helm flew in on the morning red-eye and arrived at the lobby with twenty minutes to spare. She brought a single-page printed agenda, an architecture diagram, and the laptop on which she had loaded a Halcyon-specific sandbox of our console. She did not bring Forge. She did not bring me. She did not bring slides.

Carlos arrived four minutes early. He was wearing a navy suit without a tie, carrying a leather folio with what looked like a printed version of our website's pricing page on top of a stack of yellow legal pad. He shook Helm's hand. He sat down. He did not open the folio. He looked at the agenda for perhaps ten seconds, set it gently to one side, and said, in the conversational register of a man who has done this kind of meeting often enough that he no longer needed to warm into it: I would like to skip the architecture walkthrough, if you do not mind. I want to ask you one question.

The question was this: what is the worst thing that has happened to a Stratum customer?

Helm later told me — and I have heard her tell three other people in slightly different forms — that the eight seconds she took before answering were the longest eight seconds of her year. She had prepared, on the flight, for objections about architecture, about data residency, about pricing, about our age. She had not prepared for this question, because she had not yet been asked it by anyone who had the standing to ask it. She considered, briefly, telling him that we had not yet had a customer long enough for anything truly bad to have happened. She considered, more briefly, telling him about a reconciliation error that had occurred during a Mercator test ingestion the previous week. She rejected both. She told him, instead, about a synthetic edge case our internal eval set had surfaced during the second week of the architecture phase with Halcyon itself.

The case was this. The entity-resolution layer, presented with two physicians who shared a name — common enough in any system of 2,400 employees, common to the point of inevitability in healthcare — and whose records appeared in two different source systems with overlapping but non-identical employment dates, had resolved them as a single person and surfaced the wrong compensation band to a hypothetical manager running a comparison query. The error had been caught by the eval set, not by a customer. The case was not real, in the sense that no manager had ever actually run the query against live data. But the resolution logic was real, and the failure mode was real, and the patch — which Forge and Pillar had shipped to the eval branch on the seventh of June — was real. Helm described all of this in roughly two minutes. She described what we had changed: the resolver now flagged any same-name match across heterogeneous source systems as ambiguous until human review, even if the structured fields suggested confidence. She described what we had not yet changed: we had not yet built a defensible mechanism for the manager on the other end to understand why the result they were seeing had a confidence band attached.

Carlos listened. He did not write anything down, which surprised her, and he did not interrupt, which surprised her more. When she finished, he was quiet for perhaps five seconds. Then he said: thank you. That is the answer I needed.

He went on for another nine minutes. He told her, in measured and almost gentle terms, that he was not going to commit his organisation to a company that was six weeks old. He said it twice, in two different sentences, so that there could be no possibility of ambiguity. He said that the work Avery had begun would not be discarded, because Avery's judgement carried weight inside Halcyon and because his own preliminary review had not surfaced anything disqualifying. But the partnership label, as written, presumed a reciprocal commitment that he was not yet prepared to make. He proposed, in its place, a sixty-day evaluation. The evaluation would end on the eleventh of August. It would carry no fee. It would require us to demonstrate, over those sixty days, that we could operate inside Halcyon's environment without breaking anything, and that the people we put in front of him understood not only what our product did but how his systems worked. Earn it, he said, in the closing minute of the meeting, and Helm wrote those two words on the back of the agenda and underlined them twice. Earn it, and we will revisit the partnership conversation in August.

IV.

The Compact, internally.

Helm flew back on a five-thirty evening flight, made notes during the first hour, slept for the second, and was in our Palo Alto office at nine the next morning. She had not yet called the rest of us; she had sent a single Slack message at six on the morning of the thirteenth that said only, downgrade. Talk at nine. Forge and I were already in the founders' room when she arrived. She closed the door. She told us what had happened. The conversation that followed lasted a little over two hours.

Three options were on the table, and we put them there honestly. The first was to attempt to preserve the partnership label by offering an aggressive discount — a permanent reduction on the first year's pricing in exchange for Carlos's signature on the original letter. Forge put this on the table because someone had to, and because he believed, correctly, that we should consider every possibility before we ruled it out. We ruled it out within twenty minutes. The partnership label, as Helm had heard it described, was not the kind of commitment one bought with a discount. Buying it would have told Carlos that we were the company his framed memo described.

The second was to accept the downgrade and use the sixty days seriously. Helm wanted this. I wanted this. Forge wanted this. We agreed on the shape of it before we agreed on the cost. The cost, as Forge named it within the first half-hour, was that the sixty days could not be run from Palo Alto with the existing team. Halcyon needed somebody on the ground, in Charlotte and at the regional offices, who could sit with their data team and walk their systems with them. We had no such person on payroll. We had budgeted, in our seed plan, for the first customer engineering hire to begin in September. September was eighty-eight days away.

The third option was the one I have not yet named. The third option was to walk away from the customer — to write a brief, gracious letter to Carlos thanking him for the consideration, to redirect the engineering capacity to Mercator and to the next two pilots in the pipeline, and to take the lesson without taking the bruise. Forge laid this option out clearly. He has, in our short institutional memory, a habit of insisting that the option to walk away is always on the table, because pretending it is not is the surest way to make bad decisions. We considered it for perhaps fifteen minutes. We ruled it out because the lesson we would learn from walking away would be the wrong lesson. We would learn that we could only win customers who were easy. Carlos was not the customer who was easy. He was the customer who, if we earned him, would teach us how to win the next one.

The compromise was a hiring decision we had not planned to make for another quarter. We would extend an offer to a customer engineer — a founding customer engineer, with the seniority and the stock and the title to match — and we would do it within seventy-two hours. The candidate Helm had in mind was Vector, an implementation engineer she had worked with twice at Workday, who had since spent two years at a healthcare-focused integration consultancy and who had, in Helm's words, the rare quality of preferring the data conversation to the demo conversation. Helm had been in light touch with Vector for six weeks. She had not, until that morning, been authorised to make her an offer.

The unbudgeted compensation package required Chairman approval, which we secured the same afternoon. Helm called Vector at four-fifteen Pacific. Vector asked for the weekend. Vector called back on the morning of the sixteenth, a Monday, and accepted. She gave two weeks' notice that afternoon. Her start date was the thirtieth of June. The offer-to-acceptance window, by my count, was ninety-six hours. The sixty-day evaluation had at that point used four of its days. We had fifty-six left, and now we had Vector.

Your engineer understands my pipes, not just my product. That is rare.

Carlos Reyes · Chief Operating Officer · Halcyon Health
V.

Vector's list of fourteen questions.

Vector started on the morning of the thirtieth of June, a Monday. She spent her first day reading. Her second day she spent on a call with Pillar, walking the connector code line by line. Her third day she spent on the phone with three different members of Halcyon's data team, none of whom she had yet met, asking permission to come see them. By Tuesday evening she had a flight booked to Phoenix — not Charlotte; the Halcyon data team's lead, a quietly authoritative data architect named Renata Okafor, worked from the Phoenix administrative annex Halcyon kept for its regional billing operations, and Vector had decided, correctly, that the data conversation needed to happen where the data lived. Helm did not argue. I did not argue. The flight was on Vector's corporate card by Wednesday morning.

She landed in Phoenix on the morning of the first of July. She did not bring a laptop bag, which is a thing I noticed when she walked out of our office on the thirtieth with nothing but a notebook and a pen. She did not, in any of the four hours she spent with Carlos and Renata, open a Stratum demo. She did not show a slide. She did not use the word console once. What she brought with her, and what she put on the conference table within the first three minutes of the meeting, was a single sheet of paper with fourteen hand-written questions.

The questions, paraphrased and lightly reordered, were these. How old is the Workday tenancy and which Workday release line is it pinned to. How were the custom UKG sub-tenancies that Halcyon stood up for the rural-hospital divisions in 2019 configured, and were those sub-tenancies still in a supported state by UKG's reckoning or had they drifted into the unsupported regime that the major UKG reorganisation of 2024 had created. Which of the two definitions of physician in active use at Halcyon — the human-resources definition, which treated medical-staff appointments as a categorical attribute, and the credentialing definition, which carried them as a time-bounded series of privileges by facility — was authoritative for which decisions, and how were the two reconciled in the compensation-band reporting that Renata's team produced for the finance function. How were overnight nurse shift differentials carried on the comp-band data, given that the differentials had been restructured in 2023 and the historical record predating the restructuring had been preserved in a parallel field whose schema no one currently on the data team had personally designed. What was the data-residency posture for protected health information that flowed adjacent to but not within the HR systems, and what was Renata's view of the question that the system CIO had, by Renata's own admission earlier in the call, been reluctant to surface formally with vendors. And nine others, of similar kind.

Renata read the list in silence. She looked up at Vector, then at Carlos, then back at Vector, and she said: I have been asking the last three vendors who came through this office some version of these questions, and none of them brought the questions with them. Vector, who has a dry sense of humour she deploys sparingly, said: I assumed you were tired of explaining the pipes. I read your slack-overflow posts. I thought I would do my reading first.

Renata laughed. Carlos did not laugh, but he leaned back in his chair perhaps half an inch, which from Carlos is the same thing. The three of them sat at the table for four hours and twenty minutes. Vector took notes longhand. By the end of the afternoon she had a thirty-eight-row implementation plan written on the back of the original sheet of fourteen questions and on three further sheets she borrowed from Renata's printer. The plan named, by system and by field, which parts of Halcyon's environment we could connect to inside thirty days, which parts would require a quarter of coordinated work with UKG support, and which parts — three rows, all of them about pre-2022 historical data — Vector did not yet recommend we try to read at all. Carlos asked her, as she was folding her papers, whether she could come back the following Tuesday. She said yes. He walked her to her rental car. He shook her hand at the driver's-side door, which is, I am told by Renata, something he does not do often.

VI.

What Carlos told Compass.

Two days after Vector's first Phoenix visit, on the evening of the third of July — a Thursday, the day before the long weekend — my phone rang. The number was Carlos's direct line, which I had given him on our second call and which he had not previously used. I was in my kitchen. I picked up on the second ring. He apologised for calling without a calendared invitation. I told him, truthfully, that he could call whenever he liked. He said he would not keep me long.

He had, he said, spent four hours on Tuesday with our customer engineer. He wanted me to know that he had not been certain, going into the meeting, what he would find. He had assumed Vector would arrive with a demo, attempt to walk Renata through the product, and thereby waste his afternoon. He had been prepared, he said with some humour, to leave after the first hour. He had instead stayed for four. He paused. I waited. Then he said, in a sentence I have since written down in three different places so I will not lose it: your engineer understands my pipes, not just my product. That is rare.

He did not, in that call, commit to anything. He did not say the sixty-day evaluation was over, or that the partnership was being reinstated, or that he had made a decision. He said he wanted me to know what he had seen, and that he wanted Vector back the following Tuesday, and that he had asked his finance director to pull the budget line for what a paid pilot at our standard pricing would look like inside Halcyon's current fiscal year. He said it in the tone of a man reporting on an analysis he had not yet finished. He thanked me. He hung up.

I sat in the kitchen for a while. Then I wrote down the sentence, the date, and the context, and I sent it in a single Slack message to Helm and Forge. Helm replied within four minutes with three words: print it out. The printed version, in a small black frame, now hangs on the wall of the founders' room, beside the architecture diagram from May. I look at it most mornings.

I want to be careful, here, not to make more of this than it was. A single sentence, said in confidence on a Thursday evening, is not a contract. It is not even, in the strictest sense, an indication. What it is, and what I think it is fair to say it is, is the moment the conversation changed register. We had spent twenty-four days being the company Carlos was evaluating. On the third of July, for the first time, we became — provisionally, conditionally, and not yet in writing — the company he was considering keeping.

VII.

What we don't know yet.

I am writing this on the twenty-eighth of July, and the sixty-day evaluation ends on the eleventh of August. There are fourteen days between now and then. By the time this piece is published — which, if our small editorial schedule holds, will be the morning of the twenty-ninth — there will be thirteen. We have, in those fourteen days, three more on-site visits scheduled with Vector and Renata; one joint call with the system CIO that Helm will lead; a data-residency memo we owe Carlos by the fifth; and the formal thirty-day review that Vector and Carlos have agreed will, in substance though not in name, decide the question of conversion.

Our internal conviction is high. By that I mean: every member of the Stratum team who has had a working interaction with the Halcyon environment in the last fortnight believes, in their own assessment, that we are on track to be reinstated as a design partner under a paid pilot at our standard fifty-thousand-dollar pilot fee. The thirty-eight-row implementation plan is, as of last Friday, twenty-one rows complete; eleven are scheduled to land inside the evaluation window; six are scheduled to land in the first month after. Renata has, in three successive calls with Vector, used the future indicative when describing what Halcyon will be able to do with our graph layer once it is live. Avery, from her new seat as System COO, has twice in July asked Olivia Brennan to forward us internal context she thought would be useful. None of these are signatures. All of them are signals.

Our internal certainty, however, is not high. By that I mean: we have been wrong about customers before. We were wrong, in our first week of conversations with Halcyon in early May, about how much sponsor weight Avery's signature actually represented inside the institution. We were wrong, in our first call with Carlos on the tenth of June, about how much he had already read about us before we met. We could be wrong now, in our seventh week of knowing him, about the meaning of his sentence on the third of July. He is a careful man. He has not signed anything. The sentence may, in retrospect, prove to have been an observation rather than a commitment. He owes us nothing. We have not yet earned, by his standard, the right to count him.

I have argued internally, and Helm and Forge agreed, that this piece should be published before the outcome is known. The argument was this: if we publish Halcyon only after the conversion is signed, we will have written a victory note. If we publish only after Carlos walks away, we will have written a post-mortem. Neither is the piece the customer base we are trying to earn — careful operators in cautious industries who are tired of being sold software on a promise — would benefit from reading. What might benefit them is a piece written from inside the uncertainty. A piece that names the moment the room was lost, the decisions made in response, the person hired to make the response legible, and the sentence that, on a Thursday evening, suggested the work might be working. The outcome will be what it is. We will publish it, on this page, in our August update, either way.

I will say one further thing, and then I will stop, because I have been writing for longer than the eleven-minute read this piece promises. The thing I want to say is that we have, in eighty-four days of being a company, learned a lesson at Halcyon that we did not learn at Mercator, and that the second lesson is the more durable one. The Mercator lesson was that we could win a sophisticated customer on a clean architecture argument. That lesson is true and important and it is the foundation of the product story we tell. The Halcyon lesson is that some customers, in some industries, will not be won on architecture at all. They will be won, if they are won, by a single engineer, on a Tuesday afternoon, in a windowless room in Phoenix, with fourteen questions handwritten on a single sheet of paper, and the patience to listen to the answers before speaking. That is the lesson Vector taught us. That is the lesson we are spending the next fortnight earning.
