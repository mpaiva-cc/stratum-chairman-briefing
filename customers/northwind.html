The recruiting one

I.

The CFO's question.

Lila Park, the chief human-resources officer of Northwind Studios, spent the last week of February 2026 preparing for the quarterly business review. Northwind, founded in 1998, is a creative agency network of roughly twelve hundred people working out of four cities — New York, Los Angeles, London, Berlin — and a client roster that includes Spotify, Patagonia, the New York Times, and three large CPG brands that prefer not to be named in pieces like this one. Lila joined Northwind from Pentagram fourteen months earlier. She came in, by her own description, with a tidy mental model of what the People function should produce: clarity on cost, clarity on flow, clarity on whether the money you spend on hiring is producing the people you actually want.

On the afternoon of Wednesday, the eleventh of February, while she was on a call with the London office about an open Creative Director role, a Slack message came in from Marcus Tang, Northwind's CFO. Marcus is the kind of CFO who writes in complete sentences. The message was one of them. Can you tell me, in one slide, whether the one-point-one million we spent on agencies last quarter was worth it? No question mark. No follow-up. He had asked her some version of this question in May, in August, and again in November. Each time she had produced something — a memo, a table, a five-slide deck — that she suspected, on later re-reading, did not actually answer what he had asked.

She spent the next four days trying to answer this one properly. The number she needed was simple in principle. For every recruiting agency Northwind paid in the prior twelve months, what did Northwind get? Hires, yes, but also: which hires, in which roles, at which levels, who were still at the company six months later, who were rated three or above in their first performance review, who had been promoted, and at what total agency cost. The number, expressed as a ratio of money in to money's worth out, was the answer Marcus wanted. Lila knew it was the answer Marcus wanted because she had asked him, the previous September, what shape of slide would settle the question for him, and he had told her: one number, with the methodology in a footnote, that I can show the board.

Fourteen hours of work, spread over four days, produced a twenty- three-slide deck. The deck did not answer the question. It came close to answering parts of it. Slide eleven showed agency invoices against hire counts, but not against retention. Slide fourteen showed retention by source, but not against cost. Slide nineteen showed cost-per-hire as a single corporate average, but only because the London Lever instance and the New York Greenhouse instance used incompatible source-of-hire taxonomies that her analyst Ren had reconciled by hand, and Lila knew the reconciliation was, to use Ren's word, lossy.

She sent the deck to Marcus on the Friday evening. He replied on Saturday morning with one sentence: Thanks, helpful, will read this weekend. He read it. On Monday at the QBR he did not raise the agency question at all, which Lila understood, correctly, to be a courtesy. She walked out of the room knowing that what she had produced was not what he had asked for. It was a deck about agency spending. The question had been about agency value. The deck was the closest she could get with the systems she had.

II.

Why agencies?

To understand why Marcus's question was so hard, you have to understand the shape of Northwind's recruiting economy, which is not the shape of most companies' recruiting economies. Northwind hires creative talent — designers, art directors, copywriters, strategists, group account directors, executive creative directors — and those people do not move through the same channels as software engineers or sales managers. They move through the design conference circuit, through Are.ena and Working Not Working, through the personal Slacks of senior creatives who have worked together for fifteen years, through clients who, having admired a piece of work, ask the agency who made it and then quietly call that person a year later. The talent Northwind hires is itself in the agency world.

The implication, which Lila articulated to me on a call in late June, is that Northwind's best people almost never come through job boards. They come through referrals. They come through inbound portfolio submissions from designers who admire the work. They come, occasionally, through agencies — but those agencies are, almost without exception, agencies that specialise in the upper end of the creative market, and the roles they fill are the senior roles for which the network of cold introductions is too thin to do the work.

In theory, then, agency spend should have been small and surgical. In practice it had ballooned. In 2023 Northwind paid recruiting agencies $2.1 million. In 2024 the number was $3.0 million. In 2025 it was $4.2 million — a thirty-eight percent jump in a year in which Northwind's overall headcount grew by seven percent. Each year, when the budget was set, the partners had argued that the previous year's agency spend had been an aberration; each year the following year's number had been larger. Lila could see the trend. She could not, with the systems she had, prove what was driving it or what to do about it.

III.

The Bhusri dinner.

On T+29d, Aneel Bhusri hosted a dinner in Atherton. The dinner has been described in two previous Briefings and I will not describe it again in detail here. What matters for this piece is that Lila Park was at the dinner. She was, by her own count, the fourth-youngest person in the room. Helm, Stratum's CEO, was seated to her left. The two had not previously met.

Lila is not a person who makes small talk at dinners like this one. She is a person who, when she has someone's attention, asks the question she has been holding for nine months. The question she asked Helm, somewhere between the first and second course, was this: Can you tell me, today, which of my agencies actually produce my best hires? Helm, who has trained herself not to over-promise at dinners, paused for what Lila later told me felt like a long time but was probably four seconds. Then she said: Not today. But if you let us read your ATS and your performance data, in six weeks we can.

They exchanged numbers between the second course and the cheese board. They scheduled a call for the following Tuesday. Lila has told me, on a recorded call, that what convinced her in that first thirty seconds was not the claim itself — she has heard many such claims — but the precision of the qualification. Helm did not say yes. She said not today, but in six weeks, if you let us read these two specific things. Lila is a CHRO. CHROs hear the difference.

IV.

The decision to sign.

Lila brought the conversation to Cody Ng, Northwind's chief executive, the following Monday morning. Cody is a former art director who became a chief executive the way good art directors sometimes do, which is to say reluctantly and with a permanent undertow of suspicion about anyone selling him software. He has, he told me later, been pitched HR software eleven times in eighteen months. He has bought it twice. Both times he has regretted it.

The conversation in Cody's office on the ninth of June lasted forty minutes. Lila described what Helm had said. Cody asked the two questions a sceptical CEO asks. The first was: what does it cost. Lila told him: nothing. Stratum was offering Northwind a design-partner pilot — read access, a console, weekly reviews with Stratum's CCO — at zero dollars, with no commercial conversation before October at the earliest. The second question was: and what do they want from us. Lila told him: time, attention, the right to publish what they learned with our review, and, in October, a conversation. Not a contract. A conversation.

Cody listened. He drew a small circle on a piece of paper in front of him, which is a thing he does when he is about to agree to something he is not sure about. Then he said, in a way that Lila has since repeated to me at least three times: If they can't answer Marcus's question by August, we walk away. Not a threat, exactly; a deadline. The point was not the deadline but what it implied — that the only thing Cody cared about was whether the partnership produced an answer that could survive contact with the CFO. Anything else was, in his phrase, decoration.

Lila signed the design-partner letter on the twentieth of June, the day after Briefing 003 was published. The signing came up, she has since told me, over a follow-up exchange from the Bhusri dinner network — the introduction had ripened, the timing had aligned, and her own conviction had been building for two weeks. The countersignature from Stratum came back ninety minutes later. Implementation was scheduled to begin on the Monday following.

V.

The implementation.

The implementation was, in shape, the implementation Mercator had been: a small, embedded Stratum team, a careful read across the customer's systems, a graph layer assembled in memory and written to Stratum's database as a set of resolved pointers rather than a copy. The complication, in Northwind's case, was that the four offices ran three different recruiting systems. New York and Los Angeles used a heavily customised Greenhouse instance, instrumented over six years and threaded into a slack of internal tools that bore only a passing resemblance to a stock Greenhouse deployment. London used Lever, on a separate contract, with separate field names and a separate source-of-hire taxonomy. Berlin used Personio, for which Stratum did not have a connector and for which, given the size of the office and the volume of throughput, building one was hard to justify against the implementation calendar.

Forge, Stratum's CTO, flew to London on the twenty-fourth of June. He spent two days with Northwind's London IT lead and a half-day on a video call with the Berlin office manager. He came back with a decision that he and Helm argued about on the Saturday flight home and that, on the Sunday, they agreed on. The decision was: do not build a Personio connector for Northwind. Build a one-time export — a clean, documented, well-resolved flat-file extract of the Berlin office's recruiting data, ingested into the graph once, refreshed quarterly by hand if and only if the partnership extended beyond October. The argument for the decision was that the Berlin office was about a hundred and thirty people and the throughput was small relative to New York and London; the argument against was that it would leave a known gap in the graph. They chose to leave the gap, name it, and write it into the partnership's open-issues log on day one.

The Berlin export landed in Stratum's graph on the thirtieth of June, a Tuesday. The Greenhouse connector went operational on the sixth of July. The Lever connector went operational on the thirteenth. Between those dates Vector, Stratum's founding customer engineer — who had started on the same Monday as the Northwind engagement — spent eleven working days at Northwind's New York office, working out of a small glass room on the eighth floor that Lila's chief of staff had set aside for her. Vector's job, in those eleven days, was almost entirely to reconcile field names. Greenhouse's source field and Lever's source type field overlapped about seventy percent; the remaining thirty percent — agency-specific subtypes, referral sub-channels, a now-deprecated inbound-from-event category that the London office had stopped populating in 2024 — was a small but patient cataloguing exercise that no piece of software could do on its own.

I spent two of those eleven days at the New York office myself, in part to make sure we were not, in our enthusiasm, breaking anything. We were not. Vector is, among other things, careful. By Tuesday the fourteenth of July, the graph contained roughly eight thousand hire and candidate records spanning thirty-eight months, resolved to a single taxonomy, joined to performance data from Northwind's separate performance system (a Lattice instance the partners had standardised on in 2022), and ready to query. The console went live to Lila on the eighteenth.

I spent fourteen hours producing twenty-three slides. Stratum produced one slide in forty-seven seconds. The slide was right.

VI.

The first defensible answer.

On Tuesday the twenty-second of July, at eleven-fourteen Pacific time — eight-fourteen evening in London, ten past two in the afternoon in New York where Lila was sitting — Lila opened the Stratum console on her own, for the first time, without Vector or anyone else from our team on a call. She had been using it under light supervision the previous week. This was the first time she sat with it alone. She typed, in the console's natural-language query box, a single sentence: Compare cost-per-hire by source for hires made in the last eighteen months.

The console took forty-seven seconds. It produced, in that time, a methodology block describing the date range, the source-of-hire taxonomy it had used to bucket the data, the agency-invoice reconciliation logic it had applied, and the way it had handled hires whose sources were ambiguous or missing. Beneath the methodology it produced a chart and a table. The table contained three rows. Agencies: 12% conversion from candidate to hire, $48,000 cost-per-hire averaged across the eighteen-month window. Referrals: 31% conversion, $9,000 cost-per-hire. Inbound: 22% conversion, $4,000 cost-per-hire. A note at the bottom of the table observed that seventy-one percent of agency spend in the window had been concentrated on three specific agency vendors, and offered to show the breakdown by vendor on request.

Lila read the answer three times. She read the methodology twice. She read the note about the three agencies four times. She did not, in those first minutes, look at the chart at all; she looked at the methodology. She has since told me that what convinced her in that moment was not the numbers but the reconciliation logic, which she could follow line by line, which named the choices it had made about ambiguous sources, and which was — this is the word she used — defensible. It was a methodology she could put in a footnote on a slide and survive the question Marcus Tang would ask about it.

She took a screenshot. She sent it to Marcus on Slack with no message. Marcus, who was in a meeting in Northwind's twentieth- floor conference room, looked at his phone, looked again, and typed back two words: Holy shit. Lila — and I have this detail from Marcus, not from Lila — left her laptop on her desk and walked the four flights down to the conference room rather than wait for him to come out. They spent the next ninety minutes pulling the underlying data apart. By four in the afternoon Lila had booked a call with Cody. By six she had booked a call with the board.

VII.

What changed, and what hasn't.

The board met by video on the afternoon of Thursday the twenty- fourth of July. Lila presented for thirty minutes. She put the methodology on the first slide, the three numbers on the second, the agency-concentration breakdown on the third, and a proposed re-allocation on the fourth. She asked the board to approve a $1.6 million re-allocation of the current-year recruiting budget: cut agency spend from $4.2 million to $1.2 million, allocate $800,000 to a structured referral program with a tiered bonus and a quarterly review, and allocate $600,000 to inbound — a mixture of employer-brand investment, original editorial content on the work, and three design-conference sponsorships that the New York and London offices had previously argued for and lost. The board approved it on a voice vote. Cody, whom Lila had briefed the day before, did not speak during the meeting; he voted yes.

As of this writing, the re-allocation is two weeks old. The first agency contract has been wound down; two more are in conversation; the referral program is being designed by Ren and one of the LA partners; the first design-conference sponsorship has been booked for September. Lila does not know whether the re-allocation works. She is reporting back to the board on the fifteenth of October. What she does know — and this is, I think, the actual lesson of the case study — is that she has an answer she can defend, in a form that can be re-run quarterly, on a methodology that does not depend on her senior analyst manually reconciling four field taxonomies for fourteen hours a week. The agency spend may go back up. Cody has already said, in his dry way, that he expects it will, partially. But it will not go up by accident, and it will not go up without an argument from Lila that she can produce in forty-seven seconds rather than four days.

It would be dishonest to claim more than this. The Northwind story is not the story of a recruiting budget cut in half and a company transformed. It is the story of a CHRO who, for the first time in her career, can answer her CFO's question in the form her CFO asked it, and who therefore now has standing to have the conversation about whether the answer is right. The conversation about whether the answer is right is the conversation Lila has been waiting to have for nine months. We did not have it for her. We made it possible.

One thing I want to say plainly, because Lila said it plainly to me on a recorded call on the twenty-ninth of July and asked me to include it in the published piece. The numbers were there before Stratum. They were in our systems. They were just not in a form anyone could read. What Stratum did was not magic; it was the unglamorous work of joining tables I have been trying to get my analysts to join for eighteen months. What it gave me back was time. What I did with the time was have the conversation.

I do not know whether the October numbers will tell a good story. Neither does Lila. Neither does Marcus. Neither does Cody. We have agreed that, whatever the October numbers say, we will publish an update — under Northwind's review — in the January edition of the Customer Notes. If the re-allocation worked, we will say so. If it did not, we will say so, in the same place, with the same byline, with the same methodology, with Lila's review. That is the partnership we signed up for. It is, on every dimension I can presently see, the partnership we are running.
