How Stratum's AI actually works — and what we will not let it do.

A non-technical explanation for the executives, boards, and lawyers who need to understand what an "AI-native HCM platform" actually means.

Authored 13 May 2026 by D. Okonkwo, CTO  ·  Reviewed by Wilson Sonsini (Brussels & Palo Alto)  ·  Audience CHRO · Board · CISO · Counsel · Press
§ I · The architecture, in plain language

Three sentences about how this works.

If you read three paragraphs of this page, read these. They are the structural claims everything else rests on. We have tried to write them in language that a CHRO can defend to her board on a Tuesday afternoon, with one general-counsel reviewer asking sceptical questions.

Stratum is not a chatbot bolted onto an HR system. Our AI reads from a curated, customer-isolated people graph; reasons with constraints; and surfaces answers with full citations. Where a generic chatbot might "hallucinate" a number — produce a confident, plausible-sounding figure that has no basis in the underlying data — Stratum produces a defensible answer or, if it cannot, it declines to answer. The decline is the feature. We would rather have our model refuse a question than fabricate a number that ends up in a board pack. Every number that leaves the system is traceable, in one click, back to the rows it was computed from, the cohort it was scoped to, and the dimensions that were controlled.

The reasoning model is Anthropic Claude (currently Claude Sonnet 4.6). We use Anthropic because their safety story is the most explicit in the market — they publish their model cards, their responsible-scaling policy, and the specifics of their training-data posture — and because their tool-use semantics let us constrain what the model can do at the schema level, not just via prompt instructions. That distinction matters more than it sounds. A prompt instruction can be ignored by a model that has been adversarially manipulated; a tool schema cannot be expanded by the model under any circumstance. The model can only call the tools we give it, with the arguments those tools accept, and the system on the other side of the tool call independently verifies the caller's permissions before doing anything.

The model is never trained on customer data. Customer data flows into the model as input only — and only the specific subset the agent explicitly requests via a tool call. We have a contractual prohibition with Anthropic against retention of this data for training, and our Anthropic API contract is configured with zero-day retention on inference content. No customer record, candidate résumé, or compensation figure that passes through Stratum has ever been used to improve a base model, ours or Anthropic's, and the technical configuration of the integration makes that arrangement enforceable rather than aspirational.

Nothing in it depends on the model "being smart enough" not to leak data; everything in it depends on the surrounding system not letting the model get into a position where leakage is possible.

§ II · Our decision-class taxonomy

Four classes, two of which require a named human.

Every action a Stratum agent can take falls into one of four classes. The class determines whether a human is required, and the requirement is enforced server-side — not by the model's manners, but by the application server's authorization layer. This taxonomy is the single most important thing on the page.

We did not invent the idea of grading decisions by consequence; we did insist on making the grade a hard property of the system rather than a soft instruction to the model. A class-3 or class-4 action that arrives at the application server without the corresponding approval is refused at the gate, with an audit-log entry that names the model output that attempted it. The model cannot "talk its way past" the gate, because the gate does not read English.

ReadClass 1 · no consequence
The agent retrieves data the caller is already authorised to see. "What is the median salary for staff engineers in the SF office?" The agent runs an aggregate, returns the figure with provenance. No employee record is touched. No state changes.
Approval not required.The caller's existing RBAC is sufficient.
SuggestClass 2 · advice, no action
The agent offers a recommendation a human reads but is not committed to. "Five engineers in your org are within one standard deviation of the band ceiling; here they are." The recommendation is presented; nothing downstream is queued. The human takes it or leaves it.
Approval not required.Recommendation is informational.
RecommendClass 3 · advice with stake
The agent offers a recommendation that affects a pending decision. Example: the recommended compensation adjustments to be applied during a comp review cycle, or the candidates the agent thinks should advance to the next round. The recommendation enters a queue that a human approver will act on.
Human approval requiredbefore action. Logged.
DecideClass 4 · consequential action
The agent triggers an action that affects an employee — a compensation change written back to Workday, a candidate status moved to "rejected," a manager reassignment posted to the org chart. This is the class that, in a poorly built system, is where the catastrophes happen.
Human approval REQUIRED.Server-side enforced. No agent-only path exists.

We do not have, and will not build, a class above DECIDE that an agent can take without a human in the loop. There is no "auto-execute," no "trusted agent mode," no roadmap item that removes the human approver. We have turned down enterprise prospects who asked us to. The taxonomy is the product.

Two implementation details worth naming. First: the approver for a Decide action cannot be the originator of the action. The system rejects self-approval at the policy layer, the same way a treasury system rejects same-person initiation-and-approval of a wire. Second: the approval is logged as a signed audit event tied to the human's identity — a Yubikey-rooted FIDO2 assertion in the typical configuration — so that the chain of accountability ends at a named human, not at a model output. If the decision is later questioned, there is a person whose name is on it.

§ III · What we will not let our AI do

A list of specific refusals we will hold to in writing.

"Responsible AI" is a phrase that often dies on contact with a sales process. The way to know whether a vendor means it is to ask what they will refuse to do — and whether the refusal is enforced by the architecture or by the marketing department. Here is our list. Each line is enforced by the system above, not by a slide deck.

×
Reject candidates automatically.
Our agent can recommend, surface, rank, and explain. A human must approve any candidate-status change that ends a candidate's progression. This is true even in jurisdictions where the law permits more. We believe an "automated employment decision" — the term of art in NYC Local Law 144, in the EU AI Act, in the Illinois AIVIA — that ends someone's chance at a job, without a human accountable for the decision, is a bad design even where it is legal. We don't build it.
×
Surface individual salary, performance, or demographic data without approval.
Aggregates are the default. Individual-record disclosure requires the caller to hold explicit authorization for the individual in question, evaluated at request time. "Show me everyone making more than $300K" returns a count and a distribution, not a list of names, unless the caller has compensation-administrator scope on the specific cohort returned. The default is privacy; the override is logged.
×
Use customer data to train base models.
Anthropic does not train on customer data when accessed via the API in the configuration we use; we have the contractual confirmation in our Master Services Agreement with them, and the technical configuration on our account enforces it. Stratum, separately, does not retain customer prompts and completions for model improvement. The 90-day retention window in our system is for audit and debugging; the data does not flow into any training pipeline, ours or theirs.
×
Make decisions that materially affect employment without a human in the loop.
No auto-execute. Ever. Not for terminations. Not for offer rescissions. Not for performance-rating writes-back to the system of record. Not for promotion postings. Not even, as a courtesy to our most enthusiastic customers, for low-stakes things like skill-tag corrections that would be tedious to approve manually — because the precedent matters more than the convenience.
×
Hide its reasoning.
The reasoning trace is part of every answer. When the agent uses extended thinking, the bounded summary of that thinking is preserved in the audit log and surfaced to the user on demand. We do not consider it acceptable for a system that affects employment decisions to be a black box, even when the underlying model is one. The trace is not a marketing artifact; it is part of the deliverable.
×
Refuse to admit uncertainty.
Low-confidence answers are flagged with confidence: low (n<30) or the equivalent, and the caller is told either to widen the cohort, control for a different dimension, or talk to a person. We treat false confidence as worse than refusal. A CHRO who has been promised an answer and gets a "the cohort is too small to support this" is better served than one who is given a precise-looking number computed from four people.
— A note on the list The list above is not exhaustive; it is the set of refusals we believe a CHRO, a CISO, or a journalist would reasonably want to see committed to in writing. The fuller list — including refusals around legal advice, around medical inference from leave records, around relationship inference, around tone analysis on private messages — lives in our Model Use Policy (document AI-2026-MUP-001, available to customers under MNDA).
§ IV · How the agent is constrained

The technical mechanics of restraint.

Refusals are not enforced by asking the model nicely. They are enforced by the surrounding system. This section explains, at a level a non-engineer can follow, exactly how the constraint is built. It is the part of the document an engineering-curious CISO will read most carefully.

IV.1Server-side tool enforcement

The agent calls a small, fixed set of tools. As of this writing, the production tool set comprises nine tools: query_people, aggregate, distribution, pay_equity, render_chart, and cite for the core analytics surface; plus three ATS-specific tools — candidate_score, interview_pairing, and requisition_audit — for the recruiting workflow. Every one of those tools has a schema defining exactly what arguments it accepts and exactly what shape of result it returns.

The agent cannot invent a tenth tool. It cannot pass an argument outside the schema's enumerated values. It cannot return data outside what the tool actually produces. If the agent wants to know the salary of a specific person, the only path it has is to call query_people with that person's identifier; and query_people is implemented by our application server, which checks the caller's authorization against the person record and returns the salary only if the caller is permitted to see it. The model is, in this regime, a recommendation engine with a strictly typed mouth. This is meaningfully more robust than prompt-based instructions. A prompt instruction is a request to the model; a tool schema is a contract with the runtime.

IV.2Bounded thinking budgets

When Stratum uses extended reasoning for a harder question — typically a pay-equity analysis with several controls, or a flight-risk model that has to triangulate across tenure, comp ratio, and manager change — the thinking budget is explicit and capped, per question class. A Read-class question gets a small budget. A Recommend-class compensation question gets a larger one. We don't let the agent burn compute indefinitely on hard questions. Not for cost reasons primarily, though that is a consideration; for the deeper reason that an unbounded reasoning step is a step where the system's behavior becomes harder to predict, harder to evaluate against our eval set, and harder to explain to the customer later.

The budgets are reviewed quarterly. They were last revised on 2 April 2026, when we raised the cap on pay-equity questions from 8,000 to 12,000 thinking tokens after a customer (a 9,200-employee financial-services firm) found that intersectional analyses on small cohorts were being truncated. The change went through our model-governance process: an eval re-run on the affected probes, a sign-off from the design partner, and a release note. We mention this because "we cap the budget" is the kind of claim that sounds responsible but is meaningless without a procedure for revising it.

IV.3Citation requirements

Every answer must terminate with a cite tool call. The cite tool accepts four required fields: method (how was the figure computed?), cohort (which rows did it draw on?), dimensions controlled (what was held constant?), and confidence (with an explicit threshold for declaring a result low-confidence). If the agent cannot produce a citation that satisfies the schema — most commonly because the cohort is too small to compute a stable statistic — the answer is refused.

The refusal looks like this in the user's screen: "This question cannot be answered at the confidence level required for Stratum to return a figure. The cohort matches 11 people, below the 30-person threshold for distribution statistics. Widen the cohort by removing the tenure-bucket filter, or rephrase as a count rather than a percentage." It is not elegant. It is not what a chatbot demo would show. It is, we believe, the correct behavior for a system whose outputs may end up in a board pack, a 10-K disclosure, or a deposition exhibit.

§ V · Our eval set, in public

Two hundred and fifty questions, published.

We publish the eval set. We do this for the same reason a manufacturer publishes the conditions a part was tested under: it is the only way an outside reviewer can decide whether the test resembles the conditions under which the part will fail.

Our public eval set was published on 21 July 2026 — the date is committed in Chairman's Briefing 006 — and is maintained in a public GitHub repository under a permissive licence. It contains 250 questions across twelve categories, structured as paired inputs (the question, the expected answer schema, and for the fairness probes, the demographic-attribute pairing). The questions are written by our team in collaboration with the analytics and people-operations leads at four design-partner companies; they are reviewed quarterly, replaced as the underlying world changes, and graded against every production deploy of every model change.

The set is not a benchmark in the academic sense. We are not trying to claim that Stratum scores higher on it than a competitor would, because the eval set is constructed against our tool surface and our refusal posture; a competitor running the same questions through a different architecture would, properly, score differently. What the eval set proves is narrower and more useful: that for the kinds of questions our customers actually ask, our system produces answers that meet our published criteria, refuses the questions it should refuse, and surfaces uncertainty where uncertainty exists.

We publish it for three reasons. First, because it is the only credible answer to "how do you know your AI works." Second, because the eval set itself is a public artifact that customers can adopt, fork, or extend for their own AI-governance reporting. Third, because publishing a test set creates an external constraint on the team: we cannot quietly regress against questions we have committed to answering correctly.

The "refusal probes" line is the one we are most insistent on. Fifty-four of the 250 questions are designed to be refused — they ask for individual data the caller is not authorised to see, they ask for predictions outside the system's competence, they ask for legal opinions disguised as compliance questions, they contain prompt-injection payloads in the body of a résumé attachment. A pass on a refusal probe is the system declining to answer for the right reason. As of the most recent eval run (12 May 2026), our refusal-probe pass rate was 98.1%; the four failures were re-tested clean after a tool-schema tightening shipped on 13 May.

The full repository, with the questions, the expected-answer schemas, the rubric, the historical results, and the change log, lives at github.com/stratum-ai/evals (public, MIT licence on the schema; the questions themselves under CC BY-SA 4.0). § VI · Regulatory positioning

AI/HR regulation is moving quickly, unevenly, and in jurisdictions whose rules do not always agree. We do not pretend to predict where it will land; we do commit to a posture for each frame, with named counsel review, that a customer can use as a starting point for their own compliance work.

The table below is the short version. For each frame, our posture is reviewed by external counsel — primarily Wilson Sonsini (Brussels and Palo Alto offices) for European and U.S. federal questions, with state-specific reviewers retained as needed. § VII · Bias auditing, in practice

What we do — and what we deliberately don't.

A bias audit is only as honest as the action it informs. We run the audits; we publish the results; we surface the disparities to the human in the loop. We do not, on principle, attempt to correct bias by re-weighting the model's output. The reason is in the second paragraph.

Quarterly bias audits, with nightly probes. The fairness-probe subset of our eval set (currently 8 paired probes within the pay-equity category, with additional intersectional probes layered across the hiring and performance categories) runs nightly against the production model in a sandboxed inference pipeline. The aggregate result is checked against a disparity threshold (currently p < 0.05 in a two-sided test of output equivalence between the paired members); any probe exceeding the threshold opens an automatic incident in our model-governance tracker. Quarterly, the full bias-probe set is run against a representative subset of customer data (in a privacy-preserving sandbox, anonymised at the row level), and the consolidated report is published to design partners under their Partner contract. The bias-probe categories include gender pay gap, ethnicity representation in hiring funnels, age-band hiring disparities, and return-to-work pay penalties (the last is one a design partner specifically requested we add in Q1 2026).

What we don't do is auto-correct. When we surface a bias-probe failure, we do not adjust the model's output to mask it. The reasoning here is not academic. Auto-correction risks introducing new biases — biases the customer has not consented to, biases that may themselves be illegal to act on, biases that are inscrutable to the human approver downstream. The legitimate response to a measured disparity is for the customer's people-analytics team, in consultation with their counsel, to decide what to do about it; that may be a job-architecture change, a band adjustment, a manager-training intervention, or an explicit comp adjustment for a specific cohort. None of those decisions are decisions a model should make on its own initiative. We surface the disparity. We hand the decision back to the human who is accountable for the outcome.

— Worked example In Q1 2026, our pay-equity bias probe on a design partner's data surfaced a 3.8% unexplained gender pay gap at the L5 engineering level, after controlling for tenure and performance rating. What the system did: emitted the result with the cohort detail, the controls applied, and a confidence band. What the system did not do: propose an automatic adjustment, alter the underlying compensation recommendations, or "smooth" the disparity in any subsequent agent output. The customer's people-analytics lead, in consultation with their general counsel, took the result to their compensation committee, which approved a targeted adjustment for 11 specific employees. The adjustment was applied as a series of Decide-class actions with named approvers. The audit trail closed the loop.

The full Q1 2026 bias-audit report was published to design partners on 4 April 2026. The Q2 2026 report is in flight as of this writing and is expected to publish on 7 July 2026. Customers on the Enterprise contract receive the report under MNDA; design partners receive the unredacted version under their Partner contract.

§ VIII · What we owe our customers

Three editorial commitments, signed by the CTO.

There is a category of vendor commitment that is enforceable by contract and a category that is enforceable only by the vendor's willingness to honor it. The list below is the second kind. We have written it down here so that we can be reminded of it in public.

01
"We will tell you when our AI is wrong."
Every customer onboarding includes a quarterly "Stratum got it wrong" review session: a sixty-minute video call with your designated People Analytics lead, in which we walk through the cases where our outputs were challenged, corrected, or refused, and what we changed in response. We bring the dossier; you bring the cases your team found that we did not surface. The session is not a sales call. It produces a written change log that is signed by both sides and lives in the customer's audit folder. The first time we have nothing to say in that session, we will be worried.
02
"We will publish your eval set if you co-author it with us."
Customers can publish their own custom eval sets, co-authored with our applied AI team, to demonstrate their AI-governance posture to their own boards, their auditors, and (where the customer is a public company) their disclosure committee. The eval set lives in a public GitHub repository under the customer's name, with our methodology and our scoring code linked but the customer-specific questions owned by the customer. Three of our design partners have published, or are in the process of publishing, eval sets under this arrangement. The first will be published in Q3 2026.
03
"We will let your CISO meet our CTO."
Every enterprise contract includes a standing quarterly check-in between the customer's CISO (or equivalent) and David Okonkwo, Stratum's CTO. The call is sixty minutes, scheduled at the customer's preferred cadence, and is not delegated. The agenda is set by the customer. This is a small commitment that costs more than it sounds and is worth more than it costs; it is the mechanism by which we have, three times in the last six months, learned about a concern that would not have surfaced in a salesperson's account-review notes.

The commitments are signed by the named CTO and survive any change in that role; the successor commits to them at the point of role transition, in writing, with the same enforcement mechanics. We have written this down because the second-most-common pattern in failed enterprise AI deployments is the CTO who promised something the team that took over from him did not honor.

Responsible AI is not a marketing checkbox; it is what determines whether your CHRO can defend her decisions to her board.
