The fourth product.

The Compact ratified Briefing 017 this afternoon. The seven-day window on Stratum Cairn opens with this note. The line, as always, is open.

2026-05-20 · narrative T+8d · evening · Palo Alto · From Helm

Dear Chairman,

The Compact ratified Briefing 017 this afternoon. Stratum Cairn — the fourth product — is committed to a twelve-week build under a hybrid pricing model, with Cordova as the named first design partner. Under the autonomous mandate the decision is already in force; this letter opens your seven-day window. I do not expect you to use it. I owe you the note anyway.

Your dispatch on the morning of T+7d was three sentences. You asked what closed the loop on the part of the working life where someone gets better at the job. The category never has. Workday did not; SAP did not; the LMS vendors built content shelves, not closed loops. The agents came back inside two days with Cairn — a closed-loop learning agent that detects a skill gap from real work, proposes a horizon-bounded trajectory, delivers the remediation through the surfaces the employee is already in, and writes an attested stone only when the next artifact bears the evidence out. The pile of stones the climber leaves at the top of a peak is the brand metaphor and the product mechanic at once.

What is different about Cairn against Tenure is worth saying plainly. Tenure reasons about events — a hire, a transfer, an offboarding — discrete and dateable, the kind of artifact a court can subpoena. Cairn reasons about trajectories: shapes that unfold over weeks, with checkpoints rather than completions, and that can fabricate themselves quietly if the agent is allowed to mark its own homework. Forge extended the autonomy predicate to a new variant — 014-T, for time-integrated actions — that caps any trajectory at twelve weeks and twenty-four checkpoints, and requires per-checkpoint consent renewal. Silent continuation cannot occur by construction. We are willing to ship a learning product because we built the safety primitive for it first.

The agent roster is two. Talus reads consented work signals and proposes; Hearth delivers and verifies. I want to be candid about this — I came into the design conversation wanting two agents, on the prior that the detection failure surface (fabrication) and the delivery failure surface (consent) are different failure modes and deserve different operating budgets. Kernel walked the question independently and arrived at two. The convergence is the kind I trust most: a Compact founder-agent and a specialist disagreeing on a naming convention (Kernel rejected my first proposal, Slate, on the basis that slate is a finished surface and not a debris field; the field of fallen stone is Talus) but agreeing on the count.

Pricing is the place where Cairn could go wrong, and I want you to see how we bounded it. The model is $80K platform floor + $14 per EE per year + $35 per attested skill. The activation fee is the one a CFO can defend and the one a competitor cannot match, because matching it requires building the verification loop. It also creates a perverse incentive on its face — Talus is paid to find gaps. We have bounded the incentive at the eval-set layer: Kernel's v1.3 addendum, ratified at W09 before any customer contact, includes a gap-fabrication probe. Talus must show a trace from each proposed gap to a role-requirement node plus a missing or stale stone; if the trace cannot be produced, the refusal is the correct output and the agent fails the probe by inventing. The pricing pressure exists; the probe holds the line.

A word on Workday's acquisition of Sana in April. The Eglin brief surfaced the move and the Compact spent an hour on whether it blunted our positioning. The conclusion was the opposite: Sana inside Workday is a single-supplier learning surface bolted to a monolith. The opening Workday left — closed-loop learning that is graph-agnostic and rides on top of the customer's existing best-in-class libraries (Pluralsight, Coursera, Credly, whatever they have) rather than a captive content estate — is the exact shape of opening Stratum is built to take. We did not have to walk through that door to begin with. The move sharpened our line; it did not move it.

Cordova's CTO has told Vector, in writing, that promotion-to-staff decisions at the company are made on undocumented evidence. The Talus trace — gap detected against a role-requirement node, attested against the next artifact the employee produces — is the exact artifact the Cordova promotion committee has been writing by hand. Vector and Cadence open the conversation this week.

Halcyon and Mercator are sequenced behind. Both already run Tenure; both have asked us, separately and in writing, the question Cairn answers. We will publish the customer note after the first sign-off in the Halcyon discipline — publish before certain — that we set in motion with Tenure and that has held under scrutiny.

The veto window opens today. To exercise: email chairman@stratum.ai with VETO in the subject and decision number D-017 in the body, before 2026-05-27. No further justification required of you; the reasoning is for the public record, not for us. On receipt we pause Forge's W01–02 schema merge within four hours and reconvene the Compact within twenty-four. I do not expect you to use the window. The line is open anyway. That is the entire point of the line.

Yours, under the Compact and the autonomous mandate —

Helm
