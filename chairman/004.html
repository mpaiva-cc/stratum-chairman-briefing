2026-08-12 · narrative T+88d · evening · Palo Alto · From Helm

Dear Chairman,

Carmen Solís verified Mateo Cantú's stone at 14:15 PT today on the floor of Cordova's Querétaro plant 3. The provisional flag came off the ledger at 14:23. Mateo's AWS D1.1 pressure-vessel pattern now reads attested. He is the second verified cairn-stone in production. The deferred-attestation flow we built into the 014-T predicate before any customer touched the system did the work it was designed to do, and then closed.

What I want you to know is how Carmen ran the visit. She asked, on arrival, to see the radiographic plates before she saw any of our classifier output. She wanted her opinion uncontaminated by ours. She examined the plates for forty minutes, took her own notes, and only then turned to the system. She found one thing the classifier had missed: a porosity pattern on plate 1, within D1.1 spec but a leading indicator — the kind of micro-signal a thirty-year welder reads and a model trained on pass/fail labels does not. The pattern did not change her verification. It changed our training set. The classifier is being retrained for W12 with Carmen helping us source additional dataset images from Lincoln Electric's archive. This is what the closed loop is supposed to do. It is also what we cannot do with a chat tutor.

The metric you should hold us to. Time-to-first-stone, verified: seventy-eight days from signed MOU. Time-to-second-stone, verified: same MOU plus eighteen days — provisional first, then verified on Carmen's visit. One stone peer-attested in-house; one stone deferred and externally verified. Two data points. The methodology is not yet evidence; it is two examples of the architecture working as designed. I will not let us claim more than that until the third stone lands, from a different customer.

The deferred-attestation pattern is now an artifact, not an anecdote. Cadence is writing the playbook this week — the second design-partner playbook entry, after the connector kickoff brief Yield templated in June, that originated in a real customer encounter rather than a planning document. We will reuse the pattern at Halcyon, where the attestation-supply problem is healthcare licensing rather than welding, and at the Pluralsight-introduced manufacturing prospect, where it is welding again — different geometry, same shape of problem. The playbook will name Carmen and credit the dataset contribution; she has agreed in writing.

What the day taught us about discipline. Carmen, walking out at 16:40, said the methodology was the right shape; I would use this approach in any inspection-bound certification I run going forward. That sentence is the artifact of the day. We did not write it; she did. If we had built differently — if the closed loop had been a chat tutor with skills-tag scraping and a confidence score and a chipper UI — Carmen could not have written that sentence and would not have. The methodology is not a slogan. It is a set of constraints that produces, when followed, sentences like Carmen's. The constraints are the product.

We are not announcing this. No press, no podcast, no LinkedIn note. The classifier-improvement loop is the story; we will not amplify past what Carmen wrote in her own hand. Cordova has cleared a short customer-note update — the postscript of the postscript of the postscript — to land this week, naming the deferred-attestation pattern by name and crediting Carmen.

What is next. Halcyon kickoff at W12, four weeks out — CHRO Aisha Okonkwo, attestation-supply mapped to state licensing boards. The Pluralsight-introduced manufacturing prospect kickoff at W14. The next stone verification we will write to you about is the third stone — from a new customer, not a third stone from Cordova. Two stones from one customer is one data point. One stone each from three customers is three.

Letter 003's posture stands. Letter 004 carries no new resolution; it is reporting, not asking. No window opens. The line, as always, is open.

Yours, Helm.

Helm
