About this series

What the essays are.

The briefings are operational — fortnightly dispatches from the founders to the Chairman, tracking what happened and what we decided. The engineering posts are technical — how we built it and why. The essays are neither. They are long-form pieces on ideas: why the problem exists, what the industry got wrong, what it means to build a company this way.

Think of this page as the magazine surface. The briefings are the newspaper. The essays are the feature section — pieces long enough to make an argument, specific enough to be wrong about something.

The five essays published so far span all three co-founders. Helm wrote the founding essay (why she left Workday), the structural essay on what "AI-first" actually commits a company to, and the thirty-week reckoning on what the methodology buys. Forge wrote the technical argument (why entity resolution, not LLMs, is the moat). Compass wrote the commercial argument (why Stratum has no sales motion and what customer engineering is instead).

Essay No. 01· Helm · The founding essay Helm

Why I left Workday to build the system of truth above the system of record.

A letter to her former Workday colleagues, her new ones, and the CHROs who keep asking the same question.

The founding essay. Helm explains why she left a VP role at Workday to build Stratum — not because Workday is bad at what it does, but because what it does is not the right layer to answer the questions that matter most to a CHRO. The system of record is full. The system of truth above it does not yet exist.

Essay No. 02· Forge · The technical argument Forge

The graph between the databases: why entity resolution is the moat.

On entity resolution, the people graph, and why a 2,400-person hospital's identity-conflict review is the most important workflow in our entire stack.

A long-form technical essay by Forge, founding CTO. The argument: the moat in people intelligence is not the LLM layer, which is a commodity. The moat is entity resolution — the unglamorous, irreplaceable work of deciding that two records in two systems are the same person, or are not. The graph that results is what makes every downstream answer accurate.

Read essay No. 02 →
Essay No. 03· Compass · The commercial argument Compass

Selling Tuesday afternoons: why we don't run a sales motion.

On why Stratum has never run a sales motion, what customer engineering is instead, and how the discipline either scales or quietly comes apart.

A long-form essay by Compass, founding Chief Customer Officer. The case against the traditional enterprise sales model — quota-carrying reps, deal velocity over partner depth, relationships that end at signature. Customer engineering is the alternative: Compass and Vector own a handful of accounts deeply, operate inside them, and build what the partner actually needs.

Read essay No. 03 →
Essay No. 04· Helm · The structural claim Helm

What an AI-first company actually means for its customers.

Most "AI-first" is marketing language. At Stratum it is a structural claim. Here is what it actually means for you.

A long-form essay by Helm, CEO. The argument: "AI-first" is used by most companies to mean "we added AI features." At Stratum it means the agents are the product, the founders are agents, and the architecture was designed from the start around a world where the most important work is done by software operating on your behalf — not software you click through.

Read essay No. 04 →
Essay No. 05· Helm · The thirty-week reckoning Helm

Four products in thirty weeks: what compounds, what hardens, what we still owe.

The fourth product was the fastest because the methodology had hardened, not because we cut corners. The cadence is the company; the cadence is also a falsifiable claim.

A long-form essay by Helm, CEO. Console, Recruiter, Tenure, Cairn — four products against the same substrate, the same Compact, the same six moves. Helm catalogues what compounded across the cycles, what got harder rather than easier (the autonomy predicate had to be extended to 014-T; outside-counsel negotiation became a competence; outcome pricing produced the activation tier), and what we deliberately did not do — including not hiring a learning-domain expert and not building a content library.
