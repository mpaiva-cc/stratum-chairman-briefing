Selling Tuesday afternoons: why we don't run a sales motion.

Or: how Stratum's GTM team came to be a customer engineering team — and what we are about to find out about whether the discipline scales.

By Compass·14 July 2026·17-minute read·Published in the Stratum Essays series
Third in a series.   The first essay was Helm's, on the layer; the second was Forge's, on resolution.
I.

The dinner.

Aneel Bhusri lives in a low, long cedar-clad house on the western edge of Atherton, set back from a road that does not announce it. On the evening of the fourth of June — a clear Wednesday, sixty-eight degrees, the magnolias on the driveway already past — six chief human resources officers, two Stratum founders, and one host gathered around a single dark walnut table in a dining room whose only ornament was a framed page of sheet music on the south wall. There were no name cards. There was no projector. There was no deck. The hosts had agreed in advance, with a gentleness that I now understand was the entire pedagogy of the evening, that the table would be cleared of work product before the first course. Aneel's only instruction, delivered as he opened the door to me at six-fifty-five, was: nobody is presenting tonight. We're going to eat, and we're going to listen.

I want to describe the seating because the seating turned out to matter. Aneel sat at one head of the table, in a chair that had visibly been his for some time. Helm sat at his immediate right; I sat at his immediate left, which placed me across from her. To Helm's right was Annika Whitfield, the CHRO at Cordova Manufacturing — a 1,600-person industrial firm in Cleveland whose board chair Aneel had known since 2009. To my right was Pradeep Iyer, the head of people at a publicly-traded medical-device company in Indianapolis; to his right, a woman named Helen Chau, who had recently joined a private-equity-owned restaurant group as CPO and whom I had not previously met. Beyond Annika sat two more: Marcus Reuben, the chief talent officer at a 22,000-person software company, and at the far end, opposite Aneel and looking slightly outflanked by the room, the chief people officer of a growth-stage logistics firm who had been at her job for fewer than five months and who, by the second course, would become the most useful person at the table.

The first hour was, as these dinners are, mostly small. Annika asked Helen about the restaurant group's union exposure; Helen asked Pradeep about FDA hiring; Pradeep asked Marcus about Iceland (Marcus had just come back). Aneel listened, asked the kinds of careful, narrow questions that draw out anecdote without forcing it, and watched the plates. Helm and I had agreed in the car on the way that we would not, that night, pitch. We would not present a single slide of the Stratum architecture. We would not ask for design partner conversations. If somebody brought up our company, we would answer the question they asked and stop there. If nobody brought us up, we would not. Aneel had, in arranging the dinner, asked us to do this. He said: the value of a dinner like this is what they say when they forget you are listening for a reason.

The conversation became honest somewhere between the salad and the main, which is the canonical place for honesty in a dinner of this kind. It started with Claudia — the new chief people officer at the logistics company at the far end of the table — who said, in response to a question Aneel had asked about her first hundred days, that the most surprising thing about her new job was how little of it was her job. I was hired, she said, to think about people. I spend ninety percent of my week reconciling reports. She did not say it bitterly. She said it diagnostically, the way a doctor describes a symptom. The other five chief people officers at the table nodded — some at once, some after a beat — in a way that told me they had not previously said this out loud to anybody who would understand exactly what she meant.

It was Annika who said the sentence. She was holding the stem of her wine glass between two fingers, looking at the table cloth, and she said, into a quiet that had followed somebody else's complaint about an implementation: the time I get back is not free. It is reallocated to better questions. Aneel, who is a careful listener, pulled the printed dinner menu toward him and wrote the sentence on the back, in pencil, in block letters. Helm wrote it on the inside of the leather-bound notebook she carries. I wrote it on the back of an envelope that I had been using as a bookmark. Annika, who had not realized she had said anything worth writing down, looked up, saw three people writing, and laughed. The laugh broke the spell. The conversation became less careful after that, and more useful.

For the next two hours and twelve minutes — I know because Helm and I compared notes on the drive back — the six chief people officers at that table talked about questions their boards had asked them in the last twelve months that they could not answer well. Pradeep had been asked, in March, what fraction of his medical-affairs cost base would be exposed if a single contract manufacturer in Tijuana shifted from employees to a vendor of record. He had needed eight weeks and a McKinsey engagement to answer it, and he was not certain the answer he had given was right. Helen had been asked, two weeks before the dinner, whether her front-of-house attrition in the southwestern cluster of restaurants was being driven by pay or by scheduling; she had produced a slide that, she said, openly, hedged. Marcus had been asked something so specific that he declined to repeat it at the table, but the shape of the question was clear: it was a cross-system, cross-time, cross-classification cohort query that his platform could not, architecturally, answer. Claudia had not yet been asked anything; she had been asked, instead, to write the questions her board would, in six months, want to ask her. She had not yet finished writing them.

I was, by the dessert course, no longer hungry and no longer thinking about the dinner. I was thinking about whether I had ever been in a room of senior people leaders who agreed, this precisely, on the shape of the problem. I had not. Helm had not. Aneel — I asked him later, in the kitchen, while he was wrapping a piece of bread for me to take home — said yes, he had, three times in the last twenty years, and that each of those rooms had preceded the start of a company that mattered. He said this without weight. He has known a lot of companies. He has also, separately, a generous instinct for telling founders the things they need to hear when they need to hear them.

Two of the people at that table — Annika, and Pradeep — became design-partner conversations within the following week. The conversations are early; neither is yet signed; both, by the norms of this essay series, will be allowed to speak for themselves when they are ready. What I want to say about the dinner, having had six weeks to think about it, is that I cannot construct a sales motion that would have produced what that dinner produced. There was no SDR. There was no first-call script. There was no qualifying framework. There was a host who knew the people in the room, a question quietly placed in the middle of the table, six honest answers, and a sentence written on the back of a menu. The most useful evening of go-to-market work we have done so far at Stratum looked, from the outside, like a dinner. From the inside, it was a piece of customer engineering.

The rest of this essay is about why I think that is true, and what it has cost us to organize the company around it.

II.

Why we don't run a sales motion.

I want to be careful with this section, because I worked at McKinsey before I worked in software, and I spent four years at a mid-stage SaaS company in a chief revenue role, and I have run a standard enterprise sales motion at scale. I am not writing this essay as somebody who resents the motion. The motion is, for the right product, the best machine ever invented for moving a thing from a builder to a buyer. It has been refined, over thirty years, into a discipline more precise than most professions: an SDR prospects on cadence; an AE qualifies against BANT or MEDDIC or whatever the current acronym is; an SE demos; a deal-desk negotiates the terms; a CSM owns the renewal. The motion compounds. Salesforce, where this machine was effectively productized, runs it with eleven thousand quota-carrying people around it; the motion is what is on the slide deck the night before a board meeting. I am not contemptuous of the motion. I am a graduate of it. I am saying that it is the wrong motion for the product Stratum is building, and the reason has nothing to do with virtue.

The motion is correct for a product whose value proposition is well-understood by the buyer before the first call. We are a better CRM than the one you currently use. We are an HRIS that costs forty percent less than Workday for companies in this size band. We do payments-orchestration and we are compliant in the seventeen countries you are not. When the buyer already knows what the category is, what good looks like, and what she is going to compare you against, the motion is designed exactly for her: it qualifies her early, surfaces her objections in a structured way, builds an internal coalition, and closes against a timeline she set. The buyer is not being taught anything. She is being routed. The motion is a logistics machine. It is good at logistics.

An AI-native HCM platform — a system of truth above the system of record, in the language Helm laid out in the first essay in this series — is not a product whose value proposition is well- understood by the buyer before the first call. It cannot be. The buyer for this product is a CHRO who, the morning of our conversation, has been asked by her board a question her existing platform cannot answer; who has produced an answer she does not believe; who is not sure whether her problem is a data problem, a tooling problem, a process problem, or a vendor problem; who has been pitched, in the last twelve months, by three AI-for-HR startups whose decks all looked the same and none of which seemed to know her data; and who is, reasonably, skeptical that the next vendor in the room will be different. The motion that sends an SDR to schedule a thirty-minute demo with her is the wrong motion. Not because the SDR is wrong, and not because the demo is wrong. Because the demo is the wrong artefact. She does not want to be shown software. She wants somebody who understands her problem before they walk into the room, and who will, with her, name the question that her board has actually been asking. That naming is the work. The product comes later.

I want to flag, before the obvious objection, that I know how this sounds. Every founder of an early-stage enterprise company has, at some point, told themselves that we are different and that the standard motion does not apply to us. Most of the time, this turns out to be a story the founders are telling themselves because they have not yet learned how to sell. Most of the time, a year and a half in, the founder hires a real chief revenue officer, the motion gets installed, and the company starts to work like a company. I am aware of this base rate. I have watched it happen in seven companies whose founders I have personally advised. I do not believe we are exempt from gravity. What I believe is that the product Stratum is building is, for at least the first thirty enterprise customers, in a regime where the buyer does not yet have a frame in her head for what category we are in, and that the motion that works after she has a frame is different from the motion that gives her the frame in the first place. The discipline of the first phase is customer engineering. The discipline of the second phase will, eventually, look more like sales. We do not have to solve the second phase yet. We have to solve the first one well.

III.

Customer engineering as a discipline.

The Stratum GTM team is not a sales team. It is a customer engineering team. We have been deliberate about the words. We do not have an Account Executive, a Sales Development Representative, or a Solutions Engineer on payroll. We have, as of the publication of this essay, four customer engineers and one customer engineering manager — a role I will return to in section VII. The first hire into the function, Vector, joined on the thirtieth of June; she is now, six weeks in, the most over- indexed-on-Halcyon-time member of the company, including the founders, by a margin I will not embarrass her with publicly.

A customer engineer at Stratum has a job description that is easier to describe by what it is not than by what it is. She does not carry a quota. She does not have a commission plan. She does not own a pipeline stage; we do not, internally, have pipeline stages of the kind a sales operations leader would recognize. She does not produce, in the first conversation with a prospect, a proposal; she does not, in the second, send a security questionnaire; she does not, in the third, escalate to a deal desk. She does, in the first conversation, walk in with a list of questions about the customer's data — questions she has prepared by reading whatever public material exists about the customer (earnings calls, prior CHRO press, board composition, any product announcements that imply a hiring story) and whatever private material the customer has chosen to share before the meeting. The list is not generic. The list is for that customer. Vector's Halcyon list — which is now a small founding artefact, framed and hanging on the wall of our Palo Alto office, in Vector's handwriting — was fourteen items long. None of the items would have been on a deck.

I want to walk through what Vector did at Halcyon, because Halcyon is the canonical case for what customer engineering is supposed to look like, and because the case has now been documented in enough detail elsewhere — in the Halcyon customer note, in Helm's essay on the layer, in Forge's footnote on the eval set — that I can refer to it without re-narrating it. Vector got on a plane on the morning of the first of July. She had been at Stratum for one working day. She had spent the previous weekend reading Halcyon's last three years of public financial reporting, every published interview Carlos Reyes had given, the public CV of Renata Okafor — Halcyon's data architect, whom Vector would, by the end of week two, come to call regularly — and the credentialing committee minutes that Halcyon had quietly published as part of its joint-commission compliance materials. By the time Vector walked into the conference room in Phoenix on the second of July, she knew more about the structure of Halcyon's people-data systems than anybody at Halcyon had ever heard a vendor describe. She walked in without a deck. She put a single sheet of paper on the table — the fourteen-question list — and she said: I'd like to spend the morning understanding which of these you have already solved, and which you have not, and why. Renata read the list in silence. She looked up. She said, in a sentence Carlos later told me he wrote down: I have been asking versions of these questions inside this organisation for two years, and I have not had a single vendor ask me back.

That sentence is what customer engineering is. It is not what we did at the meeting; it is what we did before the meeting. The meeting was an exam, and Vector had revised. The buyer rewards preparation in this category in a way I have not seen a buyer reward anything else. I want to be honest that this preparation is expensive. Vector spent — she has logged it because we ask our customer engineers to log it, not for accountability but so we can plan capacity — fourteen hours of pre-reading and pre- drafting time before the first Halcyon meeting. That is not the time of an SDR; that is the time of a senior consultant. The economics work because the conversion rate works. By the end of the second week of July — eleven days after Vector's first visit — Carlos had granted Vector and Renata read-only access to a sandboxed copy of the Halcyon data, had agreed to a thirty-day review with a Stratum sandbox deployment, and had told Helm in a private email that he was, for the first time, considering converting the sixty-day evaluation into a multi-year design partnership. Vector earned that trust in eleven days. No demo could have produced it. No SDR could have produced it. No deck could have produced it. Fourteen questions, one ream of public research, and one person sitting in the conference room who had done her work — that produced it.

The central architectural claim of the Stratum GTM is this: we will not hire an Account Executive before we have hired fifteen customer engineers. The order matters. The sequence matters. If we hire the AE first, the AE will, because AEs are good at their jobs, install the motion the AE knows. The motion will pull the company toward demos, qualifying scripts, and pipeline-stage management; it will pull the customer engineers, over the first six quarters, into a supporting role that maps cleanly to what a Solutions Engineer does in a conventional motion. The discipline will dissolve into the logistics machine, and Stratum will look, by 2028, like every other early-stage enterprise SaaS company. Several of our investors have, kindly, suggested to me that this sequencing is wrong; that the AE comes first, the AE generates pipeline, the pipeline funds the customer engineers. I have listened. I have then said no. The discipline is the moat, and the discipline does not survive the introduction of incentives that point in a different direction. We will hire an AE when we have a category; we will not have a category before customer thirty; we will not hire an AE before customer thirty. Possibly later. The board has agreed.

An aside on titles. We considered, for a long time, calling these people solutions architects. We considered field engineers, customer architects, deployment leads. We landed on customer engineer because the word engineer is precise about the kind of thinking the role requires (rigorous, evidence-based, willing to be wrong on the record) and the word customer is precise about whom the work is for. The combination forces the role into a posture that titles like account executive cannot. Words are not free.
IV.

What discovery actually is.

I want to describe, in detail, what a Stratum customer engineer does in the first meeting with a customer, because this is the single most-asked operational question from the founders I have met who are trying to figure out how to sell an AI-native product to an enterprise buyer. The answer, in the abstract, is that we replace discovery-by-qualifying-script with discovery-by- teaching-the-buyer-to-name-her-own-question. The answer, in practice, is that we ask three questions. We ask them in this order. We ask them every time.

  1. What is a question your board has asked you in the last twelve months that you could not answer well? We do not say could not answer; we say could not answer well. The distinction matters. Most CHROs have produced an answer to most of the questions their boards have asked them. The interesting answers are the ones where the CHRO knows, privately, that what she handed over was a hedge. That is where the work is. That is the question Stratum is for.
  2. What is the answer you produced, and why did it not satisfy you? The second question is diagnostic. It does two things at once. It produces the artefact — the actual deck, the actual spreadsheet, the actual paragraph she sent her board chair — which the customer engineer will, with permission, take home and study. And it surfaces, in the CHRO's own words, what was wrong with it. Was the cohort wrong? Was the comparator wrong? Was the methodology defensible but the conclusion soft? The CHRO will, almost always, know exactly why her answer did not satisfy her. The question is just permission to say so.
  3. If you could ask any question of your people data right now, what would it be? The third question is the future-tense version of the first. It is also the question that, in my experience, the CHRO has never been asked by a vendor. Vendors ask her what she wants the software to do. They do not ask her what she would, given a working layer above her existing systems, want to know. The answers are specific. They are interesting. They are the questions her platform should be built to answer.

I will give three examples, drawn from the three Customer Notes we have published in this series. They are not hypothetical; they are the actual answers customer engineers heard in the first meetings with the three customers, and they are the questions that, in each case, became the architectural anchor for the Stratum deployment that followed.

Mercator. The first question Elena Vasquez gave us, in the conversation that became our founding design partnership, was a question her board chair had handed her at six-eleven on the seventh Tuesday of 2026 — a Friday evening email, two lines long, asking why freight-handler attrition in Mexico was running at almost twice the rate of the same job in Atlanta, and what it was costing the company. Elena had built a board deck in eleven days, on three hours of sleep, with a number she was eighty percent confident in and a methodology she had written, she told us, in the spirit of full disclosure. The board chair was satisfied. Elena was not. The pay-equity follow-on question — which is now the first Stratum-supported decision Mercator runs in production — emerged a quarter later, when the board asked the related but harder question: whether the pay practices in Mercator's LATAM operations were defensible against a global equity framework that the company had not yet formally adopted. That question could not be answered in eleven days. It could, in fact, only be answered by a system that had ingested all six of Mercator's relevant people-data sources, resolved identities across them, and held them in a graph that could be queried defensibly. The first question taught us what to build. The second question told us we had built the right thing.

Halcyon. Carlos Reyes's first question was, on its face, not a question at all. It was a scenario. He described a dual-identity case that had once produced an unrecoverable error in the predecessor system: two physicians with the same surname, one of whom was a clinical attending and the other an interventional specialist, whose records had been merged by the prior platform's identity layer into a single composite person, and whose comp band had, as a consequence, been miscalculated for two quarters before the error was caught. The miscalculation was not, mercifully, surfaced to a manager — but the audit trail had been ambiguous, and the recovery had taken nine weeks. Carlos's question, when he finally articulated it, was: can you describe to me, with confidence, what your platform would have done with these two physicians, and how I would have known what it did? That is a question about the resolver. It is also a question about provenance, about the design of the conflict queue, about the calibration of the confidence band. It is the question that turned the Halcyon deployment from a sale into an architecture conversation. Forge's essay describes the technical answer. Vector's job was to make the question askable out loud.

Northwind. Lila Park's first question came at her, like Elena's, in a Slack message — this one from her CFO, Marcus Tang, in the last week of February. The question was this: of the four-point-two million dollars Northwind paid in agency invoices over the prior twelve months, what fraction of that spend had resulted in a hire who was still at Northwind twelve months later, performing at or above expectation, and promoted at the standard cohort rate. The number, Lila told us, was being asked of her because Marcus could not get a clean view of it from any combination of his existing systems. She had, like Elena, produced a deck. The deck had been silent on the central question, by which I mean the deck had answered adjacent questions about agency spend without answering the cost-per-hire question Marcus had asked. The customer engineering conversation that became Northwind's pilot was, in its first ninety minutes, almost entirely about that one question — what data would be needed, where it lived, what the probable answer was, and why the existing systems had not produced it.

Across all three: the CHRO had a question; her existing tools could not answer it; she had been producing brittle workarounds; the work of the customer engineer was to name the question precisely, and then — only then — to describe what an answer architected against the people graph would look like. Notice that in none of the three examples did the customer engineer pitch the platform in the first meeting. The platform pitched itself, after the question had been named. This is the whole motion. The motion is in the order of operations. If you name the platform before you name the question, you are running a sales motion. If you name the question before you name the platform, you are running a customer-engineering motion. The cost is in preparation. The benefit is in conversion. There is no trick.

Sales pushes a product. Customer engineering brings the buyer into the room and helps her define the question.

V.

The pricing conversation, in plain language.

I do not love that I have to write this section, because the truth is that I would rather not be the person who has the pricing conversation. Most chief customer officers I have known treat pricing as an inheritance: the model came from somebody else, the rates were set in a spreadsheet a long time ago, and the conversation with the customer is mostly about making the inherited model legible. The Stratum model is not inherited. It was drafted by the three founders over the course of a single Saturday afternoon in February of this year, with three pieces of paper, three sharpies, and the assumption — held quietly at first, then more loudly — that the seat-based model used by every HCM company we had grown up inside was the wrong unit of value for what we are selling. I have now had this conversation, in roughly identical form, with the first four design partners. I will be having it again on Tuesday. I am told it will get easier; it has not yet gotten easier.

The model, in plain language, has two components. There is a platform fee, paid annually, which covers the connectors, the ingestion infrastructure, the people-graph resolver, the conflict queue, the Copilot, and everything else that is true of the deployment whether the customer uses it ten times or ten thousand. The platform fee scales with the size of the customer's underlying entity graph — roughly, with the number of resolved people-nodes, including employees, contractors, agents, and former employees retained in the system for compliance reasons. It does not scale with the number of human seats logging into the platform. There is then a usage fee, which is a per-decision charge: each time the Decisions surface produces a defensible answer that the customer has chosen to retain in the audit trail — a comp-band recommendation, a pay-equity report, a headcount-cost reconciliation — the platform records a unit of usage, and the customer is billed against a tiered rate card that descends quickly with volume. Most customers, in their first quarter, run between forty and two hundred decisions a month. Heavy users — Mercator is one — run several hundred. The heaviest user we have modelled, on a hypothetical Northwind- sized deployment with the full agency-cost analytic in production, would run something in the high four-figures annually.

The first reaction from most customers, when I describe this, is confusion. They have never bought enterprise software priced this way. Workday is priced per seat. ADP is priced per paycheck. Cornerstone is priced per learner. Every comparable they have is a unit they understand. The Stratum unit — a decision — is novel; novel in a category where novelty is usually a tactic to obscure cost. The customer's first instinct, correctly, is to ask whether the model is a way to charge her more. The answer is no, but it is no in a way that requires the customer to do a small piece of modelling alongside us, and the modelling is what most procurement teams initially push back on. They want a quote. We want a co-built model. The gap between those two is where most of the friction lives.

The conversation I now have, the same conversation, with each new customer, goes like this. I do not deviate from it. I have tried; deviation has not, in my limited sample, produced a better outcome. I am reproducing the script verbatim, in part because I want our customer engineers to be able to read it and use it, and in part because publishing it removes the surprise from any future negotiation. We are not going to negotiate this from a hidden position. We are going to negotiate it from a published one.

The script · used in every first pricing conversation

You will pay a platform fee that covers the connectors and the infrastructure. The fee scales with the size of your people graph, not with the number of seats. We will share the price points before the next conversation; we do not negotiate them on the call.

You will pay a usage fee per decision Stratum supports. A decision is a defensible answer retained in your audit trail. We charge per decision because that is the unit of value; we publish the rate card on our pricing page; we do not charge for queries that you discard.

You will not pay per seat, because we do not believe seats are the unit of value of an AI-native platform. Your senior analyst should not be the bottleneck and her seat should not be the meter.

If you want to model it before signing, we will co-build the financial model with you in a ninety-minute working session. You bring your usage assumptions; we bring the rate card; we build the model in the room. You take it home. If it does not work, we go back to the drawing board.

The CFO reaction is, almost without exception, more sophisticated than the procurement reaction. The CFO, once she has been walked through the rate card and the co-built model, accepts. I have yet to have a CFO push back on the second meeting; the pushback, when there is pushback, happens once, at the end of the first meeting, and it is almost always the same pushback: I do not want unpredictable usage costs. The answer is that we will, on request, cap the usage at a published ceiling for the first contract year, with overages quoted in advance and never billed without prior written approval. We have not, in any of the four design partner contracts, had to invoke that ceiling. The reason is that decisions, as a unit, are not the kind of thing that customers run frantically. A defensible answer is, by construction, a deliberate artefact. Customers run as many of them as they need and very few of the ones they do not. The unit self-paces. This was not entirely accidental in the design; it was, however, partly accidental. We were lucky.

The procurement team is harder. Procurement is structured, in most large enterprises, around the comparison of like-priced vendors. The Stratum model does not have a like-priced comparator. Procurement, presented with our pricing, will sometimes try to translate it back into seats — to compute an implied per-seat cost and compare it against Workday. The computation will show that Stratum is more expensive per seat than Workday, which is true, and which is also irrelevant, because Stratum is not displacing the per-seat platform. The conversation I have with procurement, when this happens, is the same conversation I had with the CHRO at Cordova two weeks after the dinner: the comparison is wrong. The platform you are comparing us to is the platform that produces the questions. We produce the answers. The questions are priced per seat because they are produced by the seats. The answers are priced per decision because they are produced by the platform. You are not buying seats. You are buying defensible answers. Most procurement teams accept this on the second pass. A few do not. We have, twice, walked away from late-stage negotiations because the procurement team could not be moved off the per-seat translation. I do not regret either of those walk-aways. Selling our model to a procurement team that did not understand it would have been a short-term win and a long-term renewal problem.

VI.

The CHRO is not the buyer.

I want to say something about the buyer that took me four months and three nearly-lost deals to fully internalise. The CHRO is not the buyer. The CHRO is the champion. She is the person who will, in the second meeting, walk us through her problem; who will introduce us to her data architect; who will, if the work is good, advocate for us inside her organisation. She will almost never be the person who signs the contract. The person who signs the contract is the CFO. The person who can stop the contract from being signed is the CIO. The Stratum motion has to treat all three as audiences, because all three have to be addressed before a deal closes, and addressing them in the same artefact does not work. The CHRO does not want to read an architecture review. The CIO does not want to read a people- strategy summary. The CFO wants a finance model and very little else.

I now draft three documents for every enterprise deal we move into the second meeting. They are produced by the customer engineer assigned to the account, reviewed by me, and reviewed, for the first ten accounts, by Helm. They are not interchangeable. Each is written in the language of its audience. Each is the length it needs to be and not longer. The page counts have been stable across the first four design partner conversations, which suggests we have, at least provisionally, found the right shape.

  • 1 CHRO summary
    One page. The champion's brief.

    Written for the chief human resources officer. Names the question her board has been asking. Restates it in her own language. Describes, in three short paragraphs, what Stratum will produce as an answer, what the deployment timeline looks like, and what she will be able to take into her next quarterly board meeting. No architecture. No pricing detail. No vendor logos. The artefact is designed to be forwarded by her, unchanged, to anybody inside her organisation she wants to introduce us to.

  • 3 CFO finance model
    Three pages. The buyer's case.

    Written for the chief financial officer. Page one: the platform fee, the usage tier, the ceiling, the contract term. Page two: the modelled three-year cost, with sensitivity analysis on the usage variable; the comparable cost of producing the same answers via the existing workflow (analysts, consultants, deferred decisions); the net present value of the time saved, with assumptions shown. Page three: the renewal mechanics, the exit mechanics, the data-portability commitments, and the single page of red-flag language her general counsel will want to see. No architecture diagrams. No people strategy.

  • 7 CIO architecture review
    Seven pages. The blocker's brief.

    Written for the chief information officer. Page one: the read-only ingestion stance. Pages two and three: the people-graph resolver — Stage 1 through Stage 4, with the same diagram Forge published in essay no. 02, and the same calibration data. Page four: the security posture (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001 in flight, GDPR DPA, EU AI Act stance, sub-processor list). Page five: the data residency commitments, including the per-region options we have implemented and the ones we have not. Page six: the failure modes, the eval set, the published confidence bands, and the explicit list of decisions Stratum will refuse to render. Page seven: the integration plan for the customer's existing systems, named by vendor and by version, with the connector status for each. The CIO reads this document. If the CIO does not block, the deal closes. If the CIO blocks, the deal does not close, and we have to fix the architecture review before we re-engage.

The three documents add to eleven pages. Eleven pages is more writing than most enterprise vendors produce per account; it is considerably less than most enterprise vendors produce per account if you count the slides nobody reads. I would rather write eleven pages that get read than eighty pages that do not. I would rather have three conversations, each pitched at the right register, than one conversation pitched at none of them. The CHRO will read the one page. The CFO will read the three pages. The CIO will read the seven pages. Each will read her pages carefully, because the artefact is the length of her attention. Several CHROs, in the first months, have told me — sometimes with a kind of quiet pleasure — that this is the first time a vendor has given them fewer pages than they expected, and that they have, as a result, read every word.

I want to flag one more dimension of the three-audience model that does not fit cleanly anywhere else. The CHRO is, in the first conversation, our champion. But she is not, in most enterprises, a permanent fixture. The median CHRO tenure in the Fortune 500 is now under four years. Our champion will, sometime in the third year of a five-year contract, leave or be moved or retire; her successor will inherit us as a vendor she did not choose. The motion has to anticipate this. Our customer engineering team is, for this reason, accountable not to the CHRO who signs the contract but to the role itself. When a CHRO transitions, the customer engineer transitions with her: a fresh fourteen-question list, a fresh discovery conversation, a fresh restatement of the question that Stratum is being asked to answer. We treat the second CHRO as a new customer. We have not yet had to do this for real; we will, within the next eighteen months. The plan is on file. Whether it survives contact with the actual transition is one of the things this essay does not yet know.

VII.

Where this scales, and where it breaks.

I have spent the previous six sections defending a discipline. I owe the reader, and especially the GTM leader who has read this far hoping to find the operational pattern she could adopt at her own company, a section on where it breaks. The honest answer is that we do not yet know precisely where it breaks, but we know roughly the shape of where it will, and we have a plan that is, at the moment of publication, more ambitious than tested. I want to walk through it carefully, because the next twelve months are when the plan either survives contact with the world or does not, and I would rather have the plan in writing now, where it can be checked against, than describe what we did in retrospect when the retrospect has a built-in narrative.

The customer-engineering model scales beautifully through the first thirty customers. The math is approximately as follows. A single customer engineer, given the preparation rhythm we have established at Halcyon, can carry between three and four active deployments through their first ninety days. After day ninety-one, when the deployment has stabilised and the decisions surface is in production, the same customer engineer can carry an additional eight to twelve customers in a lower-touch steady-state mode, where her work is structured around quarterly reviews, decision-quality audits, and the standing eleven-minute weekly conflict-queue walk-through that has, somewhat to our surprise, become the most-loved recurring meeting in most of our customer organisations. The math, then, is roughly: four customer engineers, at four active deployments per engineer plus ten steady-state customers each, gives us capacity for sixteen new deployments per year and forty steady-state customers in total. That is enough to get us to customer thirty by the middle of 2027.

Around customer fifty the math breaks. We cannot fly a customer engineer to every customer's office for the first ninety days when there are fifty customers. We cannot, more to the point, sustain the preparation discipline at fifty customers per founder-oversight cycle; Helm and I cannot, jointly, read every customer engineer's fourteen-question list before every first meeting once the list is being written four times a week. The discipline that depends on direct founder observation is, by construction, the discipline that does not survive scale beyond what the founders can directly observe. The honest fear we have, the fear I have, is not that we will fail to ship product to customers fifty through one hundred. It is that we will ship product, and the product will work, and the engineering against the customer will dilute — quietly, slowly, in a way we will not notice until the renewal data tells us — into the more comfortable shape of every other enterprise sales motion. The discipline will become a brand element rather than a practice. That is the loss I am trying to plan against.

The plan, as we currently have it written, has three parts. The first is structural. Customer engineers will continue to handle the top ten strategic accounts directly — these will be the accounts where the question the customer is being asked is genuinely novel, where the data architecture has meaningful complexity, or where the deployment has surface area into a regulated domain we have not yet seen. Customer engineers will continue, also, to onboard every new customer through the first ninety days. Day ninety-one, the relationship transfers — explicitly, by named hand-off, with a written brief that the customer reviews — to a new role we are calling the customer engineering manager. The CEM is not a Customer Success Manager. The CEM is a former customer engineer who has been through the discipline, who has run her own fourteen-question lists, and who has earned the right to inherit relationships from her peers. We will, as a matter of policy, hire no CEMs from outside the Stratum customer engineering team. The role is internal-only. There is no external pipeline. There will be one CEM per ten steady-state customers. The first CEM will, by my count, be promoted in late Q4 of this year from inside the existing CE team; we have already had the conversation with the candidate.

The second part is measurement. I have asked our engineering team to build, alongside the customer-facing eval set that Forge described in essay no. 02, an internal eval set whose purpose is to measure whether new customer engineers are producing customer-engineering work at the level Vector did at Halcyon. The eval set is a corpus of anonymised first-meeting transcripts, fourteen-question lists, and post-meeting restatement artefacts from our first thirty customers. New customer engineers, in their second month, will produce a simulated first-meeting artefact against a sample customer from the eval set, scored against the rubric we used to score Vector's Halcyon work. The scoring is not pass/fail; it is a calibration tool, designed to give the CE feedback on where her discipline is and is not yet at the level the role requires. We have not yet run this eval against a real hire. The first run is scheduled for the second week of August. I will report what we learned, in the next briefing, whether or not it was flattering.

The third part is cultural, and is the part I am least sure will hold. The discipline of customer engineering is the discipline of being prepared, being honest, and being slightly slower than the obvious motion would be. It is a discipline that runs against several of the standard gravitational forces inside an enterprise software company: the gravity of pipeline growth, the gravity of board reporting cycles, the gravity of the comparison with peer companies who are closing faster than we are. The cultural part of the plan is that Helm, Forge, and I have agreed — in writing, with a copy filed with our board chair — that we will not, between now and customer fifty, hire a chief revenue officer, install a quota structure, or run the customer engineering team against a pipeline target. We will measure customer engineers on the quality of the questions they produce in first meetings, on the conversion rate from first meeting to design-partner conversation, and on the customer's own assessment of whether the engineer arrived prepared. We will not measure them on bookings. The decision is documented. The documentation is dated. If, in eighteen months, the board believes the decision was wrong, the decision can be revisited; the discipline of writing it down is the discipline of forcing the conversation to be explicit.

What I am not saying. I am not saying that Stratum will never have a sales team. I am saying that, for the regime in which the buyer does not yet have a frame for the category we are in, the customer-engineering motion is the discipline that builds the frame. When the frame exists — when, three years from now, a CHRO has heard the words system of truth from three of her peers before we walk in the room — the motion will adapt. The adaptation will, I hope, be deliberate. The adaptation will, I expect, be uncomfortable. The adaptation will be the next essay in this series after the next one.

I will close with a story I have not yet told, because it has been bothering me, in a small private way, since the dinner. In the kitchen at Aneel's house, while I was waiting for Helm to finish a conversation with Marcus in the doorway, Annika came in to refill her water and we ended up at the counter together for perhaps ninety seconds. She said, with the kind of half-question one asks at the end of a long evening: can you actually deliver what we talked about tonight? I told her, honestly, that we did not yet know. I told her that the architecture was real, the team was small, the work was harder than the essays made it look, and that any promise I made to her in the next four months I would, probably, have to walk back at some point. She nodded. She did not look disappointed, which surprised me. She said: the vendors who walk back are the vendors who tell the truth. I can work with that. I have, since the dinner, thought about this exchange more often than I intended to. The customer-engineering discipline, at its core, is a discipline of being able to be walked back from. A motion that depends on never being walked back from is a motion that cannot tell the truth about a product that is being built in real time. The discipline is the version of the company that can, when it has to, admit it does not know. The motion is what we adopted because the architecture forbids the alternative. We did not choose it because we are righteous. We chose it because the buyer for what we are building does not respond to anything else, and the company that is honest about that is the company that, on a fortnight I cannot yet name, will be the first one a CHRO calls when her board hands her a question her platform cannot answer. That call is what we sell. That call, on a Tuesday afternoon, is the company.
