§ 1 · The standards we hold ourselves to

Standards we follow.

Accessibility is not one standard. The most important is WCAG, but procurement officers and government counterparties often need to see specific framework names. Here are the standards we measure ourselves against, in the order they matter for our customers.

  • WCAG 2.1 Level AA · our target
    Our operating standard. The W3C's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.1 at Level AA — the standard most enterprise and public-sector customers expect. Most pages on the public site and the Console conform; some pages have documented gaps (see § 4).
  • WCAG 2.2 Level AA · target T+200d
    The newer revision (W3C Recommendation, October 2023) adds nine criteria around focus appearance, dragging movements, target size, accessible authentication, and consistent help. We expect to meet most of them today; a full conformance audit is planned T+200d, with an updated statement to follow.
  • Section 508 U.S. Federal · 36 CFR 1194
    We follow the Revised 508 Standards as published in 36 CFR Part 1194 (effective January 18, 2018), which incorporate WCAG 2.0 Level AA by reference for web content. Customers buying on behalf of a U.S. federal agency: we will provide a VPAT 2.5 (Section 508 edition) on request.
  • EN 301 549 EU · v3.2.1
    The European harmonized standard cited by EU public-sector procurement (Directive (EU) 2016/2102). EN 301 549 v3.2.1 also references WCAG 2.1 Level AA for web. EU customers: a VPAT 2.5 (EU edition) is available on request and is the basis we use for the EAA (Directive 2019/882), applicable to in-scope products from 28 June 2025.

Where these standards overlap — and they overlap on nearly every web-content criterion — we treat WCAG 2.2 AAA as the design target (O-1) and WCAG 2.1 AA as the minimum floor already required by law for most of our enterprise customers. Where they diverge (mostly in PDFs and document accessibility), we follow the strictest requirement that applies to the customer's context.

§ 3 · The eight checks we run on every release

Tested and verified.

Conformance is a claim; testing is the receipt. These are the checks we run on every page before merge, and on every release before deploy. Eight categories, each with a defined method.

WCAG 2.1.1 · 2.1.2

Keyboard navigation

Every interactive element reachable by Tab in a logical order; no keyboard traps; visible focus on all controls; Esc closes any modal we open. Verified manually on Chrome, Firefox, Safari.

WCAG 4.1.2 · 1.3.1

Screen reader compatibility

Tested with NVDA (Windows / Firefox), JAWS (Windows / Chrome), and VoiceOver (macOS / Safari and iOS / Safari). Names, roles, and states announced correctly on every interactive element.

WCAG 1.4.3 · 1.4.11

Color contrast

Minimum 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text and non-text UI components. Measured with WebAIM Contrast Checker and our own design-token contrast tests. Our ochre on cream is verified at 4.81:1.

WCAG 1.3.1 · 2.4.6

Headings & semantics

Every page has exactly one <h1>; heading hierarchy is logical and never skipped. Landmarks (nav, main, footer) are used as semantic HTML, not divs with ARIA. Verified by axe-core and manual rotor inspection.

WCAG 3.3.2 · 1.3.5

Form labels

Every form input has an associated <label>. Required fields are programmatically marked with aria-required. Autocomplete tokens are applied where applicable (email, given-name, tel, etc.).

WCAG 1.1.1

Alternative text

Every meaningful image has descriptive alt text. Decorative images use alt="" and are hidden from assistive tech. SVG icons used as buttons carry an accessible name; SVGs used as illustration are marked role="presentation".

WCAG 2.3.3

Reduced motion

Every animation, transition, and scroll-driven effect respects prefers-reduced-motion: reduce. The 90-second tour switches to a static, scroll-anchored summary when reduced motion is on. The Console's streaming token animation collapses to the final state.

WCAG 1.4.1

Color independence

No information conveyed by color alone. Status pills always pair a color with a text label. Charts always pair a color with a marker shape or pattern. Errors are conveyed with text, icon, and color — not color alone.

In addition to the per-page checks, we run axe-core across the full site in CI on every pull request. The build fails if a previously-passing page regresses on a Level A or AA criterion. Failures cannot be silenced — they can only be fixed or explicitly marked as a documented gap in this statement.

§ 4 · Where we fall short, and what we do about it

Known accessibility gaps.

We will not claim conformance we have not earned. Items 01–04 are the prior gaps from the T+115d audit. Items 05–09 are new WCAG findings from the 2026-05-15 AAA baseline. Item 10 (A11Y-DS01) is a design-system hygiene issue excluded from the WCAG count. Items marked Closed were fixed in the same dispatch that found them.

Gap01 WCAG 1.1.1 · 1.3.1 In progress

Console charts (Inspect view) lack full data-table alternatives

In the Console's Inspect view, charts have accessible names and short summaries but do not yet provide a complete tabular alternative for every series. This affects screen reader users who want to inspect the underlying numbers, not just the trend.

Workaround available today Every chart has an underlying data table reachable via the "show table" toggle directly beneath the chart. When prefers-reduced-motion is set, the table view is the default. The toggle is keyboard-reachable and announced.

Planned remediation: the full data-table alternative becomes the default representation for screen reader users (detected via heuristic rather than UA sniffing), with the chart as a visual companion. Target: T+160d.

Severity Moderate Tracking A11Y-22 Owner Forge / Console team Target T+160d
Gap02 WCAG 1.3.1 · 2.4.3 In progress

The 90-second guided tour (/tour/) is scroll-driven storytelling

The product tour at /tour/ uses scroll-driven progressive reveals. Screen reader users with linearized reading flow do not get the same experience — the choreographed sequence is not fully accessible.

Workaround available today A "text summary" link is the first interactive element at the top of /tour/. It opens a single, linear, fully accessible HTML page with the same content as the tour. It is keyboard-reachable, screen-reader-friendly, and works without JavaScript.

Planned remediation: a full accessibility redesign of the tour so the linearized version is not a separate page but the same page, with scroll-driven reveal as a progressive enhancement only. Target: T+200d.

Severity Moderate Tracking A11Y-31 Owner Marketing / Engineering Target T+200d
Gap03 WCAG 2.2.2 · 4.1.3 In progress

The Console answer card has a streaming-token animation

When the Console answers a question, the response streams in token by token. For some screen reader configurations the streaming creates excessive live-region noise, with each chunk re-announced as it arrives.

Workaround available today The Console has a "Show static result" toggle that disables streaming and renders the complete answer in one announcement. The toggle currently lives under Settings > Accessibility. It auto-activates when prefers-reduced-motion is on.

Planned remediation: surface the toggle as a discoverable control in the Console header (not buried in settings), with first-run detection when assistive tech is present. Target: T+150d.

Severity Moderate Tracking A11Y-09 Owner Console team Target T+150d
Gap04 WCAG 1.3.1 Partially fixed

Legacy briefings use less-semantic timestamp markup

Conversation-transcript timestamps in the briefings archive were originally rendered in a plain monospace span without a semantic <time> element. Briefings published after T+115d now use proper <time datetime="…"> markup. Pre-T+115d briefings still use the older markup.

Workaround available today The transcripts themselves remain fully readable and the timestamps remain visible — the gap is only in machine-readable semantics, not in human readability. Every transcript also has a downloadable plain-text alternative.

Planned remediation: bulk migration of all pre-T+115d briefings to the semantic <time> element. Target: T+180d.

Severity Minor Tracking A11Y-44 Owner Editorial Target T+180d
2026-05-15 baseline — new findings
Gap05 WCAG 2.4.1 · Level A Closed 2026-05-15

Skip-navigation link missing from 8 pages

Eight of nine audited pages had no skip-navigation link, leaving keyboard and screen reader users who need to bypass the nav on every page load without a path to do so. This is a Level A failure — it blocks a core task.

Fix shipped A skip link (<a href="#main" class="skip">) is now at the first position inside <body> on all affected pages. The .skip class is in assets/styles.css. Pages with inline CSS (mcp.html, graph/index.html) received a page-local copy of the rule.
Severity Critical → Closed Tracking A11Y-S01 Pages 8 of 9 Fixed 2026-05-15
Gap06 WCAG 2.4.7 · Level AA Closed 2026-05-15

No visible focus indicator system-wide

assets/styles.css had no :focus-visible rule. Browser defaults were removed by global CSS resets on several pages, leaving keyboard users with no visible focus indicator on interactive elements — a Level AA failure that makes navigation nearly impossible without a mouse.

Fix shipped A global :focus-visible { outline: 2px solid var(--ochre); outline-offset: 3px; border-radius: 2px; } rule is now in assets/styles.css. Pages with inline CSS received matching page-local rules.
Severity Critical → Closed Tracking A11Y-F01 Fixed 2026-05-15
Gap07 WCAG 1.4.3 · Level AA Closed (global CSS) · open: A11Y-C02

Link color (--ochre) fails minimum contrast on cream paper

--ochre (#b8651f) has a 3.63:1 contrast ratio against --paper (#f4ecda). WCAG 1.4.3 AA requires 4.5:1 for body text links. This was the dominant link color across all pages — a systemic Level AA failure.

Fix shipped New token --ochre-link: #7a3d0a (7.14:1 on paper — passes AAA) introduced in assets/styles.css. The global a { color } rule now uses --ochre-link. --ochre remains for decorative borders, icons, labels, and nav underlines where it appears alongside ink text. Migration note: pages with inline a { color: var(--ochre) } still need per-page update — tracked as A11Y-C02.

Also fixes WCAG 1.4.1: links were only distinguished from body text by color (no underline until hover). The fix ships a persistent border-bottom: 1px solid var(--ochre-link) on all a elements.

Severity Major → Closed (global CSS) Tracking A11Y-C01 / A11Y-C02 Fixed 2026-05-15 (assets/styles.css) · per-page inline blocks open
Gap08 WCAG 2.3.3 · Level AAA Closed 2026-05-15

prefers-reduced-motion missing on customers/ and graph/mcp.html

Both pages used CSS transitions and animations without a prefers-reduced-motion: reduce suppression block, violating 2.3.3 (AAA). Two other pages (graph/index.html, recruiter/index.html) had motion guards but covered only CSS animations, not JS-driven forces.

Fix shipped Both pages received a @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) block suppressing all animation and transition durations to 0.01ms. assets/styles.css already had the global block.
Severity Major → Closed Tracking A11Y-M01 Fixed 2026-05-15
Gap09 WCAG 2.1.1 · Level A Open

Interactive people-graph (/graph/) is not keyboard-navigable

The SVG visualization at /graph/index.html renders 40 nodes and 80+ edges. Nodes respond to mouse hover and click to focus 1-hop neighborhoods. There is no keyboard path to navigate between nodes, inspect node data, or traverse the graph — a Level A failure (2.1.1) because it blocks a core interactive task.

Workaround available today The graph data is described in the accompanying text: the schema section below the visualization lists all node and edge types. The MCP architecture spec provides the full data model in semantic HTML. For screen reader users, the schema section is fully accessible.

Planned remediation: add tabindex="0" and keydown handlers (Arrow keys to traverse, Enter to select, Escape to reset) to node elements in the SVG. Requires a structural change to the SVG rendering pipeline. Target: Sprint 2.

Severity Critical Tracking A11Y-G01 Owner Tessera / Forge Target Sprint 2
Gap10 CSS design-system Open · Sprint 3

Systemic !important declarations in CSS across non-audited pages

This is a design-system hygiene issue, not a WCAG criterion failure — excluded from the 28-finding WCAG baseline above. A sweep of the full repository found !important property declarations in 15+ files outside the 9 audited pages (intel/, financials/, ledger/, board/, privacy/, day-two/, security/, status/, real-time/, library/, briefings/002–004/012.html, recruiter/console/styles.css). While CLAUDE.md explicitly forbids this pattern, these predate the O-1 dispatch. Most are universal-selector prefers-reduced-motion blocks or responsive override hacks. The 9 audited pages and global stylesheet were cleaned in this dispatch.

Sprint 3 scope Audit all remaining .html and .css files. For each !important block: identify the specificity conflict, raise the selector weight or restructure the cascade, remove the declaration. File a sub-issue per file resolved.
Severity Minor (design-system hygiene) Tracking A11Y-DS01 Owner Tessera Target Sprint 3

§ 5 · How accessibility shows up in our build process

Our accessibility process.

Most accessibility statements stop at "we follow WCAG." That is a destination, not a method. Here is the method — the five places where accessibility is actually a gate, not a wish.

Phase 01 Design

Every new page is designed with accessibility as a first-class input, not a final-stage audit. We use the WCAG 2.2 checklist as a design input — meaning a designer cannot ship a comp without checking it. Designers verify color contrast in Figma using a token-pinned contrast plugin; focus order is annotated on every interactive frame.

Owner · Design
Phase 02 Development

axe-core runs automatically in CI on every pull request. The build fails if a previously-passing page regresses on a Level A or AA criterion. Manual testing of every page (keyboard + screen reader) happens before merge for any UI change. A failed accessibility check is not a "fix in a follow-up" — it blocks the merge.

Owner · Engineering
Phase 03 Audit

A third-party accessibility audit is scheduled quarterly. The next one is at T+180d, conducted by an independent vendor (selection finalized T+140d — we'll name them here once contracted). Audit reports are published in summary form on the Trust Center; the full report is available to customers under NDA.

Owner · Forge, CTO
Phase 04 User testing

We maintain a paid panel of eight users with disabilities — recruited via Knowbility — who give feedback on every major change to the Console or the public site. The panel includes users of NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver, switch control, voice control, and screen magnification. Sessions are recorded with consent; we cite specific feedback in our release notes when it changed our design.

Owner · Design / Research
Phase 05 Commitment to fix

Any accessibility issue we find — or that is reported to us — is fixed within thirty days of the report, unless it is complex enough to require longer (in which case we communicate a target date back to the reporter and add the gap to this statement). We do not sit on accessibility bugs.

§ 6 · The commitments we are willing to be measured against

Specific commitments.

A vague commitment is a non-commitment. These are the five concrete promises that, taken together, define what "Stratum is accessible" means in practice. If we miss one, hold us to it.

Keyboard-only navigation All primary workflows are keyboard-navigable. One known exception: the interactive SVG people-graph at /graph/ requires a mouse or touch device — there is no keyboard path to traverse nodes (Gap A11Y-G01, Critical, Sprint 2). Every other surface on the public site is keyboard-accessible. The Console's primary workflows meet this bar; chart drill-down is being hardened — see Gap 01.
Screen reader compatibility Tested monthly across NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver. Tests cover the public site and the Console's primary workflows. Test runs are logged; the latest pass/fail summary is available on request.
Text alternatives Every meaningful image has descriptive alternative text. Decorative images are marked decorative. SVG icons used as buttons have accessible names. Verified by axe-core in CI plus quarterly editorial review.
Captions & transcripts Every video we publish carries captions and a transcript. Today we publish zero videos — the commitment stands the moment we publish the first one. Office Hours recordings (audio + transcript) already include both.
Sign language Not provided by default. Available on request for important customer-facing communications (kickoff calls, executive briefings, training sessions). Request via accessibility@stratum.ai with at least 5 business days' notice.

If any of these commitments stops being true, the change shows up here before it shows up anywhere else. This statement is the source of truth.

§ 7 · How to tell us we got it wrong

Feedback.

If you encounter a barrier — missing label, broken keyboard focus, inaccessible video, anything — we want to know. Here is exactly what to do and exactly what to expect from us.

— How to report
accessibility@stratum.ai
A single inbox monitored by the accessibility owner and engineering. No forms, no tickets, no auth wall. Just write. For urgent issues affecting a current customer, prefix the subject line with "URGENT".
— Response SLA
5 / 30 / Always
5 business days for an initial response and acknowledgement. 30 days for resolution — or, if more time is required, a clear communication of why, and a target date. Always a real human reply.

To help us resolve quickly, please include:

  • URL The page or screen where you ran into the issue.
  • Browser Name and version (e.g. Firefox 124 on Windows 11).
  • AT Screen reader, voice control, or other assistive technology, with version.
  • Issue What you tried to do, what happened, and what you expected to happen.
  • Severity If you'd like to indicate — blocker, major, moderate, minor. We will defer to your judgment.
  • Optional A screenshot, screen recording, or audio of your assistive tech's output. Helpful but not required.

If the response from accessibility@stratum.ai does not meet your expectations, escalate to Forge directly at cto@stratum.ai. Accessibility is owned at the executive level — there is a real person above the inbox.

§ 11 · The honest paragraph

Limitations and alternatives.

We are six weeks into commercial operations as we write this. Some of the accessibility commitments above are aspirational right now and concrete shortly. We will not pretend to have completed work we have not yet completed — and we will not pretend that an aspiration is the same thing as a delivery.

What is true today: every page of the public site has been audited against WCAG 2.1 AA, the Console's primary workflows are screen-reader-tested on each release, our automated coverage is in place, and our user-research panel exists and has run two rounds of feedback. We have shipped the workarounds described in § 4, and we have committed to specific target dates for each remaining gap.

What is not yet true: the external third-party audit (T+180d) has not happened. The WCAG 2.2 full conformance review (T+200d) has not happened. The four documented gaps have not been closed — though each has a workaround.

If you are evaluating Stratum for a regulated procurement and the difference between an internal audit and an external one matters to you, please raise it during the security review. We will share our remediation plan, our user-research notes, and (when contracted) the name of the external audit vendor. We will not paper over the gap.

The reason we publish this statement now, before everything is finished, is that the alternative is the more common practice: silence until everything is perfect, which means silence forever. The accessibility-statement page that says nothing is a worse signal than the one that says here is what we have, here is what we don't, here is what we are doing about it.

§ 13 · What we will not ship

Refusals.

These are not aspirations — they are standing blocks. A waiver requires a time-limited written decision logged in the decision ledger and reviewed at the next Compact.

Inaccessible new patterns No new UI pattern ships without a passing WCAG 2.2 AAA check on contrast, keyboard navigation, screen-reader name, focus order, and motion.
Hidden regressions Every regression is published here with severity, date found, and ETA. "AA is industry standard" is not a defense; it is the floor we are climbing off.
Animation without motion guard No CSS animation or JS-driven motion ships without a prefers-reduced-motion suppression path. This is a non-negotiable build gate.
Generic fonts Fraunces, Newsreader, JetBrains Mono — these are load-bearing choices for readability and character width consistency. Substituting Inter or Roboto also breaks the contrast and spacing assumptions built into the system.
