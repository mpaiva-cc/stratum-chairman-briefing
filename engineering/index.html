01 People graph · T+39d · No. 001

Entity resolution on the people graph.

Forge · Founding engineer, data · 12 min read · Saved as: entity-res-001.md

On the day Stratum was incorporated — T+-8d — three of us stood at a whiteboard in a borrowed conference room in Palo Alto and tried to write down the hardest technical problem the company would face. We did not arrive at “entity resolution” quickly. We arrived at it inevitably.

Every other problem we wrote down — fast queries, defensible answers, copilot reasoning, drift detection, compliance — collapsed back into the same root: who, exactly, is this person? If you cannot answer that question with high confidence, every downstream answer the system gives is a guess wearing a suit. So we put it at the top of the list and we have been working on it, in some form, every week since.

§ 01 — The shape of the problemThe fifty-systems problem.

An enterprise of 40,000 employees does not have an employee database. It has, on average, between forty-eight and sixty-three systems that each believe they are the source of truth about who works there. Workday says one thing. The ATS (Greenhouse, often) says another. Okta has a third opinion. The badge system has a fourth, the laptop fleet system a fifth, the ticketing system a sixth. Slack has its own. So does GitHub. So does the benefits administrator. So does payroll, which is frequently a different vendor in every country.

Inside any one of those systems, the person Pillar is reasonably consistent: she has an employee ID, an email, a manager, a cost centre. Across systems, she is eleven different entities. In one she is a.pillai@stratum.ai. In another she is asha.p@stratum-corp.com because of a half-finished domain migration in 2024. In the contractor system she is apillai_ext with a different employee ID because she started as a six-month contract before converting. In Slack she is @asha. In the badge system she is EMP-441207. In the laptop fleet she is apillai-mbp-3 because her first laptop got stolen and the asset record was never reconciled.

None of this is unusual. This is what every Fortune 500 HRIS looks like under the hood. It is the normal cost of a large company having existed for a long time. But if we are going to answer questions like “what is the attrition rate for engineers in their second year, who joined as contractors, who report into VPs hired in the last eighteen months” — and we are; that is the product — then we have to know, with high confidence, that the eleven Pillars are one Pillar.

Some of this is easy. Six of the records share the email a.pillai@stratum.ai. That is a strong signal. But that email is itself an artifact of a 2025 domain consolidation, and the contractor record predates it and uses the old domain. The Greenhouse record uses a personal email — which is correct, because that is how she applied. The Slack, badge, and GitHub records have no email at all; they have a handle and a start date. Two of the records use a different start date because she actually started as a contractor in February and converted to full-time in August.

§ 03 — Why the naive approach breaksDeterministic matching, and where it fails.

The obvious thing to try first — and the thing every existing HRIS in the market does try — is deterministic matching. Pick a join key. Email works most of the time. Employee ID works when it exists. Fall back to a fuzzy name match when neither does. Build a graph of pairwise matches, run union-find, done.

It works for about seventy percent of the records. Then it fails in three specific ways that turn out to be the same three ways it always fails, in every enterprise we have looked at:

  1. The contractor-to-employee transition. Different ID, different email, different start date, often a gap in tenure. The deterministic matcher creates two entities. Tenure analytics are wrong from that moment forward.
  2. The acquisition / re-org. An employee from an acquired company gets a new email at the parent domain but keeps an old employee ID in the legacy payroll system that nobody ever migrated. Deterministic match fails on email; succeeds on ID; the matcher does not know which to trust.
  3. The data-entry collisions. Two real people named “Forge Chen” on the same team in the same office. The fuzzy matcher merges them. Their performance reviews and compensation history are now mixed.

Each of these failures is rare in isolation — single-digit percentages. In aggregate they account for between four and nine percent of records at every customer we have looked at. Four to nine percent of an HRIS is a lot of people being slightly the wrong person.

§ 04 — Our approachProbabilistic matching, with the graph as confirming evidence.

We do two things at once.

First, we do probabilistic matching on a feature vector per record: normalized name (with phonetic encoding via Double Metaphone), email local-part, email domain, employee ID prefix, start-date proximity, cost-centre, country, manager-of-record. Each pair of records gets a match score between zero and one. We tuned the threshold against a hand-labeled eval set of 4,200 record pairs from three design partners; the F1-optimal threshold landed at 0.93. Above that we propose a merge; below it we keep them separate; in the middle band (0.82 to 0.93) we route to a human reviewer with an explanation of which features agreed and which did not. [1]

Second, and this is the part that does the heavy lifting, we use the graph itself as confirming evidence. If two candidate records both report to the same manager, both sit in the same cost-centre, and both have laptop assignments adjacent in serial number, the joint probability of them being the same person goes up — not because any one signal is strong, but because independent signals compound. We borrowed the formulation from the Google entity-matching paper [2] and adapted it for the asymmetry of HR data, where some signals (manager-of-record) are far more discriminating than others (cost-centre).

The graph also gives us a way to detect bad merges after the fact. If we merge two records and the resulting entity now appears to report to two managers simultaneously, that is a signal — not a guarantee — that the merge was wrong. We flag it and a human looks at it. The reverse holds too: if a candidate merge would dissolve an inconsistency that previously existed in the graph, that is evidence the merge was correct.

Fig. 1 Eleven source records collapse to seven resolved entities. One pair sits in the review band — a contractor-to-employee transition we'll come back to in §06.

§ 05 — The tradeoffFalse positives, false negatives, and why we are more afraid of one.

Entity resolution has two failure modes and they are not equally bad.

A false negative — failing to merge two records that should be one — produces a system that under-counts people. Your tenure analytics show a discontinuity at the contractor-to-employee mark; your attrition rates are slightly off; your headcount in a slice is one or two lower than it should be. This is bad. It is also recoverable: a human can look at the data, see the gap, and fix it.

A false positive — merging two records that should be separate — is much worse. It means the system has confidently attributed one person's performance review, compensation history, or termination reason to another person. The numbers still look right. There is no obvious tell. And if a CHRO acts on that data — and the entire point of our product is that CHROs will act on this data — they have just made a decision on a phantom.

So we are asymmetric. We choose τ = 0.93, which gives us a precision of 0.991 against our eval set and a recall of 0.873. We accept that we will miss thirteen percent of legitimate merges and route them to human review, because the cost of merging two real people we shouldn't have is, in our judgment, an order of magnitude higher than the cost of asking someone to make a call.

A false negative is an inconvenience.
A false positive is a phantom in the data, wearing a name.

§ 06 — A specific bugThe temp-to-perm transition, found in 36 hours.

Pillar — the engineer, not the synthetic example — joined Stratum on her first day after a six-month contract via a staffing agency. She is a real person and graciously agreed to be cited here. On her second working day, while reviewing the entity-resolution pipeline she had not written, she found a bug.

The matcher was generating two entities for every employee who had converted from contract. The probabilistic score was sitting at 0.91 — just below threshold — because the start dates differed by months, the employee IDs were structurally different (the contractor system used a different prefix), and the email domains were different across the transition. Three independent signals all said “different person”; the name match alone said “same person.” The matcher correctly applied its rule and refused to merge.

The bug was not in the matcher. The bug was that we had not given the graph-confirmation step access to temporal continuity as a feature. Two records with the same name and phonetic encoding, where one ends exactly when the other begins, and where the second references the first in a prior_id field that we had been ignoring, should compound to well above threshold.

Pillar found it in 36 hours. The fix was twelve lines of Python. The revalidation against the full eval set took longer — and we caught a separate regression in the review-band routing that we'd shipped two weeks earlier — but the underlying lesson was hers: we had been treating each source as independent when in many cases the source already knew about the predecessor. The data was richer than our features.

We added prior_id_chain as a strong feature. Precision went up slightly. Recall went up by four percentage points. We now correctly resolve approximately ninety-two percent of contractor-to-employee transitions without human review, against approximately fifty-eight percent before her patch.

§ 07 — The open problemNon-human entities.

We do not have a good answer yet for what to do with non-human entities in the people graph. There are a lot of them. Service accounts. Bots. Contractors-of-contractors — agency employees of staffing firms who appear in the customer's systems but are not, in any meaningful sense, the customer's people. And, in the near future, agents. Stratum's own copilot will appear in our customers' audit logs as an actor. Is that a person? Is it an entity? Does it have a manager? Does it have tenure?

We think it is an entity of a different kind, and that the graph needs a typed-node model rather than a single person type. We are sketching this now. We are not yet shipping it. We mention it here because we suspect it is the next big problem we will have to solve, and because it is genuinely open — if you have thought hard about this and have a perspective, we would like to hear from you.

§ 08 — Why this is the betEntity resolution is the most important technical decision we will make.

The reason entity resolution is on the whiteboard at week zero and still on the whiteboard at week six is that every other claim Stratum makes — that our answers are defensible, that our copilot reasons over the right cohort, that our analytics can be trusted at the CHRO level — collapses if the underlying entities are wrong. Every product surface, every eval set, every customer demo, every audit-grade citation, is downstream of getting this right.

It is also the thing that, if we get right, is hardest to copy. A well-tuned probabilistic matcher with a graph-confirmation layer and three years of customer-specific eval feedback is not a moat that a competitor builds in a quarter. It is the kind of moat that compounds.

So we are going to keep writing about it. The next post in this series — Helm is drafting it now — is about why we chose a graph representation in the first place, instead of the “people record” pattern that every existing HRIS uses. The short version: a record can answer a question. A graph can answer the question after that one. We will write the long version on T+53d.

02 Infrastructure · T+34d · No. 002

What we mean when we say born compliant.

Forge · Founding engineer, data · 8 min read · Saved as: compliance-stance-001.md

Compliance is the only feature where the customer punishes you for being late, not for being early. Most startups treat it as a tax to be deferred until Series B. We've made the opposite bet: that doing the controls from week one is cheaper than retrofitting them, and that it shapes the architecture in ways we will be glad of in 2028.

§ 01 — Why this is not an afterthoughtThe four frameworks shaping the architecture.

Four regulatory regimes touch what we build. Each one would, in isolation, reshape the system. Together they define the contour of the product.

  • SOC 2 Type II — the security baseline US enterprise buyers expect before they will even take a meeting. Access controls, encryption, monitoring, incident response. Audited annually.
  • ISO 27001 — the international counterpart. Adds risk management process and broader information-security governance. Required by most European buyers and an increasing share of US Fortune 500.
  • EU AI Act — in force since August 2024, with most obligations applying from August 2026. Classifies AI systems by risk. HR-related decision systems are by default high risk, with substantial obligations — unless the system is structured such that consequential decisions require human-in-the-loop, in which case it falls to limited risk.
  • NYC AEDT (Local Law 144) — the most operationally specific. Requires bias audits of automated employment decision tools used in NYC. The audits must be public.

None of these is optional. None can be retrofitted cheaply. Each one rules out architectural decisions that would otherwise look attractive. So we built around them from the first commit.

§ 02 — Concrete consequencesHow SOC 2 shaped our identity provider.

The first place this shows up is identity. SOC 2 requires that access to production systems be (a) authenticated against a single source of truth, (b) authorized by role and reviewed quarterly, (c) auditable for the preceding twelve months, and (d) revocable within an hour of an employee departure.

You can hand-roll this. Many startups do, with a combination of GitHub teams, a shared 1Password vault, and a Notion page that says “remember to remove people when they leave.” This pattern fails audit. It also fails reality — there is always someone who left in 2024 who still has prod read on something.

We picked Okta on day one, even though there are six of us and it costs more than the engineering coffee budget, because it gives us audit logs from week zero that we will not have to reconstruct in week one hundred. Every prod system — AWS, Snowflake, GitHub, Tailscale, the Slack admin console, the Vanta admin console itself — is behind Okta SSO with SCIM provisioning. Quarterly access reviews are a checkbox-and-export. We believe this will save us approximately one engineer-month per year for the next three years.

§ 03 — The EU AI Act decisionLimited risk by construction, not by policy.

The most consequential compliance decision we have made is to position Stratum's copilot as limited risk under the EU AI Act. This is not a marketing posture; it is an architectural commitment.

The Act allows an HR-adjacent AI system to be classified as limited risk if and only if consequential decisions — hiring, firing, promotion, compensation changes, performance ratings — are not made by the system itself. The system can analyse. It can recommend. It can surface evidence. It cannot act.

We have built this constraint into the agent surface, not into a policy document. The copilot — internally now called the “console”, see Tessera's post — cannot execute a consequential action without an explicit human approval step. This is not a config flag. It is the shape of the API. The action types that the agent can directly perform are restricted at the schema level to: query, summarise, cite, draft, route. The action types that require human approval — change_compensation, terminate, promote, set_rating — are physically a different endpoint, with a different authorization scope, that the agent cannot call without a countersigning JWT issued by a human session.

The reason for putting this in the architecture rather than the policy is simple: policies degrade. Architectures do not. A future engineer cannot accidentally let the agent fire someone if the function literally does not exist on the agent's side of the boundary.

§ 04 — The training-data postureWhat we will and won't do with customer data.

We do not train foundation models on customer data. We do not fine-tune foundation models on customer data. We do not use customer data to improve the base model that any customer's tenant runs on. None of these is a future possibility we are leaving open; all three are contractually prohibited in our standard DPA and architecturally enforced.

The one place customer data, in a derivative form, may be used is in our internal eval set. The graph schema — node types, edge types, the shape of a customer's people data — is fully anonymized (all identifiers replaced, all PII stripped, only structural statistics retained) and may be used to extend our product evaluation methodology. This is disclosed in §4.2 of the DPA. We will write more about this when we publish the eval set in July.

§ 05 — The state of the posture, todayWhere we actually are at week six.

Compliance posturing is cheap. Compliance reality, less so. Here is where we actually are, with no rounding:

Forty-one of eighty-seven SOC 2 controls implemented at the policy and evidence level after six weeks is, by Vanta's benchmarks, in the top ten percent of seed-stage companies. It is also less than half. We expect to hit auditor-ready around week twenty. ISO 27001 is queued behind it; the controls overlap heavily, so the marginal cost is small once the foundation is in place.

We are six weeks old. Vanta is on a wall. We are taking compliance more seriously than a six-person company has any right to, because the cost of doing it now is small and the cost of doing it later — when we have a customer in production and a regulator's attention — is enormous. That is the entire argument.

03 Copilot · T+44d · No. 003

Designing the agent surface.

Tessera · Founding designer (pre-start, design week) · 6 min read · Saved as: console-001.md

On day one of my design week, I drew the copilot surface and counted eleven jobs it was being asked to do. By day five, it was doing four. That subtraction is the entire story.

§ 01 — The problem with eleven jobsEvery job dilutes the agent's authority.

The first draft of the copilot surface — done by the engineering team before I arrived, with the best intentions — had a chat panel, a cohort-inspector panel, a citations sidebar, a draft-document area, a notifications stream, a saved-questions list, a connector status panel, a feedback widget, a tenant-switcher, a permissions visualiser, and a changelog. Eleven jobs in one surface.

Each one of those was a reasonable thing the agent could help with. Taken together, they made the agent feel like a settings page that occasionally answered questions. The reason is mechanical, not aesthetic: every job a surface is asked to do reduces the user's confidence that any one thing the surface does is the thing it is for. An agent surface is, in its essence, a place where the user delegates judgment. You cannot delegate judgment to a Swiss army knife.

§ 02 — The subtractionWhat I cut, and why.

I removed seven of the eleven over the course of the week. Some of them got moved to other surfaces (connector status is now a thin item in the tenant admin; the changelog has its own page). Some got removed entirely (the feedback widget; we have a Slack channel). Some got folded into the four jobs that remained.

The four jobs that remain are not chosen by taste. They are chosen by necessity: any agent surface that produces defensible answers about an enterprise's people must do these four things, and any one of them removed breaks the contract.

  1. Ask. The user types a question. This is the conversation.
  2. Inspect the cohort. The user can see, at a glance, the set of people the answer is about. Not the answer — the underlying set.
  3. Cite the source. The user can see, for any number in the answer, which source system and which records produced it. Click-through, no abstraction.
  4. Confirm or revert. If the agent has taken a non-consequential action (a draft, a route, a tag), the user can accept or undo it in one keystroke.
Fig. 3 The console: four panes, four jobs. Ask · Inspect · Cite · Confirm. Anything that did not serve one of these four was cut.

§ 03 — A note on the renameFrom “copilot” to “console”.

Halfway through the week we changed the internal name. We had been calling it the copilot, which is what the market calls everything of this shape. But the word makes a promise about authority — a copilot can fly the plane — that we are explicitly not making. Stratum's agent does not act on consequential decisions. It analyses, it recommends, it cites, it drafts. Calling it a copilot was setting an expectation we would then have to apologise for the first time it asked the user to approve a change.

Console sets a different expectation. A console is an instrument. You sit in front of it. It tells you what is happening, in real time, with enough fidelity that you can make the call. It does not make the call. The word does most of the work for us.

We have not changed the customer-facing name yet — we are still calling it the Stratum copilot externally, because the market does not yet know how to think about a more restrained surface. We will likely move the public name to “console” in the first half of 2027, when we have enough customer evidence that the restraint is a feature.

§ 04 — CloseThe agent surface is a conversation.

The agent surface is the company's only conversation with its user. Every other surface — admin, billing, settings, connectors — is a tool. The console is where the user goes to think. If the surface is cluttered, the user's thinking is cluttered. If the surface is restrained, the user's thinking is restrained.

Treat it like a conversation. Most of the work of designing it is knowing what to leave out.

Tessera joined as founding designer in late June. He spent four years at Figma on the file canvas, and two at Airtable on the schema-builder. He has redesigned a copilot surface approximately fourteen times in the last three years and is not yet bored.
