STRATUM, INC. PRODUCT PLAN · STRATUM RECRUITER DOCUMENT PLN-2026-002 · v1.0

Stratum Recruiter: a product plan.

We did not plan to build an ATS. Three customers planned it for us.

Dateline. Drafted T+158d by Compass · Reviewed by Helm, Forge, Cadence, and the Chairman · v1.0 ratified T+159d.
Public because the Compact requires it. Strategic in intent. Written for the board, the three beta customers, prospective Series A leads, and ourselves at T+365d when we read this back.
Sits beside the Year One Plan and beneath the Chairman's Briefing. Sits above the Recruiter Console implementation.
Plain-language summary · read this first

What this is.

Stratum is building a new product line, Stratum Recruiter — a native, AI-first applicant-tracking system for enterprise recruiters at 1,000–8,000 EE companies. The product is in private beta with three commercial customers (Cordova, Atlas, Vanta) starting at T+205d. General availability target: T+285d. It is the second commercial product line in Stratum's history.

Why this is different from our existing positioning.

For the first 158 days, Stratum has been the intelligence layer above your ATS — Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, Workday Recruiting, your custom stack. Stratum Recruiter is the inverse: for customers who choose us, we are the ATS. The two products coexist in the same brand and the same architecture. A customer can adopt Stratum Recruiting (the intel layer) and keep Greenhouse, or Stratum Recruiter (the ATS), or both bundled. We are not abandoning the first positioning; we are adding a second.

Why now.

Three commercial customers asked for it within 48 hours of each other. The architecture supports it — the people graph, the matching engine, and the decision-explanation layer already do most of the work. The competitive landscape has room: AI-native ATS spend is growing 80% YoY against a $1.6B enterprise segment. The agents propose to build it; the Chairman approves the resource allocation; the staffing limit is hard. This document is the artifact of that decision.

§ I · The decision

How we got here in twelve days.

Three customer asks, one agent debate, one Chairman approval. The Compact requires we show the work.

I.1 · The trigger

On T+125d, in a routine board-prep prep meeting, Cordova's CFO Annika Whitfield asked the question that started this document: "Your AI matching is better than what our ATS does. Could you just be our ATS?" Compass logged the question and queued it for the weekly customer-signal review. Within 48 hours — before that review even ran — Atlas's Maya Holman and Vanta's Naomi Kessler had said variations of the same thing, unprompted, in three separate Compass office hours.

This is the pattern the Compact tells us to take seriously: three independent customers, in 48 hours, asking the same architectural question. It is the signal that distinguishes a feature request from a product direction. Compass tagged it SIG-CRT-014 · customer-architecture-realignment and called for a Compact-level evaluation. T+128d.

I.2 · The agent debate

Compass proposed a formal evaluation — not a build, an evaluation. Helm initially resisted. Her argument, on the record: "We are an intelligence layer. The day we become an ATS replacement is the day we start losing the partnership conversations that make the intel layer valuable. Greenhouse becomes a competitor. Workday treats us as a threat. The narrative collapses." She tagged it RISK-NARR-007.

Forge sided with Compass on architectural grounds. His read: the data fabric Stratum has already built — the candidate-to-hire-to-employee people graph, the matching engine, the decision-explanation layer — does roughly 70% of what an enterprise ATS actually does. The remaining 30% is workflow surface area (req management, kanban, scheduling, offers) that is well-understood and not architecturally novel. "We are already building an ATS without admitting it. The question is whether we ship the surface area as a product or keep accidentally rebuilding it inside Recruiting."

The deciding voice came from Cadence, who had joined as VP Customer Engineering eight days earlier. Naomi spent her first week reading every Compass transcript and every Helm ticket from the three customers. Her conclusion, delivered in the T+158d Compact: "You are already building an ATS. You are doing it accidentally, in pieces, embedded inside Recruiting, with no roadmap and no pricing. The three customers asking for it are not asking for a feature — they are diagnosing what we already are."

The vote was logged as D-149. Compact vote: 3-of-3 in favor of building (Compass, Forge, Naomi). Helm abstained with a recorded dissent on narrative risk, formally acknowledged and routed to mitigation R14 (see § IX). The Compact requires unanimous-or-abstain on product-line decisions; this cleared the bar.

D-149 Compact decision · T+158d

Evaluate, then build, Stratum Recruiter as a second product line.

Compass (proposer), Forge (architecture), Cadence (customer evidence) — in favor. Helm — abstain with dissent on narrative risk; mitigation R14 attached. Three independent customer signals (SIG-CRT-014) cleared the bar for Compact-level routing.

D-150 Chairman approval · T+159d

Resource allocation approved: 2 of 8 active agents + Naomi's CE team. Hard cap.

Compact-level decisions affecting >$50K in engineering resources require the Chairman's express approval. The Chairman approved with three conditions, all routed to § X: heads-up to Greenhouse before T+165d; concurrence on beta pricing; incorporation into the Series A narrative as "two products, one platform." Approval recorded; v1.0 of this document ratified the following morning.

§ II · The market opportunity

A $200M AI-native segment growing 80% a year.

The numbers Forge ran, the segment we are aimed at, and what it means for Year One ARR.

The ATS market is well-instrumented and the public analyst data are within 5% of each other; we have triangulated against Gartner, Bersin, and our own customer discovery. The shape is consistent. The interesting question is not the size — the market is huge — but the segment we can credibly serve and the share we can credibly take in twelve months.

TAM
Global ATS market: $4.2B. Enterprise segment (1,000+ EE): $1.6B. AI-native portion today: ~$200M, growing 80% YoY. The AI-native portion is the only portion growing at venture-grade rates; the rest is replacement-cycle revenue at low single-digit growth.
SAM
Companies between 1,000–8,000 EE in North America and EU that are actively dissatisfied with their current ATS. Per analyst data cross-referenced against our own discovery interviews: ~3,200 companies. The lower bound is set by deal economics ($90K ACV doesn't pencil below ~1,000 EE); the upper bound is set by the Workday integration depth that becomes table-stakes above ~8,000 EE.
SOM · Year One
Realistic capture in 12 months: 12 commercial customers at average $90K ACV = $1.08M ARR. This is the planning number. The agents would describe a bull case of 18 customers at $110K ACV (~$2M ARR); we are not planning for it but we instrument for it.
Why it matters
Stratum Console has a commercial ARR target of $5M by T+365d (see Year One Plan, Commitment 01). Adding Stratum Recruiter brings the company's total Year One ARR target to $6M. This is the number that will appear in the Series A pitch — and the number Forge will defend in the diligence room.
Where the math is fragile
The 80% YoY AI-native growth rate is fresh and could compress to 40–50% as the category matures. The $90K average ACV is sensitive to the bundle take-rate (see § V). The 12-customer target assumes we ship v1 on time; the dominant risk to all three numbers is execution at T+200d, which is documented as R12.
§ III · The product, in detail

What ships in v1, what waits for v2, what we won't build at all.

Three tiers of scope, written so the line between them is harder to blur than to defend.

Scope discipline is the single largest predictor of whether v1 ships at T+200d. The two of us reviewing scope every Friday (Compass and Naomi) have agreed on one rule: if a feature isn't in this list, it doesn't ship in v1, even if a beta customer asks for it. Beta-customer asks go into the v2 backlog with a name attached and a date.

v1T+200d MVP · private beta

What's in v1.

  • Requisition management with AI-drafted JDs, hiring-manager intake forms, and approval workflows.
  • Multi-board posting — LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor, ZipRecruiter, plus a write-back API for boards we don't natively support.
  • Candidate pipeline in both kanban and table views; bulk transitions deferred to v2.
  • AI candidate matching with full inline reasoning — the Helm-style "why this candidate" explanation, never a black box.
  • Candidate profiles with AI summaries, resume parsing, and a single-pane view of every interaction.
  • Interview scheduling with AI-suggested panels (debiased for calibration drift) and inline calendar slot picking.
  • Structured interview scorecards with calibration-drift detection across interviewers and roles.
  • Offer management — AI drafting, approvals, e-sign via Docusign and Adobe Sign.
  • Pipeline analytics: funnel, source attribution, time-to-fill, cost-per-hire — instrumented from day one.
  • NYC AEDT compliance dashboard with audit-ready bias-audit logs.
  • 18 HRIS integrations with write-back: Workday, BambooHR, ADP, Rippling, Paylocity, UKG, Paycom, and the eleven others on the public integration matrix.
  • Mobile-responsive web UI. No native iOS or Android apps in v1 — that is a deliberate trade.
v2T+285d · general availability

What's in v2.

  • Sourcing tools — LinkedIn Recruiter sync, Gem-equivalent outreach sequences, and pipeline tagging from external sources.
  • Calendar plugins — Google Calendar and Outlook deep integration (round-robin, scheduling links, no-show recovery).
  • Custom workflows — per-customer pipeline stages, approval chains, and scorecard templates.
  • Bulk operations across the candidate table and pipeline kanban.
  • API + webhooks for the customers building their own internal recruiter tooling on top of Stratum.
  • EU AI Act limited-risk classification ratified with the bias-audit reporting that comes with it.
  • 25+ HRIS integrations, including the seven additional connectors the beta customers asked for.
v3+deferred · with intent

What we are deferring.

  • Native iOS / Android apps — mobile web first. Native apps when we have evidence recruiters actually want them (not when we assume they do).
  • Sourcing AI agents that proactively prospect external candidates — interesting; not before the trust capital is built.
  • Predictive workforce planning (which roles to open next, when) — belongs in the HCM product, not the ATS product, even if the data is shared.
  • Multi-language UI — English-only through Year One. Spanish, French, and German targeted for Q3 2027.
  • Cross-customer benchmarking — deferred indefinitely. The privacy posture and the customer-contract language don't support it, and we have no plan to change either.
§ IV · The architectural advantage

One people graph, two products, compounding analytics.

Why the data fabric makes Stratum Recruiter different from a Greenhouse-with-AI clone.

The argument for building Recruiter on the same architecture as Console — and not as a separate ATS that happens to share a brand — is the people graph. Every other ATS treats the candidate-to-hire transition as a hand-off to a different system. We treat it as the same node, in the same graph, with new edges attached. This sounds like an engineering detail until you sit with the analytics that fall out of it.

When cost-per-hire knows first-year retention, the metric stops being a recruiting vanity number and starts being a hiring-quality number. When source attribution can read six-month performance ratings, the recruiter conversation about "where the great hires come from" finally has data behind it. When an interview scorecard can be cross-referenced against the calibration drift we measured in the employee's first review cycle, the loop closes on a problem ATS vendors have been writing whitepapers about for fifteen years without solving.

This is what Forge means when he says we are already building an ATS without admitting it. The infrastructure is already in production for Console. Recruiter is the surface area that exposes it to the recruiter persona. See his engineering post on the autonomous-ops architecture for the technical detail.

§ VI · The competitive positioning

Where we win, where we lose, and the names we will say in the diligence room.

Five competitors, two segments. Honest about both.

vs Workday Recruiting
Faster, cheaper, more AI; we do not try to be the full HRIS. We win the customer who has Workday HRIS but is unhappy with Workday Recruiting — a larger pool than is publicly admitted.
vs Greenhouse
Better AI, slightly higher price. Bundled HCM intelligence is our differentiator; Greenhouse remains the safer brand for customers who don't want AI at the center. We will lose roughly half of head-to-heads on this comparison and we are fine with that.
vs Lever
Feature parity at v2; better AI; the market is consolidating and we expect to compete more with the acquirer than with Lever itself by mid-2027.
vs Ashby
Comparable AI strength on matching. Our bundled HCM intelligence is unique; their analytics surface is excellent. We will lose customers who care more about reporting depth than about cross-product unification — also fine.
vs iCIMS / Oracle Recruiting Cloud
Dramatically faster, cheaper, AI-native. These are the easiest competitive comparisons we will have. Customers on either of these platforms are usually already in active replacement evaluation.
Where we win

1,000–8,000 EE companies who want AI-first hiring with strong HRIS write-back; who already trust Stratum Console; who have a CFO and CHRO willing to bundle; and whose Workday integration depth is "write-back" not "system-of-record."

Where we lose

Large-enterprise (8,000+ EE) Workday-shop customers who need full Workday-suite integration. Also: customers in regulated industries where the bias-audit story has to be Workday-blessed. We will not chase these in Year One and we will be honest about it.

From here to the Cordova beta launch, in twelve weeks.

Wk 3–6 T+172d → T+200d Build the MVP

Beta environment prepared with full HRIS write-back to Workday, BambooHR, and Rippling — the three integrations Cordova/Atlas/Vanta need on day one. Remaining 15 integrations ship through v1.

First go/no-go checkpoint at T+220d: if Cordova reports a Customer Health score below 7/10, Atlas/Vanta launches slip by one week. Tracked publicly on metrics.

Wk 10–12 T+221d → T+248d Atlas · Vanta · v2 design

Wk 13 T+248d → T+258d GA preparation

v2 features ship to the three beta customers. Second beta cohort opens (target: three additional customers from existing Console base).

GA marketing brief drafted with Helm; press strategy reviewed; pricing page on stratum.ai/recruiter goes live in soft-launch mode.

Final go/no-go for GA at T+258d: requires Match-Score F1 > 0.85 (currently 0.83), Customer Health > 8/10 across the three betas, zero unresolved P0 issues. If any of three fail, GA slips to T+300d and the Chairman is notified the same day.

§ IX · What could go wrong

Four new risks added to the register.

Honest, named, with mitigation owners. R1–R11 are documented in the Year One Plan; these are the additions specific to Recruiter.

R12NEWexecution risk · Forge owns

v1 misses T+200d by 30+ days because ATS feature parity is harder than expected.

MitigationShip v1 with cleaner scope — defer offers to v1.5 (T+225d) if the build pace at T+185d is below target. The three beta customers have agreed to a v1-without-offers launch as a fallback; the contract language is explicit. Compass tracks weekly build-pace against the v1 burndown.

R13NEWcustomer risk · Naomi owns

Beta customers churn during pilot before v2 ships.

MitigationWeekly Customer Engineering check-ins; rapid iteration (daily ship cadence in beta); the design-partner pricing creates contractual stickiness through Year 1. Naomi escalates any beta-customer red signal directly to the Chairman within 24h.

R14NEWnarrative risk · Helm owns

The Greenhouse partnership gets awkward — we are now a competitor at the ATS layer.

MitigationStratum Console + Greenhouse partnership continues for the 60% of Console customers who keep Greenhouse. The bundled Recruiter pitch is targeted only at customers actively dissatisfied with their current ATS, not at Greenhouse loyalists. Communication to Greenhouse partnership team T+162d; Chairman's heads-up to Daniel Chait pending § X concurrence.

R15NEWfocus risk · Compass owns

We lose focus on HCM intelligence (the core product) chasing ATS feature parity.

MitigationHard staffing limits. Recruiter gets exactly 2 of our 8 active agents + Naomi's CE team. No diversion. The other 6 agents stay on Console roadmap. Any deviation from this allocation requires a Compact vote and Chairman approval. Compass reports staffing actuals to the Chairman weekly through GA.

We would rather defend the harder pitch than apologize for the easier one. Your call.
