§ 01 · The four anchor commitments

Four things we have put in writing, and will be held to.

The rest of the handbook is method. These four are the load-bearing walls. If you ever find yourself wondering whether we have drifted, measure against these.

i.Commitment

We will not run a sales motion.

We hire customer engineers, not account executives. The person who signs your contract is the same person who will be on a call with your finance team at 14:00 on a Tuesday, looking at the same eval set you are looking at, answering the same question. We pay her on renewal and on the depth of the deployment, not on signature.

Sales motion as a category exists because the buyer cannot evaluate the product before purchase. Our wager is that an auditable eval set inverts that asymmetry. If the question lives in the open, the person who can answer it is an engineer, not a salesperson. So we do not have salespeople. We have engineers who carry quota and weekly office hours.

This is the most expensive of our commitments. It almost certainly slows our top-of-funnel by a factor of two. We have argued the trade-off three times in founder session and the answer has come out the same each time: the company we will be proud of in five years does not have a sales floor.

Cross-reference: Compass, on why we do not run a sales motion →
ii.Commitment

We will publish our eval set.

The methodology is auditable. The questions are public. The bias probes are documented. As of T+76d, the canonical set is 250 questions, 28 fairness probes, 12 HCM-specific edge cases. We publish the cases we fail on the same page we publish the cases we pass on. There is no separate marketing version.

An eval set is a load-bearing artifact. If it lives behind a login, what is it for? It is for the seller, not the buyer. We decided early that the eval set was the product, and that meant the customer had to be able to see it, run it, and add to it. Our customers add roughly six questions per pilot. Those go into the set, attributed, with a date.

We do not promise the set will never embarrass us. Some questions we fail. The audit trail of every quarterly run is published the Monday after the run completes. The point is not to look good. The point is to make the question of whether we look good the customer's question to answer, in public, with the same data we used.

Cross-reference: Briefing 006 · the eval set publishes on the twenty-first →
iii.Commitment

We will explain our prices in plain language.

Hybrid model. No per-seat math. No surprise renewals. The price page is a page, not a quote form. The contract is twelve pages, not eighty. Renewal terms are on page three, not page eleven, and the only auto-escalator is CPI, capped at three percent.

Per-seat pricing rewards us when our customer hires people. Our product is supposed to reduce the number of people doing reconciliation work, not increase it. So per-seat was incentive- broken from the start. We charge a platform fee for the people graph and a usage fee for decisions made through the Console. Both numbers are public.

The Mercator pricing pilot, ratified at Board Meeting No. 1, set the floor at $0.85 per decision with a bulk-discount tier above $50K per month and a 90-day ceiling of $280K. Mercator's countersignature is on file. That structure is the template; we do not negotiate it down for new customers without telling the existing ones.

Cross-reference: The pricing page · in plain language →
iv.Commitment

We will tell our customers when we should not be picked.

The right answer is sometimes "not Stratum." There are five scenarios on the compare page where the honest recommendation is a different vendor. Workday remains the right answer for a 40,000- employee company that needs payroll-of-record in eighteen countries today. Rippling is the right answer below a thousand employees. We will not pretend otherwise.

The compare page is not a competitive grid with green ticks on our column and red Xs on theirs. It is five short essays, each written by the founder who has the most experience with the competitor, each ending with a one-line recommendation. Three of the five recommendations are not Stratum.

We came to this commitment after a Halcyon call in week three of the evaluation, when Vector was asked, plainly, whether we were actually the best fit. She said: probably not, in your specific case, given the pre-merger ADP archive. The CRO thanked her for not pretending. He has since asked for a different proposal — a narrower one — and the evaluation is alive again.

§ 02 · The Compact of Three, extended

The compact we signed, in public, on day three.

The Compact was written on the founders' first session, T+0d, and ratified the same week. It was internal in Briefing 001. As of this revision it is part of the company's operating culture, and the extension to the first thirty employees is in force.

  1. i.
    Two-of-three for major decisions. No major call moves without the agreement of two founders. Major is defined as anything above $50K, any director-or-above hire, any architecture decision affecting the people graph, and any commitment that touches the eval set.
  2. ii.
    Any founder may halt a hire above $180K base for 72 hours, no reason required. The intent is to make uncomfortable second thoughts cheap. The pause has been used twice in 2026 and both offers were ultimately made; the delay produced better calibration conversations both times.
  3. iii.
    Weekly Monday meeting, 60 minutes, agenda is the previous week's decisions ledger. No status round-robin. No deck. We read the ledger entries, we ask whether each one is still right, and we add new ones.
  4. iv.
    The Chairman receives a written briefing each fortnight. It is signed by all three founders. It is the canonical channel for serious news. The public version publishes the same Sunday. The Chairman is George Holloway.
  5. v.
    If two-of-three lose confidence in the third, the third sells unvested back at cost. This is the most uncomfortable clause we wrote. We wrote it the first week, while we still liked each other most, because we knew we would not be able to write it later. The exit mechanism is named, with a number, in the founders' agreement.
  6. vi.
    Same compact extends to the next thirty employees: any IC may halt a hire above $180K base for 72 hours by writing to the founders. The first use of this power was by Pillar, the company's first non-founder hire, on T+43d. The hire she paused was made nine days later. She was right to pause it.
  7. vii.
    Decision attribution: every meaningful decision is recorded with its owner, its date, and its rationale in the public decisions ledger — the same one that appears in every briefing. The owner is a person, not a committee. If the owner is wrong later, the owner says so on the same line.
  8. viii.
    The Compact is renewed each year at the founder retrospective. Anything in it can be changed by a two-of-three vote at the retrospective. The retrospective is held on the anniversary of incorporation, T+3d. The next renewal falls on T+368d.
§ 03 · The eight operating principles

Eight principles, with the moments that earned them.

These are the eight from the careers page. Here, each one has a paragraph and a moment from the company's first sixty days. Read them as we mean them: not slogans, but the actual mechanisms we use to resolve the next thousand small disagreements.

i.Principle

Specific over impressive.

A connector that ships beats a connector we explained well. We are suspicious of decks, and even more suspicious of decks that rhyme. When the team falls in love with an abstraction, we ask: what is the most specific Tuesday-afternoon question this changes? On T+16d, Mercator's first ingestion completed in 47 minutes against 3,412 records. Forge's terminal printed a single line and he forwarded it, unedited, to the founders' Slack. The screenshot is in Briefing 001. We have not improved on its specificity since.

ii.Principle

The eval set is the product.

If a question is not in our eval set, we have not shipped the answer. Every quarter we add to it, never remove from it, and run the whole thing in CI. The set is published. The failures are published. Decision 47, dated T+64d: publish the full eval set on T+76d, including the cases we fail. The argument lasted four hours. Helm wanted to wait until we had passed everything. Forge and Compass carried the two-of-three. We have not regretted it.

iii.Principle

Tuesday afternoons.

Every roadmap conversation returns to: does this give a CHRO back her Tuesday afternoon? If the answer is no, the work is not on the roadmap. The same principle, turned inward, is why every Stratum employee takes two hours of unbroken thinking time per week, on Tuesday afternoons, with no meetings and no Slack. Mercator's Maria Holloway told us in week six of the pilot that she had read a book on Tuesday afternoon for the first time in eighteen months. That sentence is on the wall of the office.

iv.Principle

Three is the right number.

Founders. Engineering pillars. Quarterly themes. Trios, not committees. Three forces a decision; four invites a tie. We have already turned down two strong fourth co-founders and we will turn down a third. On T+8d we spent four hours with a former Workday SVP who wanted in. He was excellent. We said no by Friday because four was the wrong shape and the Compact was already signed. He sent a note last month asking to invest in the Series A.

v.Principle

Born compliant.

Compliance is a feature, not an afterthought. Engineering owns SOC 2, not Legal. The EU AI Act stance is in the README, not the wiki. Sub-processors are listed on the trust page with dates. Decision 12, dated T+13d: file the EU AI Act memo as part of the codebase, version-controlled, with a CHANGELOG. The first revision was committed by Forge before the first connector shipped. That ordering is the point.

vi.Principle

Write things down.

The decisions ledger is a load-bearing wall. Every reversible call is a one-pager; every irreversible call is a two-pager. We do not relitigate; we link. The ledger grew from 12 entries on Day 1 to 70 entries on Day 60. The Schema v2 ratification, decision 38, links to the original schema v1 decision from week one and to the three intermediate contractor/temp-to-perm corrections. A new engineer who reads those four entries in order has, in fifteen minutes, the context of three months.

vii.Principle

Composition over consensus.

We compose conflicting opinions; we do not dilute them. The strongest version of each view wins on its terms — not by averaging away the sharp edges. Decision 31, on the Halcyon downgrade: Compass wanted to withdraw, Forge wanted to extend, Helm wanted to renegotiate. The resolution was none of the three. It was a sixty-day evaluation window with a named exit date, T+117d, and a single owner. That structure was Vector's, not a founder's. We took it.

viii.Principle

Slow hire. Faster fire than is comfortable.

The cost of the wrong tenth hire is six months of slow drift. We will feel the discomfort of acting on it in week eight, not month eight. We owe that to the people who stay. We have not had to invoke this yet, which is a fact we hold lightly. The hire-process design is in the careers page; the fire-process design is here in section eleven. They were written the same week.

§ 04 · How decisions get made

A ledger, a compact, a person on every line.

Two mechanisms, working together. The ledger is the company's permanent memory. The compact is its decision protocol. Together they answer the question: who decided this, when, and why?

The decisions ledger.

Every meaningful decision is logged with a number, a date, an owner, and a rationale. The ledger has grown from 12 entries on Day 1 to 70 entries on Day 60. Every fortnightly briefing reviews recent entries. The ledger is the company's permanent memory, and the first thing a new employee reads in week one.

Decisions are not deleted. Decisions are amended, with the amendment date and the amending owner on the same line. The original is preserved. Drift is therefore visible. A new engineer who wants to understand why we built the people graph the way we did can read the entries in order; the reasoning is a trail, not a snapshot.

The compact mechanism.

Major decisions require two-of-three founders. Compact-level decisions — anything above $50K, any director-or-above hire, any acquisition discussion — require Chairman notification within 48 hours. The compact is what keeps a strong-opinioned trio from becoming a benevolent dictatorship.

The Chairman, George Holloway, receives the canonical briefing every fortnight. He is the named escalation path for anything that would otherwise lock the trio. He has used that path twice in 2026, both times to slow us down, both times correctly. The briefing format is described in section six.

§ 05 · How we work

Four operating rhythms, one calendar.

The cadence of the company. Each rhythm is timeboxed, has a named artifact, and a named owner. We do not invent new rituals; we keep these four and protect them ruthlessly.

Monday · 09:00 PT

The Monday meeting.

Sixty minutes. Founders only. Agenda is the decisions ledger. No status round-robin, no deck. We read recent entries aloud, ask whether each is still right, and add new ones. The meeting ends on time. If we run long, the surplus rolls to the next Monday.

Other employees do not attend, but the ledger they produce is the input to every employee's week. The output of the Monday meeting is a Slack message in #ledger by 11:00 PT, every Monday, without exception.

60 minutes · founders · weekly · 47 sessions to date
Tuesday · 14:00–16:00 PT

Tuesday afternoons.

Personal retreat time. Every employee is expected to take two hours per week of unbroken thinking time — no meetings, no Slack, no exceptions. Calendars are auto-blocked. The expectation is named in the offer letter.

This is our most controversial benefit, and the one customers comment on most. We hold it because the thing we are selling externally — Tuesday afternoons back, for the CHRO — has to be a thing we believe in internally. We do not sell what we do not do.

120 minutes · everyone · weekly · auto-blocked
Friday · 16:00 PT

The Friday founders' Slack.

A specific channel, #founders-friday, where the three founders post a short reflection on the week. Visible to all employees. Read-only for everyone else. The format is a paragraph, not a thread. The intent is to make the founders' weekly state legible without forcing a town hall.

Each founder posts one paragraph: what worked, what did not, what they changed their mind about. Helm posts first because she is most likely to set an honest tone. The entries are archived and searchable.

3 paragraphs · founders post, everyone reads · weekly
Sunday · before board check-in

The fortnightly briefing.

Sent to the Chairman on the Sunday before each board check-in. Signed by all three founders. The public version publishes the same day at stratum.ai/briefings. The most recent is Briefing 006.

The briefing is the canonical channel. If something belongs in the briefing and is not, that is a process failure, not a forgetting. The structure is fixed: an editor's letter, an executive snapshot, six numbered sections, and the decisions ledger appendix.

~3,500 words · fortnightly · signed by all three founders
§ 06 · How we hire

Four steps. Nine days, end to end.

The hiring process is the same for every role, from engineering to customer engineering. The steps are named, the rubrics are published, the average time-to-offer is nine days. The longer version lives on the careers page; the shorter version lives here.

Step 01

Intro.

Thirty minutes with the founder who owns the function. Not a screen. A real conversation about the question you most want to answer if you took this job. We are reading for specificity, not polish. If you want to ask us a hard question instead, that is the better use of the call.

~30 min · with the owning founder
Step 02

Craft.

A bounded piece of work, in your domain, that takes between two and four hours. The brief is real — drawn from an actual ledger entry — and your work is read by the founder and at least one engineer. We pay for the time, at $200 / hour, capped at four hours, paid the same week.

2–4 hrs · paid at $200/hr
Step 03

Architecture.

Ninety minutes with two founders or two senior engineers, depending on role. We walk through your craft submission, and then we walk through one of our open problems. We are looking for someone who can change their mind in front of us, in real time, when the data does.

90 min · with two senior people
Step 04

Compact.

A two-hour session with all three founders. We talk about the Compact, the principles, the trade-offs. We tell you the things that are likely to go wrong. You tell us why you are leaving what you have. If both sides still want each other after that conversation, we make the offer in writing the same day.

2 hrs · all three founders · offer same day

Median time from first call to written offer, across the first sixteen hires: nine days. The longest was Vector at sixteen days; she paused twice to think. We were grateful for both pauses.

§ 07 · How feedback travels

Three named channels. No anonymous ones.

Feedback that matters has a name attached to it and a route to a person who can act. We use three channels, each with a named owner. Anonymous channels create the illusion of safety without the accountability that makes feedback useful.

Customer → product

The Tuesday office hour.

Every customer engineer holds office hours every Tuesday at 14:00 PT. Anything raised in office hours becomes a candidate for the decisions ledger by Friday. Six of the last twenty ledger entries originated in an office hour. The customer is named in the entry, with their permission.

Employee → founder

The weekly thirty.

Every employee has a standing 30-minute weekly slot with one of the founders. The slot rotates among the three. No agenda is required. No notes are taken unless the employee asks for them. The slot belongs to the employee; if it goes unused for three weeks, the founder writes to ask whether the rhythm still works.

Founder → Chairman

The fortnightly briefing.

The canonical channel. Anything outside the briefing goes through the Chairman's preferred-cadence call — currently weekly, currently Wednesday at 07:30 PT. If the founders disagree on whether to escalate, the default is to escalate. We have used that default three times.

§ 08 · What we don't do

Five rituals we have deliberately not adopted.

The absence is the design. Every one of these is a thing most companies our age have started by now. We have looked at each one and decided against. We will revisit each at the next founder retrospective.

  1. ×
    Off-sites every quarter. We do two per year. The other two quarters are personal retreats — one week, unstructured, supported by the company. The argument is that forced togetherness on a fixed cadence is cheaper to the company than to the calendar of an engineer mid-deep-work.
  2. ×
    All-hands every Friday. We do them monthly. The cadence is by design. Weekly all-hands trains the company to expect a weekly performance from leadership; monthly all- hands trains the company to expect a real update once a month. The Friday founders' Slack covers the weekly need.
  3. ×
    30-day PIPs. We have a 7-day "have we got this right?" conversation followed by a 21-day plan; if the answer is still no at the end of the 21 days, we part ways with two months' severance and a written reference. The shorter clock is the kinder one, for both sides.
  4. ×
    Comp banding by location. We use US-equivalent banding adjusted to a published, public formula. The formula accounts for purchasing power, not arbitrary metro tiers. Two engineers doing the same work in San Francisco and Lisbon will see materially similar offers, in the local currency, with the formula transparent on the offer letter.
  5. ×
    Anonymous employee surveys. We use named surveys with anonymous-aggregate dashboards. The named answers are visible only to the founder pair the employee chooses, and are deleted on a sixty-day rolling window. The aggregate is published quarterly. The trade-off is intentional: signal over comfort.
§ 09 · The Stratum vocabulary

Words we use that may not mean what you expect.

A small glossary of internal terms. If you read the briefings or talk to one of us for an hour, you will encounter most of these. The definitions are deliberately specific.

The Compact The founder agreement of T+0d, eight clauses long, renewed each year at the founder retrospective. Section two of this handbook.
The Console Stratum's product. The agentic interface that turns a CHRO into a force of one thousand. Live at stratum.ai/console.
A decision A numbered entry in the decisions ledger, with a date, an owner, and a rationale. Not a conversation, not a Slack thread, not a verbal agreement.
The eval set The canonical question set against which the Console is measured. 250 questions, 28 fairness probes, 12 HCM-specific edge cases as of July 2026. Public.
Tuesday afternoons Both the buyer-promise (we give a CHRO her Tuesday afternoon back) and the internal practice (two hours of unbroken thinking time per employee per week).
A defensible answer What we sell. An answer the CHRO can take to her board, her CEO, or her regulator and defend on the evidence in the audit trail.
Customer engineering Our GTM motion. Engineers, not account executives, paid on renewal and depth, not on signature. Vector leads the function.
The graph Used two ways: the people graph (the data model — entities, edges, time) and the people graph (the underlying social structure of an enterprise). Context disambiguates.
